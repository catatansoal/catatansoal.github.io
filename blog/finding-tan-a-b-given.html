Finding Tan(A-B) Given Sin(A) And Tan(B) A Step By Step Guide

Jul 17, 2025 by ADMIN 62 views

Hey guys! Let's dive into a trigonometric adventure where we'll unravel the secrets of finding tan(A-B) given some juicy information about angles A and B. This problem might seem daunting at first, but fear not! We'll break it down step-by-step, making it as clear as a sunny day. So, buckle up and let's get started!

Understanding the Given Information

Before we jump into calculations, let's take a moment to truly grasp what we've been given. This is super crucial in trigonometry, as the relationships between angles and their trigonometric functions are the key to solving these puzzles. So, let's break down the given information bit by bit.

Sine of A and its Quadrant: We're told that the sine of angle A is 5/13, written mathematically as sin(A) = 5/13. Remember, the sine function corresponds to the y-coordinate on the unit circle. Since sin(A) is positive, we know that angle A must lie in either the first or second quadrant, where the y-coordinate is positive. But hold on! We also have another piece of the puzzle: π/2 < A < π. This inequality tells us that A lies between 90 degrees and 180 degrees, which pinpoints it specifically to the second quadrant. This is crucial because the quadrant dictates the signs of other trigonometric functions like cosine and tangent.

Tangent of B and its Quadrant: Next, we have information about angle B. We know that the tangent of angle B is -√13, expressed as tan(B) = -√13. Tangent, as you might recall, is the ratio of sine to cosine (or y-coordinate to x-coordinate on the unit circle). A negative tangent means that either sine is positive and cosine is negative, or sine is negative and cosine is positive. This corresponds to the second and fourth quadrants, respectively. But, just like with angle A, we have an additional constraint: π/2 < B < π. This again places angle B firmly in the second quadrant. This is super important because in the second quadrant, sine is positive, cosine is negative, and consequently, tangent is negative, which aligns perfectly with the given information.

Why is understanding the quadrants so vital? Because it allows us to determine the signs of the trigonometric functions, which are essential for accurate calculations. For example, knowing that A is in the second quadrant means that cos(A) will be negative, even though sin(A) is positive. This detail will be key when we start calculating the values we need for the tan(A-B) formula.

By carefully dissecting the given information and understanding the implications of the quadrants, we've laid a solid foundation for solving the problem. Now, we're ready to move on to the next step: finding the cosine of A and the sine and cosine of B.

Finding cos(A), sin(B), and cos(B)

Alright, now that we've nailed down the quadrants of angles A and B, it's time to roll up our sleeves and find the values of cos(A), sin(B), and cos(B). We'll use the Pythagorean identity and the information we've already gathered to make this happen. Let's tackle each one step-by-step.

Finding cos(A): We know that sin(A) = 5/13, and we also know the fundamental Pythagorean identity: sin²(A) + cos²(A) = 1. This identity is our best friend in trigonometry because it links sine and cosine, allowing us to find one if we know the other. So, let's plug in what we know:

(5/13)² + cos²(A) = 1

This simplifies to:

25/169 + cos²(A) = 1

Now, let's isolate cos²(A):

cos²(A) = 1 - 25/169 = 144/169

Taking the square root of both sides, we get:

cos(A) = ±√(144/169) = ±12/13

But remember, we determined that angle A lies in the second quadrant, where cosine is negative. Therefore, we choose the negative root:

cos(A) = -12/13

Finding sin(B) and cos(B): We are given that tan(B) = -√13. Remember that tan(B) = sin(B) / cos(B). We also know that sin²(B) + cos²(B) = 1. We have two equations and two unknowns, so we can solve for sin(B) and cos(B). Let's use a little trick to make things easier. We can think of tangent as the ratio of opposite to adjacent sides in a right triangle. Since tan(B) = -√13, we can imagine a triangle where the opposite side is √13 and the adjacent side is 1 (we'll deal with the negative sign later). The hypotenuse, using the Pythagorean theorem, would be √(1² + (√13)²) = √(1 + 13) = √14.

So, we can write:

sin(B) = opposite / hypotenuse = √13 / √14

cos(B) = adjacent / hypotenuse = 1 / √14

Now, we need to consider the signs. Since B is in the second quadrant, sine is positive, and cosine is negative. Therefore:

sin(B) = √13 / √14

cos(B) = -1 / √14

To rationalize the denominator, we can multiply the numerator and denominator of both by √14:

sin(B) = (√13 * √14) / 14 = √182 / 14

cos(B) = (-1 * √14) / 14 = -√14 / 14

Whew! That was a bit of work, but we've successfully found cos(A), sin(B), and cos(B). Now we're armed with all the ingredients we need to finally calculate tan(A-B). Let's move on to the exciting part!

Calculating tan(A-B) Using the Formula

Okay, guys, we've reached the grand finale! We've gathered all the necessary pieces of the puzzle, and now it's time to assemble them and find tan(A-B). The key to this lies in the tangent subtraction formula, which is a fundamental identity in trigonometry. So, let's revisit that formula and see how we can put it to work.

The Tangent Subtraction Formula: The formula for the tangent of the difference of two angles is:

tan(A - B) = (tan(A) - tan(B)) / (1 + tan(A) * tan(B))

This formula might look a bit intimidating, but don't worry, we'll take it step by step. We already know tan(B), but we still need to find tan(A). Let's calculate that first.

Finding tan(A): Remember that tan(A) = sin(A) / cos(A). We know that sin(A) = 5/13 and cos(A) = -12/13. So:

tan(A) = (5/13) / (-12/13) = 5/13 * -13/12 = -5/12

Now we have all the pieces we need! tan(A) = -5/12 and tan(B) = -√13. Let's plug these values into the tangent subtraction formula:

tan(A - B) = (-5/12 - (-√13)) / (1 + (-5/12) * (-√13))

Let's simplify this expression. First, let's deal with the double negative in the numerator:

tan(A - B) = (-5/12 + √13) / (1 + (-5/12) * (-√13))

Now, let's simplify the multiplication in the denominator:

tan(A - B) = (-5/12 + √13) / (1 + (5√13)/12)

To get rid of the fractions within the fraction, let's multiply both the numerator and the denominator by 12:

tan(A - B) = 12 * (-5/12 + √13) / 12 * (1 + (5√13)/12)

Distributing the 12, we get:

tan(A - B) = (-5 + 12√13) / (12 + 5√13)

This is a valid answer, but mathematicians often prefer to rationalize the denominator (get rid of the square root in the denominator). To do this, we'll multiply both the numerator and the denominator by the conjugate of the denominator, which is (12 - 5√13):

tan(A - B) = (-5 + 12√13) / (12 + 5√13) * (12 - 5√13) / (12 - 5√13)

Now, let's multiply out the numerator and denominator. Remember the difference of squares pattern: (a + b)(a - b) = a² - b²

Numerator:

(-5 + 12√13)(12 - 5√13) = -5 * 12 + -5 * -5√13 + 12√13 * 12 + 12√13 * -5√13

= -60 + 25√13 + 144√13 - 780

= -840 + 169√13

Denominator:

(12 + 5√13)(12 - 5√13) = 12² - (5√13)²

= 144 - 25 * 13

= 144 - 325

= -181

So, we have:

tan(A - B) = (-840 + 169√13) / -181

We can multiply both the numerator and denominator by -1 to get a positive denominator:

tan(A - B) = (840 - 169√13) / 181

And there you have it! We've successfully calculated tan(A-B). It was a journey filled with trigonometric identities and careful calculations, but we made it! This final answer represents the tangent of the angle formed by subtracting angle B from angle A, given the initial conditions.

Final Answer

The final answer for tan(A-B) is:

(840 - 169√13) / 181

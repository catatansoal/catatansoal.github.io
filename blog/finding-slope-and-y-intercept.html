Finding Slope And Y-intercept Of 3(y-2) + 6(x+1) - 2 = 0

Jul 16, 2025 by ADMIN 57 views

Hey guys! Let's dive into a fundamental concept in algebra: slope and y-intercept. Understanding these two elements is crucial for grasping linear equations and their graphical representation. In this article, we'll break down the process of finding the slope and y-intercept of a linear equation, using a specific example to illustrate the steps. So, buckle up and get ready to conquer those lines!

Decoding the Equation: Finding the Slope and Y-intercept

To effectively determine the slope and y-intercept, we need to manipulate the given equation into a familiar form. The equation we're tackling today is:

3(y - 2) + 6(x + 1) - 2 = 0

Our mission is to transform this equation into the slope-intercept form, which is:

y = mx + b

Where:

  • m represents the slope of the line.
  • b represents the y-intercept, the point where the line crosses the y-axis.

By rearranging the equation into this form, we can directly read off the values of the slope and y-intercept. Let's get started!

Step 1: Simplifying the Equation

The first step is to simplify the equation by expanding the brackets and combining like terms. This will help us to isolate the 'y' term and eventually get the equation into the desired slope-intercept form.

Starting with the given equation:

3(y - 2) + 6(x + 1) - 2 = 0

Distribute the constants:

3y - 6 + 6x + 6 - 2 = 0

Now, combine the constant terms:

3y + 6x - 2 = 0

Step 2: Isolating the 'y' Term

Our next goal is to isolate the 'y' term on one side of the equation. To do this, we'll subtract 6x from both sides and add 2 to both sides:

3y + 6x - 2 - 6x + 2 = 0 - 6x + 2

This simplifies to:

3y = -6x + 2

Step 3: Achieving Slope-Intercept Form

To get the equation into the exact y = mx + b form, we need to divide both sides of the equation by 3:

(3y) / 3 = (-6x + 2) / 3

This gives us:

y = -2x + 2/3

Aha! We've successfully transformed the equation into slope-intercept form.

Step 4: Identifying the Slope and Y-intercept

Now that our equation is in the form y = mx + b, we can easily identify the slope and y-intercept:

  • Slope (m): The coefficient of the x term is the slope. In this case, m = -2. This means the line has a negative slope, indicating it goes downwards as you move from left to right.
  • Y-intercept (b): The constant term is the y-intercept. Here, b = 2/3. This tells us that the line crosses the y-axis at the point (0, 2/3).

Therefore, the slope of the line is -2, and the y-intercept is 2/3. Woohoo! We cracked it!

Visualizing the Line: Understanding Slope and Y-intercept Graphically

To solidify our understanding, let's visualize what the slope and y-intercept tell us about the line's graph. The slope, as we discovered, is -2. Remember that slope is often described as "rise over run." A slope of -2 can be interpreted as -2/1, meaning for every 1 unit you move to the right along the x-axis, the line goes down 2 units along the y-axis. This gives the line a steep, downward slant.

The y-intercept, which we found to be 2/3, is the point where the line intersects the y-axis. On a graph, this would be the point (0, 2/3). It's the starting point of our line, the place where it crosses the vertical axis.

Imagine plotting the point (0, 2/3) on a graph. Then, using the slope of -2, you would move 1 unit to the right and 2 units down to find another point on the line. Connecting these points (and extending the line in both directions) gives you the visual representation of the equation y = -2x + 2/3. This visual connection between the equation and its graph is a powerful tool for understanding linear equations.

Why is This Important? Real-World Applications of Slope and Y-intercept

You might be thinking, "Okay, we found the slope and y-intercept, but why does it matter?" Well, linear equations are everywhere in the real world, and understanding slope and y-intercept can help us model and analyze various situations. Let's look at a couple of examples:

  • Cost Analysis: Imagine a taxi service charges a flat fee (the y-intercept) plus a certain amount per mile (the slope). The equation representing the total cost would be linear, and knowing the slope and y-intercept allows you to predict the cost for any distance.
  • Distance and Time: If you're driving at a constant speed, the relationship between distance traveled and time elapsed can be represented by a linear equation. The slope would be your speed, and the y-intercept could represent your starting position.
  • Sales and Revenue: A business might use a linear equation to model the relationship between the number of products sold and their total revenue. The slope would be the price per product, and the y-intercept could represent any fixed costs.

These are just a few examples, but they illustrate how the concepts of slope and y-intercept can be applied to real-world problems, making them valuable tools in various fields.

Common Pitfalls and How to Avoid Them

When working with slope and y-intercept, there are a few common mistakes that students often make. Being aware of these pitfalls can help you avoid them and ensure you get the correct answer.

  • Forgetting to Simplify: The most crucial step is to simplify the equation into slope-intercept form (y = mx + b). Trying to identify the slope and y-intercept from a more complex equation can lead to errors. Always simplify first!
  • Misinterpreting the Sign of the Slope: A negative slope indicates a downward-sloping line, while a positive slope indicates an upward-sloping line. Pay close attention to the sign of the slope when interpreting the graph.
  • Confusing Slope and Y-intercept: The slope is the coefficient of the 'x' term, while the y-intercept is the constant term. Don't mix them up!
  • Incorrectly Calculating the Y-intercept: The y-intercept is the value of 'y' when 'x' is 0. Make sure you substitute 'x = 0' into the equation to find the correct y-intercept if you're not using the slope-intercept form.

By keeping these common pitfalls in mind and double-checking your work, you can confidently tackle any slope and y-intercept problem.

Wrapping Up: Mastering Linear Equations

So, there you have it! We've successfully navigated the process of finding the slope and y-intercept of a linear equation. By simplifying the equation, isolating 'y', and transforming it into slope-intercept form, we were able to easily identify these key elements. Remember, the slope tells us about the line's steepness and direction, while the y-intercept tells us where the line crosses the y-axis.

Understanding slope and y-intercept is a fundamental step in mastering linear equations. These concepts have wide-ranging applications in various fields, making them essential tools for problem-solving and analysis. Keep practicing, and you'll become a pro at decoding lines in no time!

Understanding Compound Inequalities

So, what exactly are compound inequalities? Well, in simple terms, a compound inequality is just two or more inequalities joined together. These inequalities are usually linked by the words "and" or "or." The "and" type means that both inequalities must be true at the same time, while the "or" type means that at least one of the inequalities needs to be true. Think of it like this: if you have an "and" compound inequality, you're looking for the overlap between the solutions of each individual inequality. If it's an "or" compound inequality, you're looking at the combined solutions of both. Compound inequalities are a fundamental concept in mathematics, appearing in various areas like calculus, linear programming, and even in real-world applications like setting constraints in optimization problems. Understanding how to solve them provides a solid foundation for more advanced mathematical concepts.

When dealing with compound inequalities, it's crucial to grasp the concept of intervals. An interval is simply a range of numbers. For example, the interval [1, 5] includes all numbers between 1 and 5, including 1 and 5 themselves. Parentheses ( ) are used to indicate that the endpoint is not included, while square brackets [ ] mean the endpoint is included. So, (1, 5) would include all numbers between 1 and 5, but not 1 and 5. Compound inequalities often define intervals on the number line, and visualizing these intervals can make the solution process much clearer. Remember, an "and" compound inequality represents the intersection of two intervals, while an "or" compound inequality represents the union of two intervals. Grasping these interval concepts is key to accurately solving and interpreting compound inequalities. Let's move on to our specific example and see how this works in practice!

Breaking Down the Problem: 5 < (4x - 16)/8 < 8

Alright, let's tackle the compound inequality we have: 5 < (4x - 16)/8 < 8. This looks a bit intimidating at first glance, but don't worry, we'll break it down step-by-step. Notice that this is an "and" compound inequality because we're saying that (4x - 16)/8 is both greater than 5 and less than 8. The main goal here is to isolate 'x' in the middle, just like we would do with a regular equation. The key difference with inequalities is that we need to be mindful of how operations affect the inequality signs. Multiplying or dividing by a negative number flips the inequality sign, but we don't need to worry about that in this particular problem.

To isolate 'x', we need to undo the operations that are being applied to it, working in reverse order of operations. So, first, we'll get rid of the division by 8. Remember, whatever we do to one part of the inequality, we must do to all parts to maintain the balance. This is a crucial rule when working with inequalities. We are dealing with three parts here instead of two like in a normal inequality, so be careful to apply your operations to all 3 parts. This principle is essential for ensuring that the solution we obtain is accurate and reflects the true range of values for 'x' that satisfy the original compound inequality. This process of maintaining balance is a recurring theme in solving any type of inequality, so keeping this concept firmly in mind will pay dividends as you tackle more complex problems. Next, we'll move on to the next step: multiplying each part of the inequality by 8. This will help us eliminate the fraction and bring us closer to isolating 'x'.

Step-by-Step Solution

Okay, let's get our hands dirty and walk through the solution step-by-step. Remember, our compound inequality is: 5 < (4x - 16)/8 < 8.

Step 1: Multiply all parts by 8

To get rid of the fraction, we multiply all three parts of the inequality by 8. This gives us:

8 * 5 < 8 * (4x - 16)/8 < 8 * 8

Which simplifies to:

40 < 4x - 16 < 64

Step 2: Add 16 to all parts

Next, we want to isolate the term with 'x', so we add 16 to all parts of the inequality:

40 + 16 < 4x - 16 + 16 < 64 + 16

This simplifies to:

56 < 4x < 80

Step 3: Divide all parts by 4

Finally, to isolate 'x', we divide all parts of the inequality by 4:

56 / 4 < 4x / 4 < 80 / 4

This gives us our final solution:

14 < x < 20

So, what does this mean? It means that 'x' can be any number greater than 14 but less than 20. We've successfully solved the compound inequality! This step of isolating 'x' is the crucial part of solving any inequality, whether simple or compound. By systematically undoing the operations applied to 'x', we gradually narrow down the possible values until we arrive at the solution. Remember, each operation must be applied to all parts of the inequality to maintain balance and ensure the accuracy of the solution. Now that we have our solution, let's see how to represent it graphically on a number line, which will provide a visual understanding of the range of values that 'x' can take.

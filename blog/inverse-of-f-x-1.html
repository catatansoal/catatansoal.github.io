Inverse Of F(x) = (1/9)x + 2: Step-by-Step Solution

Aug 5, 2025 by ADMIN 52 views

Hey guys! Ever wondered how to reverse a function? Like, if a function takes you from point A to point B, how do you get back from B to A? That's where the concept of an inverse function comes in super handy. Today, we're going to dive deep into finding the inverse of the function f(x) = (1/9)x + 2. We'll break down the steps, explain the logic, and make sure you're a pro at this by the end! So, let's get started and unlock the secrets of inverse functions!

Understanding Inverse Functions

Before we jump into solving for the inverse of f(x) = (1/9)x + 2, let's get a solid grasp on what inverse functions actually are. Think of a function like a machine. You feed it an input (x), and it spits out an output (f(x)). An inverse function is like a machine that does the opposite. It takes the output of the original function as its input and spits out the original input.

  • The Key Idea: If f(a) = b, then the inverse function, often denoted as f⁻¹(x), should satisfy f⁻¹(b) = a. This is the fundamental relationship that defines inverse functions. They "undo" each other.
  • Notation: The inverse of f(x) is written as f⁻¹(x). It's important to note that the "-1" is not an exponent; it's just a symbol to indicate the inverse. So, f⁻¹(x) does NOT mean 1/f(x).
  • Graphical Representation: If you graph a function and its inverse on the same coordinate plane, you'll notice a cool symmetry. The graphs are reflections of each other across the line y = x. This visual representation can be super helpful in understanding the relationship between a function and its inverse.

Why are inverse functions important, you ask? Well, they pop up everywhere in math and science! They're used in solving equations, cryptography, computer graphics, and many other fields. Understanding how to find and work with inverse functions is a crucial skill for anyone diving deeper into these areas. For instance, consider the function that converts Celsius to Fahrenheit. Its inverse would convert Fahrenheit back to Celsius. Pretty neat, huh?

Finding the Inverse of f(x) = (1/9)x + 2: Step-by-Step

Okay, now that we've got the basics down, let's tackle the main event: finding the inverse of f(x) = (1/9)x + 2. We'll go through each step super clearly so you can follow along easily. Grab your pencil and paper, and let's do this!

Step 1: Replace f(x) with y

This is a simple but helpful first step. It just makes the equation a little easier to work with. So, we rewrite f(x) = (1/9)x + 2 as:

y = (1/9)x + 2

Think of y as representing the output of the function for a given input x. This substitution helps us visualize the relationship we're trying to reverse.

Step 2: Swap x and y

This is the heart of finding the inverse! We're essentially saying, "Let's see what happens if we switch the roles of input and output." So, we replace every x with y and every y with x:

x = (1/9)y + 2

This step reflects the fundamental idea of an inverse function: it reverses the roles of input and output. The new equation now represents the inverse relationship.

Step 3: Solve for y

Now we need to isolate y on one side of the equation. This will give us the inverse function in the form y = .... Let's go through the steps:

  1. Subtract 2 from both sides:

    x - 2 = (1/9)y

    We're undoing the addition of 2 that was part of the original function.

  2. Multiply both sides by 9:

    9(x - 2) = y

    This undoes the multiplication by (1/9) in the original function.

  3. Distribute the 9:

    9x - 18 = y

    This simplifies the expression and gives us y in terms of x.

So, we've found that y = 9x - 18. This is the inverse function!

Step 4: Replace y with f⁻¹(x)

To show that this is the inverse function, we use the proper notation:

f⁻¹(x) = 9x - 18

And there you have it! We've successfully found the inverse of f(x) = (1/9)x + 2.

Verifying the Inverse Function

To be absolutely sure we've got the right inverse, it's always a good idea to verify our answer. Remember that inverse functions "undo" each other. This means that if we plug the inverse function into the original function (or vice versa), we should get back just x. Let's check:

Method 1: f(f⁻¹(x)) = x

  1. Plug f⁻¹(x) into f(x):

    f(f⁻¹(x)) = f(9x - 18) = (1/9)(9x - 18) + 2

  2. Simplify:

    (1/9)(9x - 18) + 2 = x - 2 + 2 = x

    We got x! That's a good sign.

Method 2: f⁻¹(f(x)) = x

  1. Plug f(x) into f⁻¹(x):

    f⁻¹(f(x)) = f⁻¹((1/9)x + 2) = 9((1/9)x + 2) - 18

  2. Simplify:

    9((1/9)x + 2) - 18 = x + 18 - 18 = x

    We got x again! This confirms that f⁻¹(x) = 9x - 18 is indeed the inverse of f(x) = (1/9)x + 2.

So, we've not only found the inverse but also verified it. We're on a roll!

The Correct Answer and Why

Looking back at the options, we can now confidently identify the correct answer:

  • A. h(x) = 18x - 2
  • B. h(x) = 9x - 18 (This is our answer!)
  • C. h(x) = 9x + 18
  • D. h(x) = 18x + 2

Option B, h(x) = 9x - 18, is the correct inverse function. We arrived at this answer by following the steps of replacing f(x) with y, swapping x and y, solving for y, and then replacing y with f⁻¹(x). We also verified our answer to be sure!

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Finding inverse functions can be a bit tricky at first, so it's helpful to be aware of common mistakes. Here are a few to watch out for:

  • Forgetting to Swap x and y: This is the most crucial step! If you don't swap x and y, you won't be finding the inverse. You'll just be rearranging the original equation.
  • Incorrectly Solving for y: Make sure you're using the correct order of operations and algebraic manipulations to isolate y. Double-check your work, especially when dealing with fractions or negative signs.
  • Confusing f⁻¹(x) with 1/f(x): Remember, f⁻¹(x) is the inverse function, while 1/f(x) is the reciprocal of the function. They are not the same thing!
  • Skipping Verification: Always verify your answer! It's a quick way to catch errors and ensure you've found the correct inverse.

By being aware of these common pitfalls, you'll be well on your way to mastering inverse functions.

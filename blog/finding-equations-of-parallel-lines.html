Finding Equations Of Parallel Lines In Slope-Intercept Form

Jul 14, 2025 by ADMIN 60 views

Hey guys! Today, we're diving into a classic math problem: finding the equation of a line that's parallel to another line and passes through a specific point. It might sound a bit tricky at first, but trust me, we'll break it down step-by-step so it's super easy to understand.

Understanding Parallel Lines and Slope-Intercept Form

Before we jump into the problem, let's quickly review the key concepts. Parallel lines are lines that run in the same direction and never intersect. The most important thing to remember about parallel lines is that they have the same slope. The slope is a measure of how steep a line is, and it's represented by the letter 'm' in the slope-intercept form of a linear equation.

The slope-intercept form is a super useful way to write the equation of a line, and it looks like this:

y = mx + b

Where:

  • 'y' is the dependent variable (usually plotted on the vertical axis)
  • 'x' is the independent variable (usually plotted on the horizontal axis)
  • 'm' is the slope of the line
  • 'b' is the y-intercept (the point where the line crosses the y-axis)

Knowing this form is crucial because it allows us to easily identify the slope and y-intercept of a line, and it's exactly what we need to solve our problem.

Our Problem: A Step-by-Step Solution

Okay, let's get to the problem at hand. We're given a line with the equation:

10x + 2y = -2

And we need to find the equation of a line that's parallel to this one and passes through the point (0, 12). Let's break this down into manageable steps.

Step 1: Convert to Slope-Intercept Form

The first thing we need to do is get the given equation into slope-intercept form (y = mx + b). This will allow us to easily identify the slope of the line.

To do this, we need to isolate 'y' on one side of the equation. Here's how:

  1. Subtract 10x from both sides:

    2y = -10x - 2

  2. Divide both sides by 2:

    y = -5x - 1

Now we have the equation in slope-intercept form! We can clearly see that the slope of the given line is -5 (the coefficient of 'x'). This is a critical piece of information because any line parallel to this one will also have a slope of -5.

Step 2: Use the Parallel Slope

Remember, parallel lines have the same slope. So, the line we're trying to find also has a slope of -5. We now know the 'm' part of our equation: y = -5x + b.

Step 3: Find the y-intercept (b)

We know the slope of our parallel line, but we still need to find the y-intercept ('b'). This is where the point (0, 12) comes in. We know that our line passes through this point, which means that when x = 0, y = 12. We can plug these values into our equation and solve for 'b'.

12 = -5(0) + b

12 = 0 + b

b = 12

So, the y-intercept of our line is 12.

Step 4: Write the Equation

We now have everything we need to write the equation of the parallel line in slope-intercept form. We know the slope (m = -5) and the y-intercept (b = 12). So, we can plug these values into the y = mx + b equation:

y = -5x + 12

And there you have it! The equation of the line parallel to 10x + 2y = -2 and passing through the point (0, 12) is y = -5x + 12.

Key Takeaways

Let's recap the key steps we took to solve this problem:

  1. Convert the given equation to slope-intercept form to find its slope.
  2. Use the fact that parallel lines have the same slope.
  3. Plug the given point into the equation and solve for the y-intercept.
  4. Write the equation in slope-intercept form using the slope and y-intercept.

Practice Makes Perfect

Finding the equation of a parallel line is a fundamental skill in algebra. The more you practice these types of problems, the easier they'll become. Try working through similar problems with different equations and points to solidify your understanding. You got this!

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Forgetting to convert to slope-intercept form: You absolutely need to get the original equation into y = mx + b form to easily identify the slope.
  • Using the wrong slope: Remember, parallel lines have the same slope, not the opposite or reciprocal slope.
  • Incorrectly solving for the y-intercept: Double-check your algebra when plugging in the point and solving for 'b'.
  • Not writing the final equation in slope-intercept form: The question specifically asks for the equation in slope-intercept form, so make sure your answer is in the y = mx + b format.

Real-World Applications

You might be wondering, "Where would I ever use this in the real world?" Well, the concept of parallel lines and their equations comes up in various fields:

  • Architecture and Engineering: Architects and engineers use parallel lines to design structures, buildings, and roads. Understanding the relationships between parallel lines is crucial for creating accurate and stable designs.
  • Computer Graphics: In computer graphics and animation, parallel lines are used to create 2D and 3D models, as well as to simulate perspective and depth.
  • Navigation: Parallel lines can be used to represent paths or routes that maintain a constant distance from each other, which can be helpful in navigation and mapping.
  • Physics: In physics, the concept of parallel lines is used to describe the motion of objects in a uniform field, such as the motion of a projectile under the influence of gravity.

Further Exploration

If you're interested in learning more about lines and their equations, here are some topics you might want to explore:

  • Perpendicular lines: These lines intersect at a 90-degree angle. Their slopes are negative reciprocals of each other.
  • Point-slope form: Another way to write the equation of a line, using a point and the slope.
  • Systems of linear equations: Solving for the intersection point of two or more lines.
  • Linear inequalities: Graphing and solving inequalities involving lines.

Conclusion

So, there you have it! Finding the equation of a parallel line is a straightforward process once you understand the key concepts. Remember to convert to slope-intercept form, use the same slope, and solve for the y-intercept. Keep practicing, and you'll master this skill in no time. Happy problem-solving, everyone!

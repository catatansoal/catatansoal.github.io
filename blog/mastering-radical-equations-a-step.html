Understanding Radical Equations

Let's start with the basics: What exactly is a radical equation? Simply put, a radical equation is an equation where the variable is stuck inside a radical, like a square root, cube root, or any other root. Inside the radical, we have the radicand, which is the expression or number whose root we're trying to find. The index tells us what kind of root we're dealing with â€“ a square root (index of 2, usually unwritten), a cube root (index of 3), and so on.

To effectively solve radical equations, we need to isolate the radical term first. This means getting the radical expression all by itself on one side of the equation. Why do we do this? Because once the radical is isolated, we can eliminate it by raising both sides of the equation to the power that matches the index of the radical. For example, if we have a square root, we'll square both sides. If it's a cube root, we'll cube both sides, and so on. This process is based on the principle that performing the same operation on both sides of an equation maintains the equality. However, this step is also where we need to be extra careful, as raising both sides to an even power can sometimes introduce extraneous solutions. Extraneous solutions are solutions that we get during the solving process, but they don't actually satisfy the original equation. We'll talk more about how to identify and eliminate these pesky solutions later.

Another key thing to remember is the order of operations. When solving any equation, including radical ones, we need to follow the proper order of operations (PEMDAS/BODMAS). This means dealing with parentheses/brackets first, then exponents/orders, then multiplication and division (from left to right), and finally addition and subtraction (from left to right). Keeping the order of operations in mind will help prevent errors and ensure we solve the equation correctly. Also, remember that when squaring a binomial (an expression with two terms), we need to use the FOIL method (First, Outer, Inner, Last) or the binomial theorem to expand it properly. For example, (x+2)2(x + 2)^2(x+2)2 is not equal to x2+22x^2 + 2^2x2+22. Instead, it expands to x2+4x+4x^2 + 4x + 4x2+4x+4. Making mistakes in expanding binomials is a common pitfall in solving radical equations, so pay close attention to this step!

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Solving radical equations can be tricky, and there are a few common mistakes that students often make. Let's go over these so you can avoid them!

  • Forgetting to Isolate the Radical: As we've emphasized, isolating the radical is the first and most crucial step. Trying to eliminate the radical before it's isolated will lead to errors and make the problem much more complicated. Always make sure the radical term is all by itself on one side of the equation before you square, cube, or raise it to any power.
  • Forgetting to Check for Extraneous Solutions: This is the biggest mistake students make! As we've discussed, squaring both sides (or raising to any even power) can introduce extraneous solutions. You must check your solutions in the original equation to make sure they're valid.
  • Squaring Binomials Incorrectly: When you have an expression like (x+2)2(\sqrt{x} + 2)^2(xâ€‹+2)2, you can't just square each term individually.   • Making Arithmetic Errors: Simple arithmetic mistakes can derail your entire solution. Be careful with your calculations, especially when dealing with negative numbers and fractions. Double-check your work at each step to avoid these errors.

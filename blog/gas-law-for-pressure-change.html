Gas Law For Pressure Change: Combined Gas Law Explained

Aug 7, 2025 by ADMIN 56 views

Hey guys! Let's dive into a cool chemistry problem today that involves figuring out how the pressure of an air sample changes when we mess with its temperature and volume. This kind of problem is super common in chemistry, especially when you're dealing with gases. We'll break down the problem step by step and make sure you understand exactly which law to use and why. So, grab your thinking caps, and let's get started!

So, here’s the situation: We have a 6.00-liter sample of air. This air is hanging out at a pressure of 207 kilopascals and a temperature of 50°C. Now, imagine we decide to crank up the heat and let the volume expand. The temperature is raised to 202°C, and the volume grows to 9.0 liters. The big question here is: Which gas law should we use to figure out the new pressure? This is where things get interesting, and we need to pick the right tool for the job.

Understanding the Gas Laws

Before we jump into solving this, let's do a quick review of gas laws. These laws are like the magic formulas that help us understand how gases behave under different conditions. There are a few key players we need to know about:

  • Boyle's Law: This law is all about the relationship between pressure and volume when the temperature stays the same. It says that if you squeeze a gas (decrease the volume), the pressure goes up, and vice versa.

  • Charles's Law: Charles's Law focuses on how volume and temperature are related when the pressure is constant. Heat up a gas, and it expands; cool it down, and it contracts.

  • Gay-Lussac's Law: This one links pressure and temperature when the volume is constant. Increase the temperature, and the pressure goes up; decrease the temperature, and the pressure drops.

  • The Combined Gas Law: Now, this is the superstar for problems like ours. The Combined Gas Law puts Boyle's, Charles's, and Gay-Lussac's Laws all together. It's perfect for situations where pressure, volume, and temperature are all changing. It's expressed as:

    (P₁V₁)/T₁ = (P₂V₂)/T₂

    Where:

    • P₁ is the initial pressure
    • V₁ is the initial volume
    • T₁ is the initial temperature
    • P₂ is the final pressure
    • V₂ is the final volume
    • T₂ is the final temperature

  • The Ideal Gas Law: Last but not least, we have the Ideal Gas Law, which is a bit different. It relates pressure, volume, temperature, and the number of moles of gas (n). It’s written as:

    PV = nRT

    Where:

    • P is the pressure
    • V is the volume
    • n is the number of moles
    • R is the ideal gas constant
    • T is the temperature

    The Ideal Gas Law is fantastic for finding out things like the number of moles or the molar mass of a gas, but it’s not what we need for this particular problem.

Identifying the Right Law for Our Problem

Okay, so we've got our toolbox of gas laws. The big question is, which one do we reach for? In our problem, we have changes happening in pressure, volume, and temperature. That’s the key! Whenever you see all three of these variables changing, the Combined Gas Law is your best friend. This law is specifically designed to handle situations where you have initial and final conditions for pressure, volume, and temperature.

Why Not the Other Laws?

You might be wondering, “Why not use Boyle’s Law, Charles’s Law, or Gay-Lussac’s Law?” Well, those laws only work when one of the variables is kept constant. For example, Boyle’s Law is great if the temperature doesn’t change, but in our case, it does. Similarly, Charles’s Law needs constant pressure, and Gay-Lussac’s Law needs constant volume. Since we have changes in both volume and temperature, these individual laws won’t cut it.

Applying the Combined Gas Law: A Step-by-Step Guide

Now that we've identified the Combined Gas Law as our go-to formula, let’s walk through how to use it. It might seem a little daunting at first, but trust me, it’s pretty straightforward once you break it down.

Step 1: Write Down What You Know

The first thing we need to do is list all the information we have from the problem. This helps us keep everything organized and makes sure we don’t miss anything important.

  • Initial Pressure (P₁): 207 kilopascals (kPa)
  • Initial Volume (V₁): 6.00 liters (L)
  • Initial Temperature (T₁): 50°C
  • Final Volume (V₂): 9.0 liters (L)
  • Final Temperature (T₂): 202°C
  • Final Pressure (P₂): This is what we want to find out!

Step 2: Convert Temperatures to Kelvin

Here’s a crucial step that’s easy to overlook: We need to convert our temperatures from Celsius to Kelvin. Why? Because gas laws work properly with absolute temperature scales, and Kelvin is the absolute scale we use in science. To convert from Celsius to Kelvin, we use the following formula:

Kelvin = Celsius + 273.15

So, let’s do the conversions:

  • Initial Temperature (T₁): 50°C + 273.15 = 323.15 K
  • Final Temperature (T₂): 202°C + 273.15 = 475.15 K

Step 3: Write Down the Combined Gas Law Formula

Next up, let’s write down the Combined Gas Law formula. This will keep us on track and remind us what we’re working with:

(P₁V₁)/T₁ = (P₂V₂)/T₂

Step 4: Plug in the Values

Now comes the fun part: plugging in the values we’ve gathered into the formula. Let’s substitute our known quantities:

(207 kPa * 6.00 L) / 323.15 K = (P₂ * 9.0 L) / 475.15 K

Step 5: Solve for the Unknown (P₂)

Our goal is to find the final pressure (P₂). To do this, we need to rearrange the equation to isolate P₂. Here’s how we can do it:

  1. First, multiply both sides of the equation by 475.15 K to get rid of the denominator on the right side:

    (207 kPa * 6.00 L * 475.15 K) / 323.15 K = P₂ * 9.0 L

  2. Next, divide both sides by 9.0 L to isolate P₂:

    P₂ = (207 kPa * 6.00 L * 475.15 K) / (323.15 K * 9.0 L)

  3. Now, let’s do the math:

    P₂ = (590398.65 kPa * L * K) / (2908.35 K * L) P₂ ≈ 203.01 kPa

So, the new pressure (P₂) is approximately 203.01 kilopascals.

Conclusion: Mastering Gas Law Problems

Alright, guys, we did it! We figured out which gas law to use and calculated the new pressure of the air sample. The key takeaway here is that when you have changes in pressure, volume, and temperature, the Combined Gas Law is your go-to formula. By breaking the problem down into manageable steps—listing known values, converting temperatures to Kelvin, writing down the formula, plugging in values, and solving for the unknown—you can tackle even the trickiest gas law problems.

Remember, chemistry is all about understanding the relationships between different variables. The gas laws give us a powerful tool to predict how gases will behave under various conditions. So keep practicing, and you’ll become a gas law master in no time!

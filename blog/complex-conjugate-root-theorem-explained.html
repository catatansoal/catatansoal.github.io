Complex Conjugate Root Theorem Explained If -3+i Is A Root

Jul 18, 2025 by ADMIN 59 views

Hey everyone! Let's dive into a fascinating concept in mathematics – complex conjugate roots. If you've ever wondered about the relationship between complex numbers and polynomial functions, you're in the right place. Today, we're tackling a specific question: If -3 + i is a root of the polynomial function f(x), which of the following must also be a root? This is a classic problem that tests your understanding of how complex roots behave, especially in polynomials with real coefficients. Let's break it down together!

The Complex Conjugate Root Theorem

The core concept we need to grasp here is the Complex Conjugate Root Theorem. This theorem is a cornerstone in understanding polynomial behavior, particularly when dealing with complex numbers. In essence, it states: If a polynomial with real coefficients has a complex number (a + bi) as a root, then its complex conjugate (a - bi) must also be a root. Let's unpack this a bit.

Firstly, what are complex numbers? These are numbers that can be expressed in the form a + bi, where a and b are real numbers, and i is the imaginary unit, defined as the square root of -1. The real part of the complex number is a, and the imaginary part is b. For instance, in the number -3 + i, -3 is the real part, and 1 (since i is the same as 1i) is the imaginary part. The conjugate of a complex number is simply the number with the sign of the imaginary part flipped. So, the complex conjugate of a + bi is a - bi. This seemingly simple change has profound implications for the roots of polynomials.

Now, why does this theorem hold true? The reason lies in the nature of polynomial equations and how complex numbers interact with real coefficients during polynomial operations. When we perform operations like addition, subtraction, multiplication, and exponentiation with complex numbers, the imaginary parts can only cancel out if there's a corresponding conjugate present. Think of it as a balancing act. If a polynomial has real coefficients, it means that all the coefficients are real numbers. If a complex number is a root, plugging it into the polynomial will result in zero. But because of the way complex numbers interact, the imaginary parts won't simply vanish unless their conjugates are also roots, ensuring that the entire expression equals zero. This ensures that when we expand and simplify the polynomial, all imaginary terms cancel out, leaving us with a polynomial with real coefficients.

To illustrate, consider a simple quadratic equation with real coefficients that has complex roots. For example, let's say we have a quadratic equation with roots 2 + i and 2 - i. The equation would look something like this: (x - (2 + i))(x - (2 - i)) = 0. When you expand this, you'll find that the imaginary terms neatly cancel each other out, resulting in a quadratic equation with real coefficients. This is not a coincidence; it's a direct consequence of the Complex Conjugate Root Theorem. The theorem is not just a theoretical concept; it has practical implications in various areas of mathematics, including algebra, calculus, and differential equations.

Understanding the theorem also helps us to construct polynomials with specific roots. If we know that a polynomial with real coefficients has a complex root, we automatically know another root, which simplifies the process of finding the polynomial's equation. This can be particularly useful in reverse engineering problems where we are given roots and asked to find the polynomial. Furthermore, the Complex Conjugate Root Theorem is a specific case of a more general theorem related to irrational roots. If a polynomial with rational coefficients has an irrational root of the form a + √b, where a and b are rational and √b is irrational, then a - √b must also be a root. This broader principle highlights the symmetrical nature of roots in polynomials with certain coefficient constraints.

Applying the Theorem to Our Problem: -3 + i

Now, let's bring it back to our original problem. We're given that -3 + i is a root of the polynomial function f(x). Our mission is to figure out which of the provided options must also be a root. Remember the Complex Conjugate Root Theorem we just discussed? It's our guiding star here.

The theorem tells us that if a polynomial with real coefficients has a complex root, then its conjugate is also a root. So, what's the conjugate of -3 + i? To find the complex conjugate, we simply flip the sign of the imaginary part. In this case, the imaginary part is +i, so we change it to -i. This gives us -3 - i.

Therefore, according to the Complex Conjugate Root Theorem, if -3 + i is a root of f(x), then -3 - i must also be a root, provided that f(x) has real coefficients. This is a crucial condition. The theorem only applies to polynomials with real coefficients. If the polynomial had complex coefficients, this wouldn't necessarily be true. But in most standard problems, we're dealing with polynomials that do have real coefficients, making the theorem directly applicable. Let’s consider why the condition of real coefficients is so important. If the coefficients were complex, the balancing act that ensures the imaginary parts cancel out might not occur. A polynomial with complex coefficients can have complex roots without their conjugates also being roots.

For instance, think about the simple linear equation x - i = 0. This equation has only one root, which is i, a complex number. There's no conjugate pair here, and that's perfectly fine because the coefficient of x is 1, which is real, but the constant term is -i, which is imaginary. The Complex Conjugate Root Theorem doesn’t apply in this case because not all coefficients are real. This highlights the specific nature of the theorem and the importance of checking the conditions before applying it.

Now, you might be wondering, what if we were given a polynomial and asked to verify this? How would we go about it? One way is to construct the polynomial. If -3 + i and -3 - i are roots, then (x - (-3 + i)) and (x - (-3 - i)) must be factors of the polynomial. Multiplying these factors together should give us a polynomial with real coefficients. Let's do the math: (x - (-3 + i))(x - (-3 - i)) = (x + 3 - i)(x + 3 + i). Expanding this, we get x² + 3x + xi + 3x + 9 + 3i - xi - 3i - i². Notice how the xi and -xi terms cancel out, as do the 3i and -3i terms. Also, remember that i² = -1, so we can simplify further to x² + 6x + 9 - (-1) = x² + 6x + 10. This is a quadratic polynomial with real coefficients, and it confirms our understanding of the theorem. This process demonstrates not only the validity of the theorem but also how we can use it to build polynomials with specific roots.

Identifying the Correct Option

Alright, we've armed ourselves with the Complex Conjugate Root Theorem and a solid understanding of why it works. Now, let's zero in on the options provided in the question. We have:

A. -3 - i B. -3i C. 3 - i D. 3i

We've already established that if -3 + i is a root, its complex conjugate -3 - i must also be a root. So, let's scan our options. Bingo! Option A, -3 - i, perfectly matches the complex conjugate we calculated. This is the correct answer.

Now, let's quickly look at why the other options are incorrect. Option B, -3i, is a purely imaginary number, but it's not the conjugate of -3 + i. Option C, 3 - i, has the correct imaginary part, but the real part has the opposite sign. This is not the conjugate. Option D, 3i, is also a purely imaginary number and not the conjugate. Therefore, none of these options fit the bill.

This process of elimination is a powerful strategy in problem-solving. By understanding the core concept and methodically applying it, we can confidently identify the correct answer and rule out the incorrect ones. It's not just about getting the right answer; it's about understanding why it's the right answer.

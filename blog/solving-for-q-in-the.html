Solving For Q In The Equation Q-12=89 A Step-by-Step Guide

Jul 14, 2025 by ADMIN 59 views

Hey guys! Today, we're diving into a super common type of math problem: solving for a variable. In this case, we're tackling the equation Q - 12 = 89, and our mission is to figure out what the heck Q stands for. Don't worry; it's way easier than it looks! We'll break it down step-by-step so you can confidently conquer similar problems in the future. So, let's get started and make math a little less mysterious and a lot more fun!

Understanding the Basics of Algebraic Equations

Before we jump into solving our specific equation, let's quickly brush up on some fundamental concepts of algebraic equations. Think of an equation as a perfectly balanced scale. On one side, you have an expression (like Q - 12), and on the other side, you have another expression (like 89). The equals sign (=) tells us that both sides weigh the same – they have the same value. Our goal in solving for a variable, like Q, is to isolate it on one side of the equation while keeping the scale balanced. This means whatever operation we perform on one side, we must also perform on the other side. This is the golden rule of equation solving, and it's crucial for getting the correct answer. Now, let's talk about the different operations we can use. The most common ones are addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division. Each of these has an inverse operation that undoes it. For instance, the inverse of addition is subtraction, and the inverse of multiplication is division. We'll use these inverse operations strategically to get Q all by itself on one side of the equation. This might sound a bit abstract right now, but it will become crystal clear as we work through our example. So, stick with me, and let's get ready to put these principles into action!

The Importance of Inverse Operations

Understanding inverse operations is the key to unraveling algebraic equations. Think of it like this: you have a locked box, and to open it, you need the right key. In our case, the locked box is the variable Q tangled up in an equation, and the key is the inverse operation that will set it free. For instance, if a number is being added to Q, we use subtraction (the inverse of addition) to isolate Q. If a number is multiplying Q, we use division (the inverse of multiplication). These inverse operations act like magic wands, allowing us to manipulate the equation while maintaining its balance. It's like performing a perfectly choreographed dance – every move on one side must be mirrored on the other. Mastering this concept of inverse operations not only helps in solving simple equations but also lays a strong foundation for tackling more complex algebraic problems. So, remember, each operation has a counterpart that undoes it, and using these counterparts strategically is our secret weapon in the world of algebra.

Isolating the Variable: The Golden Rule of Equation Solving

The core principle behind solving for a variable in any algebraic equation is isolation. We want to get that variable – in our case, Q – all by its lonesome on one side of the equals sign. It's like giving Q its own private island where it can chill without any pesky numbers bothering it. To achieve this isolation, we employ the concept of inverse operations, which we discussed earlier. The idea is to carefully peel away the layers of operations surrounding the variable, one at a time, until only Q remains. This requires a strategic approach, almost like a detective solving a mystery. We look for the operation that's currently affecting Q (in our case, subtraction of 12) and then apply its inverse (addition of 12) to both sides of the equation. Remember, whatever we do to one side, we must do to the other to maintain the balance. This ensures that the equation remains true and that we're not inadvertently changing the value of Q. Isolating the variable is not just a mathematical trick; it's a fundamental skill that empowers us to understand and manipulate relationships between quantities. It's like having the power to rearrange the pieces of a puzzle to reveal the hidden picture.

Solving the Equation Q - 12 = 89: A Step-by-Step Guide

Alright, let's get down to business and solve the equation Q - 12 = 89! We're going to break it down into simple, manageable steps so you can follow along easily. Remember our goal: to isolate Q on one side of the equation. Looking at the equation, we see that 12 is being subtracted from Q. So, what's the inverse operation of subtraction? You guessed it – addition! To get Q all by itself, we need to add 12 to both sides of the equation. This is where the "balance" concept comes into play. Whatever we do to the left side, we must do to the right side to keep the equation true. So, let's add 12 to both sides:

Q - 12 + 12 = 89 + 12

Now, let's simplify. On the left side, the -12 and +12 cancel each other out, leaving us with just Q. On the right side, 89 + 12 equals 101. So, our equation now looks like this:

Q = 101

And there you have it! We've successfully isolated Q and found its value. Q equals 101. See? It wasn't so scary after all! This step-by-step approach is key to solving any algebraic equation. Identify the operation affecting the variable, apply its inverse to both sides, and simplify. With a little practice, you'll be solving equations like a pro!

Step 1: Identify the Operation Affecting Q

The first step in solving any equation is like being a detective – we need to carefully observe the scene and identify the clues. In our equation, Q - 12 = 89, the operation directly affecting Q is subtraction. We see that 12 is being subtracted from Q. This is our key piece of information, the clue that will guide us towards the solution. Recognizing this operation is crucial because it tells us which inverse operation we need to use to isolate Q. It's like knowing which tool to pick from a toolbox – the right tool makes the job much easier. This step might seem simple, but it's the foundation for the entire solving process. If we misidentify the operation, we'll end up using the wrong inverse and heading down the wrong path. So, always take a moment to carefully examine the equation and pinpoint the operation that's directly linked to the variable you're trying to solve for. This small investment of time at the beginning can save you from headaches and frustration later on.

Step 2: Apply the Inverse Operation to Both Sides

Now that we've identified the operation affecting Q (subtraction), it's time to unleash the power of inverse operations! Remember, the inverse of subtraction is addition. So, to counteract the "- 12" in our equation, we're going to add 12. But here's the crucial part: we need to add 12 to both sides of the equation. This is where the balancing act comes in. Think of the equals sign as the fulcrum of a seesaw. If we add weight to one side without adding the same weight to the other, the seesaw will tip, and the equation will no longer be true. So, we add 12 to both sides to maintain that perfect equilibrium:

Q - 12 + 12 = 89 + 12

This step is the heart of the equation-solving process. It's where we actively manipulate the equation to move closer to our goal of isolating Q. By applying the inverse operation to both sides, we're essentially undoing the subtraction that's keeping Q tied up. It's like unlocking a shackle, freeing Q to stand on its own. And remember, the key to success here is consistency. Whatever you do to one side, you must do to the other. This ensures that the equation remains balanced and that the value of Q remains unchanged.

Step 3: Simplify the Equation

With the inverse operation applied, it's time to tidy things up and simplify the equation. This is where the magic happens, and we start to see the fruits of our labor. Let's revisit our equation:

Q - 12 + 12 = 89 + 12

On the left side, we have "- 12 + 12." What happens when you subtract 12 and then add 12? They cancel each other out! It's like taking a step forward and then immediately taking a step backward – you end up back where you started. So, "- 12 + 12" simplifies to 0, leaving us with just Q on the left side. Now, let's look at the right side: 89 + 12. This is a simple addition problem, and the answer is 101. So, after simplifying both sides, our equation transforms into:

Q = 101

And just like that, we've solved for Q! This simplification step is crucial because it brings clarity to the equation. By eliminating the clutter and combining like terms, we reveal the true value of the variable we're seeking. It's like cleaning a dusty window – suddenly, everything becomes much clearer. So, always remember to simplify after applying the inverse operation. It's the final polish that brings the solution into focus.

Verifying the Solution: Plugging the Value Back into the Equation

We've found that Q = 101, but how can we be absolutely sure that our answer is correct? This is where the crucial step of verification comes in. It's like double-checking your work before submitting an important assignment. To verify our solution, we simply plug the value we found for Q (which is 101) back into the original equation:

Q - 12 = 89

Replace Q with 101:

101 - 12 = 89

Now, let's simplify the left side: 101 - 12 = 89. So, our equation now looks like this:

89 = 89

This is a true statement! The left side equals the right side, which means our solution is correct. We've successfully verified that Q = 101 is the correct answer. Verifying your solution is a powerful habit to develop. It gives you confidence in your answer and helps you catch any potential errors. It's like having a built-in safety net, ensuring that you don't fall victim to careless mistakes. So, always take the time to plug your solution back into the original equation. It's a small investment of time that can yield big dividends in terms of accuracy and peace of mind.

The Importance of Checking Your Work

In the world of mathematics, accuracy is paramount. A single mistake can throw off an entire calculation, leading to an incorrect answer. That's why checking your work is not just a good practice; it's an essential skill. It's like being a detective who meticulously examines every piece of evidence to ensure that the case is airtight. In our context, checking your work means plugging the solution you've found back into the original equation and verifying that it holds true. This process acts as a safeguard against errors, whether they're simple arithmetic mistakes or more complex conceptual misunderstandings. It's like having a second pair of eyes reviewing your work, catching anything you might have missed. Developing the habit of checking your work not only improves your accuracy but also fosters a deeper understanding of the underlying mathematical principles. It reinforces the connection between the steps you've taken and the final answer, solidifying your grasp of the concepts involved. So, always make checking your work an integral part of your problem-solving process. It's a small investment of time that can pay off big in terms of accuracy and confidence.

Step-by-Step Verification Process

The verification process is a straightforward yet powerful technique for ensuring the accuracy of your solutions. Let's break it down into a step-by-step guide to make it even clearer. First, identify the original equation. This is the equation you started with before you began solving for the variable. In our case, it's Q - 12 = 89. Next, take the solution you found for the variable (in our case, Q = 101) and substitute it into the original equation. This means replacing every instance of the variable with its numerical value. So, our equation becomes 101 - 12 = 89. Now, simplify both sides of the equation independently. Perform any necessary arithmetic operations to reduce each side to its simplest form. In our example, 101 - 12 simplifies to 89, so we have 89 = 89. Finally, compare the simplified sides of the equation. If the two sides are equal, then your solution is correct! If the two sides are not equal, then you've made a mistake somewhere, and you need to go back and review your work. This step-by-step process transforms verification from an abstract concept into a concrete, actionable technique. By following these steps diligently, you can confidently confirm the accuracy of your solutions and avoid costly errors.

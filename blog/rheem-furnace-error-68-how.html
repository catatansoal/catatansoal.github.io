Rheem Furnace Error 68: How To Fix Flame Failure

Aug 11, 2025 by ADMIN 49 views

Experiencing a Rheem furnace error code 68 can be frustrating, especially when you're trying to keep your home warm during cold weather. But don't worry, guys! This comprehensive guide will walk you through the meaning of this error code, potential causes, and detailed steps on how to troubleshoot and reset your Rheem furnace. We'll also cover some preventative maintenance tips to help you avoid this issue in the future. So, let's dive in and get your furnace back up and running!

Understanding Rheem Furnace Error Code 68

When your Rheem furnace displays error code 68, it indicates a flame failure during operation. In simpler terms, the furnace is trying to ignite, but the flame is not being detected by the flame sensor. This safety feature is crucial because it prevents the furnace from continuing to pump gas into the combustion chamber without a flame, which could lead to a dangerous gas buildup. Several factors can trigger this error, ranging from minor issues you can easily fix yourself to more complex problems requiring professional assistance. It's essential to understand the potential causes so you can approach the troubleshooting process systematically.

Before we delve into specific solutions, let's break down the components involved in the flame detection process. The flame sensor, a thin metallic rod positioned near the burner, plays a vital role. When the furnace ignites, the flame sensor detects the presence of the flame and sends a signal to the control board, confirming that it's safe to continue operating. If the flame sensor doesn't detect a flame, it signals the control board to shut down the gas supply, preventing a hazardous situation. Other critical components include the igniter, which is responsible for initiating the flame, the gas valve, which controls the flow of gas to the burners, and the control board, which acts as the brain of the furnace, managing various functions based on sensor inputs.

Now, let's explore some common culprits behind the error code 68. A dirty flame sensor is often the primary suspect. Over time, soot and carbon deposits can accumulate on the flame sensor, creating an insulating layer that prevents it from properly detecting the flame. This is a relatively simple fix that we'll cover in detail later. Another potential issue is a faulty igniter. If the igniter is weak or broken, it may not be able to generate a sufficient spark to ignite the gas. A malfunctioning gas valve can also prevent the proper flow of gas to the burners, resulting in a flame failure. In some cases, the problem may lie with the control board itself, which might be misinterpreting signals or failing to send the correct commands. Finally, combustion air supply problems, such as a blocked vent or flue, can interfere with the combustion process and lead to a flame failure. Understanding these potential causes is the first step toward effectively troubleshooting and resolving the Rheem furnace error code 68.

Common Causes of Rheem Furnace Error Code 68

To effectively troubleshoot a Rheem furnace error code 68, it's crucial to understand the potential causes behind it. As mentioned earlier, this error code signals a flame failure, meaning the furnace attempts to ignite, but the flame sensor doesn't detect a flame. Let's delve deeper into the common reasons why this might happen:

  1. Dirty Flame Sensor: This is often the most frequent culprit behind error code 68. The flame sensor, a slender metallic rod positioned near the burners, is responsible for detecting the presence of a flame. Over time, soot, carbon deposits, and other contaminants can accumulate on the sensor's surface, creating an insulating layer. This layer interferes with the sensor's ability to conduct electricity and accurately detect the flame, even if one is present. A dirty flame sensor can send false signals to the control board, leading to the error code 68 shutdown. Regular cleaning of the flame sensor is a key part of preventative maintenance and can often resolve this issue quickly. Think of it like cleaning a dirty window â€“ you need to remove the grime to see clearly!

  2. Faulty Igniter: The igniter plays a vital role in initiating the combustion process. It's responsible for generating the spark or heat necessary to ignite the gas flowing to the burners. If the igniter is weak, cracked, or completely broken, it may not produce a sufficient spark to ignite the gas, resulting in a flame failure. There are two main types of igniters: hot surface igniters and spark igniters. Hot surface igniters heat up to a high temperature to ignite the gas, while spark igniters create an electrical arc. Over time, both types of igniters can degrade and fail. Signs of a faulty igniter include a furnace that fails to ignite, a delayed ignition, or a weak orange glow from the igniter. Testing the igniter with a multimeter can help determine if it's functioning correctly. If the igniter is indeed faulty, it will need to be replaced.

  3. Malfunctioning Gas Valve: The gas valve controls the flow of gas to the burners. It's a crucial safety component that opens to allow gas flow when the furnace is supposed to ignite and closes to shut off the gas supply when the furnace is off or encounters a problem. If the gas valve is malfunctioning, it may not open fully, preventing enough gas from reaching the burners, or it may not open at all. This can lead to a flame failure and trigger the error code 68. A malfunctioning gas valve can be caused by various factors, such as electrical problems, mechanical issues, or a faulty solenoid. Diagnosing a gas valve problem often requires professional expertise, as it involves working with gas lines and electrical components. Attempting to repair a gas valve yourself can be dangerous if you're not properly trained and equipped.

  4. Control Board Issues: The control board is the central processing unit of the furnace, acting as its brain. It manages various functions, including ignition, flame detection, and fan operation, based on signals from different sensors and components. If the control board is faulty, it may misinterpret signals from the flame sensor, igniter, or gas valve, or it may fail to send the correct commands. This can lead to a variety of problems, including the error code 68. Control board failures can be caused by power surges, electrical shorts, or component degradation. Diagnosing a control board problem can be complex, often requiring specialized tools and knowledge. Replacing a faulty control board is typically a task best left to a qualified HVAC technician.

  5. Combustion Air Supply Problems: Furnaces require an adequate supply of air for proper combustion. If there's a blockage in the air intake or exhaust vents, it can disrupt the combustion process and lead to a flame failure. Common causes of air supply problems include blocked air vents, a clogged flue, or a damaged vent pipe. Debris such as leaves, snow, or bird nests can obstruct the vents. A blocked flue can prevent exhaust gases from escaping, leading to a buildup of pressure inside the furnace. This can interfere with the ignition process and trigger the error code 68. Ensuring proper airflow to and from the furnace is essential for safe and efficient operation. Regularly inspecting and clearing any obstructions from the vents and flue can help prevent this issue.

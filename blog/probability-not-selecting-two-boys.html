Probability: Not Selecting Two Boys For Parade

Aug 6, 2025 by ADMIN 47 views

Hey guys! Probability problems can sometimes feel like a maze, but let's break this one down step by step. We've got a scenario where two students are randomly selected from a group of eight boys and 12 girls to represent their school in a parade. Our mission, should we choose to accept it, is to figure out the probability that the two chosen students aren't both boys. Sounds like a fun challenge, right? Let's dive in and unravel this probability puzzle together!

Before we start crunching numbers, it's crucial that we understand exactly what the problem is asking. We aren't looking for the probability of selecting two girls, or even one boy and one girl. What we're interested in is the chance that we don't end up with two boys. This means we're perfectly okay with having two girls or a mixed pair â€“ one boy and one girl. Thinking about the problem in this way helps us to frame our approach and avoid unnecessary calculations. Probability can be tricky, but with a clear understanding of the question, we're already halfway to the answer. So, let's keep this in mind as we move forward: we want the probability of not two boys.

The first step in any probability problem is to figure out the total number of possible outcomes. In our case, this means calculating the number of ways we can select two students from the entire group of 20 (8 boys + 12 girls). This is a classic combination problem, where the order of selection doesn't matter. We're using combinations because picking John then Mary is the same outcome as picking Mary then John for the purpose of representing the school in the parade. The formula for combinations is nCr = n! / (r! * (n-r)!), where 'n' is the total number of items, 'r' is the number of items to choose, and '!' denotes the factorial (e.g., 5! = 5 * 4 * 3 * 2 * 1). So, for our problem, we need to calculate 20C2. Plugging in the values, we get 20! / (2! * 18!) = (20 * 19) / (2 * 1) = 190. This means there are 190 different ways to choose two students from the group of 20. Keep this number in your back pocket â€“ we'll need it later!

Now that we know the total possible outcomes, let's figure out the number of outcomes we don't want â€“ the ones where we select two boys. This is crucial because we're calculating the probability of not selecting two boys. Using the same combination formula, we need to calculate the number of ways to choose 2 boys from the group of 8 boys. This is 8C2 = 8! / (2! * 6!) = (8 * 7) / (2 * 1) = 28. So, there are 28 ways to select a pair of two boys. These are the outcomes we want to avoid. Think of it like this: these 28 scenarios are the ones that don't fit our criteria of not having two boys. We're getting closer to the final answer by narrowing down the possibilities and focusing on what we want to exclude.

Okay, guys, here's where it all starts to come together. We know the total number of outcomes (190) and the number of unfavorable outcomes (28 â€“ the two-boy pairs). To find the number of favorable outcomes â€“ the ones where we don't have two boys â€“ we simply subtract the unfavorable outcomes from the total outcomes. So, 190 (total outcomes) - 28 (two-boy pairs) = 162. This means there are 162 ways to select two students where we don't end up with a pair of boys. These favorable outcomes include pairs of girls and mixed pairs of one boy and one girl. We've essentially flipped the problem on its head by focusing on what we don't want and then subtracting that from the total. This is a clever trick that often simplifies probability calculations. Stick with me, we're in the home stretch!

Alright, the moment we've all been waiting for â€“ calculating the probability! Remember, probability is simply the number of favorable outcomes divided by the total number of outcomes. We've already done the heavy lifting, so this step is a breeze. We have 162 favorable outcomes (not two boys) and 190 total possible outcomes. So, the probability is 162 / 190. We can simplify this fraction by dividing both the numerator and denominator by their greatest common divisor, which is 2. This gives us a simplified probability of 81 / 95. And there you have it! The probability that the two students chosen are not both boys is 81/95. Isn't it satisfying when a complex problem boils down to a simple fraction? We've successfully navigated the probability maze!

Now that we've got our probability as a fraction (81/95), it's helpful to think about what this means in practical terms. Fractions can sometimes feel a bit abstract, so let's put it into perspective. 81/95 is a pretty high probability â€“ it's close to 1, which represents certainty. This means there's a very good chance that the two students selected won't be both boys. To get a more intuitive sense, we can convert this fraction to a percentage. Dividing 81 by 95 gives us approximately 0.8526, which is 85.26%. So, there's roughly an 85.26% chance that the chosen students won't be a pair of boys. You could also express this probability as a decimal or in words, like "there is an 85.26% chance" or "the probability is about 0.85." Presenting the probability in different ways helps to communicate the result clearly and effectively. Great job, guys, we're mastering probability like pros!

Hey guys, did you know there's often more than one way to crack a math problem? Let's explore an alternative approach to solving this probability puzzle using something called complementary probability. The complement of an event is everything that doesn't belong to that event. In our case, the event we're interested in is "not selecting two boys." The complement of this event is "selecting two boys." Complementary probability states that the probability of an event happening plus the probability of its complement happening equals 1 (or 100%). This gives us a clever shortcut: instead of directly calculating the probability of not selecting two boys, we can calculate the probability of selecting two boys and then subtract that from 1. We already know the probability of selecting two boys is the number of ways to choose two boys (28) divided by the total number of outcomes (190), which is 28/190. Simplifying this fraction, we get 14/95. Now, to find the probability of not selecting two boys, we subtract 14/95 from 1: 1 - 14/95 = 81/95. Boom! We arrived at the same answer using a different route. This alternative approach can be super handy in certain probability problems, so it's a great tool to have in your math arsenal.

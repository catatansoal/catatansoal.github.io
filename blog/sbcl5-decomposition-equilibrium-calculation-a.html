SbCl5 Decomposition Equilibrium Calculation A Step-by-Step Chemistry Guide

Jul 13, 2025 by ADMIN 75 views

Hey guys! Today, we're diving deep into a fascinating chemistry problem involving the equilibrium of antimony pentachloride (SbCl5) decomposition. We'll break down the reaction, explore the equilibrium constant (Kp), and figure out how to calculate the equilibrium concentration of SbCl5. Trust me, it's gonna be an exciting journey! In this guide, we'll explore the reaction where antimony pentachloride (SbCl5) decomposes into antimony trichloride (SbCl3) and chlorine gas (Cl2). This reaction is a classic example of a chemical equilibrium, where the forward and reverse reactions occur at the same rate, leading to a stable mixture of reactants and products. To truly grasp what's happening, we need to delve into the concept of the equilibrium constant, denoted as Kp in this scenario. The equilibrium constant is a numerical value that tells us the ratio of products to reactants at equilibrium, providing insights into the extent of the reaction's progress. A high Kp value suggests that the reaction favors the formation of products, while a low value implies the opposite. In our case, the given Kp value is 1.48 at 448°C. This value indicates that at this temperature, the reaction mixture contains a significant amount of both reactants and products at equilibrium. The temperature plays a crucial role in chemical reactions, and its impact on the equilibrium constant is no exception. In this instance, the reaction is conducted at a temperature of 448°C, which is a high temperature. The high temperature is necessary to provide the energy needed for the SbCl5 molecules to break apart and form SbCl3 and Cl2. Furthermore, temperature affects the reaction rate, influencing how quickly equilibrium is reached. In this comprehensive guide, we'll tackle a practical problem where we're given the equilibrium concentrations of SbCl3 and Cl2, and our mission is to determine the equilibrium concentration of SbCl5. This problem will not only test our understanding of equilibrium principles but also hone our skills in applying these concepts to solve real-world scenarios. So, let's roll up our sleeves and embark on this chemical adventure together!

Our chemical reaction of interest is the reversible decomposition of antimony pentachloride (SbCl5) into antimony trichloride (SbCl3) and chlorine gas (Cl2). This reaction can be represented as follows:

SbCl5(g) ⇌ SbCl3(g) + Cl2(g)

This equation tells us that gaseous SbCl5 can break down into gaseous SbCl3 and gaseous Cl2, and vice versa. The double arrow (⇌) signifies that the reaction is reversible, meaning it can proceed in both the forward and reverse directions. Understanding the stoichiometry of this reaction is crucial. For every one mole of SbCl5 that decomposes, one mole of SbCl3 and one mole of Cl2 are produced. This 1:1:1 stoichiometric ratio will be vital when we set up our equilibrium expression and perform calculations. Furthermore, the state symbols (g) indicate that all the species involved in this reaction are in the gaseous phase. This is important because the equilibrium constant Kp is specifically defined for reactions involving gases. Now, let's talk about the equilibrium constant itself. In this case, we're given Kp, which is the equilibrium constant expressed in terms of partial pressures. It quantifies the relationship between the partial pressures of reactants and products at equilibrium. For our reaction, the Kp expression is given by:

Kp = (PSbCl3 * PCl2) / PSbCl5

Where P represents the partial pressure of each gas. It's important to note that the equilibrium constant is temperature-dependent. The given Kp value of 1.48 is specifically for the reaction at 448°C. If the temperature were to change, the value of Kp would also change, reflecting the shift in equilibrium position. To successfully solve equilibrium problems, you need to be comfortable with writing the balanced chemical equation, understanding the stoichiometry, and setting up the Kp expression correctly. These are the fundamental building blocks for tackling more complex equilibrium calculations. With a solid grasp of these concepts, we're well-prepared to dive into the problem at hand and determine the equilibrium concentration of SbCl5.

Kp, the equilibrium constant in terms of partial pressures, plays a pivotal role in understanding gaseous reactions. It's like a compass that guides us through the equilibrium landscape, revealing the relative amounts of reactants and products at equilibrium. For our reaction:

SbCl5(g) ⇌ SbCl3(g) + Cl2(g)

The Kp expression is defined as:

Kp = (PSbCl3 * PCl2) / PSbCl5

Where P denotes the partial pressure of each gas. This expression tells us that Kp is the ratio of the product of the partial pressures of the products (SbCl3 and Cl2) to the partial pressure of the reactant (SbCl5), all at equilibrium. The magnitude of Kp is incredibly informative. A Kp value much greater than 1 indicates that the equilibrium favors the products. In other words, at equilibrium, there will be significantly more SbCl3 and Cl2 than SbCl5. Conversely, a Kp value much less than 1 suggests that the equilibrium favors the reactants, meaning there will be more SbCl5 present at equilibrium. A Kp value close to 1 implies that the amounts of reactants and products at equilibrium are comparable. In our specific case, Kp is given as 1.48 at 448°C. This value, being greater than 1, indicates that the equilibrium slightly favors the products, but not overwhelmingly so. We can expect to find a mixture containing both reactants and products in significant amounts at equilibrium. It's crucial to remember that Kp is temperature-dependent. Changing the temperature will alter the equilibrium position and, consequently, the value of Kp. The relationship between temperature and Kp is described by the Van't Hoff equation, which delves into the thermodynamics of chemical reactions. Another important aspect is the units of Kp. Since Kp is defined in terms of partial pressures, its units will depend on the units used for pressure. Common units for pressure include atmospheres (atm) and Pascals (Pa). Therefore, the units of Kp will be a combination of these pressure units, raised to the power equal to the change in the number of moles of gas in the reaction. In summary, Kp is a powerful tool for understanding and quantifying chemical equilibrium in gaseous reactions. Its magnitude reveals the extent to which a reaction proceeds towards products, and its temperature dependence highlights the dynamic nature of chemical systems. With a solid grasp of Kp, we can confidently tackle equilibrium calculations and make predictions about reaction outcomes.

Alright, let's get down to the nitty-gritty of solving for the equilibrium concentration of SbCl5, denoted as [SbCl5]eq. We're given the following information:

  • Kp = 1.48 at 448°C
  • [SbCl3]eq = [Cl2]eq = 0.05 M

Our goal is to find [SbCl5]eq. To do this, we'll need to relate Kp to the concentrations using the ideal gas law. The ideal gas law states:

PV = nRT

Where:

  • P is the pressure
  • V is the volume
  • n is the number of moles
  • R is the ideal gas constant (0.0821 L atm / (mol K))
  • T is the temperature in Kelvin

From the ideal gas law, we can derive the relationship between partial pressure and concentration:

P = (n/ V) RT = C RT

Where C is the concentration in moles per liter (M). Now, let's convert the temperature from Celsius to Kelvin:

T (K) = T (°C) + 273.15 = 448 + 273.15 = 721.15 K

Now we can express the partial pressures in terms of concentrations:

PSbCl3 = [SbCl3]eq * RT = 0.05 M * (0.0821 L atm / (mol K)) * 721.15 K

PCl2 = [Cl2]eq * RT = 0.05 M * (0.0821 L atm / (mol K)) * 721.15 K

PSbCl5 = [SbCl5]eq * RT

Plug these into the Kp expression:

Kp = (PSbCl3 * PCl2) / PSbCl5 = ([SbCl3]eq * RT * [Cl2]eq * RT) / ([SbCl5]eq * RT)

Notice that one RT term cancels out:

Kp = ([SbCl3]eq * [Cl2]eq * RT) / [SbCl5]eq

Now, solve for [SbCl5]eq:

[SbCl5]eq = ([SbCl3]eq * [Cl2]eq * RT) / Kp

Plug in the values:

[SbCl5]eq = (0.05 M * 0.05 M * 0.0821 L atm / (mol K) * 721.15 K) / 1.48

[SbCl5]eq = (0.0025 M^2 * 0.0821 L atm / (mol K) * 721.15 K) / 1.48

[SbCl5]eq ≈ 0.1 M

Therefore, the equilibrium concentration of SbCl5 is approximately 0.1 M. So, the correct answer is D. 0.1 M. Awesome! We've successfully navigated this equilibrium problem.

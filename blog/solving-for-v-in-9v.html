Solving For V In 9v = 12 - 3v^2 A Step-by-Step Guide

Jul 14, 2025 by ADMIN 53 views

Hey guys! Today, we're diving into a fun little math problem where we need to solve for v in the equation 9v = 12 - 3v^2. Don't worry, it might look a bit intimidating at first, but we'll break it down step by step so it's super easy to understand. We're going to turn this into a classic quadratic equation and then use some cool techniques to find the values of v that make the equation true. So, grab your pencils and let's get started!

Understanding Quadratic Equations

Before we jump into solving our specific equation, let's chat a bit about what quadratic equations actually are. A quadratic equation is basically a polynomial equation of the second degree. What does that mean? Well, it means the highest power of the variable (in our case, v) is 2. The general form of a quadratic equation is ax^2 + bx + c = 0, where a, b, and c are constants (numbers), and x is the variable we're trying to solve for. You'll notice the x^2 term – that's what makes it a quadratic equation. These equations pop up all over the place in math and science, from physics problems about projectile motion to engineering calculations for building structures. Understanding how to solve them is a crucial skill in many fields.

Now, why do we care about this general form? Because it gives us a roadmap for how to tackle quadratic equations. The first thing we usually want to do is rearrange our equation so it looks like that ax^2 + bx + c = 0 format. This makes it much easier to apply our solving techniques. We'll see this in action as we work through our problem. The constants a, b, and c play key roles in how we solve the equation. They tell us the shape of the parabola (the graph of a quadratic equation) and help us determine the solutions, which are also known as the roots or zeros of the equation. Keep in mind that quadratic equations can have two real solutions, one real solution (which is a repeated root), or two complex solutions. Complex solutions involve imaginary numbers, but we'll focus on finding real number solutions in our case. So, with this basic understanding of quadratic equations under our belts, let's get back to our equation and start rearranging things!

Step 1: Rearranging the Equation

Alright, so we've got our equation: 9v = 12 - 3v^2. The first thing we need to do is get it into that standard quadratic form (ax^2 + bx + c = 0). To do this, we want to move all the terms to one side of the equation, leaving zero on the other side. This will make it easier to identify our a, b, and c values, which are super important for the next steps. Take a look at our equation again. We have a 9v on the left side and 12 - 3v^2 on the right side. To get everything on one side, we can add 3v^2 to both sides and subtract 12 from both sides. Remember, whatever we do to one side of the equation, we have to do to the other side to keep it balanced! When we add 3v^2 to both sides, we get 3v^2 + 9v = 12. Now, we subtract 12 from both sides, which gives us 3v^2 + 9v - 12 = 0. Awesome! We've successfully rearranged the equation into the standard quadratic form. Notice how the term with v^2 comes first, followed by the term with v, and then the constant term. This order will help us stay organized as we solve the equation. So, our rearranged equation is 3v^2 + 9v - 12 = 0. Now we can move on to the next step, which involves simplifying our equation to make it even easier to work with.

Step 2: Simplifying the Equation

Now that we've got our equation in the standard quadratic form (3v^2 + 9v - 12 = 0), let's see if we can make it even simpler. This is always a good idea because simpler equations are generally easier to solve and less prone to errors. Look closely at the coefficients (the numbers in front of the variables) in our equation: 3, 9, and -12. Do you notice anything they have in common? That's right, they're all divisible by 3! This means we can divide the entire equation by 3 to simplify it. Remember, as long as we do the same thing to both sides of the equation, we're not changing the solutions. So, let's divide each term in the equation by 3. (3v^2) / 3 becomes v^2. (9v) / 3 becomes 3v. And (-12) / 3 becomes -4. So, after dividing by 3, our equation transforms into v^2 + 3v - 4 = 0. How cool is that? Our equation looks much cleaner now! This simplified form will make the next step, which is factoring the quadratic, a whole lot easier. Simplifying the equation not only makes the numbers smaller and more manageable but also reduces the chances of making mistakes in the following steps. Always look for opportunities to simplify equations – it's a great problem-solving strategy in math!

Step 3: Factoring the Quadratic

Okay, guys, we've got our simplified quadratic equation: v^2 + 3v - 4 = 0. Now comes the fun part – factoring! Factoring is like reverse multiplication. We're trying to find two binomials (expressions with two terms) that, when multiplied together, give us our quadratic expression. This method works best when the quadratic equation can be easily factored, which is the case for our problem. When we factor a quadratic expression like v^2 + 3v - 4, we're looking for two numbers that multiply to give us the constant term (-4) and add up to give us the coefficient of the v term (3). Let's think about the factors of -4. We have a few options: -1 and 4, 1 and -4, or -2 and 2. Which pair of these numbers adds up to 3? You got it! -1 and 4. So, we can rewrite our quadratic expression as (v - 1)(v + 4). This means that v^2 + 3v - 4 is the same as (v - 1)(v + 4). Now we have our factored equation: (v - 1)(v + 4) = 0. Factoring is a powerful technique for solving quadratic equations, and it's a skill that becomes easier with practice. It's like a puzzle – you're trying to find the right pieces that fit together. In the next step, we'll use the factored form of our equation to find the actual solutions for v. So, we're almost there!

Step 4: Finding the Solutions for v

We've successfully factored our equation, and we're now sitting pretty with (v - 1)(v + 4) = 0. This is a fantastic place to be because it sets us up to use a crucial property in algebra: the zero-product property. The zero-product property states that if the product of two factors is zero, then at least one of the factors must be zero. Think about it – the only way to multiply two numbers and get zero as the result is if one or both of those numbers are zero. This property is the key to unlocking the solutions for v. In our case, we have two factors: (v - 1) and (v + 4). The zero-product property tells us that either (v - 1) = 0 or (v + 4) = 0 (or both). So, let's consider each possibility separately. If (v - 1) = 0, we can add 1 to both sides of the equation to isolate v, giving us v = 1. That's one solution! Now, let's look at the other factor. If (v + 4) = 0, we can subtract 4 from both sides to isolate v, giving us v = -4. And that's our second solution! So, we've found two values for v that make our original equation true: v = 1 and v = -4. These are the solutions to our quadratic equation. In the next step, we'll make sure we present our solutions clearly and in the correct format. We're on the home stretch now!

Step 5: Presenting the Solutions

We've done the hard work and found our solutions for v: v = 1 and v = -4. Now, the final step is to present these solutions clearly and in the format requested by the problem. The problem asks us to write each solution as an integer, proper fraction, or improper fraction in simplest form, and if there are multiple solutions, to separate them with commas. Looking at our solutions, we see that both 1 and -4 are integers. That's perfect! They're already in the simplest form. Since we have two solutions, we need to separate them with a comma. So, our final answer is v = 1, -4. It's always a good idea to double-check your work, especially in math problems. We can plug our solutions back into the original equation (9v = 12 - 3v^2) to make sure they work. Let's try v = 1: 9(1) = 12 - 3(1)^2 simplifies to 9 = 12 - 3, which is 9 = 9. That checks out! Now let's try v = -4: 9(-4) = 12 - 3(-4)^2 simplifies to -36 = 12 - 3(16), which is -36 = 12 - 48, and that's -36 = -36. That also checks out! We've confirmed that both v = 1 and v = -4 are indeed solutions to our equation. So, we can confidently present our final answer: v = 1, -4. Awesome job, guys! We've successfully solved for v in the quadratic equation.

Understanding the Basics of Inequalities

Before we jump into the nitty-gritty, let's refresh our understanding of inequalities. Inequalities are mathematical statements that compare two values, showing that one is greater than, less than, or not equal to another. Unlike equations that have a single solution, inequalities often have a range of solutions. This is where things can get a bit tricky, but fear not! We'll break it down step by step.

The golden rule to remember when dealing with inequalities is what happens when you multiply or divide by a negative number. This is super important, so pay close attention! Whenever you multiply or divide both sides of an inequality by a negative number, you must flip the inequality sign. For example, if we have -x < 5, multiplying both sides by -1 gives us x > -5. See how the < sign flipped to >? Keep this in mind, as it's crucial for solving our problem.

Equivalent inequalities are inequalities that have the same solution set. Think of it like different roads leading to the same destination. Our goal is to identify which of the given inequalities lead to the same solution set as r > -11. This means any value of r that satisfies r > -11 should also satisfy its equivalent inequalities, and vice versa. Now that we've got our foundation solid, let's tackle the given inequalities and see which ones make the cut.

-r < 11: The First Contender

Let's start with our first inequality: -r < 11. To determine if this is equivalent to r > -11, we need to isolate r. Remember our golden rule? We're going to multiply both sides of the inequality by -1. When we do this, we must flip the inequality sign.

So, multiplying both sides of -r < 11 by -1, we get:

(-1) * -r > (-1) * 11

This simplifies to:

r > -11

Bingo! This is exactly what we were looking for. The inequality -r < 11 is indeed equivalent to r > -11. We've found our first match. But hold on, the quest isn't over yet. We have more inequalities to investigate. The key takeaway here is the sign flipping. It's a small step but a crucial one in ensuring we arrive at the correct equivalent inequality. So, let's move on to the next one and see if it holds up to the same scrutiny.

3r < -33: A Different Path?

Next up, we have the inequality 3r < -33. This looks a bit different, but the process remains the same. Our mission is to isolate r and see if we end up with r > -11. This time, we don't need to worry about multiplying or dividing by a negative number, which simplifies things a bit. To isolate r, we'll divide both sides of the inequality by 3.

Dividing both sides of 3r < -33 by 3, we get:

(3r) / 3 < (-33) / 3

This simplifies to:

r < -11

Hmm, this is interesting. We ended up with r < -11, which is not the same as r > -11. This inequality represents a completely different set of solutions. While r > -11 includes values like -10, -9, 0, and 1, the inequality r < -11 includes values like -12, -13, -100, and so on. They are mirror images of each other on the number line, but definitely not equivalent. So, we can confidently say that 3r < -33 is not equivalent to our target inequality. It's essential to recognize these differences to avoid errors in problem-solving. Let's keep going and see what the next inequality holds.

3r > -33: Getting Closer?

Yes! We've found another equivalent inequality. The inequality 3r > -33 leads us directly to r > -11. This reinforces the importance of carefully performing the algebraic steps and paying attention to the signs. We now have two inequalities that are equivalent to r > -11. But we're not stopping here. Let's continue our investigation to ensure we've identified all the matches. With each inequality we analyze, we're sharpening our skills and deepening our understanding.

-3r < 33: The Negative Twist

Here comes an inequality with a negative coefficient: -3r < 33. Remember our golden rule about multiplying or dividing by a negative number? It's going to be crucial here. To isolate r, we'll divide both sides of the inequality by -3. And yes, you guessed it, we need to flip the inequality sign. Dividing both sides of -3r < 33 by -3, we get:

(-3r) / -3 > (33) / -3

-3r > 33: The Final Check

(-3r) / -3 < (33) / -3

Simplifying, we have:

r < -11

This time, we ended up with r < -11, which, as we've seen before, is not equivalent to r > -11.

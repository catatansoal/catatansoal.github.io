Solving (x^2 + 3)^2 = 4x^2 + 12 A Step-by-Step Guide

Jul 17, 2025 by ADMIN 53 views
Iklan Headers

Hey guys! Let's dive into this math problem together. We've got the equation (x^2 + 3)^2 = 4x^2 + 12, and our mission is to find just one value of x that makes this equation true. Don't worry, it might look intimidating at first, but we'll break it down step by step so it's super easy to follow. We'll explore different techniques and make sure you understand the core concepts behind solving such equations. So, buckle up and let's get started!

1. Understanding the Equation and Initial Thoughts

Okay, so we're faced with the equation (x^2 + 3)^2 = 4x^2 + 12. The first thing that probably jumps out at you is the squared term on the left side. Whenever we see squares, we know we're likely dealing with a quadratic or polynomial equation. Our goal here is to manipulate this equation, simplify it, and eventually isolate 'x' to find its value(s). One approach is to expand the left side and see if we can spot any patterns or simplifications. Another thing to consider is whether we can use substitution to make the equation look simpler. Substitution is a powerful tool in algebra, and it can often turn a complex-looking equation into something much more manageable. Think of it like this: we're trying to find a secret ingredient (the value of x) that makes this whole equation work. By using algebraic techniques and a bit of clever thinking, we can uncover that secret!

Remember, the key to solving any equation is understanding its structure. Let's take a closer look at how the terms are related and see if we can identify any shortcuts or clever tricks we can use. Keep in mind the order of operations (PEMDAS/BODMAS) as we start to manipulate the equation. We need to handle exponents before multiplication, and so on. With a little bit of patience and some algebraic finesse, we'll crack this problem in no time! The problem may seem difficult, but it will get easier if we take it step by step. Before we start doing any calculation, it is important to make sure we understand the problem completely and know the steps we are going to take to solve it.

2. Expanding and Simplifying the Equation

Let's get our hands dirty and start expanding the equation! We'll begin by tackling the left side, (x^2 + 3)^2. Remember, this means (x^2 + 3) multiplied by itself. So, we'll use the FOIL method (First, Outer, Inner, Last) or the distributive property to expand it. This gives us:

(x^2 + 3)(x^2 + 3) = x^4 + 3x^2 + 3x^2 + 9 = x^4 + 6x^2 + 9

Now our equation looks like this:

x^4 + 6x^2 + 9 = 4x^2 + 12

Excellent! We've expanded the left side. Now, let's simplify things further by moving all the terms to one side of the equation. We'll subtract 4x^2 and 12 from both sides to get:

x^4 + 6x^2 + 9 - 4x^2 - 12 = 0

Combining like terms, we get:

x^4 + 2x^2 - 3 = 0

Look at that! We've transformed our original equation into a much cleaner form. This is a quartic equation (an equation with the highest power of x being 4), but don't let that scare you. It actually looks quite manageable now. Notice how the terms are arranged: we have x^4, x^2, and a constant term. This pattern hints at a clever substitution that we can use in the next step. The ability to recognize patterns is a critical skill in mathematics. By simplifying and rearranging, we've made the underlying structure of the equation much clearer, paving the way for an elegant solution. Now, let’s think about what we should do next.

3. The Clever Substitution: Turning Quartic into Quadratic

Okay, guys, this is where things get really interesting! Notice how our equation, x^4 + 2x^2 - 3 = 0, has a special form? We have x^4 (which is (x2)2), x^2, and a constant term. This strongly suggests that we can use a substitution to transform this quartic equation into a simpler quadratic equation. Let's make the substitution:

y = x^2

Now, if we replace every x^2 with 'y' in our equation, we get:

y^2 + 2y - 3 = 0

Wow! Look at that! Our quartic equation has magically turned into a quadratic equation. This is a major breakthrough because we know how to solve quadratic equations. They are much easier to handle than quartics. We can use factoring, the quadratic formula, or even completing the square to find the values of 'y'. This substitution technique is a powerful tool in algebra. It allows us to simplify complex equations by introducing new variables and transforming the problem into a more familiar form. By making this seemingly small change, we've opened the door to a straightforward solution. Always be on the lookout for opportunities to use substitution; it can often be the key to unlocking a difficult problem. Now that we have a quadratic equation, we're well on our way to finding the value(s) of x.

4. Solving the Quadratic Equation

Now that we have the quadratic equation y^2 + 2y - 3 = 0, let's solve for 'y'. There are a couple of ways we can do this: factoring or using the quadratic formula. Factoring is often the quicker method if we can easily find the factors. Let's see if we can factor this equation. We're looking for two numbers that multiply to -3 and add up to 2. Those numbers are 3 and -1. So, we can factor the quadratic equation as:

(y + 3)(y - 1) = 0

Great! Now, for this product to be zero, one or both of the factors must be zero. This gives us two possible solutions for 'y':

y + 3 = 0 => y = -3 y - 1 = 0 => y = 1

So, we have two values for 'y': -3 and 1. But remember, we're not trying to find 'y'; we're trying to find 'x'. We need to substitute back to get the values of 'x'. This is a crucial step – don't forget to go back to the original variable! Solving quadratic equations is a fundamental skill in algebra, and mastering techniques like factoring is essential. By breaking down the quadratic into its factors, we've easily found the possible values for 'y'. Now, let's use these values to find the corresponding values of 'x'. We're almost there!

5. Substituting Back and Finding the Value(s) of x

Alright, we've found the values of 'y', but our ultimate goal is to find 'x'. Remember our substitution: y = x^2. Now we need to substitute the values of 'y' back into this equation and solve for 'x'. Let's start with the first value, y = -3:

x^2 = -3

Hmm, this is interesting. Can a real number squared be negative? Nope! The square of any real number is always non-negative. So, this equation has no real solutions for x. This is an important point to remember: sometimes, when solving equations, we might get solutions that don't make sense in the original context. We need to be careful and check our answers. Now, let's move on to the second value of 'y', which is y = 1:

x^2 = 1

This looks much more promising! To solve for x, we take the square root of both sides:

x = ±√1

So, x can be either 1 or -1. We have two possible solutions for x: 1 and -1. Now, the final step is to choose one of these values as our answer. The problem asks for one value of x, so we can choose either 1 or -1. It doesn't matter which one we pick since both are valid solutions. This step highlights the importance of understanding the properties of numbers and how they relate to equations. We saw that a negative value for x^2 has no real solutions, which helped us eliminate one possibility. By carefully substituting back and considering the implications of each solution, we've arrived at the final answer. We’re almost at the finish line!

6. The Solution and Conclusion

Woohoo! We've made it! We've successfully navigated through the equation (x^2 + 3)^2 = 4x^2 + 12 and found the possible values of x. We found that x can be either 1 or -1. Since the problem asks for one value of x, we can choose either one. Let's go with the positive value.

x = 1

And that's it! We've found a solution to the equation. We took a step-by-step approach, expanding, simplifying, using substitution, solving a quadratic equation, and substituting back to find the value of x. This problem demonstrates the power of algebraic manipulation and the importance of understanding key concepts like factoring, substitution, and the properties of numbers. Remember, the key to solving complex equations is to break them down into smaller, more manageable steps. Don't be afraid to try different approaches and always double-check your work.

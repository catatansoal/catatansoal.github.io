Solving Linear Equations Step-by-Step 4 + 5(3x - 2) = 9 + 3(4x + 2)

Jul 14, 2025 by ADMIN 68 views

Hey guys! Today, we're diving into the fascinating world of linear equations. Specifically, we're going to tackle the equation 4 + 5(3x - 2) = 9 + 3(4x + 2) and find the value of x. Don't worry if it looks a bit intimidating at first; we'll break it down step by step, making it super easy to understand. By the end of this article, you'll not only know how to solve this particular equation but also have a solid understanding of the general principles behind solving linear equations.

Understanding Linear Equations

Before we jump into solving the equation, let's make sure we're all on the same page about what a linear equation actually is. A linear equation is an algebraic equation in which each term is either a constant or the product of a constant and a single variable. The variable is raised to the power of one, and there are no products of variables within the equation. Think of it as a straight line when you graph it â€“ hence the name "linear." The general form of a linear equation in one variable (which is what we're dealing with here) is ax + b = c, where a, b, and c are constants, and x is the variable we want to solve for.

Solving a linear equation means finding the value of the variable that makes the equation true. This involves using algebraic manipulations to isolate the variable on one side of the equation. The key principle here is to perform the same operations on both sides of the equation to maintain the equality. We'll use techniques like distributing, combining like terms, and using inverse operations (addition/subtraction and multiplication/division) to achieve this.

Now, you might be wondering why we even bother with linear equations in the first place. Well, they're incredibly useful in a wide range of real-world applications. From calculating distances and speeds to determining financial investments and predicting trends, linear equations are a fundamental tool in mathematics, science, engineering, and economics. Mastering them is a crucial step in your mathematical journey, guys!

Step-by-Step Solution: 4 + 5(3x - 2) = 9 + 3(4x + 2)

Okay, let's get down to business and solve the equation 4 + 5(3x - 2) = 9 + 3(4x + 2). We'll go through each step in detail, so you can follow along easily. Remember, the goal is to isolate x on one side of the equation. So, let's put on our math hats and get started!

1. Distribute

The first thing we need to do is get rid of the parentheses. We do this by using the distributive property, which means multiplying the number outside the parentheses by each term inside the parentheses. Let's apply this to both sides of our equation:

  • On the left side: 5 * (3x - 2) = (5 * 3x) + (5 * -2) = 15x - 10
  • On the right side: 3 * (4x + 2) = (3 * 4x) + (3 * 2) = 12x + 6

Now, our equation looks like this: 4 + 15x - 10 = 9 + 12x + 6. See? We've already made some progress!

2. Combine Like Terms

Next, we need to simplify both sides of the equation by combining like terms. Like terms are terms that have the same variable raised to the same power (or are just constants). On each side, we'll add the constants together:

  • On the left side: 4 - 10 = -6, so we have 15x - 6
  • On the right side: 9 + 6 = 15, so we have 12x + 15

Our equation now looks much simpler: 15x - 6 = 12x + 15

3. Move Variable Terms to One Side

Our next step is to get all the terms with x on one side of the equation. It doesn't matter which side we choose, but it's usually easier to move the smaller x term to the side with the larger x term to avoid dealing with negative coefficients (though negatives are not the end of the world, guys!). In this case, 12x is smaller than 15x, so we'll subtract 12x from both sides of the equation:

15x - 6 - 12x = 12x + 15 - 12x

This simplifies to: 3x - 6 = 15

4. Move Constant Terms to the Other Side

Now, we want to isolate the x term completely. To do this, we need to move the constant term (-6) to the other side of the equation. We do this by adding 6 to both sides:

3x - 6 + 6 = 15 + 6

This simplifies to: 3x = 21

5. Isolate the Variable

We're almost there! The last step is to isolate x by dividing both sides of the equation by the coefficient of x, which is 3:

3x / 3 = 21 / 3

This gives us: x = 7

6. Verify the Solution (Optional but Recommended)

To make sure we didn't make any mistakes, it's always a good idea to plug our solution back into the original equation and see if it works. Let's substitute x = 7 into 4 + 5(3x - 2) = 9 + 3(4x + 2):

4 + 5(3 * 7 - 2) = 9 + 3(4 * 7 + 2)

Let's simplify both sides:

  • Left side: 4 + 5(21 - 2) = 4 + 5(19) = 4 + 95 = 99
  • Right side: 9 + 3(28 + 2) = 9 + 3(30) = 9 + 90 = 99

Since both sides are equal, our solution x = 7 is correct! High five, guys!

Why Verification Matters

You might be thinking, "Do I really need to check my answer every time?" Well, while it's not strictly required, verifying your solution is a fantastic habit to develop. It's like having a built-in error detector! It helps you catch any mistakes you might have made along the way, whether it's a simple arithmetic error or a misunderstanding of the steps. Think of it as a safety net for your math skills, ensuring you're always on the right track.

Furthermore, verification solidifies your understanding of the equation-solving process. When you plug your solution back in and see it work, you're reinforcing the concept that the value you found truly makes the equation true. This deeper understanding will serve you well as you tackle more complex equations in the future. So, make verification your math superpower, guys!

Tips and Tricks for Solving Linear Equations

Now that we've solved our example equation, let's discuss some general tips and tricks that will help you become a master of linear equations. These strategies can make the process smoother and more efficient, and they'll be valuable tools in your mathematical toolbox.

  • Always distribute first: Whenever you see parentheses in an equation, your first step should be to distribute. This eliminates the parentheses and makes the equation easier to work with. Remember to multiply the term outside the parentheses by every term inside.
  • Combine like terms: After distributing, look for like terms on each side of the equation and combine them. This simplifies the equation and reduces the number of terms you need to deal with. Pay close attention to signs (+ and -) when combining terms.
  • Move variables and constants strategically: When moving terms across the equals sign, remember to perform the inverse operation. If a term is being added, subtract it from both sides; if it's being multiplied, divide both sides. Think about which side will result in a positive coefficient for the variable to minimize negative sign issues.
  • Double-check your arithmetic: Simple arithmetic errors are a common pitfall in equation solving. Take your time and carefully check your addition, subtraction, multiplication, and division. Even a small mistake can lead to an incorrect solution.
  • Stay organized: Write your steps clearly and neatly. This makes it easier to track your work and spot any errors. Use a logical layout, and don't try to skip steps in your head. A well-organized approach is key to accurate problem-solving.
  • Practice, practice, practice: The best way to become proficient at solving linear equations is to practice. Work through a variety of examples, and don't be afraid to make mistakes. Mistakes are learning opportunities! The more you practice, the more confident and skilled you'll become.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Even with a good understanding of the steps involved, it's easy to make mistakes when solving linear equations. Let's look at some common errors and how to avoid them. Being aware of these pitfalls can help you develop good habits and improve your accuracy.

  • Incorrect distribution: A frequent mistake is not distributing correctly, especially when there's a negative sign involved. For example, in the expression - (x + 3), remember to distribute the negative sign to both terms inside the parentheses: -1 * x + (-1) * 3 = -x - 3. Always double-check your distribution to avoid sign errors.
  • Combining unlike terms: You can only combine terms that have the same variable raised to the same power. For instance, you can combine 3x and 5x to get 8x, but you cannot combine 3x and 5x^2. Make sure you're only combining like terms.
  • Forgetting to perform the same operation on both sides: The golden rule of equation solving is that whatever you do to one side, you must do to the other. If you add a number to one side, you must add the same number to the other side. Failing to do this will break the equality and lead to an incorrect solution.
  • Sign errors: Sign errors are a common source of mistakes. Be careful when adding, subtracting, multiplying, and dividing negative numbers. A simple sign error can throw off your entire solution. Take your time and pay attention to the signs.
  • Incorrect order of operations: Remember the order of operations (PEMDAS/BODMAS): Parentheses/Brackets, Exponents/Orders, Multiplication and Division (from left to right), Addition and Subtraction (from left to right). Make sure you're performing operations in the correct order to avoid errors.

By being mindful of these common mistakes and actively working to avoid them, you'll significantly improve your accuracy and confidence in solving linear equations.

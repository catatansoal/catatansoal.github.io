Finding Roots Of Polynomial Equation X³ - 7x = 6x - 12 With Graphing Calculator

Jul 13, 2025 by ADMIN 80 views

Hey guys! Today, we're diving into the fascinating world of polynomial equations. Specifically, we're going to tackle the equation x³ - 7x = 6x - 12 and discover its roots using a graphing calculator and the system of equations approach. It might sound intimidating at first, but trust me, we'll break it down into easy-to-understand steps. We'll not only find the answer but also explore the underlying concepts, so you'll be a polynomial-solving pro in no time! Let's embark on this mathematical adventure together.

Understanding Polynomial Equations and Roots

Before we jump into solving our specific equation, let's take a moment to understand what polynomial equations and their roots actually are. In essence, a polynomial equation is an equation that involves variables raised to non-negative integer powers, combined with coefficients and constants. Our equation, x³ - 7x = 6x - 12, perfectly fits this description. The highest power of the variable (x) in the equation determines its degree. In this case, the degree is 3, making it a cubic equation. The roots of a polynomial equation, also known as solutions or zeros, are the values of the variable (x) that make the equation true. Geometrically, these roots represent the points where the graph of the polynomial function intersects the x-axis. Finding these roots is a fundamental problem in algebra, with applications in various fields like engineering, physics, and computer science. There are several methods to find the roots, including factoring, using the quadratic formula (for quadratic equations), and numerical methods like using a graphing calculator. In our case, we'll be employing a graphing calculator and the system of equations approach to unveil the roots of the given cubic equation. This method offers a visual and intuitive way to understand the solutions. So, stay tuned as we move forward and demystify the process of finding the roots!

Setting Up the Equation for Graphing

Okay, now that we have a grasp of what we're trying to achieve, let's get our hands dirty and prepare the equation for graphing. The first step is to rearrange the equation x³ - 7x = 6x - 12 into a standard form. This means getting all the terms on one side of the equation, leaving zero on the other side. To do this, we'll subtract 6x from both sides and add 12 to both sides. This will give us: x³ - 7x - 6x + 12 = 0. Now, let's simplify by combining like terms: x³ - 13x + 12 = 0. Great! We now have our polynomial equation in the standard form, which is much easier to work with. The next step is to think about how we can represent this equation graphically. Remember, we're aiming to use a graphing calculator to find the roots. To do this, we'll treat the left-hand side of the equation (x³ - 13x + 12) as a function, which we can call y. So, we have y = x³ - 13x + 12. This represents a cubic function, and its graph will be a curve that might intersect the x-axis at one or more points. These intersection points are precisely the roots of our original equation! Now that we have our function, we're ready to fire up the graphing calculator and plot this curve. In the next section, we'll walk through the steps of using the graphing calculator to visualize the function and identify its roots.

Using a Graphing Calculator to Find Roots

Alright, guys, let's bring in the power of technology! It's time to fire up your graphing calculators and visualize our cubic function. If you're new to graphing calculators, don't worry; we'll go through it step by step. The first thing you'll want to do is access the equation editor on your calculator. This is usually done by pressing the "Y=" button. You'll see a list of functions like Y1=, Y2=, and so on. We'll enter our function, y = x³ - 13x + 12, into one of these slots. So, type in x^3 - 13x + 12. Remember to use the correct keys for the exponent (usually a ^ symbol) and the negative sign. Once you've entered the function, it's time to graph it! Press the "GRAPH" button, and you should see a curve appear on the screen. If the graph doesn't look quite right, you might need to adjust the window settings. This allows you to change the range of x and y values displayed on the screen. A good starting point is the standard window, which you can usually access by pressing the "ZOOM" button and selecting "ZStandard" or "Zoom Standard." Now, take a good look at the graph. You'll notice that it crosses the x-axis at several points. These points are the roots of our equation! To find the exact values of these roots, we'll use the calculator's "zero" or "root" finding feature. This is usually found under the "CALC" menu (often accessed by pressing "2nd" and then "TRACE"). Select the "zero" or "root" option, and the calculator will guide you through the process. You'll need to specify a left bound, a right bound, and a guess for each root. The calculator will then use numerical methods to find the root within that interval. By repeating this process for each intersection point, we can identify all the roots of the equation. In the next section, we'll explore an alternative approach using a system of equations, which can provide a different perspective on finding the roots.

Solving with a System of Equations

Now, let's explore another interesting way to find the roots of our equation: using a system of equations. This method might seem a bit different at first, but it provides a powerful way to visualize and understand the solutions. Remember our original equation, x³ - 7x = 6x - 12? Instead of rearranging it into the standard form as we did before, we're going to treat each side of the equation as a separate function. Let's define two functions: y1 = x³ - 7x and y2 = 6x - 12. Now, we have a system of two equations. The solutions to this system are the points where the graphs of these two functions intersect. These intersection points are crucial because they represent the x-values where y1 is equal to y2, which is exactly what our original equation states. So, the x-coordinates of these intersection points are the roots of our polynomial equation! To find these intersection points, we can again use our trusty graphing calculator. Enter y1 = x³ - 7x into one of the function slots (like Y1=) and y2 = 6x - 12 into another slot (like Y2=). Then, press the "GRAPH" button. You'll see two graphs: a cubic curve (y1) and a straight line (y2). Look for the points where these two graphs intersect. The calculator has a handy feature for finding these intersection points. It's usually found under the "CALC" menu, similar to the "zero" finding feature. Select the "intersect" option, and the calculator will guide you through the process. You'll need to select the two curves you want to find the intersection of (y1 and y2 in our case) and provide a guess for the intersection point. The calculator will then calculate the coordinates of the intersection. By repeating this process for each intersection point, you'll find all the solutions to the system of equations, which are also the roots of our original polynomial equation. This method offers a great visual way to confirm the roots we found earlier using the single-function graphing approach.

Identifying the Roots and the Correct Answer

Okay, guys, we've done the hard work of setting up the equation, using the graphing calculator, and exploring the system of equations approach. Now comes the exciting part: identifying the roots and choosing the correct answer from the given options. Whether you used the single-function graphing method or the system of equations method, you should have found three points where the graphs intersect the x-axis or each other. These points represent the roots of our equation, x³ - 7x = 6x - 12. If you used the graphing calculator's "zero" or "intersect" feature, you should have obtained approximate numerical values for these roots. Let's consider the options provided: A. -6, 6 B. -4, 1, 3 C. -3, -1, 4 D. 1, 3 By examining the graph on your calculator or the intersection points you found, you should be able to match the roots you obtained with one of these options. Remember, the roots are the x-values where the graph crosses the x-axis (in the single-function method) or where the two graphs intersect (in the system of equations method). Carefully compare the roots you found with the options. You might notice that some of the options contain numbers that are close to your calculated roots, but only one option will contain the exact values (or very close approximations, depending on the calculator's precision). In this case, by graphing the equation or the system of equations, you'll find that the roots are -4, 1, and 3. Therefore, the correct answer is B. -4, 1, 3. Congratulations! You've successfully navigated the world of polynomial equations and found the roots using both a graphing calculator and the system of equations approach. Let's recap the key takeaways from this adventure. First, we learned what polynomial equations and their roots are. This method provided a visual confirmation of the roots we found using the single-function graphing method.

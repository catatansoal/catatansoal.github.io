The reason why 5G can affect voice call quality boils down to network switching. Diving Deeper into 5G Technology and Its Quirks

To truly grasp why 5G might sometimes be a troublemaker, let's peek under the hood and explore the techy bits. 5G, or the fifth generation of wireless technology, isn't just one thing. It‚Äôs a collection of different technologies and frequency bands, each with its own strengths and weaknesses. Understanding these nuances can help you appreciate the complexities involved in delivering a reliable 5G experience.

One of the key factors influencing 5G performance is the frequency band being used. 5G operates on a range of frequencies, broadly categorized into three groups: low-band, mid-band, and high-band (mmWave). Low-band 5G operates on frequencies similar to 4G LTE, offering good coverage but only moderately faster speeds. Mid-band 5G strikes a balance between speed and coverage, providing a significant speed boost over 4G LTE while still offering decent range. High-band 5G, or mmWave, is where the magic happens in terms of speed, delivering gigabit-level performance. However, mmWave has a very short range and is easily blocked by obstacles like walls and trees. This means that mmWave 5G is typically deployed in dense urban areas and requires a dense network of small cell sites to provide consistent coverage. This is where the complexity of providing a consistent 5G experience begins to become clearer.

The architecture of 5G networks also plays a crucial role in their performance. Unlike previous generations of cellular technology, 5G is designed with a more flexible and software-defined architecture. This allows network operators to deploy 5G in different configurations, depending on their specific needs and the available infrastructure. One common configuration is Non-Standalone (NSA) 5G, which relies on the existing 4G LTE infrastructure for certain functions, such as call setup and mobility management. This means that even if you're connected to a 5G network for data, your phone might still be using 4G LTE for voice calls. This dependency on 4G LTE can introduce complexities and potential points of failure, especially during network handovers. A standalone (SA) 5G architecture, on the other hand, operates independently of 4G LTE, offering the full benefits of 5G technology, including lower latency and improved network efficiency. However, SA 5G deployments are still relatively limited, and most 5G networks today are based on the NSA architecture.

The message you see on your iPhone is essentially acknowledging the transitional nature of 5G deployment. As network operators continue to build out their 5G infrastructure and transition to SA 5G, the reliability and performance of 5G networks will continue to improve. However, for the time being, there are inherent challenges in managing the interplay between 5G and older network technologies, particularly when it comes to voice calls. The technology behind 5G is incredibly advanced, but the practical challenges of deploying and managing such a complex network mean that hiccups can occur, especially in the early stages of its rollout. The iPhone, in its effort to provide the best possible user experience, sometimes needs to juggle between different network types, which can occasionally lead to less-than-ideal results.

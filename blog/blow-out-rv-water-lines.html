Blow Out RV Water Lines: Winterizing Guide

Aug 9, 2025 by ADMIN 43 views

As the temperatures dip and winter's chill sets in, RV enthusiasts know it's time to think about winterizing their beloved vehicles. Winterizing your RV is crucial to protect it from freezing temperatures, which can cause significant damage to your plumbing system. One of the most effective methods for winterizing is to blow out the water lines with air. This process removes water from the pipes, preventing them from freezing and potentially bursting. In this comprehensive guide, we'll walk you through each step of how to blow out RV water lines with air, ensuring your RV stays in top shape throughout the winter months. So, buckle up, RV adventurers, and let's get started on this essential maintenance task!

Why Blowing Out Water Lines is Essential

Before we dive into the step-by-step process, let's discuss why blowing out your RV's water lines is so important. Water expands when it freezes, and this expansion can exert tremendous pressure on your RV's plumbing system. This pressure can crack pipes, damage fittings, and even ruin your water pump and water heater. Repairs can be costly and time-consuming, which is why preventive maintenance like blowing out the water lines is essential. Imagine the scenario: you've parked your RV for the winter, thinking everything is fine, only to discover in the spring that your pipes have burst and your RV is flooded. This is a nightmare scenario that can easily be avoided with proper winterization. Blowing out the water lines with air is a simple yet effective way to prevent this disaster. By removing the water, you eliminate the risk of freezing and expansion. Think of it as an insurance policy for your RV's plumbing system. Plus, it’s a task you can easily do yourself, saving you money on professional winterization services. So, taking the time to blow out your RV's water lines is an investment in the longevity and health of your RV.

Moreover, consider the hidden areas in your RV's plumbing system where water might linger. Small amounts of water can get trapped in low points in the lines, elbows, and other fittings. These are prime spots for freezing and subsequent damage. Blowing out the lines ensures that even these small pockets of water are cleared out, providing comprehensive protection. Ignoring this step can lead to slow leaks that go unnoticed until significant damage has occurred. These leaks can cause mold and mildew growth, which poses health risks and can be expensive to remediate. A thorough blowout, combined with the use of RV antifreeze in the traps and drains, offers the best defense against winter damage. By spending a few hours on this task, you can avoid potentially thousands of dollars in repairs and ensure your RV is ready for your next adventure when spring rolls around. Don't underestimate the importance of this step – it's a cornerstone of responsible RV ownership.

Gathering Your Supplies

To properly blow out your RV water lines, you'll need to gather a few essential supplies. Having everything on hand before you start will make the process smoother and more efficient. Here’s a list of what you'll need:

  1. Air Compressor: A small to medium-sized air compressor with an adjustable regulator is crucial. You'll want to set the pressure between 30-40 PSI (pounds per square inch) to avoid damaging your RV's plumbing. Too much pressure can be just as harmful as freezing temperatures, so precision is key. Think of your air compressor as the heart of this operation – it's what will power the water out of your lines. A compressor with a decent tank capacity will also reduce the number of times it needs to cycle on, making the job quicker.
  2. Air Compressor Adapter: This adapter connects your air compressor hose to your RV's city water inlet. It's a small but vital piece of equipment, ensuring a secure and airtight connection. You can find these adapters at most RV supply stores or online. The adapter usually features a quick-connect fitting on one end for the compressor hose and a standard fitting on the other end to screw onto the city water inlet. A quality adapter will prevent air leaks and ensure efficient blowing.
  3. Wrench: You'll need a wrench to remove the water filter and drain plugs. An adjustable wrench is a versatile tool that can handle various sizes of nuts and bolts. Make sure your wrench is in good condition and fits comfortably in your hand. Having the right tools makes the job safer and more straightforward.
  4. Screwdriver: A screwdriver (usually a Phillips or flathead) might be necessary to remove access panels or drain plugs. Having a set of screwdrivers in different sizes is a good idea for any RV owner. Just like the wrench, ensure your screwdriver fits snugly into the screw heads to avoid stripping them.
  5. RV Antifreeze: This non-toxic antifreeze is specifically designed for RV plumbing systems. It's used to protect the P-traps in your sinks and showers and the toilet bowl from freezing. Regular automotive antifreeze is toxic and should never be used in your RV's potable water system. RV antifreeze is typically pink in color and is readily available at RV supply stores and online retailers. The amount you'll need depends on the size of your RV, but a few gallons should be sufficient for most rigs.
  6. Water Heater Bypass Kit (if applicable): If your RV has a water heater bypass kit, now is the time to use it. This kit allows you to isolate the water heater, preventing it from filling with antifreeze. If your RV doesn't have a bypass kit, you'll need to fill the water heater with antifreeze, which requires a significant amount. Check your RV's manual for instructions on how to bypass your water heater.
  7. Gloves and Eye Protection: Safety first! Wear gloves to protect your hands from dirt and grime, and eye protection to prevent any debris or water from splashing into your eyes. Safety glasses are inexpensive and a crucial part of any DIY project.

With your supplies gathered, you're well-prepared to tackle the task of blowing out your RV's water lines. Remember, taking the time to gather the right tools and materials is an investment in a successful and stress-free winterization process.

Step-by-Step Guide to Blowing Out RV Water Lines

Now that you have your supplies ready, let's walk through the step-by-step process of blowing out your RV's water lines with air. Follow these instructions carefully to ensure a thorough and effective winterization.

1. Drain the Water System

The first step is to drain all the water from your RV's system. This includes the fresh water tank, water heater, and all the water lines. Start by draining the fresh water tank. Locate the drain valve, usually found underneath the tank, and open it to allow the water to flow out. Make sure you have a clear path for the water to drain, and consider using a bucket to catch the water if you're in a sensitive area. Next, drain the water heater. Before draining the water heater, make sure it's turned off and the water has cooled down to prevent burns. Remove the drain plug, usually located at the bottom of the water heater, and let the water drain out. Be aware that sediment may have accumulated in the water heater, so be prepared for some dirty water. Once the water heater is drained, replace the drain plug, but don't tighten it completely just yet. Finally, open all the faucets, both hot and cold, and flush the toilet to relieve pressure in the system. This step ensures that as much water as possible is removed before you start blowing out the lines.

2. Bypass the Water Heater

If your RV has a water heater bypass kit, engage it now. This will prevent antifreeze from entering the water heater, saving you a significant amount of antifreeze and time. The bypass kit usually consists of a series of valves that redirect the water flow, isolating the water heater. Refer to your RV's manual for specific instructions on how to use the bypass kit. If your RV doesn't have a bypass kit, you'll need to fill the water heater with antifreeze later in the process. Bypassing the water heater is a crucial step for efficiency and cost-effectiveness. It reduces the amount of antifreeze needed and ensures that your water heater is protected without unnecessary chemical exposure.

3. Connect the Air Compressor

Attach the air compressor adapter to your RV's city water inlet. Ensure the connection is secure and airtight. Connect your air compressor hose to the adapter and set the pressure regulator on the compressor to 30-40 PSI. It's crucial not to exceed this pressure, as higher pressures can damage your RV's plumbing. The goal here is to gently push the water out of the lines, not blast it out with excessive force. Think of this step as carefully applying the right amount of pressure, like a gentle breeze instead of a hurricane. Double-check the connections to ensure there are no leaks before proceeding.

4. Blow Out the Water Lines

With the air compressor connected, start blowing out the water lines. Open one faucet at a time, starting with the one closest to the city water inlet. Allow the air to flow until no more water comes out, and then close the faucet. Repeat this process for all the faucets, including the shower and outdoor shower if you have one. Don't forget to flush the toilet as well. It's important to do both the hot and cold water sides of each faucet. You may see some sputtering and spurting at first, but eventually, the water flow should turn into a steady stream of air. Be patient and allow sufficient time for the air to push all the water out. This process may take several minutes per faucet. The key is to be thorough and methodical, ensuring that every line is clear of water.

5. Address Low-Point Drains

Many RVs have low-point drains, which are located at the lowest points in the plumbing system. These drains help to remove any remaining water that might be trapped in the lines. Open the low-point drains and allow any remaining water to drain out. You might be surprised at how much water can still come out even after blowing out the lines. These low-point drains are essential for a complete winterization, as they target the areas where water is most likely to collect. Make sure to close the low-point drains once all the water has drained out.

6. Add RV Antifreeze

Now it's time to add RV antifreeze to protect the P-traps and toilet. Pour RV antifreeze into each drain (sinks, shower) and the toilet bowl. The antifreeze will displace any remaining water and prevent freezing. Use enough antifreeze to fill the P-traps, which are the curved sections of pipe under the sinks and shower. These traps are designed to hold water to prevent sewer gases from entering your RV, but they also need protection from freezing. For the toilet, pour enough antifreeze into the bowl to cover the bottom and protect the flush valve. Remember, use only RV antifreeze, as other types of antifreeze are toxic and can contaminate your RV's water system. This step is the final safeguard against freezing damage, ensuring that even the most vulnerable parts of your plumbing system are protected.

7. Final Checks and Preparations

Finally, disconnect the air compressor and replace any drain plugs you removed earlier. Tighten the drain plug on the water heater, but don't overtighten it. Store your water hose and any other winterizing equipment. Make sure all faucets are closed and the toilet is in the closed position. You might also want to add some antifreeze to your RV's holding tanks to prevent any residual water from freezing. As a final precaution, consider leaving cabinet doors open to allow warm air to circulate around the pipes. This can help prevent freezing in extreme cold conditions. With these final checks and preparations completed, you can rest assured that your RV is well-protected for the winter.

Additional Tips for Winterizing Your RV

Blowing out the water lines is a crucial step in winterizing your RV, but there are other important tasks to consider for comprehensive protection. Here are some additional tips to help you winterize your RV effectively:

  1. Inspect and Seal: Check for any cracks or gaps in your RV's exterior and seal them with caulk or sealant. This will help prevent drafts and keep the interior warmer, reducing the risk of freezing. Pay close attention to windows, doors, and any openings where pipes or wires enter the RV. Sealing these areas can make a significant difference in maintaining a stable temperature inside your RV.
  2. Remove or Protect Batteries: RV batteries can be damaged by freezing temperatures. If possible, remove the batteries and store them in a warm, dry place. If you can't remove them, disconnect them and ensure they are fully charged. Consider using a battery maintainer to keep them charged throughout the winter. Proper battery care is essential for ensuring they are ready to go when spring arrives.
  3. Cover the RV: A good RV cover can protect your RV from snow, ice, and UV damage. Choose a cover that is breathable to prevent moisture buildup and mildew growth. Covering your RV is an investment in its long-term condition, protecting it from the elements and extending its lifespan.
  4. Propane Tanks: Fill your propane tanks before winterizing. Propane is used for heating and cooking, and having full tanks ensures you're ready for any cold snaps. Inspect the tanks and connections for leaks and ensure they are stored safely.
  5. Tires: Protect your RV's tires by covering them or parking the RV on leveling blocks. This will prevent the tires from freezing to the ground and reduce the risk of damage. Check the tire pressure regularly, as it can fluctuate with temperature changes.
  6. Clean the Interior: Give your RV a thorough cleaning before winterizing. Remove any food items that could attract pests and clean out the refrigerator and freezer. This will help prevent odors and maintain a clean environment inside your RV.
  7. Check the Roof: Inspect your RV's roof for any damage or leaks. Repair any issues before winter to prevent water damage. A well-maintained roof is crucial for protecting your RV from the elements.

By following these additional tips, you can ensure that your RV is fully protected from the winter elements. Winterizing your RV is an investment in its longevity and ensures that it will be ready for your next adventure when the weather warms up.

Conclusion

Blowing out your RV water lines with air is a critical step in the winterization process. By following the steps outlined in this guide, you can effectively protect your RV's plumbing system from freezing temperatures and costly damage. Remember to gather your supplies, drain the water system, bypass the water heater, connect the air compressor, blow out the lines, add RV antifreeze, and perform final checks. Additionally, consider the extra tips for comprehensive winterization to ensure your RV is fully protected. Taking the time to winterize your RV properly is an investment in its longevity and your peace of mind. So, fellow RVers, take these precautions, and your home on wheels will be ready for many more adventures to come! Happy travels, and stay warm!

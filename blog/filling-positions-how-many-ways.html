Filling Positions: How Many Ways From 15 People?

Aug 6, 2025 by ADMIN 49 views

Hey guys! Ever wondered how many different ways you can fill a few positions when you have a bunch of talented people to choose from? It's a classic problem in the world of mathematics, specifically in the area of combinatorics. Let's dive into a scenario where we have 15 amazing individuals and three distinct roles to fill. We'll break down how to calculate the possibilities, making it super easy to understand. So, grab your thinking caps, and let's get started!

The Core Concept: Permutations

At the heart of this problem lies the concept of permutations. Permutations deal with arranging items in a specific order. Think of it this way: if you're choosing a president, a vice-president, and a treasurer from a club, the order in which you pick them matters. Picking Alice as president, Bob as VP, and Carol as treasurer is different from picking Bob as president, Carol as VP, and Alice as treasurer. This is precisely what distinguishes permutations from combinations, where the order doesn't matter.

In our case, each of the three positions ‚Äì let's call them Position A, Position B, and Position C ‚Äì is unique. Therefore, the order in which we select people to fill them is significant. This means we're dealing with a permutation problem.

So, how do we calculate the number of permutations? Well, it's all about considering the choices available at each step. For Position A, we have 15 candidates. Once we've filled Position A, we have 14 people left to choose from for Position B. And after filling Positions A and B, we have 13 people remaining for Position C. To find the total number of ways to fill these three positions, we multiply the number of choices at each step together. This is where the fundamental principle of counting comes into play, a cornerstone of combinatorics. It essentially states that if you have 'm' ways to do one thing and 'n' ways to do another, then you have m * n ways to do both. We extend this principle when we have multiple steps, like filling these three positions.

Therefore, the total number of ways to fill the positions is 15 * 14 * 13. This calculation is the key to understanding the problem and arriving at the correct solution. We're not just picking three people; we're arranging them in a specific order within the three positions, and that's what makes this a permutation.

Step-by-Step Calculation: The Solution Unveiled

Now, let‚Äôs walk through the calculation step-by-step to solidify our understanding. We start with 15 candidates for the first position. This is our starting point, and it's crucial because it sets the stage for the subsequent selections.

Once you've chosen someone for Position A, that person is no longer available for the other positions. This is because each person can only hold one position at a time. So, for Position B, we're left with 14 candidates. Our list has shrunk by one, and we now have a slightly smaller pool to choose from. This reduction in the number of choices is a key aspect of permutations ‚Äì each selection affects the subsequent choices.

With Positions A and B filled, we move on to Position C. Now, we have only 13 candidates remaining. Two people have already been assigned their roles, leaving us with a smaller group for the final position. This demonstrates how the number of possibilities decreases as we fill each position, reflecting the sequential nature of the selection process.

To get the total number of ways to fill all three positions, we multiply the number of choices at each step: 15 * 14 * 13. This is a direct application of the fundamental principle of counting, which allows us to calculate the total number of outcomes when we have a series of independent choices. Multiplying these numbers gives us 2730. This means there are 2730 different ways to arrange 3 people out of a group of 15 into these distinct positions. This is a substantial number, highlighting the power of permutations in creating diverse arrangements.

So, the correct answer is D) 15 * 14 * 13. We've arrived at this answer by carefully considering the number of choices at each step and applying the principle of permutations. This step-by-step approach ensures that we understand not just the answer, but also the why behind it.

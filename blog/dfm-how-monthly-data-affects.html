It appears you're encountering a common challenge when working with mixed-frequency data in dynamic factor models (DFM) using statsmodels. Specifically, you've observed that incorporating new monthly data for December doesn't change the nowcast for your quarterly variable, and you're wondering why. Understanding Dynamic Factor Models and Mixed-Frequency Data

Before we get into the specifics of your code, let's quickly review the key concepts. Dynamic factor models are powerful tools for analyzing multivariate time series data. The core idea is to represent the observed variables as linear combinations of a few unobserved common factors and idiosyncratic errors. This dimensionality reduction makes it easier to understand the underlying dynamics of the system.

When dealing with mixed-frequency data, such as monthly and quarterly series, things get a bit more complex. The model needs to handle the fact that some variables are observed more frequently than others. In your case, you have monthly variables (x1, x2) and a quarterly variable (y). The quarterly variable is only observed at the end of each quarter, so its values are NaN for the other months.

The challenge here is how to use the information from the higher-frequency monthly data to improve the nowcasts and forecasts of the lower-frequency quarterly data. Intuitively, we expect that new monthly data should influence our understanding of the common factors, and this, in turn, should affect the nowcast of the quarterly variable. So, why isn't this happening in your case?

Key Concepts in Dynamic Factor Models

To really understand what's happening, let's break down some of the key concepts behind Dynamic Factor Models (DFMs). These models are super useful for dealing with time series data, especially when you've got variables that are measured at different frequencies.

  • Latent Factors: At the heart of DFMs are these unobserved, or latent, factors. Think of them as the hidden drivers behind the variables you actually see. For instance, in economics, a latent factor might represent the overall state of the economy, influencing things like GDP, employment rates, and inflation. The model tries to figure out these factors from the data you feed it. These factors evolve over time, and they influence the observed variables. In your case, you have one latent factor (k_factors=1), which is meant to capture the shared dynamics of your monthly and quarterly variables. The goal is for this factor to incorporate the information from the monthly data and, consequently, affect the quarterly nowcast.
  • Loadings: The loadings are like the weights that connect the latent factors to the observed variables. They tell you how much each factor influences each variable. So, if a variable has a high loading on a particular factor, that means it's strongly influenced by that factor. The model estimates these loadings to figure out how the latent factors translate into the observed data. They quantify the relationship between the latent factors and the observed variables. If a variable has a high loading on a factor, it means that the factor has a strong influence on that variable.
  • State-Space Representation: DFMs are often expressed in what's called a state-space form. This is a fancy way of saying that the model is written as two sets of equations: the measurement equation and the transition equation. The measurement equation relates the observed variables to the latent factors, while the transition equation describes how the latent factors evolve over time. This framework is crucial for handling mixed-frequency data because it allows the model to update its estimates of the latent factors as new data becomes available. State-space representation is a way of writing the model as two sets of equations: the measurement equation (how observed variables relate to latent factors) and the transition equation (how latent factors evolve). This is key for handling mixed-frequency data and updating estimates as new data comes in.
  • Estimation and the Kalman Filter: To actually use a DFM, you need to estimate its parameters. This is often done using a technique called the Kalman filter. The Kalman filter is a recursive algorithm that updates the model's estimates of the latent factors as new data points are observed. It's like a smart updating mechanism that continuously refines the model's understanding of the underlying factors. This is a recursive algorithm that updates the model's estimates of the latent factors as new data points are observed. It's the engine that drives the model's learning process.

It recursively updates the model's estimates of the latent factors as new data becomes available. However, the Kalman filter's ability to incorporate new information depends on the model's structure and the data patterns.

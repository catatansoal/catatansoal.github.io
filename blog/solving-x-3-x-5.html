Solving (x-3)(x-5)=35 A Step By Step Guide

Jul 14, 2025 by ADMIN 43 views

Hey guys! Let's dive into solving a quadratic equation today. We've got a fun one: (x-3)(x-5)=35. Our mission is to figure out which of the given options—A. x=-8, B. x=-5, C. x=2, or D. x=10—actually makes this equation true. Buckle up, because we're about to break it down step-by-step!

Understanding Quadratic Equations

Before we jump into the nitty-gritty, let’s quickly recap what a quadratic equation is. Quadratic equations are polynomial equations of the second degree, meaning the highest power of the variable (in our case, x) is 2. They generally take the form ax² + bx + c = 0, where a, b, and c are constants. These equations pop up all over the place in math and real-world applications, from physics to engineering to even finance. Solving them is a crucial skill, and there are several methods to do it. You might remember factoring, using the quadratic formula, or completing the square. In our case, we'll start by simplifying and rearranging the equation to get it into that standard quadratic form. This will involve expanding the brackets and moving all terms to one side. Once we have it in the standard form, we can then decide on the best approach to solve for x. It's like getting all the ingredients ready before you start cooking – you need to set the stage for success! Remember, the goal is to find the values of x that make the equation balance perfectly. This often means we'll find two solutions because of the x² term. So, let's roll up our sleeves and get started on the first step: expanding those brackets! This is where the fun really begins, as we transform the original equation into something we can work with more easily. Stay tuned, because the next steps will bring us closer to uncovering the solution!

Step 1: Expanding and Simplifying the Equation

Alright, let's get our hands dirty and start by expanding the left side of the equation (x-3)(x-5)=35. To do this, we'll use the good old FOIL method (First, Outer, Inner, Last). This helps us make sure we multiply each term in the first set of parentheses by each term in the second set. So, here we go:

  • First: x * x = x²
  • Outer: x * -5 = -5x
  • Inner: -3 * x = -3x
  • Last: -3 * -5 = 15

Now, let's put it all together: x² - 5x - 3x + 15. We can simplify this further by combining the like terms (-5x and -3x), which gives us x² - 8x + 15. Great! So, our equation now looks like this: x² - 8x + 15 = 35. But remember, to solve a quadratic equation, we need it in the standard form ax² + bx + c = 0. This means we need to get that 35 on the right side over to the left side. To do that, we'll subtract 35 from both sides of the equation. This keeps the equation balanced and moves us closer to our goal. So, subtracting 35 from both sides, we get: x² - 8x + 15 - 35 = 0. Simplifying the left side gives us our final quadratic equation in standard form: x² - 8x - 20 = 0. Phew! That was a lot of algebraic maneuvering, but we've successfully transformed the original equation into a form that's much easier to work with. We're now in a great position to start solving for x. The next step? We'll explore our options for finding those solutions. Keep your thinking caps on, because we're about to dive into factoring and see if we can crack this equation wide open!

Step 2: Solving the Quadratic Equation

Now that we've got our equation in the standard form x² - 8x - 20 = 0, it's time to find the values of x that make this true. There are a couple of ways we can tackle this: factoring or using the quadratic formula. Factoring is often the quicker route if we can find two numbers that multiply to -20 and add up to -8. Let's give it a shot! Think about pairs of factors of -20. We've got 1 and -20, -1 and 20, 2 and -10, -2 and 10, 4 and -5, and -4 and 5. Which of these pairs adds up to -8? Bingo! 2 and -10 fit the bill. So, we can rewrite our quadratic equation in factored form as (x + 2)(x - 10) = 0. This is a crucial step because it allows us to use the zero-product property. This property states that if the product of two factors is zero, then at least one of the factors must be zero. In other words, if (x + 2)(x - 10) = 0, then either x + 2 = 0 or x - 10 = 0 (or both!). Now we've turned one tricky quadratic equation into two simple linear equations. Let's solve them: For x + 2 = 0, we subtract 2 from both sides to get x = -2. For x - 10 = 0, we add 10 to both sides to get x = 10. So, we've found our two solutions: x = -2 and x = 10. It's always a good idea to double-check our work, especially with quadratic equations. We can plug these values back into the original equation to make sure they really work. But before we do that, let's take a look at the answer choices we were given and see if any of them match our solutions. This can sometimes save us a bit of time. Ready to move on to the next step and match our solutions to the options? Let's do it!

Step 3: Matching the Solution with the Options

Okay, we've done the hard work of solving the equation (x-3)(x-5)=35, and we found two solutions: x = -2 and x = 10. Now, let's compare these solutions with the options provided to us:

  • A. x = -8
  • B. x = -5
  • C. x = 2
  • D. x = 10

Looking at our solutions and the options, we can see that x = 10 matches option D. But what about x = -2? It's not there! This is a good reminder that quadratic equations often have two solutions, but not all the options might include both. In this case, we only need to find one correct solution among the choices given. So, the solution that matches one of our findings is x = 10, which corresponds to option D. We can confidently say that option D is a solution to the equation. However, just to be super sure and demonstrate best practices, let’s quickly verify this by plugging x = 10 back into the original equation: (10 - 3)(10 - 5) = 35. This simplifies to (7)(5) = 35, which is indeed true! So, we've not only found the solution but also verified it. It's like completing a puzzle and then checking the picture on the box to make sure all the pieces fit correctly. We’ve successfully matched our solution with the given options. Option D, x=10, is the correct answer. High five! But hold on, let’s do a quick recap to make sure we’ve got all the key steps down pat.

Recap: Solving the Quadratic Equation

Alright, let's take a quick stroll down memory lane and recap how we solved the quadratic equation (x-3)(x-5)=35. This is like reviewing the map after a successful journey to make sure we remember the key landmarks. First, we expanded and simplified the equation. We used the FOIL method to multiply (x-3)(x-5), which gave us x² - 8x + 15. Then, we subtracted 35 from both sides to get the equation in standard quadratic form: x² - 8x - 20 = 0. Next up, we solved the equation by factoring. We looked for two numbers that multiply to -20 and add up to -8, and we found 2 and -10. This allowed us to rewrite the equation as (x + 2)(x - 10) = 0. Using the zero-product property, we set each factor equal to zero and solved for x, giving us x = -2 and x = 10. Finally, we matched our solutions with the options. We saw that x = 10 was one of the choices (option D), and we confirmed that it was indeed a solution. So, to recap, here are the key steps:

  1. Expand and simplify the equation.
  2. Solve the quadratic equation (in this case, by factoring).
  3. Match the solution(s) with the given options.

By following these steps, you can tackle any similar quadratic equation with confidence. Quadratic equations might seem intimidating at first, but with practice, they become much more manageable. It’s like learning to ride a bike – a little wobbly at first, but soon you’re cruising along smoothly. Keep practicing, and you'll become a quadratic equation-solving pro in no time! And remember, if you ever get stuck, there are plenty of resources available, from textbooks to online tutorials to your friendly neighborhood math whiz. So, keep your chin up, your pencil sharp, and your brain engaged. You’ve got this!

Conclusion

So, there you have it! We've successfully navigated the world of quadratic equations and found the solution to (x-3)(x-5)=35. By expanding, simplifying, factoring, and applying the zero-product property, we discovered that x = 10 is indeed a solution, making option D the correct answer. Remember, solving quadratic equations is a fundamental skill in mathematics, and mastering it opens doors to more advanced concepts and real-world applications. Keep practicing, keep exploring, and most importantly, keep enjoying the journey of learning. Until next time, happy solving!

Simplify 4(2u - 2) - 3(u + 2): A Step-by-Step Guide

Aug 6, 2025 by ADMIN 52 views

Introduction

Hey guys! Today, we're diving into simplifying algebraic expressions, which is a fundamental skill in mathematics. We'll be tackling the expression 4(2u - 2) - 3(u + 2). If you're just starting out with algebra or need a refresher, you're in the right place. Simplifying expressions helps us make them easier to understand and work with, which is super useful in solving equations and other math problems. So, grab your pencils, and let's get started!

Breaking Down the Expression

To simplify the expression 4(2u - 2) - 3(u + 2), we need to follow the order of operations, often remembered by the acronym PEMDAS (Parentheses, Exponents, Multiplication and Division, Addition and Subtraction). The first thing we need to address are the parentheses. We'll use the distributive property to get rid of them. What's the distributive property, you ask? It's a fancy term for multiplying the term outside the parentheses by each term inside the parentheses. Trust me; it's easier than it sounds!

Applying the Distributive Property

Let's start with the first part of the expression: 4(2u - 2). We need to distribute the 4 to both the 2u and the -2 inside the parentheses. So, we multiply 4 by 2u, which gives us 8u. Then, we multiply 4 by -2, which gives us -8. So, 4(2u - 2) simplifies to 8u - 8. See? Not so scary, right?

Now, let's move on to the second part of the expression: -3(u + 2). Notice the -3 outside the parentheses. This negative sign is super important! We need to distribute -3 to both the u and the +2 inside the parentheses. Multiplying -3 by u gives us -3u. Then, multiplying -3 by +2 gives us -6. So, -3(u + 2) simplifies to -3u - 6. Remember to keep that negative sign in mind – it makes a big difference!

Combining Like Terms

Now that we've distributed, our expression looks like this: 8u - 8 - 3u - 6. The next step is to combine like terms. What are like terms? They're terms that have the same variable raised to the same power. In our expression, 8u and -3u are like terms because they both have the variable u raised to the power of 1. Similarly, -8 and -6 are like terms because they're both constants (numbers without variables).

To combine like terms, we simply add or subtract their coefficients (the numbers in front of the variables). Let's start with the u terms. We have 8u - 3u. Subtracting the coefficients, 8 - 3, gives us 5. So, 8u - 3u simplifies to 5u. Now, let's combine the constant terms. We have -8 - 6. Adding these numbers gives us -14. So, -8 - 6 simplifies to -14. Putting it all together, our simplified expression is 5u - 14.

Final Simplified Expression

After applying the distributive property and combining like terms, we've successfully simplified the expression 4(2u - 2) - 3(u + 2) to 5u - 14. This is our final answer! Simplifying expressions like this is a crucial step in algebra and will help you tackle more complex problems down the road. You've got this!

Step-by-Step Breakdown

Let's quickly recap the steps we took to simplify this expression. This can be a handy reference for future problems. Here’s a step-by-step breakdown:

  1. Distribute the numbers outside the parentheses to the terms inside the parentheses. Remember to pay attention to negative signs!
  2. Combine like terms. This means adding or subtracting terms that have the same variable raised to the same power, as well as combining constant terms.
  3. Write the simplified expression. Make sure you’ve combined all like terms, and you’re left with the simplest form of the expression.

By following these steps, you can simplify a wide variety of algebraic expressions. Practice makes perfect, so don't be afraid to try out more examples!

Common Mistakes to Avoid

When simplifying expressions, there are a few common mistakes that students often make. Being aware of these pitfalls can help you avoid them and improve your accuracy. Let's go through some of these common errors:

Forgetting to Distribute to All Terms

One of the most frequent mistakes is forgetting to distribute the number outside the parentheses to all the terms inside. Remember, you need to multiply the outside number by each term inside the parentheses. For example, in 4(2u - 2), you need to multiply 4 by both 2u and -2. If you only multiply by 2u and forget the -2, you'll end up with the wrong answer. Always double-check that you've distributed correctly!

Mismanaging Negative Signs

Negative signs can be tricky, especially when distributing. Make sure you're paying close attention to whether the number outside the parentheses is positive or negative. A negative number changes the signs of the terms inside the parentheses when you distribute. For instance, in -3(u + 2), the -3 needs to multiply both u and +2, resulting in -3u - 6. Neglecting the negative sign can completely change the outcome, so be extra careful!

Incorrectly Combining Like Terms

Combining like terms involves adding or subtracting the coefficients of terms with the same variable and power. A common mistake is trying to combine terms that aren't like terms. For example, you can't combine 5u and -14 because 5u has a variable (u) and -14 is a constant. Make sure you're only combining terms that are truly alike. Also, double-check your arithmetic when adding or subtracting the coefficients to avoid simple calculation errors.

Order of Operations Errors

Following the correct order of operations (PEMDAS) is crucial. If you perform addition or subtraction before multiplication or distribution, you'll likely get the wrong answer. Always handle parentheses and distribution before combining like terms. Keeping the order of operations in mind will help you approach simplification problems systematically and accurately.

Rushing Through the Steps

Math problems can sometimes seem tedious, but rushing through the steps can lead to careless errors. Take your time, write out each step clearly, and double-check your work as you go. It's better to spend a few extra moments ensuring accuracy than to rush and make mistakes that could have been avoided. Slow and steady wins the race, especially in math!

By keeping these common mistakes in mind, you can significantly improve your accuracy and confidence when simplifying algebraic expressions. Practice identifying and avoiding these errors, and you'll be well on your way to mastering algebra!

Real-World Applications

You might be wondering, "Why is simplifying expressions even important?" Well, simplifying expressions isn't just an abstract math concept; it has many practical applications in real-world situations. From managing finances to designing structures, the ability to simplify expressions is a valuable skill. Let's explore some scenarios where simplifying expressions can come in handy.

Financial Planning

Imagine you're planning a budget. You have a certain amount of income, and you need to account for various expenses. Some expenses might be fixed, while others might vary depending on your choices. For example, you might have a fixed rent payment and variable spending on groceries and entertainment. Simplifying expressions can help you create a budget formula and determine how much you can spend in different categories.

For instance, let's say your monthly income is I, your rent is R, and you estimate spending x dollars per week on groceries and y dollars per week on entertainment. Your total monthly expenses on groceries and entertainment would be 4x + 4y (since there are approximately four weeks in a month). If you also have a fixed monthly expense of F for other bills, your total monthly expenses would be R + 4x + 4y + F. To figure out how much you have left for savings or other expenses, you can subtract your total expenses from your income: I - (R + 4x + 4y + F). Simplifying this expression can help you easily see how your income relates to your expenses and make informed financial decisions.

Engineering and Design

In fields like engineering and design, simplifying expressions is crucial for calculating measurements, forces, and other quantities. For example, when designing a bridge, engineers need to calculate the forces acting on the structure and ensure it can withstand those forces. These calculations often involve complex algebraic expressions that need to be simplified to make them manageable.

Consider a simple example where an engineer is calculating the total length of material needed for a project. They might have several pieces of material with lengths expressed as algebraic terms, such as 2L + 3, 3L - 1, and L + 4, where L represents a variable length. To find the total length, the engineer needs to add these expressions together: (2L + 3) + (3L - 1) + (L + 4). Simplifying this expression involves combining like terms: 2L + 3L + L gives 6L, and 3 - 1 + 4 gives 6. So, the total length is 6L + 6. Simplifying the expression makes it easier to calculate the total length for different values of L.

Everyday Problem Solving

Simplifying expressions isn't just for specialized fields; it can also help you solve everyday problems. To calculate your final cost, you need to add up the prices of the items, subtract the coupon discount, and possibly add sales tax. This can involve several steps, but simplifying the expressions can help you keep track of your calculations and ensure you get the correct total. The ability to break down complex situations into simpler algebraic expressions is a valuable life skill that can save you time and money.

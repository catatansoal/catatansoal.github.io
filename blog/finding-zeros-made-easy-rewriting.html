Understanding the Zeros of a Function

Before we jump into rewriting the function, let's quickly recap what the zeros of a function actually are. In simple terms, the zeros (also known as roots or x-intercepts) are the values of x that make the function f(x) equal to zero. Graphically, these are the points where the parabola intersects the x-axis. Finding these zeros is a fundamental skill in algebra and has applications in various fields, from physics to engineering.

They tell us where the function crosses the x-axis, which is crucial for understanding the graph's shape and position. In real-world applications, zeros can represent solutions to problems, such as finding the time when a projectile hits the ground or the break-even point for a business. Option C: The Factored Form - The Zero-Revealing Champion

Let's analyze option C, f(x) = (x + 10)(x - 2). This form is called the factored form of a quadratic equation. And guess what? It's the champion when it comes to easily identifying zeros! Why? Because it directly shows us the values of x that make the function equal to zero. Remember, if any factor in the equation equals zero, the entire product becomes zero.

So, in this case, we have two factors: (x + 10) and (x - 2). To find the zeros, we simply set each factor equal to zero and solve for x:

  • x + 10 = 0 => x = -10
  • x - 2 = 0 => x = 2

Boom! There you have it. The zeros of the function are x = -10 and x = 2. See how straightforward that was? The factored form makes the zeros jump right out at you. This is because the factored form directly represents the x-intercepts of the parabola. Each factor corresponds to a zero of the function. Understanding this relationship allows us to quickly determine the zeros without having to go through complex calculations. Moreover, the factored form also gives us insight into the behavior of the function near its zeros. The factored form is particularly useful when dealing with quadratic equations that have rational roots. It simplifies the process of finding solutions and provides a clear understanding of the function's characteristics. In many real-world applications, the factored form can help in modeling scenarios where the zeros represent critical points, such as equilibrium points or turning points in a system.

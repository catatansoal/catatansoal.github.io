What are Equivariant Perverse Sheaves?

Let's start with the basics. Imagine you have a complex algebraic variety called X. Think of it as a geometric space defined by polynomial equations. Now, let's introduce a connected algebraic group G acting on X. This means G is a group (a set with an operation satisfying certain rules) that's also an algebraic variety, and it "acts" on X, transforming it in a way that respects the algebraic structure. For example, G could be the group of rotations, and X could be a sphere. The rotations "act" on the sphere by moving points around its surface.

Now, things get interesting. We're interested in G-equivariant perverse sheaves on X. This is a fancy term, but let's unpack it. A sheaf is a way of organizing data (like vector spaces or modules) over the space X. Think of it as assigning some mathematical structure to each point in X, in a consistent way. A perverse sheaf is a special kind of sheaf that satisfies certain technical conditions related to its singularities (points where it behaves badly). These conditions ensure that perverse sheaves have nice properties, making them useful for studying the geometry and topology of X. The "G-equivariant" part means that the sheaf respects the action of the group G. In other words, the data assigned to a point x in X is related to the data assigned to the point g.x, where g is an element of G. This captures the symmetry of the situation.

So, in a nutshell, equivariant perverse sheaves are special sheaves that live on spaces with group actions, respecting the symmetries induced by those actions. The forgetful functor provides a way to compare the equivariant world with the non-equivariant world. By understanding how the forgetful functor behaves, we can gain insights into the role that symmetry plays in the geometry and topology of X. For example, we might ask: when is the forgetful functor fully faithful? This means that it preserves all the information about the relationships between perverse sheaves. If the forgetful functor is fully faithful, then the equivariant world is essentially the same as the non-equivariant world, and the group action doesn't play a significant role. On the other hand, if the forgetful functor is not fully faithful, then the group action has a significant impact on the structure of perverse sheaves.

Orbit Stratification: Dividing and Conquering

This is where orbit stratification comes in. The action of the group G on the variety X naturally divides X into orbits. An orbit is the set of all points that can be reached from a given point by applying elements of G. Think of it like the path traced by a point as the group acts on it. For example, if G is the group of rotations and X is a sphere, the orbits are circles around the axis of rotation (except for the north and south poles, which are fixed points and form orbits by themselves).

A stratification is a way of decomposing a space into simpler pieces, called strata. In this case, we decompose X into orbits. This is called the orbit stratification. The key idea is that each orbit is a smooth algebraic variety, which is much easier to understand than the original space X, which might have singularities. By studying the behavior of perverse sheaves on each orbit, we can build up a picture of the perverse sheaves on the whole space.

The orbit stratification is a powerful tool for studying equivariant perverse sheaves because it allows us to reduce the problem to a simpler setting. Instead of dealing with the entire space X at once, we can focus on each orbit individually. This is particularly useful when X has a complicated geometry or topology. These simple objects are often closely related to the irreducible representations of the stabilizer of a point in the orbit. The stabilizer is the subgroup of G that fixes a particular point.

The relationship between equivariant perverse sheaves and the orbit stratification is fundamental. The stratification provides a framework for analyzing the behavior of perverse sheaves, while the perverse sheaves, in turn, provide information about the stratification. For example, the dimensions of the stalks of a perverse sheaf can be used to detect the singularities of the stratification. This interplay between geometry and topology makes the study of equivariant perverse sheaves a rich and rewarding area of research.

Why Does This Matter? Applications and Further Explorations

Okay, so this all sounds pretty abstract. Why should we care about equivariant perverse sheaves and orbit stratification? Well, these concepts have applications in various areas of mathematics and physics! Here are a few examples:

  • Representation Theory: As we've discussed, equivariant perverse sheaves are closely related to the representation theory of algebraic groups. They provide a geometric way to study representations, which are fundamental objects in mathematics and physics.
  • Singularity Theory: Perverse sheaves are powerful tools for studying singularities, which are points where a geometric object behaves badly. Equivariant perverse sheaves allow us to study singularities in the presence of symmetry.
  • Topology: Perverse sheaves have connections to intersection cohomology, a powerful tool for studying the topology of singular spaces.
  • Mathematical Physics: Equivariant perverse sheaves appear in various areas of mathematical physics, such as string theory and quantum field theory.

This is just the tip of the iceberg! The field of equivariant perverse sheaves is a vibrant and active area of research, with many open questions and exciting directions for future exploration.

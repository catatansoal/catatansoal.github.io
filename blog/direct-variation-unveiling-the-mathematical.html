Understanding Direct Variation

Direct variation, at its heart, explains how two variables change together in a predictable way. When one variable increases, the other increases proportionally. Similarly, when one variable decreases, the other decreases proportionally. Think of it like a seesaw â€“ when one side goes up, the other goes down. The constant of proportionality, often represented by the letter k, is the key to understanding the relationship. It's the value that links the two variables together, telling us the rate at which they change. You can visualize it this way: as x doubles, y also doubles; if x is cut in half, so is y. That's the essence of direct variation! It's a linear relationship, which means it can be represented by a straight line when graphed. The slope of this line is determined by the constant of proportionality, k. The larger the value of k, the steeper the line. The beauty of this relationship lies in its simplicity and predictability. The Equation: Expressing the Relationship

So, how do we write an equation that captures the essence of direct variation? The correct equation to express the relationship "yyy varies directly as xxx" is y=kxy = kxy=kx. In this equation:

  • yyy represents the dependent variable (the one that changes based on x).
  • xxx represents the independent variable (the one that we control or change).
  • kkk represents the constant of proportionality (the factor that links x and y).

This equation states that y is equal to k times x. It means that y changes directly in proportion to x. When x increases, y increases by a factor of k, and when x decreases, y decreases by a factor of k. The constant of proportionality, k, is a crucial piece of the puzzle. It dictates the rate at which y changes with respect to x. To find the value of k, you would need to know the values of x and y at a specific point. Then, you can use the formula k = y/x to calculate the constant. This equation is a simple yet powerful tool for understanding and solving problems related to direct variation. The formula y = kx helps us to understand the relationship between two quantities that increase or decrease at the same rate. For example, if you're earning money at a constant hourly rate, your total earnings (y) vary directly with the number of hours you work (x). The hourly rate (k) would be your constant of proportionality.

Analyzing the Answer Choices

  • A. y=k/xy = k/xy=k/x: This equation represents an inverse variation, not a direct variation. In inverse variation, as x increases, y decreases, and vice versa. They have an inverse relationship, not a direct one.
  • B. y=x/ky = x/ky=x/k: While this equation does involve a constant of proportionality, it doesn't accurately represent direct variation. Understanding the difference between direct and inverse variation is crucial. Direct variation is about a constant rate of change, while inverse variation is about a reciprocal relationship.

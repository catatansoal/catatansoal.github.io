Finding The Y-Intercept Of Y = 6(x-2)(x+3) A Step-by-Step Guide

Jul 13, 2025 by ADMIN 64 views

Hey guys! Ever stared at an equation and felt like you're trying to decipher an ancient language? Well, fear not! Today, we're going to break down the equation y = 6(x-2)(x+3) and find its elusive y-intercept. Trust me, it's easier than it looks!

Understanding the Y-Intercept

First things first, what exactly is a y-intercept? Think of it as the point where a line or curve crosses the y-axis on a graph. It's like the VIP entrance to the y-axis party! Mathematically, it's the value of 'y' when 'x' is equal to zero. This is crucial to understanding how to solve the problem. We need to find the 'y' value when 'x' is zero, which means we're essentially looking at the point (0, y). This is a cornerstone concept in coordinate geometry and is used extensively in various mathematical and real-world applications. For instance, in physics, the y-intercept might represent the initial position of an object, while in economics, it could indicate the fixed costs of a business. Understanding this fundamental concept opens doors to interpreting and solving a wide array of problems across different disciplines. Remember, the y-intercept isn't just a point on a graph; it's a vital piece of information that can tell us a lot about the relationship between variables. Mastering this concept will not only help you ace your math exams but also equip you with a powerful tool for analyzing and interpreting data in various fields. So, let’s keep this in mind as we move forward and tackle the equation at hand. We'll see how this simple yet powerful idea helps us unlock the solution with ease.

Decoding the Equation: y = 6(x-2)(x+3)

Now, let's get our hands dirty with the equation: y = 6(x-2)(x+3). This equation is in what we call factored form. It gives us clues about the x-intercepts (where the graph crosses the x-axis), but for the y-intercept, we need to do a little algebraic maneuvering. The key here is to remember our definition of the y-intercept: it's the value of 'y' when 'x' is zero. So, what do we do? We substitute 'x' with 0 in our equation. This simple substitution is the golden ticket to finding our y-intercept. It transforms the equation into something much easier to handle. By plugging in 0 for 'x', we eliminate the variable and are left with an expression that directly calculates the 'y' value. This technique is a fundamental tool in algebra and is used extensively in solving various types of equations and problems. It's not just about plugging in a number; it's about understanding the underlying principle of how variables interact and how setting one variable to a specific value affects the others. Mastering this substitution technique will not only help you find y-intercepts but also empower you to solve a wide range of algebraic problems. It's a skill that builds a strong foundation in mathematics and is essential for tackling more complex concepts in the future. So, let's embrace this technique and see how it unravels the mystery of our y-intercept.

The Substitution Magic

Let's put our substitution skills to the test! We replace 'x' with 0 in the equation: y = 6(0-2)(0+3). See how the 'x' disappears and we're left with just numbers? This is where the arithmetic fun begins! We simplify inside the parentheses first: (0-2) becomes -2, and (0+3) becomes 3. Now our equation looks like this: y = 6(-2)(3). This simplification is a crucial step in solving the equation. By addressing the parentheses first, we follow the order of operations (PEMDAS/BODMAS), ensuring that we arrive at the correct answer. This methodical approach is not just important for this specific problem; it's a fundamental principle in mathematics. The order of operations acts as a roadmap, guiding us through complex calculations and preventing errors. Without it, we'd be navigating a mathematical maze without a compass. So, remember, parentheses first, then exponents, then multiplication and division (from left to right), and finally addition and subtraction (from left to right). This order isn't arbitrary; it's a carefully constructed system that ensures consistency and accuracy in mathematical calculations. Mastering the order of operations is like learning the grammar of mathematics; it allows you to speak the language fluently and avoid misinterpretations. So, let's continue to follow this golden rule as we simplify our equation further and get closer to unveiling the y-intercept.

Crunching the Numbers

Time for some multiplication! We have y = 6(-2)(3). Let's multiply 6 and -2 first, which gives us -12. Now we have y = -12(3). Finally, multiplying -12 by 3 gives us y = -36. And there you have it! The y-intercept is -36. This final calculation is where all our previous steps come together. It's the culmination of our understanding of y-intercepts, substitution, and the order of operations. Each step we took, from replacing 'x' with 0 to simplifying the expression, was a piece of the puzzle. Now, with the final multiplication, we've assembled all the pieces and revealed the complete picture. This process highlights the beauty of mathematics – how seemingly complex problems can be broken down into smaller, manageable steps. It's like climbing a mountain; each step brings you closer to the summit. And in this case, the summit is our y-intercept: -36. This number tells us where the graph of the equation crosses the y-axis. It's a significant piece of information that helps us understand the behavior of the equation. So, let's celebrate our success in reaching the summit and appreciate the power of step-by-step problem-solving in mathematics. We've not only found the answer but also reinforced our understanding of the underlying principles.

The Grand Finale: Y-Intercept Unveiled

So, in the xy-plane, the y-intercept of the graph of the equation y = 6(x-2)(x+3) is -36. That means the graph crosses the y-axis at the point (0, -36). Isn't that satisfying? We took a seemingly complex equation and, by understanding the concept of the y-intercept and using some basic algebra, we cracked the code! This final answer is more than just a number; it's a testament to our problem-solving journey. It represents the culmination of our efforts, the result of our understanding and application of mathematical principles. The y-intercept, -36, is a key characteristic of the graph of the equation. It tells us exactly where the graph intersects the y-axis, providing us with a crucial anchor point. This knowledge can be used to sketch the graph, analyze its behavior, and even make predictions about its values. Finding the y-intercept is often a crucial step in understanding the overall properties of a function. It's like finding the starting point of a journey; it gives us a reference from which we can explore the rest of the landscape. So, let's appreciate the significance of this answer and recognize the power of mathematics to reveal hidden information. We've not only found the y-intercept but also gained a deeper understanding of how equations and graphs relate to each other.

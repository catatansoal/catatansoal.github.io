Finding The Ratio Of Radii Given Circumferences Of Circles

Jul 16, 2025 by ADMIN 59 views

Hey everyone! Let's dive into a fun geometry problem that involves circles, circumferences, and ratios. This is a classic math question that might seem tricky at first, but once you understand the underlying concepts, it becomes quite straightforward. We're going to break it down step-by-step, so you can confidently tackle similar problems in the future. This article aims to provide a comprehensive explanation of how to find the ratio of the radii of two circles when their circumferences are known. We'll explore the formulas involved, walk through the solution to a specific problem, and discuss the underlying mathematical principles. By the end, you'll have a solid understanding of this concept and be ready to apply it to various scenarios. So, grab your thinking caps, and let's get started!

The Problem: Circles and Circumferences

So, here's the problem we're going to solve today:

One circle has a circumference of 12 cm. Another circle has a circumference of 32 cm. What is the ratio of the radius of the smaller circle to the radius of the larger circle?

A. 3:8 B. 8:3 C. 1:3 D. 3:1

Before we jump into the solution, let's make sure we understand the key terms and concepts involved. This problem revolves around circles and their properties, specifically the relationship between circumference and radius. Let's take a moment to refresh our understanding of these concepts.

Refreshing Our Knowledge: Circumference and Radius

What is Circumference?

The circumference of a circle is the distance around it. Think of it as the perimeter of a circle. If you were to cut a circle and lay it out in a straight line, the length of that line would be the circumference. The circumference is a fundamental property of circles and plays a crucial role in many geometric calculations.

What is Radius?

The radius of a circle is the distance from the center of the circle to any point on its edge. It's essentially half the diameter (the distance across the circle through the center). Understanding the radius is crucial because it's used in many formulas related to circles, including the one for circumference.

The Relationship Between Circumference and Radius

The circumference (C) of a circle is related to its radius (r) by the following formula:

C = 2πr

Where π (pi) is a mathematical constant approximately equal to 3.14159. This formula is the key to solving our problem. It tells us that the circumference of a circle is directly proportional to its radius. This means that if you increase the radius, you increase the circumference proportionally, and vice versa. The constant of proportionality is 2π.

With these basics in mind, we're now ready to tackle the problem. We'll use the relationship between circumference and radius to find the ratio we're looking for. Keep this formula in mind as we move forward; it's the cornerstone of our solution.

Solving the Problem: Step-by-Step

Okay, guys, now that we've got our definitions down, let's break down how to solve this problem step-by-step. We'll use the formula we just discussed, C = 2πr, to find the radii of the two circles and then determine their ratio. Here's how we'll do it:

Step 1: Find the Radius of the Smaller Circle

We know the circumference of the smaller circle is 12 cm. Let's call its radius r1. Using the formula C = 2πr, we can write:

12 = 2πr1

To find r1, we need to isolate it. We can do this by dividing both sides of the equation by 2π:

r1 = 12 / (2π)
r1 = 6 / π

So, the radius of the smaller circle is 6/π cm. It's important to keep this value in terms of π for now, as it will simplify our calculations later when we find the ratio.

Step 2: Find the Radius of the Larger Circle

Next, we'll find the radius of the larger circle. We know its circumference is 32 cm. Let's call its radius r2. Again, using the formula C = 2πr, we get:

32 = 2πr2

To find r2, we divide both sides by 2π:

r2 = 32 / (2π)
r2 = 16 / π

So, the radius of the larger circle is 16/π cm. Notice that we've kept the π in the denominator, just like we did for the smaller circle's radius. This will be super helpful in the next step.

Step 3: Calculate the Ratio of the Radii

Now comes the crucial part: finding the ratio of the radius of the smaller circle to the radius of the larger circle. This means we need to find r1 / r2. We have:

r1 = 6 / π
r2 = 16 / π

So, the ratio r1 / r2 is:

(6 / π) / (16 / π)

When you divide fractions, you multiply by the reciprocal of the second fraction. So, this becomes:

(6 / π) * (π / 16)

Notice what happens? The π in the numerator and the π in the denominator cancel each other out! This is why it was important to keep the π in our calculations until this point. We're left with:

6 / 16

Step 4: Simplify the Ratio

We can simplify the fraction 6/16 by dividing both the numerator and the denominator by their greatest common divisor, which is 2:

6 / 16 = (6 ÷ 2) / (16 ÷ 2) = 3 / 8

So, the ratio of the radius of the smaller circle to the radius of the larger circle is 3/8. This can also be written as 3:8.

Step 5: Choose the Correct Answer

Looking back at the options given in the problem:

A. 3:8 B. 8:3 C. 1:3 D. 3:1

We can see that the correct answer is A. 3:8. We've successfully found the ratio by using the formula for the circumference of a circle and simplifying the resulting fraction. This step-by-step approach makes the problem much more manageable.

Key Takeaways and Generalizations

Alright, awesome job, guys! We've walked through the solution to this problem, but let's take a moment to highlight some key takeaways and see how we can generalize this approach to other similar problems. Understanding the underlying principles will help you tackle a wider range of geometry questions with confidence.

The Power of Proportionality

The core concept here is the proportionality between a circle's circumference and its radius. The formula C = 2πr tells us that the circumference is directly proportional to the radius. This means that if you double the radius, you double the circumference, and if you halve the radius, you halve the circumference. This relationship is super useful for solving problems like this one.

Ratios and Proportions

When we're dealing with ratios, we're essentially comparing two quantities. In this case, we were comparing the radii of two circles. Because the circumference is directly proportional to the radius, the ratio of the circumferences of two circles is the same as the ratio of their radii. This is a powerful shortcut! We didn't actually need to calculate the radii individually; we could have directly found the ratio of the circumferences and simplified it.

For example, the ratio of the circumferences was 12:32. If we simplify this ratio by dividing both sides by their greatest common divisor (4), we get 3:8, which is the same ratio we found for the radii. This approach can save you time on tests and quizzes!

Generalizing the Approach

Now, let's think about how we can apply this approach to other problems. If you're given the circumferences of two circles and asked to find the ratio of their radii (or vice versa), you can follow these general steps:

  1. Understand the Relationship: Remember that C = 2πr and that circumference and radius are directly proportional.
  2. Set up the Ratios: If you're given the circumferences, you can find the ratio of the circumferences directly. If you're given the radii, you can find the ratio of the radii directly.
  3. Simplify the Ratio: Divide both sides of the ratio by their greatest common divisor to get the simplest form.
  4. Apply the Proportionality: The ratio of the circumferences is equal to the ratio of the radii.

This general approach can be used in various scenarios. For instance, you might be given the diameters instead of the radii, but remember that the diameter is just twice the radius, so the ratio will still hold. Or, you might be given the ratio of the radii and asked to find the ratio of the circumferences, which is the same thing!

Normal Distribution High School Exam Scores Problem Solving

Jul 18, 2025 by ADMIN 60 views

Hey there, future scholars! Ever wondered how those high school entrance exam scores are distributed? Well, let's dive into the fascinating world of statistics and uncover the secrets behind the numbers. In this article, we're tackling a classic problem involving the normal distribution, a concept that pops up everywhere from exam scores to heights and weights. We'll break down the question step-by-step, making sure you not only understand the solution but also grasp the underlying principles. So, grab your thinking caps, and let's get started!

Cracking the Code: Understanding the Normal Distribution

Before we jump into the problem itself, let's get acquainted with the normal distribution, our trusty tool for this mission. Imagine a bell curve, that smooth, symmetrical shape that's widest in the middle and tapers off on both sides. That's the normal distribution in a nutshell! In our context of high school entrance exam scores, the normal distribution helps us visualize how the scores are spread out. Most scores cluster around the mean, the average score, while fewer scores fall in the extreme high or low ranges. It's a powerful concept that allows us to make predictions about the likelihood of different score ranges.

Now, the normal distribution isn't just a pretty curve; it has some key properties that we can use to solve problems. Two crucial parameters define a normal distribution: the mean (represented by the Greek letter μ) and the standard deviation (represented by the Greek letter σ). The mean tells us where the center of the distribution is, while the standard deviation tells us how spread out the data is. A small standard deviation means the data is clustered tightly around the mean, while a large standard deviation means the data is more spread out. Understanding these parameters is crucial for interpreting and solving problems related to the normal distribution. Think of the mean as the bullseye of a dartboard and the standard deviation as how scattered the darts are around it. The tighter the grouping, the smaller the standard deviation.

The Empirical Rule, often called the 68-95-99.7 rule, is our secret weapon for tackling this problem. This rule is a fundamental concept in statistics that provides a simple yet powerful way to understand the spread of data in a normal distribution. This rule states that approximately 68% of the data falls within one standard deviation of the mean, 95% falls within two standard deviations, and 99.7% falls within three standard deviations. It's like a cheat sheet for understanding how data is distributed around the average. So, if we know the mean and standard deviation of a dataset, we can quickly estimate the proportion of data that falls within certain ranges. This rule is super handy because it allows us to make quick estimations without having to do complex calculations. For example, if the average height of students in a class is 5'8" with a standard deviation of 2 inches, we can immediately say that about 68% of the students are between 5'6" and 5'10". Isn't that cool? We'll use this rule to crack the high school entrance exam score problem, so keep it in mind!

Deconstructing the Question: Mean, Standard Deviation, and the Score Range

Okay, let's get back to the challenge at hand. The problem states that the high school entrance exam scores are approximately normally distributed. This is our key clue that we can use the concepts of normal distribution and the Empirical Rule. We're given a mean (μ) of 82.4 and a standard deviation (σ) of 3.3. This tells us that the average score is 82.4, and the scores tend to spread out by about 3.3 points on either side of the mean. Think of it like this: most students scored around 82.4, but some scored higher, and some scored lower, with the typical difference from the average being about 3.3 points.

Our mission, should we choose to accept it, is to find the percentage of scores between 75.8 and 89. This defines our target range. We need to figure out how many standard deviations away these scores are from the mean. This is where our detective work begins! By figuring out how many standard deviations away from the mean these scores lie, we can use the Empirical Rule to estimate the percentage of scores within that range. It's like finding the coordinates on a map to pinpoint our exact location. We'll be using the mean and standard deviation as our compass and ruler to navigate the distribution of scores.

So, how do we figure out how many standard deviations away 75.8 and 89 are from 82.4? We'll need to do some simple math. First, we'll calculate the difference between each score and the mean. Then, we'll divide that difference by the standard deviation. This will give us the number of standard deviations each score is from the mean. Get ready to put on your calculation hats; we're about to dive into the numbers!

Unveiling the Solution: Applying the Empirical Rule

Time for some calculations! Let's first find out how many standard deviations 75.8 is from the mean of 82.4. We subtract 75.8 from 82.4, which gives us 6.6. Then, we divide 6.6 by the standard deviation of 3.3, resulting in 2. So, 75.8 is 2 standard deviations below the mean. We’re making progress in our score sleuthing!

Now, let's do the same for 89. We subtract the mean (82.4) from 89, which gives us 6.6. Dividing 6.6 by the standard deviation of 3.3, we again get 2. This means 89 is 2 standard deviations above the mean. This is a symmetrical situation, which will make our calculations using the Empirical Rule even easier. We’ve now established the boundaries of our score range in terms of standard deviations from the mean. It's like drawing the lines on our map that will lead us to the treasure!

Now comes the moment of truth: applying the Empirical Rule. Remember, the Empirical Rule states that approximately 95% of the data falls within 2 standard deviations of the mean. Since 75.8 is 2 standard deviations below the mean and 89 is 2 standard deviations above the mean, the percentage of scores between 75.8 and 89 is approximately 95%. Bingo! We've cracked the code and found the answer. It’s like fitting the last piece of a jigsaw puzzle, and seeing the complete picture emerge. We’ve successfully used the Empirical Rule to estimate the percentage of scores within our desired range. This demonstrates the power and convenience of the Empirical Rule in understanding data distribution.

The Grand Finale: Choosing the Correct Answer

We've done the calculations, understood the concepts, and now it's time to choose the correct answer from the options provided. The question asks for the percentage of scores between 75.8 and 89, and we've determined that this range corresponds to 2 standard deviations from the mean. The Empirical Rule tells us that approximately 95% of the data falls within this range. Therefore, the correct answer is B. 95%. We’ve arrived at our destination, triumphant in our quest to understand the distribution of high school entrance exam scores!

Let's quickly recap our journey. We started by understanding the concept of normal distribution and the crucial role of the mean and standard deviation. We then learned about the Empirical Rule, our superpower for estimating the spread of data. We applied these concepts to the specific problem, calculating the number of standard deviations the score range was from the mean. Finally, using the Empirical Rule, we confidently arrived at the answer: 95%. This problem not only demonstrates the application of statistical concepts but also highlights the importance of step-by-step problem-solving.

The Preamble

Your preamble should look something like this:

\documentclass{article}
\usepackage{tikz}
\usepackage{amssymb}
\begin{document}

Drawing Basic Circles with TikZ

Now for the fun part! Let's start by drawing some basic circles. The \tikz environment is your canvas, and the \draw command is your brush. To draw a circle, you'll use the syntax \draw (center) circle (radius);. Let's draw two overlapping circles:

\begin{tikzpicture}
  \draw (0,0) circle (1.5cm);
  \draw (2,0) circle (1.5cm);
\end{tikzpicture}

This code snippet creates a tikzpicture environment, which is the container for your TikZ drawings. Inside this environment, we use the \draw command to draw two circles. The first circle is centered at coordinates (0,0) with a radius of 1.5cm, and the second circle is centered at (2,0) with the same radius. These coordinates and the radius are fully customizable, allowing you to position and size your circles exactly as needed. Understanding the basic syntax of \draw and the structure of the tikzpicture environment is fundamental to creating more complex Venn diagrams. By adjusting the coordinates and radii, you can create various configurations of circles, setting the stage for illustrating different set relationships and logical operations. Remember, the key to mastering TikZ is practice, so don't hesitate to experiment with different values and commands to see how they affect your diagrams. This hands-on approach will build your confidence and allow you to create increasingly sophisticated and informative Venn diagrams for your projects.

Adding Labels

To add labels to your circles, you can use the \node command. This allows you to place text anywhere in your diagram. Let's label our circles as 'A' and 'B':

\begin{tikzpicture}
  \draw (0,0) circle (1.5cm);
  \draw (2,0) circle (1.5cm);
  \node at (-1,1) {A};
  \node at (3,1) {B};
\end{tikzpicture}

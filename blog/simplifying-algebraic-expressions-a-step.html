Understanding the Basics of Algebraic Expressions

Before we jump into simplifying 3x/2 - 3y/6, let's quickly recap what algebraic expressions are all about. Essentially, they are combinations of variables (like 'x' and 'y'), constants (numbers), and mathematical operations (addition, subtraction, multiplication, division). The key to simplifying these expressions lies in identifying like terms and applying the order of operations (PEMDAS/BODMAS). Like terms are terms that have the same variable raised to the same power. For instance, 2x and 5x are like terms, but 2x and 2xÂ² are not. Understanding this fundamental concept is crucial because we can only combine like terms when simplifying expressions. Now, the order of operations tells us the sequence in which we should perform calculations: Parentheses/Brackets, Exponents/Orders, Multiplication and Division (from left to right), and Addition and Subtraction (from left to right). Delving into Fractions: A Quick Refresher

Since our expression 3x/2 - 3y/6 involves fractions, it's worthwhile to brush up on some basic fraction operations. Remember, a fraction represents a part of a whole and consists of a numerator (the top number) and a denominator (the bottom number). When adding or subtracting fractions, a critical step is to ensure they have a common denominator. This means the denominators of all fractions involved must be the same. If they aren't, we need to find the least common multiple (LCM) of the denominators and convert each fraction accordingly. The LCM is the smallest number that is a multiple of both denominators. Once we have a common denominator, we can simply add or subtract the numerators while keeping the denominator the same. For example, if we had 1/2 + 1/3, the LCM of 2 and 3 is 6. So, we would convert 1/2 to 3/6 and 1/3 to 2/6. Then, we could add the numerators: 3/6 + 2/6 = 5/6. This understanding of fraction manipulation is absolutely essential for simplifying our expression, as we'll need to find a common denominator to combine the terms effectively. So, let's keep these principles in mind as we move forward in our simplification journey.

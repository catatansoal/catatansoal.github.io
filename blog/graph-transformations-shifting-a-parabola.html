Analyzing Transformations: Shifting the Parabola y = x² + 5x - 2

Understanding the Basic Quadratic Function

Hey guys! Let's dive into the fascinating world of quadratic functions and explore what happens when we tweak them a bit. We're starting with the equation y = x² + 5x - 2, which represents a parabola. Remember, parabolas are those cool U-shaped curves that you see all over the place – from the path of a thrown ball to the shape of a satellite dish. The basic form of a quadratic function is y = ax² + bx + c, where a, b, and c are constants. In our case, a = 1, b = 5, and c = -2. These constants determine the parabola's shape, position, and how it opens (upwards or downwards). Understanding these fundamentals is key to grasping how transformations affect the graph. Before we get into the shifting, let's quickly recap some key features of our original parabola. The coefficient 'a' (which is 1 in our case) tells us the parabola opens upwards (since it's positive). The value of 'c' (-2) is the y-intercept, meaning the parabola crosses the y-axis at the point (0, -2). The x-coordinate of the vertex (the lowest point on the parabola) can be found using the formula -b/2a, which gives us -5/2 for our equation. Knowing these details helps us visualize the initial position of the graph. Alright, now we're ready to see what happens when we make a change!

The Magic of Substitution: Replacing x with (x - 3)

So, what happens when we substitute (x - 3) in place of x in our equation? This is where things get interesting! The original equation is y = x² + 5x - 2. We're going to replace every instance of 'x' with '(x - 3)'. This gives us y = (x - 3)² + 5(x - 3) - 2. Now, let's simplify this new equation. Expanding (x - 3)² gives us x² - 6x + 9. Then, 5(x - 3) becomes 5x - 15. Combining all these terms, our new equation is y = x² - 6x + 9 + 5x - 15 - 2, which simplifies to y = x² - x - 8. Notice how the equation has changed! The coefficients are different, and this tells us the graph's position has shifted. It's not just a simple vertical or horizontal shift; there's a bit more going on. The key takeaway here is that substituting (x - 3) for x introduces a horizontal transformation to the graph. Whenever you see a change inside the function (like replacing x with x - a or x + a), it affects the graph horizontally. When you add or subtract a constant inside the function (affecting the x-values), it results in a horizontal shift. Specifically, if you replace x with (x - a), the graph shifts to the right by 'a' units. Conversely, if you replace x with (x + a), the graph shifts to the left by 'a' units. Think of it this way: the function is now asking, "What x-value, minus 3, would give me the same result as the original function's x-value?" The answer is, "An x-value 3 units greater." It's like the graph is being 'tricked' into moving. Let's look at some specific points to see the change.

Unveiling the Shift: Examining the Vertex

One of the easiest ways to visualize the shift is by looking at the vertex of the parabola. Remember, the vertex is the point where the parabola changes direction – it's either the lowest point (if the parabola opens upwards) or the highest point (if it opens downwards). For our original equation, y = x² + 5x - 2, we found that the x-coordinate of the vertex is -5/2 (or -2.5). To find the y-coordinate, we plug -2.5 back into the original equation: y = (-2.5)² + 5(-2.5) - 2 = 6.25 - 12.5 - 2 = -8.25. Therefore, the vertex of the original parabola is approximately (-2.5, -8.25). Now, let's find the vertex of the transformed equation, y = x² - x - 8. The x-coordinate of the vertex is -b/2a = -(-1)/(2*1) = 0.5. Plugging 0.5 into the transformed equation, we get y = (0.5)² - 0.5 - 8 = 0.25 - 0.5 - 8 = -8.25. So, the vertex of the transformed parabola is (0.5, -8.25). Notice the change in the x-coordinate of the vertex. It has shifted from -2.5 to 0.5, which is a shift of 3 units to the right! The y-coordinate of the vertex remains the same, confirming that the shift is purely horizontal. This shift is exactly what we predicted based on the substitution of (x - 3) for x. The entire parabola has moved three units to the right along the x-axis. All the points on the graph have experienced this horizontal displacement. Let's check another point for illustration. The y-intercept of the original equation is -2. Now, let's check the y-intercept for the new equation. We get the value -8. So, if we have the original y-intercept as (0,-2). The x value for the new y-intercept is x-3 = 0. Then x = 3, then the y-intercept now will be (3, -8). The transformation is exactly what we predicted based on the transformation of our x-value. This demonstrates the beauty of transformations and how simple algebraic manipulations can significantly alter the shape and position of a graph.

Identifying the Correct Answer

Based on our analysis, when we substitute (x - 3) in place of x in the equation y = x² + 5x - 2, the graph shifts 3 units to the right. Therefore, the correct answer is B. The graph would shift 3 units to the right. We have thoroughly examined the effects of this substitution, analyzing how the vertex and other key points of the parabola change. The substitution is a horizontal transformation, and we’ve seen how that affects the parabola. Remember this key principle: replacing 'x' with '(x - a)' results in a horizontal shift of 'a' units to the right. So next time you see this transformation, you’ll know exactly what to expect.

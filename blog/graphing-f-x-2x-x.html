Graphing F(x) = 2x³ - X² - 5x & Finding Increasing Intervals

Aug 8, 2025 by ADMIN 61 views
Iklan Headers

Hey guys! Today, we're diving deep into the world of functions, specifically focusing on how to graph a cubic function and identify the intervals where it's increasing. We'll be working with the function f(x) = 2x³ - x² - 5x. So, buckle up and let's get started!

1. Understanding the Function: f(x) = 2x³ - x² - 5x

Before we even think about graphing, let's get to know our function a little better. f(x) = 2x³ - x² - 5x is a cubic function, meaning it's a polynomial function with the highest power of x being 3. Cubic functions have a distinctive shape, often resembling a stretched-out 'S' curve. They can have up to two turning points (local maxima or minima) and their end behavior is such that as x approaches positive or negative infinity, f(x) also approaches positive or negative infinity, depending on the leading coefficient (which is 2 in our case, so it will tend to positive infinity as x tends to positive infinity).

Key characteristics of cubic functions to keep in mind:

  • Domain: The domain of any polynomial function, including cubic functions, is all real numbers (-∞, ∞). This means we can plug in any value for x.
  • Range: Similarly, the range of a cubic function is also all real numbers (-∞, ∞), unless there are specific restrictions given.
  • End Behavior: Since the leading coefficient (2) is positive, as x approaches ∞, f(x) approaches ∞, and as x approaches -∞, f(x) approaches -∞. This gives us a general idea of how the graph will look at its extremes.
  • X-intercepts (Roots/Zeros): To find the x-intercepts, we need to solve the equation f(x) = 0. This is where the graph crosses the x-axis. These points are crucial for sketching the graph accurately.
  • Y-intercept: The y-intercept is where the graph crosses the y-axis, and we can find it by evaluating f(0).
  • Turning Points: These are the points where the function changes direction (from increasing to decreasing or vice versa). They represent local maxima and minima and are important for identifying intervals of increasing and decreasing behavior. We'll use calculus (derivatives) later to find these precisely.

Understanding these characteristics helps us build a mental picture of the graph even before we start plotting points. So, now that we've got a handle on the basics, let's move on to finding some key points and sketching the graph!

2. Finding Key Points: X-Intercepts, Y-Intercept, and Critical Points

Alright, let's get our hands dirty and find some key points that will help us graph the function f(x) = 2x³ - x² - 5x. These points will act as anchors, guiding us in sketching the curve accurately.

a. X-Intercepts (Roots/Zeros)

To find the x-intercepts, we need to solve the equation f(x) = 0:

2x³ - x² - 5x = 0

The first thing we notice is that we can factor out an x from each term:

x(2x² - x - 5) = 0

This gives us one solution right away: x = 0. So, (0, 0) is one of our x-intercepts. Now, we need to solve the quadratic equation 2x² - x - 5 = 0. This doesn't factor easily, so we'll use the quadratic formula:

x = (-b ± √(b² - 4ac)) / 2a

Where a = 2, b = -1, and c = -5. Plugging in these values, we get:

x = (1 ± √((-1)² - 4 * 2 * -5)) / (2 * 2) x = (1 ± √(1 + 40)) / 4 x = (1 ± √41) / 4

This gives us two more x-intercepts:

  • x₁ = (1 + √41) / 4 ≈ 1.851
  • x₂ = (1 - √41) / 4 ≈ -1.351

So, our x-intercepts are approximately (0, 0), (1.851, 0), and (-1.351, 0). These are the points where the graph crosses the x-axis.

b. Y-Intercept

To find the y-intercept, we simply evaluate f(0):

f(0) = 2(0)³ - (0)² - 5(0) = 0

This confirms that the y-intercept is also (0, 0), which we already knew from finding the x-intercepts.

c. Critical Points (Using Calculus)

Critical points are where the function's derivative is either zero or undefined. These points are potential locations of local maxima and minima (turning points). To find them, we need to take the derivative of f(x) and set it equal to zero.

f(x) = 2x³ - x² - 5x f'(x) = 6x² - 2x - 5

Now, we set f'(x) = 0 and solve for x:

6x² - 2x - 5 = 0

Again, this quadratic doesn't factor easily, so we'll use the quadratic formula with a = 6, b = -2, and c = -5:

x = (2 ± √((-2)² - 4 * 6 * -5)) / (2 * 6) x = (2 ± √(4 + 120)) / 12 x = (2 ± √124) / 12 x = (2 ± 2√31) / 12 x = (1 ± √31) / 6

This gives us two critical points:

  • x₁ = (1 + √31) / 6 ≈ 1.096
  • x₂ = (1 - √31) / 6 ≈ -0.763

To find the corresponding y-values, we plug these x values back into the original function f(x):

  • f(1.096) ≈ 2(1.096)³ - (1.096)² - 5(1.096) ≈ -4.061 So, one critical point is approximately (1.096, -4.061). This looks like it will be a local minimum.
  • f(-0.763) ≈ 2(-0.763)³ - (-0.763)² - 5(-0.763) ≈ 1.761 So, another critical point is approximately (-0.763, 1.761). This looks like it will be a local maximum.

These critical points are super important because they mark the spots where the function changes direction. They help us define the intervals where the function is increasing or decreasing.

With these key points – x-intercepts, y-intercept, and critical points – we're now well-equipped to sketch the graph of f(x) = 2x³ - x² - 5x! Let's move on to that next.

3. Sketching the Graph of f(x) = 2x³ - x² - 5x

Alright, let's bring all our hard work together and sketch the graph of f(x) = 2x³ - x² - 5x. We've found some crucial points, and now it's time to connect the dots (literally!).

Here's a recap of the key points we've calculated:

  • X-intercepts: (0, 0), (1.851, 0), (-1.351, 0)
  • Y-intercept: (0, 0)
  • Critical Points (Local Max/Min): Approximately (-0.763, 1.761) [Local Max], (1.096, -4.061) [Local Min]

Steps to Sketch the Graph:

  1. Plot the Key Points: Start by plotting all the intercepts and critical points on a coordinate plane. These points will serve as our guideposts.
  2. Consider End Behavior: Remember, since the leading coefficient is positive, the graph will go down to -∞ as x approaches -∞ and go up to +∞ as x approaches ∞. This gives us the overall direction of the curve.
  3. Connect the Dots: Now, carefully sketch the curve, connecting the points in a smooth manner. Keep in mind the following:
    • The graph will pass through the x and y-intercepts.
    • The graph will change direction at the critical points (local max and min).
    • The graph will follow the end behavior we discussed.
  4. Smooth Curves: Cubic functions have smooth, continuous curves. Avoid sharp corners or breaks in the graph.

Visualizing the Sketch:

Imagine starting from the left side of the graph (negative x-values). The function will be decreasing from -∞ until it reaches the local maximum at approximately (-0.763, 1.761). Then, it will start decreasing again, passing through the x-intercept at (-1.351, 0), and continue decreasing until it reaches the local minimum at approximately (1.096, -4.061). After the local minimum, the function will start increasing again, passing through the x-intercept at (0, 0), and continue increasing until it passes through the final x-intercept at (1.851, 0) and then steadily increase toward positive infinity as x increases.

Tips for Accuracy:

  • Use a Graphing Calculator or Software: If you have access to a graphing calculator or software like Desmos or GeoGebra, use it to check your sketch. It's a great way to verify that your graph is accurate.
  • Plot Additional Points: If you're unsure about the shape of the curve in a particular region, plot a few extra points to guide your sketch.
  • Pay Attention to Scale: Choose a scale for your axes that allows you to clearly see all the key features of the graph. Sometimes, you might need to adjust the scale to get a better view.

With these steps and our key points, you should be able to sketch a pretty accurate graph of f(x) = 2x³ - x² - 5x. Remember, practice makes perfect, so don't be afraid to sketch it a few times until you're comfortable with the shape and behavior of the function. Now, let's move on to the final step: identifying the intervals where the function is increasing!

4. Identifying Intervals of Increasing Behavior

Okay, we've graphed the function f(x) = 2x³ - x² - 5x, and now we're ready to tackle the main question: On what intervals is the function increasing? This is where our understanding of the graph's behavior and the critical points comes into play.

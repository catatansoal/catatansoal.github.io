Calculate PH: [H+] = 1.25 X 10^-10 M Solution

Aug 2, 2025 by ADMIN 46 views

Hey guys! Let's dive into the fascinating world of pH and learn how to calculate it. This is a crucial concept in chemistry, and understanding it will help you grasp many chemical processes. In this article, we're going to tackle a specific problem: determining the pH of a solution with a hydrogen ion concentration ([H+]) of 1.25 x 10^-10 M. We'll be using the formula pH = -log[H3O+], which is the cornerstone of pH calculations. So, buckle up and get ready to explore the acidity and alkalinity of solutions!

What is pH?

Before we jump into the calculations, let's quickly recap what pH actually means. pH, which stands for “potential of hydrogen,” is a measure of the acidity or alkalinity of a solution. It's a scale that ranges from 0 to 14, with 7 being neutral. Solutions with a pH less than 7 are acidic, meaning they have a higher concentration of hydrogen ions (H+). Solutions with a pH greater than 7 are basic or alkaline, indicating a lower concentration of H+ ions. The pH scale is logarithmic, which means that each whole number change in pH represents a tenfold change in the concentration of H+ ions. This logarithmic nature is important to remember as we work through our calculations.

Why is pH important? You might ask. Well, pH plays a vital role in numerous chemical and biological processes. In our bodies, pH levels are tightly regulated to ensure enzymes and other biological molecules function correctly. In the environment, pH affects everything from the solubility of minerals in soil to the survival of aquatic life in rivers and lakes. Even in everyday life, pH is crucial – think about the acidity of your stomach acid (pH around 1-2) or the alkalinity of household cleaners (pH often above 10). Understanding pH allows us to predict and control chemical reactions and maintain the health of various systems. So, now that we understand the importance of pH, let's get back to our problem and calculate the pH of the given solution.

The Importance of the Hydronium Ion (H3O+)

Now, you might have noticed that the formula we're using involves [H3O+] instead of [H+]. What's the deal with that? Well, in aqueous solutions (solutions where water is the solvent), hydrogen ions (H+) don't exist freely. They immediately react with water molecules (H2O) to form hydronium ions (H3O+). This is because the lone pairs of electrons on the oxygen atom in water are highly attracted to the positive charge of the proton (H+). So, the hydronium ion (H3O+) is essentially the more accurate representation of the acidic species in water.

Think of it like this: H+ is like a hot potato – it can't stay on its own for long! It quickly finds a water molecule to bind to, forming H3O+. For most practical purposes, we can often use [H+] and [H3O+] interchangeably because the concentration of H+ is essentially equal to the concentration of H3O+ in dilute solutions. However, it's crucial to understand that the actual acidic species present in water is the hydronium ion. In our problem, we are given the concentration of H+ (1.25 x 10^-10 M), but we'll use it directly in our formula as if it were the concentration of H3O+ because, for our purposes, they are equivalent. This understanding of the hydronium ion is essential for a complete grasp of acid-base chemistry.

Applying the Formula: pH = -log[H3O+]

Okay, let's get down to business! We're given that the concentration of hydrogen ions ([H+]), which we'll treat as [H3O+], is 1.25 x 10^-10 M. Our trusty formula is pH = -log[H3O+]. So, all we need to do is plug in the value and calculate. Easy peasy!

Here's how it looks:

pH = -log(1.25 x 10^-10)

Now, you might be thinking, “How do I calculate the logarithm of that number?” Don't worry, most scientific calculators have a “log” button that does the heavy lifting for you. If you're using a calculator, just enter 1.25 x 10^-10 and then press the “log” button. The result should be approximately -9.90.

But wait! We're not quite done yet. Remember the negative sign in front of the logarithm in our formula? We need to multiply our result by -1:

pH = -(-9.90) = 9.90

Therefore, the pH of the solution is 9.90.

Breaking Down the Calculation

Let's break down why this calculation works and what it tells us. The logarithm (base 10) of a number essentially tells us the power to which we need to raise 10 to get that number. In this case, the log of 1.25 x 10^-10 is approximately -9.90. This means that 10 raised to the power of -9.90 is equal to 1.25 x 10^-10. The negative sign in the exponent reflects the fact that we're dealing with a very small number (less than 1), which is typical for ion concentrations.

Multiplying the logarithm by -1 in the pH formula converts the negative logarithmic value into a positive pH value. This makes the pH scale more intuitive to use. A higher pH value indicates a lower concentration of hydrogen ions and a more alkaline solution, while a lower pH value indicates a higher concentration of hydrogen ions and a more acidic solution. This transformation is critical for making the pH scale user-friendly and easy to interpret.

Interpreting the Result: Is the Solution Acidic or Basic?

Now that we've calculated the pH to be 9.90, what does that actually tell us about the solution? Remember our pH scale? A pH of 7 is neutral, below 7 is acidic, and above 7 is basic. Since 9.90 is significantly greater than 7, we can confidently say that the solution is basic (or alkaline). This makes sense because the concentration of hydrogen ions (1.25 x 10^-10 M) is quite low. In a basic solution, there are fewer hydrogen ions and more hydroxide ions (OH-). Our calculated pH value perfectly reflects this.

Knowing that the solution is basic gives us a lot of information about its chemical behavior. Basic solutions tend to feel slippery, react with acids, and can have different effects on indicators (substances that change color depending on pH). For example, if we were to add a pH indicator like phenolphthalein to this solution, it would turn pink or purple, indicating a pH above 8.3.

Understanding the relationship between hydrogen ion concentration and pH allows us to predict the properties and reactivity of a solution. This is fundamental knowledge in chemistry and has practical applications in many fields, from medicine to environmental science.

Common Mistakes to Avoid When Calculating pH

Alright, guys, before we wrap up, let's quickly go over some common mistakes people make when calculating pH. Avoiding these pitfalls will ensure you get the correct answer every time!

  1. Forgetting the negative sign: The most common mistake is forgetting the negative sign in the formula pH = -log[H3O+]. Remember, you need to multiply the logarithm by -1 to get the correct pH value.
  2. Using the wrong concentration: Make sure you're using the concentration of hydrogen ions (H+) or hydronium ions (H3O+) in your calculation. If you're given the concentration of hydroxide ions (OH-), you'll need to use a different approach to calculate pH (which we might cover in another article!).
  3. Incorrectly entering the scientific notation: When dealing with scientific notation (like 1.25 x 10^-10), make sure you enter it correctly into your calculator. Use the “EE” or “EXP” button on your calculator to input the exponent. Don't try to type it out as “x 10 ^” – that can lead to errors.
  4. Not paying attention to significant figures: Remember to round your final pH value to the correct number of significant figures. The number of decimal places in the pH should match the number of significant figures in the concentration. In our case, the concentration (1.25 x 10^-10 M) has three significant figures, so our pH (9.90) should have two decimal places.
  5. Misunderstanding the logarithmic scale: It's crucial to remember that the pH scale is logarithmic. This means that a change of one pH unit represents a tenfold change in acidity or alkalinity. Don't treat the pH scale as linear – small changes in pH can have significant effects.

By being aware of these common mistakes, you'll be well on your way to mastering pH calculations! Practice makes perfect, so try working through more examples to solidify your understanding.

Conclusion: Mastering pH Calculations

So, there you have it! We've successfully calculated the pH of a solution with [H+] = 1.25 x 10^-10 M. We found that the pH is 9.90, indicating that the solution is basic. We've also discussed the importance of pH, the role of the hydronium ion, common mistakes to avoid, and how to interpret pH values. Hopefully, this article has demystified pH calculations for you and given you a solid foundation for understanding acidity and alkalinity.

pH is a fundamental concept in chemistry, and mastering it opens the door to understanding a wide range of chemical and biological phenomena.

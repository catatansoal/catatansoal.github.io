Understanding Perpendicular Lines

Before we jump into solving the problem, let's make sure we're all on the same page about what perpendicular lines actually are. Perpendicular lines are lines that intersect each other at a right angle, which is 90 degrees. This intersection forms a perfect 'L' shape. The key to identifying perpendicular lines lies in their slopes. The slope of a line tells us how steep it is and in which direction it's going. In the slope-intercept form of a linear equation, which is $y = mx + b$, the coefficient 'm' represents the slope, and 'b' represents the y-intercept (the point where the line crosses the y-axis).

Now, here's the crucial part: Two lines are perpendicular if and only if the product of their slopes is -1. This means that if one line has a slope of 'm', the slope of a line perpendicular to it will be $-1/m$. This is also known as the negative reciprocal of the slope. To find the negative reciprocal, you flip the fraction (reciprocal) and change the sign (negative). For example, if a line has a slope of 2, the slope of a line perpendicular to it would be $-1/2$. If a line has a slope of $-3/4$, the slope of a line perpendicular to it would be $4/3$. This relationship is the cornerstone of identifying perpendicular lines, and understanding it is essential for solving problems like the one we're about to tackle.

Understanding the relationship between slopes is critical. If two lines are perpendicular, their slopes are negative reciprocals of each other. This means you flip the fraction and change the sign. For instance, if the slope of one line is 2/3, the slope of a perpendicular line would be -3/2. This property allows us to quickly identify perpendicular lines when given their equations in slope-intercept form. Remember, the slope-intercept form is y = mx + b, where 'm' represents the slope and 'b' represents the y-intercept. When dealing with problems involving perpendicularity, always focus on identifying the slopes first. Once you have the slopes, you can easily determine if they are negative reciprocals of each other. Solving the Problem: Finding the Perpendicular Line

Now, let's get back to our original problem. We're given the line $y = 3x + 2$ and asked to find which of the given options is perpendicular to it. The first step is to identify the slope of the given line. Looking at the equation $y = 3x + 2$, we can see that it's in slope-intercept form (y = mx + b). The coefficient of 'x' is 3, which means the slope of this line is 3. So, to find a line perpendicular to this one, we need to find a line with a slope that is the negative reciprocal of 3. What's the negative reciprocal of 3? Remember, we flip the number and change the sign. Thinking of 3 as 3/1, the reciprocal is 1/3, and the negative of that is $-1/3$. Therefore, we're looking for a line with a slope of $-1/3$.

Now, let's examine the given options:

  • A. $y = rac{1}{3}x + 3$: The slope here is 1/3. This is the reciprocal of 3, but it's not the negative reciprocal, so it's not perpendicular.
  • B. $y = rac{1}{3}x + 2$: Again, the slope is 1/3. This is not the negative reciprocal, so it's not perpendicular.
  • C. $y = - rac{1}{3}x + 4$: Aha! The slope here is $-1/3$. This is exactly what we're looking for â€“ the negative reciprocal of 3. So, this line is perpendicular to the given line.
  • D. $y = 3x + rac{1}{2}$: The slope here is 3, the same as the original line. Lines with the same slope are parallel, not perpendicular.

Therefore, the correct answer is C. $y = - rac{1}{3}x + 4$. This line is perpendicular to $y = 3x + 2$ because its slope is the negative reciprocal of the original line's slope.

To reinforce this concept, let's consider why the other options are incorrect. Options A and B both have a slope of 1/3, which is the reciprocal of the original slope but not the negative reciprocal. This means these lines are neither parallel nor perpendicular to the original line. Option D has the same slope as the original line (3), indicating that the two lines are parallel. Parallel lines run in the same direction and never intersect, so they cannot be perpendicular. This leaves you with option C, which you can then confirm by verifying that its slope is indeed the negative reciprocal of the original line's slope.

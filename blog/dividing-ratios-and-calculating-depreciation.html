Dividing Ratios And Calculating Depreciation Step-by-Step Guide

Jul 13, 2025 by ADMIN 64 views

Hey guys! Let's tackle a classic math problem involving ratios. Ratios, in essence, are ways of comparing two or more quantities. They help us understand how much of one thing we have relative to another. In this case, we're diving into the world of proportions by figuring out how to divide 240 ml into a ratio of 2:1. This kind of problem is super practical – imagine you're mixing a drink, splitting ingredients for a recipe, or even sharing something fairly with a friend. Understanding ratios is a key skill in everyday life. When we say a ratio is 2:1, it means that for every two parts of one thing, there's one part of another. So, how do we actually split our 240 ml? The first step is to figure out the total number of parts in our ratio. We do this by simply adding the numbers in the ratio together: 2 + 1 = 3. This tells us that we're dividing our total volume into three equal parts. Now comes the crucial part: figuring out the size of each of those parts. To do this, we take our total volume (240 ml) and divide it by the total number of parts (3): 240 ml / 3 = 80 ml. So, each part in our ratio represents 80 ml. But we're not done yet! We need to figure out how much each side of the ratio gets. One part of the ratio is '2', and the other part is '1'. This means that the first part gets two lots of 80 ml, and the second part gets one lot of 80 ml. Let's calculate: For the first part (ratio of 2), we have 2 * 80 ml = 160 ml. For the second part (ratio of 1), we have 1 * 80 ml = 80 ml. And there we have it! We've successfully divided 240 ml in the ratio 2:1. The first portion is 160 ml, and the second portion is 80 ml. To double-check our work, we can add these two amounts together: 160 ml + 80 ml = 240 ml. This matches our original volume, so we know we've done it right! Mastering ratios opens up a world of possibilities, from cooking and baking to mixing chemicals in a lab. The core concept is all about understanding proportions and dividing quantities accurately. Keep practicing, and you'll be a ratio whiz in no time! Remember, understanding ratios is not just a math skill; it's a life skill. You will encounter scenarios where you need to divide things proportionally, and having a solid grasp of ratios will make those situations much easier to navigate. This could be anything from splitting a bill fairly among friends to adjusting ingredient quantities in a recipe. So, don't underestimate the power of ratios! Now, let's move on to our next challenge: calculating depreciation.

Now, let's shift gears and dive into the world of finance, specifically depreciation. Depreciation, in simple terms, is the decrease in the value of an asset over time. Think of it like this: when you buy a brand-new car, it starts losing value the moment you drive it off the lot. This is because of wear and tear, market conditions, and the simple fact that it's no longer "new." In this problem, we're looking at Mr. Phakathi's new car, which he bought for E 450,000 (we'll assume E stands for a currency like Emalangeni). The car depreciates at a rate of 8% per annum, which means it loses 8% of its value each year. Our goal is to figure out how much the car will be worth after 5 years. There are a couple of ways we can approach this problem. One way is to calculate the depreciation amount for each year and subtract it from the previous year's value. However, there's a more efficient formula we can use, especially for longer time periods. This formula leverages the concept of compound depreciation, similar to how compound interest works, but in reverse. The formula for calculating the value of an asset after depreciation is: Final Value = Initial Value * (1 - Depreciation Rate)^Number of Years. Let's break down each part of this formula in the context of Mr. Phakathi's car: Initial Value: This is the original price Mr. Phakathi paid for the car, which is E 450,000. Depreciation Rate: This is the annual rate at which the car loses value, expressed as a decimal. In this case, 8% is equivalent to 0.08 (8 divided by 100). Number of Years: This is the time period we're interested in, which is 5 years. Now, let's plug these values into our formula: Final Value = E 450,000 * (1 - 0.08)^5. First, we need to calculate the value inside the parentheses: 1 - 0.08 = 0.92. Next, we raise 0.92 to the power of 5: 0. 92^5 ≈ 0.6590815232. Finally, we multiply this result by the initial value: E 450,000 * 0.6590815232 ≈ E 296,586.68. So, after 5 years, the value of Mr. Phakathi's car will be approximately E 296,586.68. It's important to note that depreciation is a significant factor to consider when buying a car or any other asset that loses value over time. Understanding how depreciation works can help you make informed financial decisions, whether you're buying a car, investing in equipment, or managing your business finances. Depreciation is a crucial concept in finance, and mastering this calculation is a valuable skill. It helps you understand the true cost of owning assets and make informed decisions about when to sell or replace them. Remember, the rate of depreciation can vary significantly depending on the type of asset and market conditions, so it's always a good idea to do your research and get professional advice when needed. Now that we've tackled both ratios and depreciation, you've added two powerful tools to your mathematical arsenal. Keep practicing these concepts, and you'll be well-equipped to handle a wide range of real-world problems!

Solving For Radicals Finding The Missing Number In \$\sqrt{675}=15 \sqrt{\square}$\$

Jul 14, 2025 by ADMIN 85 views

Hey everyone! Today, we're diving into the fascinating world of radicals and tackling a problem that might seem a bit tricky at first glance. But don't worry, we'll break it down step by step and by the end, you'll be a radical-solving pro! We aim to determine the number that fits perfectly under the radical symbol to make the equation $\sqrt{675}=15 \sqrt{\square}\$ true. This involves understanding how to simplify radicals and manipulate them in equations. Let's embark on this mathematical journey together!

Understanding the Basics of Radicals

Before we jump into solving the problem, let's quickly recap what radicals are all about. A radical, often represented by the square root symbol ($\sqrt{\ }$), is essentially the inverse operation of squaring a number. For instance, the square root of 9 ($\sqrt{9}\$ ) is 3 because 3 multiplied by itself (3 squared) equals 9. Understanding this fundamental relationship is key to mastering radical simplification and manipulation.

Now, let's talk about simplifying radicals. The core idea here is to break down the number under the radical (the radicand) into its prime factors. Prime factors are those numbers that are only divisible by 1 and themselves (e.g., 2, 3, 5, 7, etc.). By identifying pairs of prime factors, we can pull them out of the radical, simplifying the expression. For example, to simplify $\sqrt{36}\$ , we first find the prime factors of 36, which are 2 x 2 x 3 x 3. Since we have two 2s and two 3s, we can pull out a 2 and a 3, resulting in 2 x 3 = 6. So, $\sqrt{36}\$ simplifies to 6. This technique will be crucial in solving our main problem.

Furthermore, it's important to remember that we can manipulate radicals in equations. Just like with any other algebraic expression, we can perform operations on both sides of an equation involving radicals, as long as we do it consistently. This might involve squaring both sides, dividing by a constant, or, as we'll see in our problem, simplifying and comparing coefficients. With these basics in mind, we're well-equipped to tackle the challenge ahead. So, let's move on to the next step and start unraveling the mystery of the missing number under the radical!

Deconstructing the Equation: $\sqrt{675}=15 \sqrt{\square}\$

Okay, let's get down to business and really dissect the equation we're faced with: $\sqrt{675}=15 \sqrt{\square}\$ . Our main goal here is to figure out what number should sit snugly under that radical symbol ($\sqrt{\square}\$ ) to make this whole statement ring true. To do this effectively, we're going to use a strategy that involves simplifying the radicals and then matching things up on both sides of the equals sign. Think of it like fitting puzzle pieces together – we need to manipulate the equation until we can clearly see what the missing piece is.

First things first, let's focus on simplifying the left side of the equation, which is $\sqrt{675}\$ . As we discussed earlier, the key to simplifying radicals is to break down the number under the square root (in this case, 675) into its prime factors. So, let's do that. If we break down 675, we find that it's equal to 3 x 3 x 3 x 5 x 5. Now, remember that for every pair of identical prime factors, we can pull one out from under the square root. We've got a pair of 3s and a pair of 5s, so we can pull out a 3 and a 5. This leaves us with one 3 still under the radical. Therefore, $\sqrt{675}\$ simplifies to 3 x 5 x $\sqrt{3}\$ , which is 15$\sqrt{3}\$ .

Now, let's bring this simplified form back into our original equation. We now have 15$\sqrt{3}\$ = 15$\sqrt{\square}\$ . This looks much cleaner and easier to work with, doesn't it? We've managed to simplify the left side, and now we can clearly see how it relates to the right side. The next step is to compare the two sides of the equation and pinpoint the missing number. By carefully looking at both sides, we can figure out the value that completes the equation and makes it a true statement. Let's move on to that crucial comparison now!

Solving for the Missing Number: A Step-by-Step Guide

Alright, let's get to the heart of the matter and nail down the missing number in our equation. We've simplified the equation to 15$\sqrt{3}\$ = 15$\sqrt{\square}\$ . Now, this is where things get really interesting, because we're going to use a bit of mathematical deduction to figure out what goes in that box.

If you take a good, hard look at both sides of the equation, you'll notice something pretty cool: we've got a 15 sitting outside the square root on both sides. This is a major clue! Since we have the same factor (15) multiplying the square root on both sides, we can essentially focus our attention on what's happening inside the square roots. It's like saying, "Okay, the 15s are taken care of, so what must be under the square root to make the equation balance?"

So, let's zoom in on the square roots themselves. On the left side, we have $\sqrt{3}\$ , and on the right side, we have $\sqrt{\square}\$ . For these two expressions to be equal (and remember, the whole equation has to be equal), the numbers under the square root have to be the same. It's that simple! If $\sqrt{3}\$ is equal to $\sqrt{\square}\$ , then the number in the box ($\square$) must be 3.

Therefore, the missing number is 3. We've successfully solved the puzzle! By simplifying the radical, comparing the two sides of the equation, and using a bit of logical thinking, we've found the value that makes the statement true. Now, let's take a moment to recap the entire process and make sure we've got a solid understanding of how we got to the answer.

Final Answer: Putting It All Together

Okay, guys, let's recap what we've done and solidify our understanding of how we cracked this radical riddle. We started with the equation $\sqrt{675}=15 \sqrt{\square}\$ and our mission was to find the number that fits under the radical on the right side.

First, we rolled up our sleeves and simplified $\sqrt{675}\$ . By breaking down 675 into its prime factors (3 x 3 x 3 x 5 x 5), we were able to pull out pairs of factors and rewrite $\sqrt{675}\$ as 15$\sqrt{3}\$ . This simplification was a key step because it made the equation much easier to handle.

Next, we plugged this simplified form back into the original equation, giving us 15$\sqrt{3}\$ = 15$\sqrt{\square}\$ . At this point, we used our mathematical detective skills to compare both sides of the equation. We noticed that the factor of 15 was present on both sides, which meant we could focus on the expressions under the square roots.

Finally, we zeroed in on the square roots themselves: $\sqrt{3}\$ on the left and $\sqrt{\square}\$ on the right. For these two expressions to be equal, the numbers under the square root had to be the same. This led us to the clear conclusion that the missing number, the one that goes in the box, is 3.

So, the answer is 3! We've successfully navigated the world of radicals, simplified expressions, and solved for a missing value. This problem demonstrates the power of breaking down complex problems into smaller, manageable steps. By understanding the fundamentals of radicals and using logical reasoning, we can conquer even the trickiest mathematical challenges. Keep practicing, and you'll become a radical-solving whiz in no time!

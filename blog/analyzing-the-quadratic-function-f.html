Analyzing The Quadratic Function F(x) = -3x² - 6x + 144

Jul 17, 2025 by ADMIN 56 views

Hey guys! Today, we're diving deep into the fascinating world of quadratic functions, and we're going to dissect a specific one: f(x) = -3x² - 6x + 144. We'll explore its graph, pinpoint its intercepts, and locate its vertex. Get ready to uncover all the secrets this function holds! This exploration is not just about crunching numbers; it's about understanding the behavior of parabolas and how they manifest in the real world. From the trajectory of a ball thrown in the air to the design of satellite dishes, quadratic functions are everywhere. Understanding them gives you a powerful tool for analyzing and predicting various phenomena. So, buckle up, and let's embark on this mathematical journey together!

Unveiling the Direction of the Graph

First, let's talk about the direction of the graph. The leading coefficient, which is the coefficient of the x2x^2x2 term, plays a crucial role here. In our function, f(x) = -3x² - 6x + 144, the leading coefficient is -3. This negative value is a big indicator! Remember, a negative leading coefficient means the parabola opens downwards, like a frown. This tells us that the function has a maximum value, which will be located at the vertex. Conversely, if the leading coefficient were positive, the parabola would open upwards, like a smile, and the function would have a minimum value. This simple observation is fundamental in understanding the overall shape and behavior of any quadratic function. Thinking about real-world applications, a downward-opening parabola could represent the path of a projectile under the influence of gravity, while an upward-opening parabola might describe the cost function of producing a certain number of items. So, the direction of the graph isn't just an abstract concept; it's a powerful visual representation of the function's behavior.

Pinpointing the Y-Intercept

Next up, we'll find the y-intercept. The y-intercept is where the graph crosses the y-axis. This happens when x = 0. So, to find the y-intercept, we simply substitute x = 0 into our function:

f(0) = -3(0)² - 6(0) + 144 = 144

Therefore, the y-intercept is at y = 144. This means the parabola intersects the y-axis at the point (0, 144). The y-intercept is often the easiest intercept to find, as it only requires a simple substitution. In practical terms, the y-intercept can represent the initial value of a quantity. For example, if our function represented the height of a plant over time, the y-intercept would be the initial height of the plant when we started measuring. It's a crucial point of reference for understanding the function's starting point and its subsequent behavior. Visualizing this point on the graph gives us a concrete understanding of where the parabola begins its journey.

Discovering the X-Intercepts

Now, let's tackle the x-intercepts. The x-intercepts are where the graph crosses the x-axis, and this occurs when f(x) = 0. So, we need to solve the quadratic equation:

-3x² - 6x + 144 = 0

To make our lives easier, let's divide the entire equation by -3:

x² + 2x - 48 = 0

Now, we can factor this quadratic equation. We're looking for two numbers that multiply to -48 and add up to 2. Those numbers are 8 and -6. So, we can factor the equation as:

(x + 8)(x - 6) = 0

This gives us two solutions:

x + 8 = 0 => x = -8 x - 6 = 0 => x = 6

Therefore, the x-intercepts are at x = -8 and x = 6. These points are where the parabola intersects the x-axis, and they represent the roots or zeros of the quadratic function. Finding the x-intercepts is crucial for understanding where the function's value becomes zero, which can have significant implications in various applications. For example, if our function represented the profit of a business, the x-intercepts would represent the break-even points, where the profit is zero. These points are critical for decision-making and strategic planning.

Locating the Vertex

Finally, let's find the vertex. The vertex is the highest or lowest point on the parabola. Since our parabola opens downwards, the vertex will be the highest point (the maximum value of the function). There are a couple of ways to find the vertex. One way is to use the vertex formula, which is given by:

x_vertex = -b / 2a

where a and b are the coefficients of the quadratic term and the linear term, respectively. In our case, a = -3 and b = -6. So,

x_vertex = -(-6) / (2 * -3) = 6 / -6 = -1

Now, to find the y-coordinate of the vertex, we substitute this x_vertex value back into our original function:

f(-1) = -3(-1)² - 6(-1) + 144 = -3 + 6 + 144 = 147

Therefore, the vertex is at the point (-1, 147). This point represents the maximum value of the function. The vertex is a key feature of the parabola, as it indicates the extreme point of the function. In practical applications, the vertex can represent the maximum height reached by a projectile, the minimum cost of production, or the optimal value in various scenarios. Understanding the vertex allows us to analyze and optimize real-world situations modeled by quadratic functions. The x-coordinate of the vertex also represents the axis of symmetry of the parabola, a vertical line that divides the parabola into two symmetrical halves. This symmetry is a fundamental property of parabolas and can be useful in sketching the graph and understanding its behavior.

Summarizing Our Findings

So, to recap, for the function f(x) = -3x² - 6x + 144:

  • The graph opens downwards.
  • The y-intercept is at y = 144.
  • The x-intercepts are at x = -8 and x = 6.
  • The vertex is at (-1, 147).

By finding these key features, we've gained a comprehensive understanding of the behavior of this quadratic function. We can now visualize its graph accurately and interpret its properties in various contexts. This exercise demonstrates the power of mathematical analysis in unraveling the mysteries of functions and their real-world applications. Keep exploring, keep questioning, and keep learning, guys! The world of mathematics is full of fascinating discoveries waiting to be made.

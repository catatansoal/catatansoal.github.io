Understanding Quadratic Equations and Solutions

Before we jump into the specific problem, let's quickly recap what quadratic equations are and how their solutions work. A quadratic equation is basically an equation that can be written in the standard form of ax² + bx + c = 0, where a, b, and c are constants (numbers), and 'x' is our variable. These equations are super common in math and physics, and they pop up in all sorts of real-world scenarios.

The solutions to a quadratic equation are the values of 'x' that make the equation true. In other words, if you plug these values back into the equation, the left side will equal the right side (which is usually zero). Quadratic equations can have two solutions, one solution (a repeated root), or even no real solutions, depending on the coefficients a, b, and c. Finding these solutions is a fundamental skill in algebra.

One of the most popular ways to find solutions to quadratic equations is by using the quadratic formula. This formula is a lifesaver and looks like this:

x = (-b ± √(b² - 4ac)) / (2a)

Where: a, b, and c are the coefficients from our standard quadratic equation ax² + bx + c = 0. The ± symbol means we actually have two solutions: one where we add the square root part and one where we subtract it. The expression inside the square root, b² - 4ac, is called the discriminant. It tells us a lot about the nature of the solutions: If it's positive, we have two real solutions; if it's zero, we have one real solution (a repeated root); and if it's negative, we have no real solutions.

Think of the quadratic formula as a universal key that unlocks the solutions to any quadratic equation, no matter how complicated it looks! Understanding this formula is crucial for solving problems like the one we have today. By recognizing how the coefficients a, b, and c influence the solutions, we can work backward from the given solutions to identify the correct quadratic equation. So, keep this formula in mind as we move forward – it's going to be our best friend in this mathematical adventure.

Working Backwards: Reconstructing the Quadratic Equation

Now, let’s get to the heart of our problem. We’re given the solutions x = (5 ± 2√7) / 3, and our mission is to find the quadratic equation that produces these solutions. This is like being a detective, but instead of clues, we have math! We’re going to work backwards from the solutions to reconstruct the equation.

Our solutions look a lot like the result of applying the quadratic formula. So, let’s compare our given solutions with the general form of the quadratic formula: x = (-b ± √(b² - 4ac)) / (2a). Our goal here is to match the parts of our solutions with the corresponding parts of the quadratic formula. This will help us figure out the values of a, b, and c, which we need to build our equation.

First, let's focus on the denominator. In the quadratic formula, the denominator is 2a. In our solutions, the denominator is 3. So, we can deduce that 2a = 3, which means a = 3/2. However, we typically want our coefficients to be integers (whole numbers) to match the given options. To achieve this, we can consider a = 3. This means we might need to adjust the other coefficients accordingly later on.

Next, let's look at the term before the ± sign in our solutions, which is 5 in the numerator. In the quadratic formula, this corresponds to -b. So, -b should be related to 5. Since we've decided to stick with a = 3, let’s plug it back into our comparison with the quadratic formula. The numerator before the ± sign is -b, so -b should correspond to something that gives us 5 when divided by 2a (which is 2 * 3 = 6). Thus, -b/6 should be something close to 5/3. A good guess would be -b = -10 which means b = 10. This gives us a good starting point for our 'b' value.

Finally, let's tackle the term under the square root. In our solutions, we have 2√7. Remember, the quadratic formula has √(b² - 4ac) in this position. So, we need to figure out what value of 'c' will make b² - 4ac equal to (2√7)². Let's calculate (2√7)² first: (2√7)² = 4 * 7 = 28. Now we know b² - 4ac should equal 28. We already have a value for b (which is 10) and a value for a (which is 3). Let's plug those in: 10² - 4 * 3 * c = 28, which simplifies to 100 - 12c = 28. Solving for 'c', we get 12c = 72, so c = 6.

Putting it all together, we have a = 3, b = 10, and c = 6. This gives us the quadratic equation 3x² - 10x + 6 = 0. So, by carefully working backwards and comparing our solutions to the quadratic formula, we've successfully reconstructed the equation!

Testing the Options: A Practical Approach

Now that we've understood how to reconstruct a quadratic equation from its solutions, let's put our knowledge to the test. We have a few options for the equation, and we need to figure out which one has the solutions x = (5 ± 2√7) / 3. We could dive right into applying the quadratic formula to each option, but let's think smart and see if we can eliminate some choices first.

Our given solutions x = (5 ± 2√7) / 3 already give us some clues. Remember the quadratic formula? x = (-b ± √(b² - 4ac)) / (2a). We can look at the structure of our solutions and compare it to the structure of the quadratic formula. This will help us narrow down our options.

Let's focus on the denominator first. In our solutions, the denominator is 3. This part comes from the 2a in the quadratic formula. So, we need an equation where 2a = 3, which means a should be 3/2. But since quadratic equations usually have integer coefficients, we can infer that 'a' is likely to be 3 (we might need to adjust other coefficients accordingly if we multiply 'a' by 2). This quick check doesn't eliminate any options yet, as all the provided equations have 'a' as 3.

Next, let’s look at the part before the square root in our solutions. We have 5 in the numerator. In the quadratic formula, this part comes from -b. So, -b, when divided by 2a (which is 6, since a = 3), should give us something related to 5/3. This implies that -b should be around 10, meaning b should be around -10. This narrows our search to the equations with a -10x term, which are:

  • 3x² - 10x + 6 = 0
  • 3x² - 10x - 1 = 0

We've already eliminated half of the options without doing a ton of calculations! Now, we only need to test these two equations to see which one gives us the correct solutions.

Let's use the quadratic formula on the first remaining option: 3x² - 10x + 6 = 0. Here, a = 3, b = -10, and c = 6. Plugging these values into the quadratic formula, we get:

x = (-(-10) ± √((-10)² - 4 * 3 * 6)) / (2 * 3) x = (10 ± √(100 - 72)) / 6 x = (10 ± √28) / 6 x = (10 ± 2√7) / 6 x = (5 ± √7) / 3

Wait a minute! Our solution doesn't quite match the given solutions (5 ± 2√7) / 3. This tells us that 3x² - 10x + 6 = 0 isn't the correct equation. But don't worry, we're super close! We only have one option left, so let's give it a try.

Now, let’s test the final option: 3x² - 10x - 1 = 0. Here, a = 3, b = -10, and c = -1. Plugging these values into the quadratic formula, we get:

x = (-(-10) ± √((-10)² - 4 * 3 * (-1))) / (2 * 3) x = (10 ± √(100 + 12)) / 6 x = (10 ± √112) / 6 x = (10 ± √(16 * 7)) / 6 x = (10 ± 4√7) / 6 x = (5 ± 2√7) / 3

Bingo! These solutions match the given solutions x = (5 ± 2√7) / 3. So, the correct equation is 3x² - 10x - 1 = 0.

By strategically using the quadratic formula and comparing the structure of our solutions, we were able to find the correct equation efficiently. Remember, math is often about finding the smartest way to solve a problem, not just the first way that comes to mind.

The Correct Equation and Why It Works

Alright, guys, after our mathematical adventure, we've nailed it! We've figured out that the quadratic equation with the solutions x = (5 ± 2√7) / 3 is:

3x² - 10x - 1 = 0

But let's not just stop at the answer. It's super important to understand why this is the correct equation. This understanding is what really cements our knowledge and helps us tackle similar problems in the future. So, let’s break down why this equation works and what makes it the perfect fit for our solutions.

As we discussed earlier, the key to solving this problem is the quadratic formula: x = (-b ± √(b² - 4ac)) / (2a). This formula is like a magic decoder for quadratic equations, allowing us to find the solutions based on the coefficients a, b, and c. In our case, a = 3, b = -10, and c = -1. When we plug these values into the quadratic formula, we should get our given solutions, x = (5 ± 2√7) / 3. Let's walk through it again to make sure it clicks:

x = (-(-10) ± √((-10)² - 4 * 3 * (-1))) / (2 * 3) x = (10 ± √(100 + 12)) / 6 x = (10 ± √112) / 6

Now, let’s simplify the square root. We know that 112 can be factored into 16 * 7, and the square root of 16 is 4. So:

x = (10 ± √(16 * 7)) / 6 x = (10 ± 4√7) / 6

Finally, we can simplify the fraction by dividing both the numerator and the denominator by 2:

x = (5 ± 2√7) / 3

And there it is! We got exactly the solutions we were given. This confirms that 3x² - 10x - 1 = 0 is indeed the correct quadratic equation. But what makes this equation unique? It's the specific combination of coefficients a, b, and c that leads to these solutions. The 'a' value of 3 affects the denominator, while the 'b' value of -10 influences the term before the square root. The 'c' value of -1, in combination with 'a' and 'b', determines the value inside the square root.

If we had chosen a different 'c' value, for example, the value inside the square root would change, and we'd get different solutions. This is why understanding the relationship between the coefficients and the solutions is so crucial. By recognizing this relationship, we can not only solve quadratic equations but also manipulate them to achieve desired solutions. This is a powerful skill in mathematics and can be applied in many different areas.

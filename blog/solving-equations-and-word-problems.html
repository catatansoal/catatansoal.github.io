Solving Equations And Word Problems A Step-by-Step Guide

Jul 13, 2025 by ADMIN 57 views

Hey guys! Ever feel like math problems are just a jumble of numbers and symbols? Don't worry, we've all been there. Today, we're going to break down a problem step-by-step, making it super easy to understand. We'll cover solving equations and tackling word problems ‚Äì skills that are essential not just for math class, but for everyday life! So, grab your pencils, and let's dive in!

Understanding the Problem: Dolls and Pucar√° Bulls

Let's start with our problem. We have two equations:

  • 5y = 95
  • x + [blank] = 33

And a word problem that goes with it: "Therefore, [blank] cloth dolls and [blank] Pucar√° bulls were sold." This looks like a classic system of equations problem, often used to represent real-world scenarios. The key here is to carefully read the problem and understand what each variable represents.

The first equation, 5y = 95, likely represents a total cost or revenue related to one of the items (either the dolls or the bulls). The 'y' is a crucial variable, representing the quantity of one of these items. Our first task is to solve for 'y'. We need to isolate this variable, to find its value, which will give us a piece of the puzzle. We will use the fundamental concept of inverse operations to find out the value of y. This process involves identifying the operation performed on the variable (in this case, multiplication) and then performing the inverse operation (division) on both sides of the equation. This ensures that the equation remains balanced while isolating the variable, which will give us the numerical value of the unknown.

The second equation, x + [blank] = 33, is where things get a little more interesting. We have 'x', another variable representing the quantity of the other item, and a blank space that we need to fill. This equation probably represents a relationship between the number of dolls and bulls sold, such as a total quantity. To complete this equation, we'll need to use the information we gain from solving the first equation. We're going to substitute the value of 'y' we found earlier into this equation. This substitution allows us to eliminate one variable and obtain an equation that solely depends on 'x', making it easier to solve for. By plugging in the known value of 'y', we transform the equation into a more manageable form, where we can apply algebraic manipulations to isolate and find the value of 'x'. This process demonstrates a powerful technique in solving systems of equations, where substitution helps simplify complex relationships and arrive at individual solutions.

Finally, the statement "Therefore, [blank] cloth dolls and [blank] Pucar√° bulls were sold" is the ultimate question we need to answer. This is where we connect the math back to the real world. Once we've solved for 'x' and 'y', we'll need to figure out which variable represents the dolls and which represents the bulls. The context of the problem or any additional information provided will help us in making this determination. The final step is to fill in the blanks with the values we've calculated, clearly stating the number of dolls and bulls sold. This final answer is not just about providing the correct numbers; it's also about conveying the solution in a clear and understandable manner, ensuring that the answer directly addresses the question posed in the problem.

Solving for 'y': A Simple Division

The first step is to solve the equation 5y = 95. Remember, '5y' means 5 multiplied by 'y'. To isolate 'y', we need to do the opposite operation: divide both sides of the equation by 5.

  • 5y / 5 = 95 / 5

Dividing both sides by 5 keeps the equation balanced ‚Äì a fundamental principle in algebra. Anything we do to one side, we must do to the other. On the left side, the 5s cancel out, leaving us with just 'y'. On the right side, 95 divided by 5 is 19. This process of isolating the variable by performing inverse operations is the core of solving linear equations. Understanding this principle allows us to tackle more complex equations by systematically undoing the operations that are applied to the variable. By carefully applying inverse operations, we can unravel the equation and reveal the value of the unknown, which is a critical skill in algebra and beyond.

  • y = 19

So, we've found that y = 19! That's one variable down. But what does this mean in the context of our problem? Remember, 'y' represents the quantity of either dolls or bulls. We'll need to keep this in mind as we move forward. The result, y = 19, is a significant step forward in solving the problem. It gives us a concrete value for one of the unknowns, which we can then use to find the value of the other unknown. The importance of finding this value cannot be overstated, as it forms the basis for the subsequent steps in solving the problem. This process of systematically solving for variables one at a time is a common strategy in algebra, allowing us to break down complex problems into smaller, more manageable parts. With 'y' now known, we're one step closer to fully understanding the relationship between dolls and bulls and solving the word problem.

Plugging it In: Solving for 'x'

Now that we know y = 19, we can substitute this value into the second equation: x + [blank] = 33. But wait, there's a blank! This is where we need to think critically about the problem's context. We know that 'y' is likely the number of one item (dolls or bulls), and the blank is likely related to 'y'. The equation probably represents the total number of items sold. The value of 19, which we found for 'y', should logically fit into the blank space, representing the contribution of 'y' to the total count. This step underscores the importance of connecting the algebraic manipulations with the real-world scenario described in the problem. It's not just about solving equations in isolation; it's about understanding what the variables and equations represent in the given context.

Let's assume the equation is x + 5y = 33. This makes sense because we already know the value of 5y (it's 95 from our first equation!), and this equation could represent a total number of items sold, or some other relationship between 'x' and 'y'. This assumption transforms the problem from one with an unknown blank into a fully defined algebraic equation. It allows us to proceed with the solution process by applying the principles of algebra to find the value of 'x'. The assumption is a critical step because it completes the equation, making it solvable and aligning it with the information available in the problem. Without this assumption, we would be stuck with an incomplete equation, unable to proceed with solving for 'x'.

Now we substitute y = 19 into the equation:

  • x + 19 = 33

To solve for 'x', we need to isolate it. We do this by subtracting 19 from both sides:

  • x + 19 - 19 = 33 - 19
  • x = 14

Awesome! We've found that x = 14. This is a significant result! We now know the values of both 'x' and 'y'. The final step is to interpret these values in the context of the word problem, which will provide us with the answer we're looking for. The process of finding 'x' involved applying the same algebraic principles we used to find 'y': isolating the variable by performing inverse operations. Each step, from substitution to subtraction, is crucial for arriving at the correct value of 'x'. This demonstrates the systematic approach that is fundamental to solving algebraic problems, where each step builds upon the previous one to progressively reveal the solution.

Putting it Together: Answering the Question

We know that y = 19 and x = 14. Now, we need to figure out which variable represents the cloth dolls and which represents the Pucar√° bulls. Let's revisit the original problem. Since the equation 5y = 95 was used first, and it likely represents a total cost or revenue, 'y' probably corresponds to the item with a fixed price. Pucar√° bulls are often more unique and potentially more expensive, so let's hypothesize that 'y' represents the number of Pucar√° bulls. This logical deduction is a crucial part of problem-solving. It's not just about crunching numbers; it's about understanding the context and making informed decisions based on the information available. In real-world problems, there's often not a single, clear-cut path to the solution. We need to consider different possibilities, evaluate their plausibility, and choose the one that makes the most sense within the context of the problem.

If 'y' is the number of Pucar√° bulls, then 'x' must be the number of cloth dolls. So, we sold 14 cloth dolls and 19 Pucar√° bulls! The final step is to connect our mathematical results back to the original question asked in the problem. This is where we bridge the gap between abstract algebra and the concrete scenario it represents. We've solved the equations, found the values of the variables, and now we need to express the answer in a way that directly addresses the question. This final interpretation is crucial because it demonstrates our understanding of the problem as a whole, not just our ability to solve equations. It's about translating the mathematical solution into meaningful information that answers the question posed in the word problem.

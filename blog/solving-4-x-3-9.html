Understanding the Problem

Before we jump into the solution, let's break down the problem. We have an exponential equation, which means our variable, xxx, is in the exponent. Our goal is to isolate xxx and find its value. Now, exponential equations can seem intimidating at first, but don't worry, we've got the perfect tool for this – logarithms. Logarithms are the inverse operation of exponentiation, meaning they "undo" exponents. Think of it like this: if you have ab=ca^b = cab=c, then log⁡ac=b\log_a c = bloga​c=b. This relationship is the key to unlocking our solution.

Now, the equation we're dealing with is 4x+3=94^{x+3} = 94x+3=9. Notice that we can't easily express 9 as a power of 4. That's where logarithms come to the rescue. We'll use logarithms to bring the exponent down and solve for xxx. But there's a catch! Most calculators only have logarithms in base 10 (common logarithm) or base eee (natural logarithm). So, how do we handle a base 4? That's where the change of base formula comes in super handy. This formula allows us to convert logarithms from one base to another, making our calculations much easier.

So, the change of base formula is our secret weapon here, allowing us to transform the logarithm into a form we can easily compute. Using this formula, we can express the logarithm in base 4 in terms of logarithms in base 10 or base eee, which we can then calculate using a calculator. This is a fundamental technique in solving exponential equations, and mastering it will greatly enhance your mathematical toolkit. Remember, the core concept here is to use the inverse relationship between exponents and logarithms, and the change of base formula is the bridge that allows us to apply this concept effectively in various scenarios.

The Change of Base Formula: Our Secret Weapon

So, what exactly is this magical change of base formula? It states that for any positive numbers aaa, bbb, and yyy (where ae1a e 1ae1 and be1b e 1be1), we have:

log⁡by=log⁡aylog⁡ab\log_b y = \frac{\log_a y}{\log_a b}logb​y=loga​bloga​y​

In simple terms, this formula lets us change the base of a logarithm. We can rewrite a logarithm in any base bbb as a fraction of logarithms in another base aaa. The numerator is the logarithm of the original argument (yyy) in the new base (aaa), and the denominator is the logarithm of the original base (bbb) in the new base (aaa). This might sound a bit confusing, but it's actually quite straightforward in practice.

Why is this formula so important? Well, as we mentioned earlier, most calculators only have built-in functions for common logarithms (base 10, written as log⁡\loglog) and natural logarithms (base eee, written as ln⁡\lnln). The change of base formula allows us to calculate logarithms in any base using these functions. log⁡49=log⁡9log⁡4\log_4 9 = \frac{\log 9}{\log 4}log4​9=log4log9​

Now we can simply use a calculator to find the values of log⁡9\log 9log9 and log⁡4\log 4log4 and divide them to get the result. Pretty neat, right?

The beauty of the change of base formula lies in its flexibility. We can choose any base aaa we want, as long as it's positive and not equal to 1. In most cases, using base 10 or base eee is the most convenient, as these are readily available on calculators. However, there might be situations where choosing a different base simplifies the problem. Understanding this flexibility is crucial for mastering logarithms and their applications. The change of base formula isn't just a mathematical trick; it's a fundamental tool that empowers us to manipulate logarithms and solve complex problems effectively. It highlights the interconnectedness of different logarithmic scales and provides a bridge to translate between them.

Solving 4x+3=94^{x+3}=94x+3=9: Step-by-Step

  1. Take the logarithm of both sides: To bring the exponent down, we'll take the logarithm of both sides of the equation. We can use any base we want, but for convenience, let's use the common logarithm (base 10):

This is a key step in solving exponential equations, as it transforms the equation from a form where the variable is in the exponent to a form where the variable is multiplied by a constant. This transformation is essential for isolating the variable and finding its value. Remember, the power rule is a direct consequence of the definition of logarithms and exponents, and understanding its derivation can help solidify your understanding of logarithmic operations.

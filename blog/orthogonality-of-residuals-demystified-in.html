Why Orthogonality Matters

So, why should you care about this orthogonality stuff? Well, it's not just a theoretical curiosity. It has some serious practical implications. First off, it's a sign of a well-behaved model. Secondly, orthogonality makes our lives easier when we're evaluating our model. It allows us to decompose the total variation in the dependent variable into the part explained by the model and the part that's left unexplained (the residuals). This decomposition is the basis for measures like R-squared, which tells us how well our model fits the data. Orthogonality simplifies the math and gives us a clear way to assess our model's performance. Moreover, understanding orthogonality helps us diagnose potential problems with our model. This is a cue to re-examine our assumptions and potentially add more predictors or consider a different type of model. Orthogonality also plays a crucial role in the statistical properties of our model. It ensures that the estimators of our regression coefficients are unbiased and have minimum variance, which means that our estimates are as accurate and reliable as possible. This is a big deal because it allows us to make more confident inferences about the relationships between our variables. In essence, orthogonality is a cornerstone of linear regression.

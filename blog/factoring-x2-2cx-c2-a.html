Common Mistakes to Avoid

Factoring can be tricky, and it's easy to make mistakes if you're not careful. One common mistake is confusing the perfect square trinomial pattern with the difference of squares pattern. The difference of squares pattern is A2−B2=(A+B)(A−B)A^2 - B^2 = (A + B)(A - B)A2−B2=(A+B)(A−B), which is similar but distinct from the perfect square trinomial pattern. In our case, we have a plus sign in the middle term (2cx2cx2cx), which indicates a perfect square trinomial, not a difference of squares.

Another common mistake is incorrectly applying the perfect square trinomial pattern. For example, some people might mistakenly think that x2+2cx+c2x^2 + 2cx + c^2x2+2cx+c2 factors to (x+c)(x−c)(x + c)(x - c)(x+c)(x−c), which is incorrect. Remember, the middle term is crucial in determining the correct factored form. If the middle term is positive, like in our case, then the binomial should have a plus sign. If the middle term were negative, then the binomial would have a minus sign.

It's also important to avoid expanding the factored form incorrectly. If you're unsure about your factoring, always take the time to expand your answer and see if it matches the original expression. This is a great way to catch any errors and build your confidence.

Finally, be mindful of the signs. A simple sign error can throw off the entire factoring process. Pay close attention to the signs of each term in the expression and make sure they are consistent throughout your work.

Pruning Techniques: Step-by-Step Guide

Identifying Branches to Remove

Start by looking for dead, diseased, or damaged branches. These should be removed first. Then, look for branches that are crossing or rubbing against each other. These branches can cause damage and should also be removed. If the tree is overgrown, thin out the canopy by removing some of the inner branches. Making the Cuts

Make your cuts at a slight angle, just above a node or a branching point. This will allow water to run off and prevent rot. Cut the branch as close as possible to the main stem, without damaging the collar. The collar is the slightly swollen area at the base of the branch. For larger branches, you should use the three-cut method. First, make an undercut a few inches from the main stem. Then, make a second cut from the top of the branch, a few inches beyond the first cut, to remove the weight of the branch. Finally, make the final cut just outside the branch collar. Clean, sharp cuts will heal more quickly and reduce the risk of disease. Avoid making ragged cuts, as these can be difficult for the tree to heal and can invite pests and diseases. Always clean your pruning tools after each cut to prevent the spread of disease.

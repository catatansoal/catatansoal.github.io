Equivalent Inequalities To R > -11 A Step By Step Guide

Jul 14, 2025 by ADMIN 56 views

Hey everyone! Today, we're diving deep into the world of inequalities, specifically focusing on those equivalent to r > -11. Inequalities might seem a bit tricky at first, but once you grasp the fundamental concepts, they become super manageable. We'll break down each option step-by-step, ensuring you understand exactly why some inequalities are equivalent and others aren't. So, let's put on our math hats and get started!

Understanding the Basics of Inequalities

Before we jump into the specific problem, let's quickly review what inequalities are and how they work. Inequalities are mathematical statements that compare two values, indicating that they are not equal. Unlike equations, which use an equals sign (=), inequalities use symbols like greater than (>), less than (<), greater than or equal to (≥), and less than or equal to (≤). Understanding how to manipulate inequalities is crucial, and it involves a few key rules that we'll explore as we tackle each option.

The inequality r > -11 simply means that the variable r represents any number greater than -11. For example, -10, -9, 0, 1, 100, and so on, all satisfy this inequality. Our goal is to determine which of the given options also represent this same set of solutions. This involves applying algebraic operations to the inequalities while keeping in mind how these operations affect the inequality sign. One of the most important rules to remember is that multiplying or dividing both sides of an inequality by a negative number reverses the direction of the inequality sign. This is a critical concept that we will use repeatedly in our analysis. So, with these basics in mind, let's begin our exploration of the given inequalities and see which ones match our target.

The Golden Rule: Flipping the Sign

The most critical rule to remember when working with inequalities is this: when you multiply or divide both sides of an inequality by a negative number, you must reverse the direction of the inequality sign. This is the key to correctly manipulating inequalities and determining equivalency. For example, if we have the inequality -r < 11, to solve for r, we need to get rid of the negative sign in front of r. We do this by multiplying both sides by -1. When we do, we must flip the less than sign (<) to a greater than sign (>). This rule ensures that the inequality remains true. Think of it like balancing a scale; if you change the weight on one side, you need to adjust the other side to maintain the balance, or in this case, the correct relationship between the two sides of the inequality.

Now, let's apply this rule and others as we dissect each option. Remember, our aim is to transform each inequality to see if it truly reflects the same solution set as r > -11. By carefully applying these rules, we can confidently identify the equivalent inequalities and understand why they hold true. This process not only helps us solve this specific problem but also builds a solid foundation for tackling more complex inequality problems in the future. So, keep this golden rule in mind, and let's dive into our first option.

Analyzing the Inequalities

Now, let's carefully examine each inequality to see if it's equivalent to r > -11. We'll go through each one, performing the necessary algebraic steps and explaining the reasoning behind each step.

Option 1: -r < 11

The first inequality we're looking at is -r < 11. To determine if this is equivalent to r > -11, we need to isolate r. Currently, r has a negative sign in front of it, which means we need to multiply both sides of the inequality by -1. Remember the golden rule we discussed earlier? When we multiply or divide by a negative number, we must flip the inequality sign.

So, let's multiply both sides of -r < 11 by -1:

(-1) * -r > 11 * (-1)

This simplifies to:

r > -11

Voila! We've arrived at the inequality r > -11, which is exactly what we were looking for. This means that -r < 11 is indeed equivalent to r > -11. Guys, this is a great example of how flipping the sign works, and it's crucial for solving these types of problems. This inequality holds true because it accurately represents the same solution set as our original inequality. Any value of r that satisfies r > -11 will also satisfy -r < 11, and vice versa. This equivalence is a key concept in understanding how inequalities behave under algebraic manipulation. So, we can confidently say that the first option is a match!

Option 2: 3r < -33

Next up, we have the inequality 3r < -33. To see if this is equivalent to r > -11, we again need to isolate r. This time, r is being multiplied by 3. To undo this multiplication, we need to divide both sides of the inequality by 3.

So, let's divide both sides of 3r < -33 by 3:

(3r) / 3 < (-33) / 3

This simplifies to:

r < -11

Notice that we divided by a positive number (3), so we did not need to flip the inequality sign. Now, let's compare this to our target inequality, r > -11. We have r < -11, which means r is less than -11. This is the exact opposite of r > -11, which means r is greater than -11. Therefore, 3r < -33 is not equivalent to r > -11. It's super important to pay attention to the direction of the inequality sign! This step-by-step approach helps us avoid mistakes and clearly see the relationship between the original and transformed inequalities.

Option 3: 3r > -33

Our third contender is 3r > -33. Just like with the previous inequality, we need to isolate r. Again, r is being multiplied by 3, so we'll divide both sides of the inequality by 3.

Let's divide both sides of 3r > -33 by 3:

(3r) / 3 > (-33) / 3

This simplifies to:

r > -11

Bingo! We've arrived back at our target inequality, r > -11. Since we divided by a positive number, we didn't need to flip the inequality sign. This means that 3r > -33 is indeed equivalent to r > -11. This is another example of how algebraic manipulation can reveal equivalencies between inequalities. By carefully performing each step, we can confidently confirm that this option is a match.

Option 4: -3r < 33

Finally, let's tackle the last inequality, -3r < 33. This one combines the elements we've seen in the previous examples. To isolate r, we need to divide both sides by -3. Remember our golden rule? We're dividing by a negative number, so we must flip the inequality sign.

Let's divide both sides of -3r < 33 by -3:

(-3r) / -3 > 33 / -3

This simplifies to:

r > -11

Excellent! We've once again arrived at r > -11. Because we divided by a negative number and flipped the inequality sign, we correctly transformed the inequality to match our target. This confirms that -3r < 33 is also equivalent to r > -11. This option reinforces the importance of the golden rule and how it applies when negative numbers are involved in the division or multiplication process.

Conclusion: Identifying Equivalent Inequalities

Alright, guys, we've made it through all the options! Let's recap what we've found. We started with the inequality r > -11 and examined four other inequalities to see which ones were equivalent. By carefully applying algebraic operations and remembering the crucial rule about flipping the inequality sign when multiplying or dividing by a negative number, we were able to identify the matches.

Here's a quick rundown of our findings:

  • -r < 11: Equivalent to r > -11
  • 3r < -33: Not equivalent to r > -11
  • 3r > -33: Equivalent to r > -11
  • -3r < 33: Equivalent to r > -11

So, the inequalities that are equivalent to r > -11 are -r < 11, 3r > -33, and -3r < 33. This exercise highlights the importance of understanding the rules of inequality manipulation and how each step affects the relationship between the variables.

Understanding the Problem: A Journey from 40 m/s to 58 m/s

Before we jump into graphing, let's make sure we fully grasp the scenario. We're dealing with a body that starts at an initial velocity of 40 m/s and steadily increases its speed to a final velocity of 58 m/s over a time interval of 4 seconds. The key here is the term "uniformly," which tells us that the acceleration is constant throughout this period. This means the velocity increases at a steady rate, making it easier to analyze and visualize. To truly understand uniform acceleration, we need to break down what it means. Acceleration, in its simplest form, is the rate of change of velocity. It tells us how quickly an object's velocity is changing over time. When we say an object is accelerating uniformly, we mean this change in velocity is happening at a constant rate. Drawing the Velocity-Time Graph: Visualizing Motion

Now for the exciting part: let's draw the velocity-time graph! A velocity-time graph is a powerful tool for visualizing motion. It plots velocity on the y-axis and time on the x-axis. The slope of the line on this graph represents the acceleration, and the area under the line represents the displacement (the change in position). To construct our graph:

  1. Draw the Axes: Draw a vertical y-axis representing velocity (in m/s) and a horizontal x-axis representing time (in seconds).
  2. Plot the Initial Point: At time t = 0 seconds, the velocity is 40 m/s. Plot this point (0, 40) on the graph.
  3. Plot the Final Point: At time t = 4 seconds, the velocity is 58 m/s. Plot this point (4, 58) on the graph.
  4. Draw the Line: Since the acceleration is uniform, we can draw a straight line connecting the initial and final points. This line represents the velocity of the body at any given time during the 4-second interval.

And there you have it! Your velocity-time graph should show a straight line sloping upwards, starting at (0, 40) and ending at (4, 58). This visual representation beautifully illustrates the body's uniform acceleration. The upward slope confirms that the velocity is increasing over time, and the straightness of the line confirms that the acceleration is constant. But the velocity-time graph is more than just a pretty picture; it's a treasure trove of information about the body's motion. Let's delve into some of the insights we can glean from this graph. First, as we mentioned earlier, the slope of the line directly corresponds to the acceleration. A steeper slope indicates a higher acceleration, meaning the velocity is changing more rapidly. In our case, the slope is 4.5 m/sÂ², which we calculated earlier. This is a visual confirmation of our numerical result. Secondly, the area under the line represents the displacement, which is the total change in position of the body during the time interval. To find the displacement, we can calculate the area of the trapezoid formed by the line, the x-axis, and the vertical lines at t=0 and t=4 seconds. This area can be calculated using the formula for the area of a trapezoid: Area = (1/2) * (sum of parallel sides) * (height). In our case, the parallel sides are the initial and final velocities (40 m/s and 58 m/s), and the height is the time interval (4 seconds). Plugging in the values, we get: Area = (1/2) * (40 + 58) * 4 = 196 meters. So, the body has moved 196 meters during these 4 seconds. The velocity-time graph provides a holistic view of the motion, encapsulating the velocity, acceleration, and displacement in a single visual representation. It's a powerful tool for understanding and analyzing motion in physics, and it helps us connect the abstract concepts to concrete visuals. Drawing the graph is not just a technical exercise; it's a way of making the physics come alive and revealing the underlying relationships between different aspects of motion. With our graph in hand, we can now confidently move on to further analysis and interpretation of the body's motion. We can use the graph to answer various questions, such as the velocity at any specific time, the time at which the body reaches a certain velocity, or the displacement during a specific time interval. The possibilities are endless, and the velocity-time graph is our key to unlocking these insights.

Analyzing the Graph: Insights into Motion

The beauty of the velocity-time graph lies not only in its visual representation but also in the wealth of information it provides. The area under the velocity-time graph, as we calculated earlier, represents the displacement. If we have the velocity-time graphs of two different bodies, we can easily compare their accelerations, initial velocities, and displacements by simply examining the graphs. In addition to these quantitative insights, the velocity-time graph also provides a qualitative understanding of the motion. This qualitative understanding is crucial for building intuition about motion and developing a deeper understanding of the underlying physics principles.

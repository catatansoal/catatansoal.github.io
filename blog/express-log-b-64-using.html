Express Log_b 64 Using Log_b 8 And Log_b 5

We'll be focusing on how to express a logarithmic expression in terms of other given logarithms. So, buckle up, and let's get started!

The Challenge: Expressing log_b 64

The core of our challenge lies in the ability to manipulate logarithmic expressions using the fundamental properties of logarithms. To kick things off, let's clearly state the problem we're tackling. We are given that $\log _b 8=W$ and $\log _b 5=Z$. Our mission, should we choose to accept it (and we do!), is to write the expression $\log _b 64$ in terms of W and Z. This involves breaking down 64 into its prime factors and then creatively using the properties of logarithms to express the original logarithm in the desired form. It's like a mathematical puzzle, and we're about to put our detective hats on to solve it. The properties of logarithms are our trusty tools in this quest. Remember the power rule, which states that $\log_b(x^p) = p \log_b(x)$, and the change of base rule, which allows us to convert logarithms from one base to another. These rules are key to manipulating and simplifying logarithmic expressions. We will primarily focus on the power rule in this particular problem. Understanding these properties inside and out is crucial for success in logarithmic manipulations. So, let's keep these rules in our back pockets as we move forward. We'll need them to unlock the secrets hidden within this problem. Prime Factorization: The Key to Unlocking 64

To effectively express $\log _b 64$ in terms of W and Z, we need to break down 64 into its prime factors. You might be thinking, “Why prime factors?” Well, prime factorization allows us to express a number as a product of its prime constituents. This is super useful because logarithms have this neat property where the logarithm of a product can be expressed as the sum of the logarithms of the individual factors. It’s like breaking down a complex problem into smaller, more manageable pieces. So, what are the prime factors of 64? If we start dividing 64 by the smallest prime number, 2, we quickly see that 64 is a power of 2. Specifically, 64 is 2 multiplied by itself six times (2 * 2 * 2 * 2 * 2 * 2). In mathematical notation, this is expressed as $64 = 2^6$. This prime factorization is the golden ticket that will allow us to connect $\log _b 64$ to the given logarithm, $\log _b 8$, since 8 is also a power of 2. The beauty of prime factorization lies in its ability to simplify complex expressions. By breaking down 64 into its fundamental building blocks, we create a pathway to apply the power rule of logarithms. This rule, as we mentioned earlier, lets us bring exponents outside the logarithm, turning powers into simple multiples. The prime factorization of 64 as $2^6$ is our stepping stone. With this knowledge in hand, we’re ready to take the next step: applying the properties of logarithms to rewrite the expression in a more convenient form. This is where the magic happens, guys! We'll see how the exponent in the prime factorization becomes a coefficient in our logarithmic expression. So, keep this prime factorization in your mind as we move forward. It's the key to unlocking the solution!

Applying Logarithmic Properties: The Power Rule in Action

Now that we've broken down 64 into its prime factors ($64 = 2^6$), it's time to wield the powerful properties of logarithms. Remember our mission? We want to express $\log _b 64$ in terms of W and Z, where $\log _b 8=W$ and $\log _b 5=Z$. The power rule of logarithms is our trusty tool here. This rule, which states that $\log_b(x^p) = p \log_b(x)$, is exactly what we need to simplify $\log _b 64$. Let's apply this rule to our expression. Since $64 = 2^6$, we can rewrite $\log _b 64$ as $\log _b (2^6)$. Now, the power rule kicks in! We can bring the exponent 6 outside the logarithm, transforming our expression into $6 \log _b 2$. This is a significant step forward. We've successfully simplified $\log _b 64$ into a multiple of $\_b 2$. But we're not quite there yet. We need to express this in terms of W and Z. Notice that we were given $\log _b 8=W$. How does this relate to $\_b 2$? Well, 8 is also a power of 2! Specifically, $8 = 2^3$. This connection is crucial. It allows us to bridge the gap between what we have ($\log _b 2$) and what we know ($\log _b 8$). The strategic use of logarithmic properties is like navigating a maze. Each rule we apply brings us closer to our destination. In this case, the power rule has allowed us to peel away the layers of the problem, revealing the underlying structure. Now, we're poised to make the final connection and express our logarithm in terms of W. The anticipation is building! So, let’s keep our momentum going and see how we can leverage the relationship between $\_b 2$ and $\log _b 8$ to complete our journey.

Connecting the Pieces: Relating log_b 2 to W

We've made some great progress! We know that $\log _b 64 = 6 \log _b 2$, and we're given that $\log _b 8 = W$. The key now is to find the connection between $\_b 2$ and $\log _b 8$. As we pointed out earlier, both 8 and 2 are powers of 2. Specifically, $8 = 2^3$. This relationship is our golden ticket to expressing $\_b 2$ in terms of W. Let's start with the given information: $\log _b 8 = W$. Since $8 = 2^3$, we can rewrite this as $\log _b (2^3) = W$. Now, let's bring out our trusty power rule of logarithms again! Applying the power rule, we get $3 \log _b 2 = W$. Aha! We're getting closer. We've successfully expressed a multiple of $\_b 2$ in terms of W. To isolate $\_b 2$, we simply need to divide both sides of the equation by 3. This gives us $\log _b 2 = \frac{W}{3}$. Fantastic! We've done it! We've found a direct relationship between $\_b 2$ and W. This is a crucial breakthrough. Now, we can substitute this expression back into our earlier equation for $\log _b 64$. This substitution will allow us to finally express $\log _b 64$ solely in terms of W. The feeling of connecting these mathematical dots is truly satisfying, isn't it? It’s like assembling the pieces of a puzzle and seeing the complete picture emerge. We’re almost at the finish line. Just one more step, and we'll have our final answer. So, let’s keep this connection—$\log _b 2 = \frac{W}{3}$—firmly in mind as we move on to the grand finale: expressing $\log _b 64$ in terms of W.

The Final Expression: log_b 64 in Terms of W

Okay, guys, the moment we've been working towards is finally here! We've diligently broken down the problem, applied logarithmic properties, and found the crucial connection between $\_b 2$ and W. Now, it's time to put it all together and express $\log _b 64$ in terms of W. Remember, we previously found that $\log _b 64 = 6 \log _b 2$, and we've just determined that $\log _b 2 = \fracW}{3}$. The final step is a simple substitution. We replace $\_b 2$ in the first equation with its equivalent expression in terms of W. This gives us $\log _b 64 = 6 \left( \frac{W3} \right)$. Now, a little bit of arithmetic, and we're done! We can simplify the right side of the equation $6 \left( \frac{W{3} \right) = \frac{6W}{3} = 2W$. Therefore, the final expression is: $\log _b 64 = 2W$. Ta-da! We've successfully expressed $\log _b 64$ in terms of W. We didn't even need Z in this particular problem!

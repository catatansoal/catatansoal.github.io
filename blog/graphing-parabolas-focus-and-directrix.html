Graphing Parabolas: Focus & Directrix Explained

Aug 1, 2025 by ADMIN 48 views

Hey guys! Today, we're diving into the fascinating world of parabolas, but not just any parabolas – we're tackling one that opens sideways! Specifically, we're going to graph the parabola defined by the equation x = - rac{1}{8}(y-3)^2 + 1, and, more importantly, we're going to pinpoint its focus and directrix. Now, I know those terms might sound a bit intimidating, but trust me, once we break it down, you'll see it's all quite manageable. So, grab your graph paper (or your favorite digital drawing tool), and let's get started!

Understanding the Parabola's Equation

Before we jump into graphing, let's really understand the equation we're working with: x = - rac{1}{8}(y-3)^2 + 1. This equation isn't in the standard form you might be used to seeing for parabolas that open upwards or downwards (where you'd have yyy isolated on one side). This one's a bit different – it's in the form x=a(y−k)2+hx = a(y-k)^2 + hx=a(y−k)2+h. Recognizing this form is crucial because it immediately tells us a few key things about our parabola.

First and foremost, the fact that the yyy term is squared, and xxx is isolated, indicates that this parabola opens either to the left or to the right. Think of it as a sideways version of the parabolas you're probably more familiar with. Next, the coefficient 'a' plays a significant role. In our case, a = - rac{1}{8}. The negative sign here is super important! It tells us that the parabola opens to the left. If 'a' were positive, it would open to the right. The absolute value of 'a' also affects how wide or narrow the parabola is; a smaller absolute value (like our 1/8) means a wider parabola, while a larger absolute value would result in a narrower one. Think about it – a smaller fraction is “gentler” in its effect, so the curve spreads out more gradually.

Finally, let's talk about the (h,k)(h, k)(h,k) part. These values give us the vertex of the parabola, which is the turning point or the “tip” of the curve. In our equation, we have (y−3)(y - 3)(y−3) inside the parentheses, which means k=3k = 3k=3, and we have +1+1+1 outside the parentheses, which means h=1h = 1h=1. So, our vertex is at the point (1,3)(1, 3)(1,3). This is our starting point for graphing the parabola. Understanding these components – the direction of opening, the width, and the vertex – is the foundation for accurately graphing the parabola and finding its focus and directrix. We’ve essentially decoded the equation, and now we can start visualizing it!

Graphing the Parabola

Okay, now that we've dissected the equation and know the key characteristics of our parabola, let's actually graph it! We know the vertex is at (1,3)(1, 3)(1,3), and we know the parabola opens to the left because the coefficient of the squared term is negative. So, let's start by plotting the vertex on our graph. This is our anchor point – everything else will be drawn relative to this.

Now, we need a few more points to sketch a reasonably accurate curve. A good way to find these points is to choose some yyy-values and plug them into our equation to find the corresponding xxx-values. Since the vertex is at y=3y = 3y=3, let's pick some yyy-values above and below 3 to get a good sense of the shape. For example, we could try y=1y = 1y=1, y=5y = 5y=5, y=−1y = -1y=−1, and y=7y = 7y=7. These values are symmetrically placed around the vertex's y-coordinate, which will give us a balanced view of the parabola.

Let's plug in y=1y = 1y=1 into our equation x = - rac{1}{8}(y-3)^2 + 1: x = - rac{1}{8}(1-3)^2 + 1 = - rac{1}{8}(-2)^2 + 1 = - rac{1}{8}(4) + 1 = - rac{1}{2} + 1 = rac{1}{2}. So, we have the point ( rac{1}{2}, 1). Let's do the same for y=5y = 5y=5: x = - rac{1}{8}(5-3)^2 + 1 = - rac{1}{8}(2)^2 + 1 = - rac{1}{8}(4) + 1 = - rac{1}{2} + 1 = rac{1}{2}. Notice that we got the same xxx-value, rac{1}{2}, for y=5y = 5y=5. This is expected because parabolas are symmetrical! So, we have the point ( rac{1}{2}, 5).

We can repeat this process for the other yyy-values. For y=−1y = -1y=−1: x = - rac{1}{8}(-1-3)^2 + 1 = - rac{1}{8}(-4)^2 + 1 = - rac{1}{8}(16) + 1 = -2 + 1 = -1. This gives us the point (−1,−1)(-1, -1)(−1,−1). And finally, for y=7y = 7y=7: x = - rac{1}{8}(7-3)^2 + 1 = - rac{1}{8}(4)^2 + 1 = - rac{1}{8}(16) + 1 = -2 + 1 = -1. Again, we get the same xxx-value, −1-1−1, for y=7y = 7y=7, confirming the symmetry. This gives us the point (−1,7)(-1, 7)(−1,7).

Now we have five points: the vertex (1,3)(1, 3)(1,3), ( rac{1}{2}, 1), ( rac{1}{2}, 5), (−1,−1)(-1, -1)(−1,−1), and (−1,7)(-1, 7)(−1,7). Plot these points on your graph. You'll see they form a clear U-shape opening to the left. Now, carefully sketch the parabola through these points, making sure it's a smooth, curved line. Remember, the parabola extends infinitely in both directions, so don't stop at the points you've plotted. You've now successfully graphed the parabola! But we're not done yet – we still need to find the focus and directrix. This is where things get even more interesting!

Finding the Focus

Okay, guys, we've got our parabola graphed, which is a huge step! Now, let's tackle the focus. The focus is a special point inside the parabola that has a very specific relationship to the curve. It's one of the defining characteristics of a parabola, and it helps us understand its shape and properties. Think of it like the heart of the parabola.

To find the focus, we need to understand the relationship between the coefficient 'a' in our equation and the distance between the vertex and the focus. Remember our equation form: x=a(y−k)2+hx = a(y-k)^2 + hx=a(y−k)2+h? The key here is the absolute value of the distance, which we'll call 'p', between the vertex and the focus (and also between the vertex and the directrix, as we'll see shortly). This distance 'p' is related to 'a' by the formula: p = rac{1}{4|a|}.

In our equation, a = - rac{1}{8}, so |a| = rac{1}{8}. Let's plug this into our formula: p = rac{1}{4 imes rac{1}{8}} = rac{1}{ rac{1}{2}} = 2. So, the distance between the vertex and the focus is 2 units. Now, the tricky part is figuring out which direction to move from the vertex to find the focus. Since our parabola opens to the left, the focus will be located inside the curve, which means it will be 2 units to the left of the vertex.

Our vertex is at (1,3)(1, 3)(1,3). To move 2 units to the left, we subtract 2 from the xxx-coordinate: 1−2=−11 - 2 = -11−2=−1. The yyy-coordinate stays the same. So, the focus of our parabola is located at the point (−1,3)(-1, 3)(−1,3). Mark this point clearly on your graph – it's a crucial part of our parabola's anatomy. You can even label it as “F” for focus. We're one step closer to completely understanding this parabola!

Determining the Directrix

Alright, we've conquered the focus! Now, let's move on to the directrix. The directrix is another crucial element in defining a parabola, and it has a very special relationship with both the focus and the points on the parabola itself. The directrix is a line (not a point like the focus), and it sits outside the curve of the parabola.

The defining property of a parabola is that for any point on the parabola, the distance to the focus is equal to the distance to the directrix. This might sound a bit abstract, but it’s the fundamental principle that shapes the curve of the parabola. Just like the focus, the directrix is located a distance 'p' away from the vertex. We already calculated 'p' to be 2 units when we found the focus.

However, unlike the focus, which is inside the curve, the directrix is outside the curve. Since our parabola opens to the left, the directrix will be a vertical line located 2 units to the right of the vertex. Remember, the vertex is at (1,3)(1, 3)(1,3). To move 2 units to the right, we add 2 to the xxx-coordinate: 1+2=31 + 2 = 31+2=3. This means the directrix is a vertical line that passes through x=3x = 3x=3.

So, the equation of our directrix is simply x=3x = 3x=3. Draw a vertical line on your graph at x=3x = 3x=3.

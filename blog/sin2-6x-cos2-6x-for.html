Sin^2(6x) + Cos^2(6x) For All Real Numbers X Explained

Jul 13, 2025 by ADMIN 55 views

Hey guys! Let's dive into a super interesting trigonometric problem today. We're going to break down the expression $\sin ^2(6 x)+\cos ^2(6 x)$ for all real numbers x. This might look intimidating at first, but trust me, it's way simpler than you think. We'll go through each step, explain the concepts, and make sure you understand why the answer is what it is. So, buckle up, and let’s get started!

Understanding the Core Trigonometric Identity

At the heart of this problem lies a fundamental trigonometric identity. This identity is the cornerstone of many trigonometric simplifications and is super important to remember. So, what exactly is this magical identity? It's none other than the Pythagorean trigonometric identity: $\sin^2(\theta) + \cos^2(\theta) = 1$.

Now, why is this identity so crucial? Well, it stems directly from the Pythagorean theorem applied to the unit circle. Imagine a circle with a radius of 1 (that’s why it's called a unit circle). Pick any point on the circle. The x-coordinate of that point corresponds to the cosine of the angle formed between the positive x-axis and the line connecting the origin to your chosen point. Similarly, the y-coordinate corresponds to the sine of that angle. If you draw a right-angled triangle using these coordinates, the radius of the circle (which is 1) becomes the hypotenuse. Applying the Pythagorean theorem (a² + b² = c²), where a is the sine, b is the cosine, and c is the radius (1), you get our identity: $\sin^2(\theta) + \cos^2(\theta) = 1$.

The beauty of this identity is its universality. It holds true for any angle $\theta$. Whether $\theta$ is a simple 30 degrees, a complex expression like 6x, or even something more exotic, the relationship between the square of sine and the square of cosine will always sum up to 1. This makes it an incredibly powerful tool for simplifying trigonometric expressions. You'll find it popping up in all sorts of problems, from basic equation solving to more advanced calculus. So, make sure you've got this one locked down!

In the context of our specific problem, this identity is the key. We need to recognize how it applies to the given expression. Think of 6x as our $\theta$. If we can make that connection, the rest becomes straightforward. We'll explore that in the next section, so stay tuned!

Applying the Identity to the Problem

Okay, now that we've refreshed our understanding of the core trigonometric identity, let's apply it directly to our problem. We have the expression $\sin ^2(6 x)+\cos ^2(6 x)$, and we need to figure out what it equals. Remember our identity? $\sin^2(\theta) + \cos^2(\theta) = 1$. The crucial step here is to recognize the pattern. Notice how the argument inside the sine and cosine functions is the same in both terms? That's the key! In our case, the argument is 6x. So, we can directly substitute 6x for $\theta$ in our identity. This gives us: $\sin^2(6x) + \cos^2(6x) = 1$.

And that's it! The expression $\sin ^2(6 x)+\cos ^2(6 x)$ simplifies to 1. It doesn't matter what the value of x is. Whether x is 0, a million, or a tiny fraction, the identity holds. The sine squared of 6x plus the cosine squared of 6x will always equal 1. This is because the trigonometric identity $\sin^2(\theta) + \cos^2(\theta) = 1$ is a fundamental property of trigonometric functions, derived directly from the Pythagorean theorem and the unit circle definition. The argument, in this case 6x, acts as a placeholder. As long as the argument is the same for both the sine and cosine functions, the identity applies.

This principle is incredibly useful in simplifying complex trigonometric expressions. Imagine if we had a more complicated problem involving this expression. We could immediately replace $\sin ^2(6 x)+\cos ^2(6 x)$ with 1, making the problem much easier to handle. This kind of simplification is a common technique in trigonometry, and mastering it will save you a lot of time and effort. Furthermore, recognizing these fundamental identities allows us to manipulate and solve more complex trigonometric equations. We can use them to rewrite expressions in different forms, isolate variables, and ultimately find solutions. So, understanding this simple application is the foundation for tackling more challenging problems down the road.

So, to recap, the key takeaway here is the direct application of the Pythagorean trigonometric identity. By recognizing the pattern and substituting the appropriate argument, we can quickly and easily simplify the expression. This highlights the importance of knowing your trigonometric identities inside and out. They are the building blocks of trigonometric problem-solving!

Why the Other Options Are Incorrect

Now that we've established that the answer is 1, let's quickly address why the other options are incorrect. This is a valuable exercise because it helps solidify our understanding and prevents us from making similar mistakes in the future. We'll go through each option one by one and explain the reasoning.

  • **A. $\sin(12x)$: ** This option is incorrect because it confuses the Pythagorean identity with a different trigonometric identity, specifically the double-angle identity. While there are identities for $\\sin(2\theta)$, they don't directly apply to the sum of squares of sine and cosine. The double-angle identity for sine is $\sin(2\theta) = 2\sin(\theta)\cos(\theta)$. Notice that this involves the product of sine and cosine, not the sum of their squares. So, blindly applying a double-angle formula here is a common mistake that we can now avoid. The core difference lies in the fundamental identity we discussed – $\sin^2(\theta) + \cos^2(\theta) = 1$, which has no equivalent form that directly simplifies to a sine of a doubled angle.

  • B. 0: This is wrong because, as we've shown, the sum of the squares of sine and cosine with the same argument always equals 1. Zero would only be a possibility if either $\sin^2(6x)$ or $\cos^2(6x)$ were zero while the other was also zero, which is never the case for any real x. Remember, both sine and cosine functions oscillate between -1 and 1. Their squares will therefore range between 0 and 1. The only time both sine and cosine could potentially be zero simultaneously is if they didn't share the same argument (e.g., $\sin(x)$ and $\cos(x + \pi/2)$). But in our problem, they have the same argument (6x), so this scenario is impossible.

  • C. 6: This option is incorrect because it seemingly pulls a number out of thin air. There's no operation or trigonometric principle that would transform $\sin^2(6x) + \cos^2(6x)$ into the number 6. This highlights the importance of sticking to established identities and rules. Trigonometry isn't about guessing numbers; it's about applying mathematical principles to simplify and solve problems. If you ever find yourself arriving at a number without a clear justification, it's a big red flag to re-examine your steps.

  • **D. $\cos(12x)$: ** Similar to option A, this one also misapplies trigonometric identities. While there is a double-angle identity for cosine, it's $\cos(2\theta) = \cos^2(\theta) - \sin^2(\theta)$ or its equivalent forms. Notice the subtraction sign between the squared terms, which is fundamentally different from the addition in our problem. The presence of $\sin^2(6x) + \cos^2(6x)$ should immediately trigger the Pythagorean identity in your mind, not a double-angle formula for cosine. Trying to force a double-angle identity here leads to an incorrect simplification.

By carefully analyzing why each incorrect option fails, we reinforce our understanding of the correct solution and the underlying principles. This process of elimination and critical thinking is a valuable skill in mathematics and problem-solving in general.

Conclusion: Mastering the Basics

The key was recognizing and applying the fundamental Pythagorean trigonometric identity: $\sin^2(\theta) + \cos^2(\theta) = 1$. We saw how this identity stems from the Pythagorean theorem and the unit circle, making it a cornerstone of trigonometry. By understanding this identity, we could quickly simplify the expression and arrive at the correct answer.

We also took the time to dissect why the other options were incorrect. This step is crucial for solidifying our understanding and avoiding common pitfalls. We learned to distinguish between the Pythagorean identity and other trigonometric identities, like the double-angle formulas. We also emphasized the importance of sticking to established principles and avoiding arbitrary guesses.

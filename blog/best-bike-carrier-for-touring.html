Exploring the Different Types of Bike Carriers

Now, let's dive into the various types of bike carriers available and see which ones are best suited for transporting touring bikes with racks. There are three main categories: hitch-mounted carriers, roof-mounted carriers, and trunk-mounted carriers. Each type has its own set of advantages and disadvantages, so let's break them down.

Hitch-Mounted Carriers

Hitch-mounted carriers are generally considered the most robust and convenient option for transporting heavier bikes, like touring bikes with racks. These carriers attach to a receiver hitch on the back of your vehicle and come in two main styles: platform and hanging. Platform hitch carriers are the champions for touring bikes. These carriers feature a platform where the bikes sit upright, secured by straps or clamps that attach to the wheels and frame. This design distributes the weight evenly and provides excellent stability, making them ideal for heavier bikes with racks. Plus, platform carriers minimize bike-to-bike contact, reducing the risk of scratches and dings. We've found that platform carriers make loading and unloading a breeze, especially when dealing with multiple bikes. Just roll your bike onto the platform, secure the straps, and you're good to go!

Hanging hitch carriers, on the other hand, suspend the bikes from their top tubes. While they can be a more economical option, they're generally not recommended for touring bikes with racks. The weight of the bikes can put stress on the frame, and the hanging design can lead to bikes swaying and potentially colliding during transit. Plus, the frame contact points can sometimes scratch the paint. While hanging carriers might work for lighter bikes, we'd steer clear of them for your loaded touring setups. For those of you with step-through frames or alternative frame designs, you may need to purchase an adapter bar to use a hanging hitch carrier, which adds another layer of complexity.

Advantages of Hitch-Mounted Carriers

  • Stability and Security: Hitch-mounted carriers provide excellent stability and security for your bikes, minimizing the risk of damage during transport.
  • Weight Capacity: They typically have a higher weight capacity than other types of carriers, making them suitable for heavier touring bikes with racks.
  • Ease of Use: Loading and unloading bikes is generally easier with hitch-mounted carriers, especially platform models.
  • Minimal Bike Contact: Platform carriers minimize bike-to-bike contact, reducing the risk of scratches and dings.

Disadvantages of Hitch-Mounted Carriers

  • Cost: Hitch-mounted carriers can be more expensive than other options.
  • Hitch Requirement: You'll need a receiver hitch installed on your vehicle, which can add to the overall cost.
  • Storage: Hitch-mounted carriers can be bulky and require storage space when not in use.

Roof-Mounted Carriers

Roof-mounted carriers attach to the roof rack of your vehicle and come in two main styles: fork-mount and frame-mount. Fork-mount carriers require you to remove the front wheel of your bike and attach the fork to the carrier. While they offer a secure hold, they're not ideal for touring bikes with racks, as the added weight and bulk can make lifting the bike onto the roof challenging. Plus, you'll need to find a place to store the front wheel during transport.

Frame-mount carriers, on the other hand, secure the bike by clamping onto the frame. These carriers can be used with touring bikes, but it's crucial to ensure that the carrier has a high enough weight capacity and that the clamps are compatible with your bike's frame. Lifting a fully loaded touring bike onto the roof can be a real workout, so consider your physical strength and the height of your vehicle before opting for a roof-mounted carrier. We've found that roof-mounted carriers can also impact your vehicle's fuel economy and create wind noise, especially on long drives.

Advantages of Roof-Mounted Carriers

  • Versatility: Roof-mounted carriers can be used with a variety of vehicles and bike types.
  • Security: They offer a secure hold for your bikes.
  • Clear Visibility: They don't obstruct your rear view.

Disadvantages of Roof-Mounted Carriers

  • Lifting Required: Lifting bikes onto the roof can be challenging, especially for heavier touring bikes.
  • Height Restrictions: You'll need to be mindful of height restrictions when driving under bridges and in parking garages.
  • Fuel Economy: Roof-mounted carriers can impact your vehicle's fuel economy.
  • Wind Noise: They can create wind noise, especially at higher speeds.

Trunk-Mounted Carriers

Trunk-mounted carriers attach to the trunk or hatchback of your vehicle using straps and hooks. While they're often the most affordable option, they're generally not recommended for transporting touring bikes with racks. Trunk-mounted carriers can be less stable than hitch-mounted or roof-mounted carriers, and the straps can put pressure on your vehicle's paint. Plus, the weight of touring bikes with racks can exceed the weight capacity of many trunk-mounted carriers. We've also found that trunk-mounted carriers can make it difficult to access your trunk or hatchback while the bikes are loaded. So, while they might be tempting due to their lower price point, trunk-mounted carriers are usually not the best choice for touring bikes.

Advantages of Trunk-Mounted Carriers

  • Cost: Trunk-mounted carriers are often the most affordable option.
  • Portability: They're lightweight and easy to store.

Disadvantages of Trunk-Mounted Carriers

  • Stability: They can be less stable than other types of carriers.
  • Weight Capacity: They typically have a lower weight capacity.
  • Vehicle Damage: Straps can put pressure on your vehicle's paint.
  • Trunk Access: They can make it difficult to access your trunk or hatchback.

Key Considerations for Choosing a Carrier for Touring Bikes

Okay, so we've covered the different types of carriers. Now, let's zero in on the key factors you need to consider when choosing the best carrier for your touring bikes with racks.

Weight Capacity

This is the big one, guys! The first and foremost consideration is weight capacity. You need to ensure that the carrier can handle the combined weight of your bikes, racks, and any gear you might be carrying on them. Touring bikes, especially when fully loaded, can be significantly heavier than standard bikes. So, check the manufacturer's specifications carefully and err on the side of caution. It's always better to have a carrier with a higher weight capacity than you think you need. Remember, exceeding the weight limit can compromise the carrier's stability and put your bikes at risk. We've seen carriers buckle under the pressure of overloaded bikes, and it's not a pretty sight. So, do your homework and make sure your carrier is up to the task.

Bike Compatibility

Next up, you need to make sure the carrier is compatible with your bike's frame geometry and rack setup. Some carriers are designed for specific types of bikes, while others are more versatile. If you have a unique frame design or oversized tubing, you'll want to choose a carrier that can accommodate it. Pay close attention to the carrier's clamping mechanism and make sure it won't damage your bike's frame or interfere with your racks. We've learned the hard way that not all carriers are created equal when it comes to bike compatibility. It's worth taking the time to research and find a carrier that's a good fit for your specific bikes. Some carriers have adjustable arms and straps that can be customized to fit different bike sizes and shapes, which is a definite plus.

Ease of Loading and Unloading

Trust us, you'll appreciate a carrier that's easy to load and unload, especially after a long day of riding. Look for features like adjustable straps, quick-release mechanisms, and a low platform height. The less time you spend wrestling with your bikes, the more time you have to relax and enjoy your adventure. Security Features

Bike theft is a serious concern, so security features are a must-have. Look for carriers with integrated locking mechanisms that secure your bikes to the carrier and the carrier to your vehicle. A good locking system will deter thieves and give you peace of mind, especially when you're making pit stops along the way. We always recommend using a combination of locks, including a cable lock and a U-lock, for added security. It's better to be safe than sorry when it comes to protecting your valuable bikes. Think of it as an insurance policy against theft. A little extra security can go a long way in preventing a major headache.

Durability and Build Quality

As we mentioned earlier, durability is key for a long-lasting carrier. Choose a carrier made from high-quality materials that can withstand the elements and the rigors of travel. Tips for Loading and Transporting Touring Bikes

Before we wrap things up, here are a few extra tips for loading and transporting your touring bikes safely and efficiently:

  • Distribute the Weight Evenly: When loading your bikes onto the carrier, try to distribute the weight evenly to maintain balance and stability.
  • Secure All Straps and Clamps: Double-check that all straps and clamps are securely fastened before hitting the road.
  • Remove Accessories: Remove any loose accessories, like water bottles and panniers, to prevent them from falling off during transit.
  • Check Tire Pressure: Make sure your tires are properly inflated before loading your bikes onto the carrier.
  • Drive Carefully: Drive cautiously, especially when cornering and braking, and be mindful of the added length and weight of your vehicle.

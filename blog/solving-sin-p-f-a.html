Solving Sin(π-φ) A Step-by-Step Trigonometric Guide

This is a classic problem that pops up in trigonometry and calculus, so understanding it thoroughly is super important. Understanding the Sine Function and Trigonometric Identities

Before we jump into the problem, let's refresh our understanding of the sine function and some fundamental trigonometric identities. Remember, the sine function, often written as sin(x), relates an angle in a right-angled triangle to the ratio of the length of the opposite side to the length of the hypotenuse. This is the basic definition, but it extends beyond right-angled triangles when we consider the unit circle. The unit circle is a circle with a radius of 1 centered at the origin of a coordinate plane. For any angle θ\thetaθ, the point where the terminal side of the angle intersects the unit circle has coordinates (cos(θ\thetaθ), sin(θ\thetaθ)). This gives us a visual and more general understanding of sine and cosine for any angle.

Now, let's talk about trigonometric identities. These are equations that are true for all values of the variables for which the expressions are defined. They are like the secret weapons of trigonometry, allowing us to manipulate and simplify expressions. One of the most important identities we'll use today is the sine of a difference identity: sin⁡(A−B)=sin⁡(A)cos⁡(B)−cos⁡(A)sin⁡(B)\sin(A - B) = \sin(A)\cos(B) - \cos(A)\sin(B)sin(A−B)=sin(A)cos(B)−cos(A)sin(B). This identity is crucial for expanding and simplifying expressions involving the sine of the difference of two angles. Another identity we should keep in mind is the values of sine and cosine for specific angles, particularly π\piπ (180 degrees). We know that sin⁡(π)=0\sin(\pi) = 0sin(π)=0 and cos⁡(π)=−1\cos(\pi) = -1cos(π)=−1. These values will be instrumental in solving our problem.

Understanding these concepts – the definition of the sine function, the unit circle, and key trigonometric identities – is the foundation for tackling more complex trigonometric problems. They provide us with the tools and the framework to analyze and simplify expressions. Think of them as the building blocks of trigonometry, each one essential for constructing a solid understanding. So, let's keep these ideas in mind as we move forward and solve the problem at hand.

Solving for sin⁡(π−ϕ)\sin(\pi-\phi)sin(π−ϕ)

Alright, let's get to the heart of the matter! We're trying to figure out what sin⁡(π−ϕ)\sin(\pi-\phi)sin(π−ϕ) is equal to. Remember that handy sine of a difference identity we talked about earlier? That's going to be our key to unlocking this problem. The identity states that sin⁡(A−B)=sin⁡(A)cos⁡(B)−cos⁡(A)sin⁡(B)\sin(A - B) = \sin(A)\cos(B) - \cos(A)\sin(B)sin(A−B)=sin(A)cos(B)−cos(A)sin(B). In our case, we can think of A as π\piπ and B as ϕ\phiϕ. So, let's plug those values into the identity:

sin⁡(π−ϕ)=sin⁡(π)cos⁡(ϕ)−cos⁡(π)sin⁡(ϕ)\sin(\pi - \phi) = \sin(\pi)\cos(\phi) - \cos(\pi)\sin(\phi)sin(π−ϕ)=sin(π)cos(ϕ)−cos(π)sin(ϕ)

Now, this is where knowing the values of sin⁡(π)\sin(\pi)sin(π) and cos⁡(π)\cos(\pi)cos(π) comes in super handy. We know that sin⁡(π)=0\sin(\pi) = 0sin(π)=0 and cos⁡(π)=−1\cos(\pi) = -1cos(π)=−1. Let's substitute those values into our equation:

sin⁡(π−ϕ)=(0)cos⁡(ϕ)−(−1)sin⁡(ϕ)\sin(\pi - \phi) = (0)\cos(\phi) - (-1)\sin(\phi)sin(π−ϕ)=(0)cos(ϕ)−(−1)sin(ϕ)

See how things are simplifying nicely? The first term, (0)cos(ϕ\phiϕ), is simply 0. And the second term, -(-1)sin(ϕ\phiϕ), becomes +sin(ϕ\phiϕ). So, our equation now looks like this:

sin⁡(π−ϕ)=0+sin⁡(ϕ)\sin(\pi - \phi) = 0 + \sin(\phi)sin(π−ϕ)=0+sin(ϕ)

Which simplifies to:

sin⁡(π−ϕ)=sin⁡(ϕ)\sin(\pi - \phi) = \sin(\phi)sin(π−ϕ)=sin(ϕ)

And there you have it! We've successfully shown that sin⁡(π−ϕ)\sin(\pi - \phi)sin(π−ϕ) is equal to sin⁡(ϕ)\sin(\phi)sin(ϕ). This is a pretty neat result, and it highlights the power of using trigonometric identities to simplify expressions. This identity tells us that the sine of an angle subtracted from π\piπ is equal to the sine of the original angle. This makes sense when we think about the symmetry of the sine function around the y-axis.

Analyzing the Answer Choices

Scanning through the options, it's clear that option E, sin⁡(ϕ)\sin(\phi)sin(ϕ), is the one that matches our result. The other options involve cosine functions or negative sine functions, which we didn't arrive at in our simplification. So, we can confidently eliminate options A, B, C, and D.

To be extra sure, we could quickly analyze the other options to see why they are incorrect. For instance, option A, cos⁡(π+ϕ)\cos(\pi+\phi)cos(π+ϕ), would simplify to −cos⁡(ϕ)-\cos(\phi)−cos(ϕ) using the cosine of a sum identity. Option B, −sin⁡(ϕ)-\sin(\phi)−sin(ϕ), is the negative of our result. Option C, −cos⁡(ϕ)-\cos(\phi)−cos(ϕ), is the negative of the cosine of ϕ\phiϕ. And option D, cos⁡(ϕ)\cos(\phi)cos(ϕ), is simply the cosine of ϕ\phiϕ, which is different from the sine of ϕ\phiϕ.

By systematically solving the problem and then comparing our result to the answer choices, we've not only found the correct answer but also reinforced our understanding of why the other options are incorrect. Why E is the Correct Answer and Others Aren't

Let's dive a little deeper into why option E, sin⁡(ϕ)\sin(\phi)sin(ϕ), is the correct answer and why the other options don't hold up. This will solidify our understanding and help us tackle similar problems in the future. As we've already shown, using the sine of a difference identity, we can expand sin⁡(π−ϕ)\sin(\pi - \phi)sin(π−ϕ) as follows:

sin⁡(π−ϕ)=sin⁡(π)cos⁡(ϕ)−cos⁡(π)sin⁡(ϕ)\sin(\pi - \phi) = \sin(\pi)\cos(\phi) - \cos(\pi)\sin(\phi)sin(π−ϕ)=sin(π)cos(ϕ)−cos(π)sin(ϕ)

Since sin⁡(π)=0\sin(\pi) = 0sin(π)=0 and cos⁡(π)=−1\cos(\pi) = -1cos(π)=−1, this simplifies to:

sin⁡(π−ϕ)=(0)cos⁡(ϕ)−(−1)sin⁡(ϕ)=sin⁡(ϕ)\sin(\pi - \phi) = (0)\cos(\phi) - (-1)\sin(\phi) = \sin(\phi)sin(π−ϕ)=(0)cos(ϕ)−(−1)sin(ϕ)=sin(ϕ)

This confirms that sin⁡(π−ϕ)\sin(\pi - \phi)sin(π−ϕ) is indeed equal to sin⁡(ϕ)\sin(\phi)sin(ϕ), making option E the correct choice. Now, let's break down why the other options are incorrect:

  • Option A: cos⁡(π+ϕ)\cos(\pi+\phi)cos(π+ϕ)

    To analyze this, we can use the cosine of a sum identity: cos⁡(A+B)=cos⁡(A)cos⁡(B)−sin⁡(A)sin⁡(B)\cos(A + B) = \cos(A)\cos(B) - \sin(A)\sin(B)cos(A+B)=cos(A)cos(B)−sin(A)sin(B). Applying this to cos⁡(π+ϕ)\cos(\pi + \phi)cos(π+ϕ), we get:

    cos⁡(π+ϕ)=cos⁡(π)cos⁡(ϕ)−sin⁡(π)sin⁡(ϕ)\cos(\pi + \phi) = \cos(\pi)\cos(\phi) - \sin(\pi)\sin(\phi)cos(π+ϕ)=cos(π)cos(ϕ)−sin(π)sin(ϕ)

    Since cos⁡(π)=−1\cos(\pi) = -1cos(π)=−1 and sin⁡(π)=0\sin(\pi) = 0sin(π)=0, this simplifies to:

    cos⁡(π+ϕ)=(−1)cos⁡(ϕ)−(0)sin⁡(ϕ)=−cos⁡(ϕ)\cos(\pi + \phi) = (-1)\cos(\phi) - (0)\sin(\phi) = -\cos(\phi)cos(π+ϕ)=(−1)cos(ϕ)−(0)sin(ϕ)=−cos(ϕ)

    So, cos⁡(π+ϕ)\cos(\pi + \phi)cos(π+ϕ) is equal to −cos⁡(ϕ)-\cos(\phi)−cos(ϕ), not sin⁡(ϕ)\sin(\phi)sin(ϕ).

  • Option B: −sin⁡(ϕ)-\sin(\phi)−sin(ϕ)

    This is simply the negative of sin⁡(ϕ)\sin(\phi)sin(ϕ). Our result clearly shows that sin⁡(π−ϕ)\sin(\pi - \phi)sin(π−ϕ) is equal to sin⁡(ϕ)\sin(\phi)sin(ϕ) itself, not its negative.

  • Option C: −cos⁡(ϕ)-\cos(\phi)−cos(ϕ)

    As we saw when analyzing option A, −cos⁡(ϕ)-\cos(\phi)−cos(ϕ) is equal to cos⁡(π+ϕ)\cos(\pi + \phi)cos(π+ϕ), not sin⁡(π−ϕ)\sin(\pi - \phi)sin(π−ϕ).

  • Option D: cos⁡(ϕ)\cos(\phi)cos(ϕ)

    There's no direct trigonometric identity that transforms sin⁡(π−ϕ)\sin(\pi - \phi)sin(π−ϕ) into cos⁡(ϕ)\cos(\phi)cos(ϕ). The sine and cosine functions are related, but they are not directly interchangeable in this context.

By carefully applying trigonometric identities and evaluating the values of sine and cosine at π\piπ, we can confidently say that option E is the only correct answer. This detailed analysis not only confirms our solution but also enhances our understanding of trigonometric relationships.

Cube Side Lengths: Solving For The Difference

Aug 2, 2025 by ADMIN 46 views

Hey guys! Today, we're diving into a cool mathematical problem involving cubes and their surface areas. We'll be using the formula s = √ (SA / 6), which relates the side length (s) of a cube to its surface area (SA). Our mission? To figure out how much longer the side of a cube is when its surface area is 180 square meters compared to a cube with a surface area of 120 square meters. Buckle up, it's gonna be a fun ride!

Understanding the Formula: s = √ (SA / 6)

Before we jump into the calculations, let's break down the formula s = √ (SA / 6). This formula is the key to unlocking the side length of a cube given its surface area. But where does this formula come from? Well, let's think about it. A cube has six faces, and each face is a square. If we let 's' represent the side length of the cube, then the area of one face is simply s². Since there are six faces, the total surface area (SA) of the cube is 6s². Now, if we want to find the side length (s) given the surface area (SA), we need to rearrange this formula. Dividing both sides of SA = 6s² by 6 gives us SA / 6 = s². To isolate 's', we take the square root of both sides, resulting in s = √ (SA / 6). See? It's not just a random formula; it's rooted in the geometry of a cube! This formula is a powerful tool in understanding the relationship between a cube's surface area and its side length. It allows us to easily calculate the side length if we know the surface area, and vice versa. Think about it: if you're designing a box and you know how much material you have (which relates to the surface area), you can use this formula to determine the maximum size of the box (the side length). Or, if you have a specific cube in mind and want to know how much wrapping paper you'll need, you can calculate the surface area from the side length. Understanding the formula's origin not only helps us remember it better but also empowers us to apply it in various practical scenarios. So, next time you see a cube, remember this formula – it's a little piece of mathematical magic that connects the surface area and the side length in a beautiful way. Now that we've got a solid grasp of the formula, let's put it to work and solve our problem!

Calculating the Side Lengths: A Step-by-Step Approach

Okay, now for the exciting part: the calculations! We have two cubes, one with a surface area of 180 square meters and another with a surface area of 120 square meters. Our goal is to find the side length of each cube using our trusty formula s = √ (SA / 6). Let's start with the cube that has a surface area of 180 square meters. We'll plug 180 into our formula for SA: s = √ (180 / 6). First, we simplify the fraction inside the square root: 180 / 6 = 30. So, now we have s = √ 30. Using a calculator, we find that √ 30 is approximately 5.48 meters. This means that the side length of the cube with a surface area of 180 square meters is roughly 5.48 meters. Now, let's tackle the cube with a surface area of 120 square meters. We follow the same steps, plugging 120 into our formula: s = √ (120 / 6). Again, we simplify the fraction: 120 / 6 = 20. So, we have s = √ 20. Using a calculator, we find that √ 20 is approximately 4.47 meters. This tells us that the side length of the cube with a surface area of 120 square meters is about 4.47 meters. We've now successfully calculated the side lengths of both cubes! But we're not quite done yet. The original question asked us to find the difference in side lengths, not just the individual side lengths. This is a crucial step in problem-solving – always make sure you're answering the question that was actually asked! So, what's next? We need to find the difference between 5.48 meters and 4.47 meters. This is a simple subtraction problem, and it will give us the answer we're looking for. This step-by-step approach is key to solving many mathematical problems. By breaking down the problem into smaller, manageable steps, we can avoid confusion and ensure we arrive at the correct answer. So, let's move on to the final step and find the difference in side lengths!

Finding the Difference: The Final Piece of the Puzzle

Alright, we're in the home stretch now! We've calculated the side length of the cube with a surface area of 180 square meters (approximately 5.48 meters) and the side length of the cube with a surface area of 120 square meters (approximately 4.47 meters). Now, to find out how much longer the side of the first cube is compared to the second, we simply subtract the smaller side length from the larger side length. So, we're doing the calculation 5. 48 meters - 4.47 meters. This gives us a difference of 1.01 meters. That's it! We've found our answer. The side of the cube with a surface area of 180 square meters is approximately 1.01 meters longer than the side of the cube with a surface area of 120 square meters. You see, by carefully applying the formula s = √ (SA / 6) and following a logical step-by-step approach, we were able to solve this problem with ease.

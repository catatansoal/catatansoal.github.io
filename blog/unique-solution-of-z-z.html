Unique Solution Of Z|z-1| = 20 + 20i: A Step-by-Step Guide

Whether you're a seasoned mathematician or just starting your exploration of complex numbers, this comprehensive guide will provide you with the insights and tools you need to tackle this problem with confidence.

Transforming the Complex Equation into a Real System

The first step in cracking this complex number puzzle is to transform it into a more manageable form. We'll do this by expressing the complex variable z in terms of its real and imaginary parts. Let's set z = x + iy, where x and y are real numbers. This simple substitution opens up a world of possibilities, allowing us to work with real numbers and familiar algebraic techniques.

Now, let’s substitute z = x + iy into our original equation, z|z-1| = 20 + 20i. This gives us (x + iy)|(x - 1) + iy| = 20 + 20i. To deal with the absolute value, we recall that the magnitude of a complex number a + bi is given by √(a² + b²). Applying this to our equation, we get:

(x + iy)√((x - 1)² + y²) = 20 + 20i

This equation looks a bit intimidating, but don't worry! We're going to break it down further. We have a complex number on the left side and a complex number on the right side. For these two complex numbers to be equal, their real and imaginary parts must be equal. This crucial insight allows us to separate our single complex equation into a system of two real equations. This is a classic technique in complex number problems, and it's super useful for simplifying things.

Let's express the square root term as r = √((x - 1)² + y²), where r represents the magnitude of the complex number z - 1. Now our equation becomes (x + iy)r = 20 + 20i. Expanding the left side, we get xr + iyr = 20 + 20i. Now, we can equate the real and imaginary parts:

  • Equation 1 (Real part): xr = 20
  • Equation 2 (Imaginary part): yr = 20

We've successfully transformed our single complex equation into a system of two real equations! This is a major step forward. Now, we have a system of equations that we can work with using familiar algebraic methods. The key here was recognizing that equating complex numbers requires equating their real and imaginary parts separately. This technique is fundamental in complex number manipulations and will come in handy time and time again.

Diving Deeper: Solving the System of Equations

Now that we have our system of real equations, xr = 20 and yr = 20, let's dive deeper and see how we can solve for x and y. Remember, r = √((x - 1)² + y²), so we have three unknowns and three equations. This gives us a good fighting chance to find a unique solution. Our goal is to eliminate variables and simplify the equations until we can isolate x and y.

From the first two equations, we can immediately see a relationship between x and y. Since both xr and yr are equal to 20, we can write xr = yr. If r is not zero, we can divide both sides by r to get x = y. This is a crucial piece of information! It tells us that the real and imaginary parts of our complex number z are equal. This significantly simplifies our problem, as we've reduced the number of independent variables.

But what about the case where r = 0? Let's think about what this means. If r = √((x - 1)² + y²) = 0, then both (x - 1)² and y² must be zero. This implies that x = 1 and y = 0. However, if we plug these values back into our original equations, we get 1 * 0 = 20 and 0 * 0 = 20, which are clearly contradictions. So, the case where r = 0 is not a valid solution. This is an important check to perform whenever you're dealing with equations involving square roots or magnitudes.

Now that we know x = y and r ≠ 0, let's substitute x for y in the expression for r. We have r = √((x - 1)² + x²). Substituting y = x into the equation xr = 20, we get x√((x - 1)² + x²) = 20. This is a single equation with a single unknown, x. It looks a bit scary, but we can handle it!

To get rid of the square root, let's square both sides of the equation. This gives us x²((x - 1)² + x²) = 400. Expanding and simplifying, we get x²(x² - 2x + 1 + x²) = 400, which further simplifies to x²(2x² - 2x + 1) = 400. This results in the quartic equation 2x⁴ - 2x³ + x² - 400 = 0. Quartic equations can be tricky to solve in general, but we're going to use some clever techniques to find our solution.

Tackling the Quartic Equation: Finding the Real Root

We've arrived at the quartic equation 2x⁴ - 2x³ + x² - 400 = 0. Now, the challenge is to find the real roots of this equation. While there are general formulas for solving quartic equations, they can be quite complex and cumbersome. Instead, let's use a combination of analytical reasoning and numerical methods to find our solution. This approach is often more practical and insightful, especially in problem-solving scenarios.

First, let's try to understand the behavior of the function f(x) = 2x⁴ - 2x³ + x² - 400. We're looking for the values of x where f(x) = 0. Notice that this is a polynomial of even degree, which means its end behavior is the same in both directions (as x approaches positive or negative infinity, f(x) approaches positive infinity). This tells us that if there are any real roots, they must occur within some finite interval.

Let's analyze the derivative of f(x) to understand its increasing and decreasing behavior. The derivative is f'(x) = 8x³ - 6x² + 2x = 2x(4x² - 3x + 1). Setting f'(x) = 0, we find the critical points. One critical point is clearly x = 0. The quadratic 4x² - 3x + 1 has a discriminant of (-3)² - 4(4)(1) = 9 - 16 = -7, which is negative. This means the quadratic has no real roots, so x = 0 is the only real critical point. The coefficient of the leading term in f'(x) is positive, so the derivative changes sign from negative to positive at x = 0. This indicates that f(x) has a local minimum at x = 0.

Now, let's evaluate f(0). We have f(0) = 2(0)⁴ - 2(0)³ + (0)² - 400 = -400. Since f(0) is negative and the function goes to positive infinity as x goes to positive or negative infinity, there must be exactly two real roots: one positive and one negative. However, we know that x must be positive because xr = 20 and r is a magnitude, which is always positive. Therefore, we only need to focus on the positive real root.

To approximate the positive root, we can use numerical methods like the Newton-Raphson method or simply try plugging in some values. Let's try x = 4. We have f(4) = 2(4)⁴ - 2(4)³ + (4)² - 400 = 512 - 128 + 16 - 400 = 0. Wow! We got lucky and found an integer solution. So, x = 4 is a real root of the quartic equation.

Since we know there's only one positive real root, we've found our x value. This was a combination of analytical reasoning (using the derivative to understand the function's behavior) and a bit of luck (guessing x = 4). Sometimes, in problem-solving, a little bit of intuition can go a long way!

Unveiling the Unique Solution: Putting It All Together

We've done the hard work, guys! We've transformed the complex equation, solved the system of real equations, and tackled the quartic equation. Now, it's time to put all the pieces together and reveal the unique solution to our original problem: z|z-1| = 20 + 20i. This is the moment we've been building up to, so let's make sure we've got everything right.

We found that x = 4 is the positive real root of the quartic equation 2x⁴ - 2x³ + x² - 400 = 0. We also know that x = y, so y = 4 as well. This gives us the complex number z = x + iy = 4 + 4i. We're almost there, but we need to verify that this solution actually satisfies our original equation. It's always a good practice to check your solutions, especially when dealing with non-linear equations.

Let's plug z = 4 + 4i back into the original equation: z|z-1| = (4 + 4i)|(4 + 4i) - 1| = (4 + 4i)|3 + 4i|. Now, we need to find the magnitude of 3 + 4i, which is √(3² + 4²) = √(9 + 16) = √25 = 5. So, we have (4 + 4i) * 5 = 20 + 20i. This matches the right side of our original equation! This confirms that z = 4 + 4i is indeed a solution.

But the problem asked us to prove that there is exactly one solution. We've found one solution, but how do we know there aren't any others? This is where our earlier analysis comes into play. We showed that if there is a solution, it must satisfy the quartic equation 2x⁴ - 2x³ + x² - 400 = 0. We also showed that this quartic equation has only one positive real root, which we found to be x = 4. Since x uniquely determines y (because x = y) and z is determined by x and y, there can be only one solution for z.

Therefore, we've successfully proven that the equation z|z-1| = 20 + 20i has exactly one solution in the complex plane, and that solution is z = 4 + 4i. Woohoo! We did it!

Imagine drawing a circle centered at 1 with radius |z - 1|. The point z must lie on this circle.

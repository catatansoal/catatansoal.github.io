Polynomial Long Division: Find R(x)!

Aug 4, 2025 by ADMIN 37 views
Iklan Headers

Hey guys! Today, we're diving into the fascinating world of polynomial division. Specifically, we're going to tackle a problem where we need to divide one polynomial, f(x), by another, d(x), and express the result in a specific format. This format highlights the quotient and the remainder, which are key components in understanding how polynomials relate to each other. Polynomial division might seem a bit daunting at first, but trust me, once you grasp the core concept and the steps involved, it becomes a pretty cool tool in your mathematical arsenal. We'll break it down step by step, making sure everyone's on board. So, let's jump right in and conquer this polynomial problem together!

Understanding the Problem

Before we get our hands dirty with the actual division, let's make sure we fully understand the problem at hand. We're given two polynomials:

  • f(x) = 2x⁴ + 3x³ - 9x² + 4x + 3 (This is the polynomial we're going to divide – the dividend.)
  • d(x) = 2x - 3 (This is the polynomial we're dividing by – the divisor.)

Our mission is to divide f(x) by d(x) and express the result in the following form:

f(x)d(x)=Q(x)+R(x)d(x)\frac{f(x)}{d(x)} = Q(x) + \frac{R(x)}{d(x)}d(x)f(x)​=Q(x)+d(x)R(x)​

Where:

  • Q(x) represents the quotient (the result of the division).
  • R(x) represents the remainder (what's left over after the division).

In this particular problem, we're specifically asked to find R(x), the remainder. This means we need to perform the polynomial long division and carefully identify the remainder term. Understanding this setup is crucial because it guides our entire approach. We're not just dividing; we're dividing with a specific goal in mind – to isolate and identify the remainder. This focus helps us stay on track and ensures we extract the correct information from the division process. So, with the problem clearly defined, let's move on to the actual division!

Setting Up Polynomial Long Division

Alright, let's get this division party started! The first step is to set up the polynomial long division problem. If you've done long division with numbers, this will feel familiar, just with polynomials instead of digits. We write the dividend, f(x), inside the "division house" and the divisor, d(x), outside. Make sure both polynomials are written in descending order of their exponents (which they already are in this case – nice!). This organization is key to keeping things clear and preventing errors. Think of it like organizing your workspace before starting a project; a neat setup leads to a smoother process. So, let's visualize what this looks like:

 2x - 3 | 2x⁴ + 3x³ - 9x² + 4x + 3

Now, before we jump into the division steps, there's a small but important detail to check: Are there any missing terms in our dividend? By "missing terms," I mean if we skip any powers of x. For example, if we had 2x⁴ + 4x + 3, we'd be missing the x³ and x² terms. If there are missing terms, we need to insert them with a coefficient of 0. This acts as a placeholder and keeps our columns aligned during the division process. In our case, we have all the terms from x⁴ down to the constant term, so we're good to go. However, always remember to check for those missing terms – it's a common pitfall! With our problem set up and the polynomials ready, we're now prepped to perform the long division itself. Let's move on to the exciting part – the actual dividing!

Performing the Polynomial Long Division

Okay, guys, here comes the main event: performing the polynomial long division! This process might seem a bit intricate at first, but it's really just a series of repeating steps: divide, multiply, subtract, and bring down. We'll go through each step meticulously, so you can see how it all unfolds. Remember, practice makes perfect, so don't worry if it doesn't click immediately. Let's get started!

  1. Divide: We begin by dividing the leading term of the dividend (2x⁴) by the leading term of the divisor (2x). 2x⁴ / 2x = x³. This x³ is the first term of our quotient, Q(x). We write it above the x³ term in the dividend.
  2. Multiply: Next, we multiply the entire divisor (2x - 3) by the term we just wrote in the quotient (x³). x³ * (2x - 3) = 2x⁴ - 3x³. We write this result below the corresponding terms in the dividend.
  3. Subtract: Now, we subtract the result from the previous step (2x⁴ - 3x³) from the corresponding terms in the dividend (2x⁴ + 3x³). This is where paying attention to signs is crucial! (2x⁴ + 3x³) - (2x⁴ - 3x³) = 6x³. Make sure you're subtracting the entire expression, not just the first term.
  4. Bring Down: We bring down the next term from the dividend (-9x²) and write it next to the result from the subtraction (6x³). This gives us 6x³ - 9x². This new expression becomes our new "dividend" for the next iteration of the process.

Now, we repeat these steps with our new dividend (6x³ - 9x²):

  1. Divide: Divide the leading term of the new dividend (6x³) by the leading term of the divisor (2x). 6x³ / 2x = 3x². This is the next term of our quotient, so we write it next to the x³ in the quotient.
  2. Multiply: Multiply the divisor (2x - 3) by the new term in the quotient (3x²). 3x² * (2x - 3) = 6x³ - 9x². We write this below the 6x³ - 9x².
  3. Subtract: Subtract the result from the previous step from the corresponding terms in our current dividend. (6x³ - 9x²) - (6x³ - 9x²) = 0. Wow, it canceled out completely!
  4. Bring Down: Bring down the next term from the dividend (+4x). Our new dividend is now 4x.

Let's keep going! Repeat the steps again:

  1. Divide: Divide 4x by 2x. 4x / 2x = 2. This is the next term in the quotient.
  2. Multiply: Multiply 2 by (2x - 3). 2 * (2x - 3) = 4x - 6.
  3. Subtract: Subtract (4x - 6) from 4x. 4x - (4x - 6) = 6.
  4. Bring Down: Bring down the last term (+3). Our new dividend is 6 + 3 = 9.

One last time:

  1. Divide: We try to divide 9 by 2x. But wait! The degree of 9 (which is 0, since it's a constant) is less than the degree of 2x (which is 1). This means we can't divide any further. We've reached the end!

So, after all these steps, what have we got? We've successfully performed the polynomial long division. The expression remaining after the last subtraction, which is 9, is our remainder, R(x). The expression we built up above the division house, x³ + 3x² + 2, is our quotient, Q(x). Now, let's put it all together and see how we can extract the answer we need.

Identifying the Remainder R(x)

Alright, guys, we've made it through the long division process, and now it's time to reap the rewards! Remember, our main goal was to find R(x), the remainder. After diligently performing all the steps of polynomial long division, we've arrived at the final subtraction, and the value that's left over is our remainder. In our specific case, after the last round of divide, multiply, subtract, and bring down, we were left with 9. This means that:

R(x) = 9

That's it! We've successfully identified the remainder. This might seem like a small victory, but it's a crucial piece of the puzzle. In many polynomial problems, the remainder provides valuable information about the relationship between the dividend and the divisor. It can tell us, for instance, if the divisor is a factor of the dividend (if the remainder is 0) or help us solve equations and analyze polynomial functions. So, give yourselves a pat on the back for reaching this point! We've conquered the long division and extracted the key piece of information we needed. Now, let's take a step back and see how this remainder fits into the bigger picture.

Expressing the Result

Now that we've found the remainder, R(x) = 9, let's put everything together in the format the problem asked for. Remember, we were aiming to express the division of f(x) by d(x) as:

f(x)d(x)=Q(x)+R(x)d(x)\frac{f(x)}{d(x)} = Q(x) + \frac{R(x)}{d(x)}d(x)f(x)​=Q(x)+d(x)R(x)​

We've already found R(x). We also determined the quotient, Q(x), during the long division process. It was the expression that we built up above the division house: Q(x) = x³ + 3x² + 2. Now we have all the pieces of the puzzle. Let's plug them into the equation:

R(x) = 9

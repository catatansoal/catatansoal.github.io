Solving Expressions Using Order Of Operations PEMDAS BODMAS Explained

Hey guys! Today, we are going to dive into the fascinating world of mathematics, where numbers dance and operations follow a precise order. Our mission? To find the value of a seemingly complex expression by unraveling it using the rules of order of operations. Trust me, it's like solving a puzzle, and we're going to crack it together!

Understanding the Expression

Before we jump into solving, let's take a good look at our expression:

510⋅(610)2−24\frac{5}{10} \cdot\left(\frac{6}{10}\right)^2-\frac{2}{4}105​⋅(106​)2−42​

At first glance, it might seem intimidating with all the fractions, parentheses, exponents, and operations. But don't worry! We'll break it down piece by piece. The key to solving this is understanding the order of operations, often remembered by the acronym PEMDAS or BODMAS. This handy rule tells us the sequence in which we should perform mathematical operations:

  • Parentheses (or Brackets)
  • Exponents (or Orders)
  • Multiplication and Division (from left to right)
  • Addition and Subtraction (from left to right)

With our trusty PEMDAS/BODMAS by our side, let's embark on this mathematical adventure!

Step 1 Taming the Parentheses

Our first stop is the parentheses: (610)2\left(\frac{6}{10}\right)^2(106​)2. Inside the parentheses, we have a fraction, 610\frac{6}{10}106​. However, there are no operations to perform inside the parentheses at this stage, so we move on to the next operation in PEMDAS/BODMAS, which is exponents.

Step 2 Exponent Expedition

Now, we tackle the exponent: (610)2\left(\frac{6}{10}\right)^2(106​)2. This means we need to square the fraction 610\frac{6}{10}106​. Squaring a fraction involves squaring both the numerator (the top number) and the denominator (the bottom number). So,

(610)2=62102=6⋅610⋅10=36100\left(\frac{6}{10}\right)^2 = \frac{6^2}{10^2} = \frac{6 \cdot 6}{10 \cdot 10} = \frac{36}{100}(106​)2=10262​=10⋅106⋅6​=10036​

Great! We've successfully conquered the exponent. Our expression now looks like this:

510⋅36100−24\frac{5}{10} \cdot \frac{36}{100} - \frac{2}{4}105​⋅10036​−42​

Step 3 Multiplication Maneuvers

Next in line is multiplication. We have 510⋅36100\frac{5}{10} \cdot \frac{36}{100}105​⋅10036​. To multiply fractions, we simply multiply the numerators together and the denominators together:

510⋅36100=5⋅3610⋅100=1801000\frac{5}{10} \cdot \frac{36}{100} = \frac{5 \cdot 36}{10 \cdot 100} = \frac{180}{1000}105​⋅10036​=10⋅1005⋅36​=1000180​

Our expression is slimming down nicely. Now it reads:

1801000−24\frac{180}{1000} - \frac{2}{4}1000180​−42​

Step 4 Subtraction Showdown

Finally, we arrive at subtraction. To subtract fractions, they need to have a common denominator (the same bottom number). Currently, our denominators are 1000 and 4. We need to find the least common multiple (LCM) of 1000 and 4. Since 1000 is divisible by 4, the LCM is 1000.

So, we need to convert 24\frac{2}{4}42​ into an equivalent fraction with a denominator of 1000. To do this, we ask ourselves: what do we multiply 4 by to get 1000? The answer is 250. Therefore, we multiply both the numerator and denominator of 24\frac{2}{4}42​ by 250:

24=2⋅2504⋅250=5001000\frac{2}{4} = \frac{2 \cdot 250}{4 \cdot 250} = \frac{500}{1000}42​=4⋅2502⋅250​=1000500​

Now we can perform the subtraction:

1801000−5001000=180−5001000=−3201000\frac{180}{1000} - \frac{500}{1000} = \frac{180 - 500}{1000} = \frac{-320}{1000}1000180​−1000500​=1000180−500​=1000−320​

Step 5 Simplification Symphony

We're almost there! Our final step is to simplify the fraction −3201000\frac{-320}{1000}1000−320​. Both the numerator and denominator are divisible by 10, so let's start there:

−3201000=−32100\frac{-320}{1000} = \frac{-32}{100}1000−320​=100−32​

We can further simplify by dividing both by 4:

−32100=−825\frac{-32}{100} = \frac{-8}{25}100−32​=25−8​

And there you have it! We've successfully simplified the fraction.

Therefore, the value of the expression 510⋅(610)2−24\frac{5}{10} \cdot\left(\frac{6}{10}\right)^2-\frac{2}{4}105​⋅(106​)2−42​ is −825\frac{-8}{25}25−8​

Another Method to Solve the Expression

There's another cool way to tackle this problem! Before diving into the operations, we can simplify some of the fractions to make the calculations easier. This is like finding a shortcut on our mathematical journey!

Let's revisit our expression:

510⋅(610)2−24\frac{5}{10} \cdot\left(\frac{6}{10}\right)^2-\frac{2}{4}105​⋅(106​)2−42​

Notice that 510\frac{5}{10}105​ can be simplified to 12\frac{1}{2}21​ by dividing both numerator and denominator by 5. Also, 610\frac{6}{10}106​ can be simplified to 35\frac{3}{5}53​ by dividing both by 2, and 24\frac{2}{4}42​ simplifies to 12\frac{1}{2}21​.

So, our expression transforms into:

12⋅(35)2−12\frac{1}{2} \cdot\left(\frac{3}{5}\right)^2-\frac{1}{2}21​⋅(53​)2−21​

Now, let's follow PEMDAS/BODMAS:

  1. Parentheses and Exponents:

    • (35)2=3252=925\left(\frac{3}{5}\right)^2 = \frac{3^2}{5^2} = \frac{9}{25}(53​)2=5232​=259​

    Our expression now looks like this:

    12⋅925−12\frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{9}{25} - \frac{1}{2}21​⋅259​−21​

  2. Multiplication:

    • 12⋅925=1⋅92⋅25=950\frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{9}{25} = \frac{1 \cdot 9}{2 \cdot 25} = \frac{9}{50}21​⋅259​=2⋅251⋅9​=509​

    Our expression becomes:

    950−12\frac{9}{50} - \frac{1}{2}509​−21​

  3. Subtraction:

    To subtract, we need a common denominator. The least common multiple of 50 and 2 is 50. So, we convert 12\frac{1}{2}21​ to a fraction with a denominator of 50:

    • 12=1⋅252⋅25=2550\frac{1}{2} = \frac{1 \cdot 25}{2 \cdot 25} = \frac{25}{50}21​=2⋅251⋅25​=5025​

    Now we can subtract:

    • 950−2550=9−2550=−1650\frac{9}{50} - \frac{25}{50} = \frac{9 - 25}{50} = \frac{-16}{50}509​−5025​=509−25​=50−16​

  4. Simplification:

    Finally, we simplify the fraction −1650\frac{-16}{50}50−16​ by dividing both numerator and denominator by their greatest common divisor, which is 2:

    • −1650=−825\frac{-16}{50} = \frac{-8}{25}50−16​=25−8​

We arrive at the same answer! Simplifying the fractions at the beginning made the numbers smaller and easier to work with. This illustrates that sometimes, a little simplification can go a long way in making mathematical problems more manageable.

Why is Order of Operations Important?

Imagine you're baking a cake. You wouldn't add the eggs after the cake is already baked, would you? Similarly, in math, the order in which you perform operations matters significantly. The order of operations importance stems from the need for consistent and unambiguous mathematical communication. It's a universal convention that ensures everyone arrives at the same solution when evaluating an expression. This consistency is crucial in various fields, from engineering and finance to computer science and everyday problem-solving.

Let's consider a simple example to illustrate this. Take the expression 2 + 3 * 4. If we perform the addition first, we get 5 * 4 = 20. But if we perform the multiplication first, we get 2 + 12 = 14. These are two very different answers! The correct order of operations, however, dictates that we perform multiplication before addition, leading to the correct answer of 14. This highlights why a standardized order is essential.

The PEMDAS/BODMAS Acronym

The PEMDAS acronym (or BODMAS acronym, as it's known in some regions) is your best friend when it comes to remembering the order of operations. It stands for:

  • Parentheses (or Brackets)
  • Exponents (or Orders)
  • Multiplication and Division (from left to right)
  • Addition and Subtraction (from left to right)

Think of it as a roadmap for solving expressions. Each letter represents a level of priority. We always start with Parentheses/Brackets, then move on to Exponents/Orders, and so on. It's important to note that Multiplication and Division have the same priority, so we perform them from left to right. Similarly, Addition and Subtraction have the same priority and are performed from left to right.

Breaking Down PEMDAS/BODMAS

  1. Parentheses/Brackets: These are like VIP sections in an expression. Operations within parentheses or brackets are always performed first. This allows us to group parts of the expression and dictate the order in which they're evaluated. For example, in the expression 3 * (2 + 4), we first add 2 and 4 because they are inside parentheses.
  2. Exponents/Orders: Exponents represent repeated multiplication (e.g., 2^3 means 2 * 2 * 2). After dealing with parentheses, we tackle exponents. They tell us how many times a number is multiplied by itself.
  3. Multiplication and Division: These operations share the same level of priority. When both multiplication and division appear in an expression, we perform them from left to right. This is crucial to avoid errors. For example, in the expression 10 / 2 * 5, we divide 10 by 2 first, then multiply by 5.
  4. Addition and Subtraction: Similar to multiplication and division, addition and subtraction have the same priority and are performed from left to right. For example, in the expression 8 - 3 + 2, we subtract 3 from 8 first, then add 2.

Applying Order of Operations Example Problems

Let's put our PEMDAS/BODMAS knowledge to the test with some examples:

Example 1:

Solve: 10 + 2 * (6 - 3) / 2

  1. Parentheses: (6 - 3) = 3 The expression becomes: 10 + 2 * 3 / 2
  2. Multiplication and Division (from left to right):
    • 2 * 3 = 6
    • 6 / 2 = 3 The expression becomes: 10 + 3
  3. Addition: 10 + 3 = 13

So, the solution is 13.

Example 2:

Solve: 18 / (2 + 4) + 3^2 - 1

  1. Parentheses: (2 + 4) = 6 The expression becomes: 18 / 6 + 3^2 - 1
  2. Exponents: 3^2 = 9 The expression becomes: 18 / 6 + 9 - 1
  3. Division: 18 / 6 = 3 The expression becomes: 3 + 9 - 1
  4. Addition and Subtraction (from left to right):
    • 3 + 9 = 12
    • 12 - 1 = 11

So, the solution is 11.

Example 3:

Solve: 4 * [3 + (5 - 1) / 2] - 7

  1. Innermost Parentheses: (5 - 1) = 4 The expression becomes: 4 * [3 + 4 / 2] - 7
  2. Brackets (Division inside): 4 / 2 = 2 The expression becomes: 4 * [3 + 2] - 7
  3. Brackets (Addition inside): 3 + 2 = 5 The expression becomes: 4 * 5 - 7
  4. Multiplication: 4 * 5 = 20 The expression becomes: 20 - 7
  5. Subtraction: 20 - 7 = 13

So, the solution is 13.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Forgetting the Order: The most common mistake is not following the correct order of operations. Always write down PEMDAS/BODMAS as a reminder.
  • Treating Multiplication and Division (or Addition and Subtraction) as having different priorities: Remember, these operations are performed from left to right.
  • Ignoring Parentheses/Brackets: Parentheses and brackets are your friends! They tell you what to do first.
  • Incorrectly Handling Exponents: Make sure you understand what an exponent means (repeated multiplication).

Tips for Success

  • Write it Down: When solving expressions, write down each step clearly. This helps you avoid mistakes and keeps your work organized.
  • Double-Check: After you've solved an expression, take a moment to review your steps and make sure you haven't made any errors.
  • Practice Regularly: The more you practice, the more comfortable you'll become with the order of operations.
  • Use Calculators Wisely: Calculators can be helpful, but they can also make mistakes if you don't enter the expression correctly. Understand the order of operations yourself before relying on a calculator.

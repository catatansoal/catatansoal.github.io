Understanding the Problem: Gram Matrices and Vector Rotations

So, what's the big deal? Let's break it down. At the heart of this problem lies the Gram matrix. A Gram matrix, denoted as GGG, is constructed from a set of vectors a1,a2,...,aka_1, a_2, ..., a_ka1​,a2​,...,ak​ that span the real space Rd\mathbb{R}^dRd. The entries of this matrix are simply the dot products of these vectors, i.e., Gij=ai⋅ajG_{ij} = a_i \cdot a_jGij​=ai​⋅aj​. Gram matrices pop up in various areas, from machine learning (think kernel methods) to signal processing. They're super useful for capturing the relationships between vectors.

Now, the question is: Can we use this Gram matrix to significantly change the direction of a vector? In other words, if we multiply a vector xxx by a Gram matrix GGG, can the resulting vector GxGxGx be almost orthogonal (nearly 90 degrees apart) from the original vector xxx? This is not just an abstract math problem; it has implications for understanding how linear transformations, especially those represented by Gram matrices, can distort or preserve geometric relationships.

The problem statement gives us a fixed collection of vectors a1,...,aka_1, ..., a_ka1​,...,ak​ that spans Rd\mathbb{R}^dRd. This “spanning” part is crucial because it means that any vector in Rd\mathbb{R}^dRd can be written as a linear combination of these aia_iai​ vectors. It also provides a normalized vector xxx in Rd\mathbb{R}^dRd, meaning its length (or norm) is 1. The core question revolves around finding a Gram matrix GGG formed from the aia_iai​ vectors and assessing whether the dot product between xxx and GxGxGx can be made arbitrarily small. A small dot product implies that the angle between xxx and GxGxGx is close to 90 degrees, because the dot product is related to the cosine of the angle between the vectors.

Initial Thoughts and Challenges

My initial thought was to try and construct a Gram matrix that would effectively “nullify” certain components of the vector xxx, pushing the result in an orthogonal direction. However, the spanning condition on the aia_iai​ vectors makes this tricky. We can't just arbitrarily choose vectors that are orthogonal to xxx because they need to span the entire space. This is where the problem gets interesting. We need a more nuanced approach, perhaps involving careful scaling or projections to achieve the desired rotation.

One major challenge is the inherent positive semi-definiteness of Gram matrices. Since GGG is a Gram matrix, it is guaranteed to be positive semi-definite. This means that for any vector yyy, yTGy≥0y^T G y \geq 0yTGy≥0. In our case, this implies x⋅Gx≥0x \cdot Gx \geq 0x⋅Gx≥0, which restricts how “negative” the dot product can be. We can’t just make it any arbitrarily small negative number. We're limited to making it close to zero from the positive side.

Another challenge is dealing with the interplay between the vectors aia_iai​ and the vector xxx. If xxx happens to align closely with one of the aia_iai​ vectors, it might be difficult to achieve a significant rotation. On the other hand, if xxx is “equally spread out” among the aia_iai​ vectors, we might have a better chance. This suggests that the geometry of the vectors aia_iai​ relative to xxx is a critical factor.

Step 1 to Step 2: The Distributive Property in Action

In the journey from Step 1 to Step 2, Paula performs a crucial operation: distribution. The original equation in Step 1, -4(x+8)-2x=25, presents us with a parenthesis, a mathematical package that needs opening. Paula does this by multiplying the -4 across the terms inside the parenthesis, the x and the 8. This is a classic application of the distributive property. It's like delivering -4 to each resident inside the (x+8) house.

So, -4 times x gives us -4x, and -4 times 8 gives us -32. The equation then transforms into -4x-32-2x=25. Step 2 to Step 3: Combining Like Terms for Simplification

Stepping from Step 2 to Step 3 involves a strategic maneuver: combining like terms. In Step 2, we have the equation -4x-32-2x=25. Notice that there are two terms featuring the variable x: -4x and -2x. These are like terms, meaning they can be combined because they share the same variable raised to the same power (in this case, x to the power of 1). It's like grouping apples with apples and oranges with oranges – we're organizing the equation's components.

Paula combines -4x and -2x to get -6x. This is a straightforward addition of coefficients: -4 plus -2 equals -6. The equation then becomes -6x-32=25. The -32 remains as it is, since it doesn't have any other constant terms to combine with on the left side of the equation. The right side, 25, also stays put, patiently waiting for the next move. This step is about tidying up, making the equation more compact and manageable. It's like streamlining a recipe, reducing the number of ingredients we need to focus on.

Step 3 to Step 4: Isolating the Variable Term Using Addition

Moving from Step 3 to Step 4 is a pivotal moment in our equation-solving journey. We're transitioning from -6x-32=25 to -6x=57. The key operation here is isolating the term containing our variable, x. Right now, -6x is accompanied by -32 on the left side of the equation, which is like having an unwanted guest at a party. We need to get rid of it to bring x closer to being alone on its side.

Paula achieves this isolation by adding 32 to both sides of the equation. This is a critical application of the addition property of equality, which states that adding the same value to both sides of an equation maintains the equality. It's like balancing a scale: if you add weight to one side, you must add the same weight to the other to keep it balanced.

Adding 32 to -32 on the left side cancels them out, leaving us with just -6x. On the right side, 25 plus 32 equals 57. So, we arrive at -6x=57. We've successfully isolated the -6x term, bringing us one step closer to solving for x. Dividing -6x by -6 isolates x on the left side, which is exactly what we want. On the right side, 57 divided by -6 gives us -9.5, which can also be expressed as the mixed number -9 \frac{1}{2}. The Division Property of Equality: Our Key Discovery

So, after our careful examination of each step, the answer becomes clear. Paula used the division property of equality between Step 4 and Step 5. It was in this transition that she divided both sides of the equation by -6 to isolate x and find its value.

Understanding the Division Property of Equality

Now, let's zoom in on this division property of equality a bit more. What's the big idea behind it? Simply put, it's a fundamental rule in algebra that allows us to manipulate equations while keeping them balanced. Think of an equation like a perfectly balanced scale. If you do something to one side, you must do the exact same thing to the other side to maintain that balance. That's the essence of the equality properties.

The division property specifically says that if you divide both sides of an equation by the same non-zero number, the equation remains true. Why non-zero? Well, dividing by zero is a big no-no in mathematics – it's undefined and leads to all sorts of problems. So, as long as we're dividing by a number other than zero, we're good to go.

Why is it so important?

The division property is a workhorse in algebra. It's one of the primary tools we use to solve for variables. When a variable is multiplied by a coefficient (like the -6 in our example), division is the operation we use to undo that multiplication and isolate the variable. Without it, solving many equations would be impossible!

Examples in Action

Let's look at a couple of quick examples to solidify this concept:

  • Example 1: 3x = 12. To solve for x, we divide both sides by 3: (3x)/3 = 12/3, which gives us x = 4.
  • Example 2: -2y = -10. To solve for y, we divide both sides by -2: (-2y)/-2 = -10/-2, which gives us y = 5.

In both cases, the division property allowed us to isolate the variable and find its value. It's a powerful and versatile tool that you'll use again and again in your mathematical adventures, guys!

Mastering Equation-Solving: Key Takeaways

Okay, we've successfully navigated Paula's equation and pinpointed the use of the division property of equality. But what broader lessons can we extract from this experience? What are the key takeaways for mastering equation-solving in general? Let's distill the wisdom we've gained into some actionable insights.

1. Understand the Properties of Equality

First and foremost, a solid grasp of the properties of equality is absolutely essential. These properties are the rules of the game in algebra, the guidelines that dictate how we can manipulate equations without breaking them. We've already discussed the division property, but there are others, like the addition, subtraction, and multiplication properties. Each one gives us a specific way to transform an equation while preserving its balance.

Think of these properties as your mathematical superpowers. The more you understand them, the more powerful you become at solving equations. They allow you to move terms around, simplify expressions, and ultimately isolate the variable you're trying to find. So, dedicate time to truly understanding these properties, guys! They are the foundation upon which all equation-solving is built.

2. Inverse Operations are Your Friends

Another crucial concept is the idea of inverse operations. Every mathematical operation has an inverse that undoes it. Addition and subtraction are inverses of each other, and multiplication and division are inverses. Recognizing these relationships is key to strategically solving equations.

In Paula's equation, we saw how addition was used to undo subtraction (in Step 3 to Step 4) and how division was used to undo multiplication (in Step 4 to Step 5). This is a common pattern in equation-solving: we use inverse operations to peel away the layers surrounding the variable until it stands alone. So, when you're faced with an equation, ask yourself: what operations are being applied to the variable, and what are their inverses? This will guide you toward the right steps to take.

3. Simplify, Simplify, Simplify!

Before you start applying properties of equality, it's often helpful to simplify the equation as much as possible. This means combining like terms, distributing where necessary, and generally tidying things up. A simpler equation is easier to solve, just like a decluttered workspace is easier to navigate.

In Paula's equation, we saw simplification in action in Steps 2 and 3. Distributing in Step 2 removed the parentheses, and combining like terms in Step 3 made the equation more compact. These steps didn't directly solve for x, but they paved the way for easier manipulation later on. So, don't underestimate the power of simplification! It can often make the difference between a daunting equation and a manageable one.

4. Practice Makes Perfect

Finally, and perhaps most importantly, practice is key to mastering equation-solving. The more equations you solve, the more comfortable you'll become with the properties of equality, inverse operations, and simplification techniques. It's like learning any skill: the more you do it, the better you get at it. So, don't be afraid to tackle lots of different equations, guys!

Start with simpler ones to build your confidence, and then gradually move on to more challenging problems. Pay attention to the steps you're taking, and think about why you're taking them. If you get stuck, don't give up! Review the concepts, look for examples, and try again. With consistent practice, you'll develop the intuition and skills you need to conquer any equation that comes your way.

By understanding the properties of equality, using inverse operations strategically, simplifying expressions, and practicing consistently, you can become a true equation-solving master. So, embrace the challenge, enjoy the process, and remember that every equation you solve brings you one step closer to mathematical mastery.

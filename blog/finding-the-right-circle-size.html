Finding The Right Circle Size For Liliana's Vase Project

Jul 17, 2025 by ADMIN 57 views

Hey guys! Let's dive into a fun math problem Liliana's got on her hands. She's crafting a vase with a circular base and needs our help to figure out the right size. The area of the base needs to be just right, between 135 square centimeters and 155 square centimeters. So, we're going to play detective and figure out which circle could be the perfect fit for her vase. To do this, we'll be dusting off our knowledge of circles, areas, and a little bit of algebra. We'll also be using 3.14 as an approximation for pi (π), which is super important when dealing with circles. Think of it like this: we're helping Liliana's artistic vision come to life by using math – how cool is that? So, grab your thinking caps, and let's get started on this circular adventure! We're going to break down the problem, explore the formula for the area of a circle, and then use that knowledge to find the possible radii (that's the distance from the center of the circle to the edge) that would work for Liliana's vase. This isn't just about numbers; it's about understanding how math applies to real-world projects and designs. So, let's jump in and see how we can help Liliana create her masterpiece!

Understanding the Area of a Circle

So, to crack this problem, the area of a circle is our key concept. Remember that formula from math class? It's Area = πr², where π (pi) is approximately 3.14, and 'r' is the radius of the circle. The radius is simply the distance from the center of the circle to any point on its edge. This formula tells us how much space a circle takes up, which is exactly what Liliana is concerned with for the base of her vase. Now, Liliana needs the area to be between 135 cm² and 155 cm². That means we need to find a radius (or radii) that, when plugged into our area formula, gives us a result within this range. Think of it like a Goldilocks situation – not too big, not too small, but just right. We're not just looking for one specific answer, but rather a range of possible radii that would work. This is where our problem-solving skills come into play. We'll need to do a little bit of algebraic maneuvering to figure out what those possible radii are. But don't worry, we'll take it step by step and make sure it's clear. Understanding the relationship between the radius and the area is crucial here. A larger radius will result in a larger area, and vice versa. So, we're essentially trying to find the sweet spot for the radius that keeps the area within Liliana's desired range. This involves not just knowing the formula but also understanding how to apply it to a real-world scenario. And that's what makes math so powerful – it helps us solve practical problems and bring ideas to life!

Calculating the Possible Radii

Alright, let's get down to the nitty-gritty and calculate the possible radii for Liliana's vase base. We know the area needs to be between 135 cm² and 155 cm². So, we'll use our trusty formula, Area = πr², and work backward to find the corresponding radii. First, let's consider the lower bound of the area, 135 cm². We need to solve the equation 135 = 3.14 * r² for 'r'. To do this, we'll divide both sides by 3.14, which gives us r² ≈ 42.99. Then, we take the square root of both sides to find r ≈ 6.56 cm. This means that a circle with a radius of about 6.56 cm would have an area close to 135 cm². Now, let's tackle the upper bound, 155 cm². We follow the same steps: 155 = 3.14 * r². Dividing both sides by 3.14 gives us r² ≈ 49.36. Taking the square root, we get r ≈ 7.03 cm. So, a circle with a radius of about 7.03 cm would have an area close to 155 cm². What does this tell us? It tells us that any circle with a radius between approximately 6.56 cm and 7.03 cm would work for Liliana's vase base. We've essentially found a range of possible radii that satisfy her requirements. This is super helpful because it gives Liliana some flexibility in choosing the exact size of her vase base. She's not limited to one specific measurement, but rather has a range of options to work with. Now, the next step would be to look at some specific circle options and see if their radii fall within this range.

Identifying the Correct Circle Options

Okay, now that we've calculated the range of possible radii (between 6.56 cm and 7.03 cm), it's time to identify which circle options could work for Liliana's vase. This is where we'd typically be presented with a list of circles, each with a different radius or diameter (remember, the diameter is twice the radius). For each circle option, we need to check if its radius falls within our calculated range. Let's imagine we have a few options to consider: Circle A has a radius of 6 cm, Circle B has a radius of 6.7 cm, Circle C has a radius of 7.2 cm, and Circle D has a radius of 7 cm. To determine which circles are suitable, we simply compare their radii to our range of 6.56 cm to 7.03 cm. Circle A, with a radius of 6 cm, is too small – it's outside our acceptable range. Circle B, with a radius of 6.7 cm, falls perfectly within our range, so it's a good option for Liliana. Circle C, with a radius of 7.2 cm, is too large, exceeding our upper limit. And Circle D, with a radius of 7 cm, also fits nicely within our range. So, in this example, both Circle B and Circle D would be viable choices for Liliana's vase base. This process of comparing given values to a calculated range is a common problem-solving technique in math and science. It allows us to narrow down possibilities and make informed decisions based on specific criteria. In Liliana's case, it helps her choose the perfect circular base for her artistic creation!

Solution

Alright, let's dive into solving this problem step-by-step. First, we need to figure out the total charge that flows through the device. Remember, current (I) is the rate of flow of charge (Q) over time (t). Mathematically, this is expressed as: $I = \fracQ}{t}$. We know the current (I = 15.0 A) and the time (t = 30 s), so we can rearrange this equation to solve for the charge (Q) $Q = I \times t$. Plugging in the values, we get: $Q = 15.0 \text{ A \times 30 \text s} = 450 \text{ C}$. So, a total of 450 Coulombs of charge flowed through the device. Now, we need to figure out how many electrons make up this 450 Coulombs. Each electron has a charge of approximately $1.602 \times 10^{-19}$ Coulombs. To find the number of electrons (n), we divide the total charge (Q) by the charge of a single electron (e) $n = \frac{Qe}$. Substituting the values, we have $n = \frac{450 \text{ C}{1.602 \times 10^{-19} \text{ C/electron}} \approx 2.81 \times 10^{21} \text{ electrons}$. Thatâ€™s a whole lot of electrons! Always double-check your units before plugging them into formulas. Another common error is forgetting the charge of an electron.

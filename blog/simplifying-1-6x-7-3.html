Simplifying (1.6x + 7.3) + (-0.6x + 2) - (7.8 - 3.4x) A Step-by-Step Guide

But don't worry, we'll break it down step-by-step and make sure you understand exactly how to simplify it. Our mission is to simplify the expression (1.6x + 7.3) + (-0.6x + 2) - (7.8 - 3.4x). This kind of problem is super common in algebra, and mastering it will definitely boost your math skills. So, let's get started and make algebra a little less intimidating!

Understanding Algebraic Expressions

Before we jump into the solution, let's quickly recap what algebraic expressions are all about. Think of them as mathematical phrases that combine numbers, variables (like 'x'), and operations (addition, subtraction, multiplication, division). The key to simplifying these expressions lies in understanding the order of operations and how to combine like terms. Like terms are those that have the same variable raised to the same power (e.g., 2x and 3x) or are just constants (e.g., 5 and -2). When we simplify, we're essentially tidying up the expression to make it easier to work with. This often involves removing parentheses, combining like terms, and rearranging the terms in a standard order.

When dealing with algebraic expressions, the distributive property is your best friend. It allows you to multiply a single term by multiple terms inside parentheses. For example, a(b + c) becomes ab + ac. This is crucial when you have a minus sign in front of parentheses, as you need to distribute the negative sign to each term inside. Another important concept is the commutative property, which states that the order in which you add or multiply numbers doesn't change the result (e.g., a + b = b + a). This allows us to rearrange terms to group like terms together, making simplification easier. Keep these concepts in mind, and you'll be well-equipped to tackle any algebraic expression that comes your way!

Now, when you're faced with an expression like the one we're working on today, it's tempting to just jump in and start combining things. But a strategic approach is key. First, focus on removing those parentheses, paying close attention to any negative signs lurking outside. Then, identify and group your like terms â€“ all the 'x' terms together and all the constants together. Finally, perform the necessary operations (addition and subtraction) to combine those like terms. Remember, algebra is like a puzzle, and each step brings you closer to the solution. So, stay patient, stay organized, and you'll crack it every time!

Step-by-Step Solution

Let's dive into simplifying our expression: (1.6x + 7.3) + (-0.6x + 2) - (7.8 - 3.4x). We'll take it one step at a time to make sure we don't miss anything.

Step 1: Removing Parentheses

The first step is to get rid of those parentheses. For the first two sets, it's pretty straightforward since we're adding. We can simply remove them.

So, (1.6x + 7.3) + (-0.6x + 2) becomes 1.6x + 7.3 - 0.6x + 2.

The tricky part comes with the third set of parentheses because of the minus sign in front: -(7.8 - 3.4x). Remember, this minus sign needs to be distributed to both terms inside the parentheses. Think of it as multiplying each term by -1.

Distributing the negative sign, -(7.8 - 3.4x) becomes -7.8 + 3.4x. Notice how the signs of the terms inside the parentheses change.

Now, our expression looks like this: 1.6x + 7.3 - 0.6x + 2 - 7.8 + 3.4x. We've successfully removed all the parentheses!

Step 2: Grouping Like Terms

Next, we need to group the like terms together. This means putting all the terms with 'x' together and all the constant terms together. This step makes it easier to see which terms we can combine.

Let's rearrange the expression: 1.6x - 0.6x + 3.4x + 7.3 + 2 - 7.8. See how we've moved the terms around so that the 'x' terms are together and the constants are together? We're using the commutative property here, which allows us to change the order of addition and subtraction without changing the result.

Step 3: Combining Like Terms

Now comes the fun part: combining the like terms. We'll add and subtract the coefficients of the 'x' terms and then do the same for the constant terms.

Let's start with the 'x' terms: 1.6x - 0.6x + 3.4x. Adding these together, we get 1.6x - 0.6x = 1x, and then 1x + 3.4x = 4.4x. So, the combined 'x' term is 4.4x.

Now, let's combine the constant terms: 7.3 + 2 - 7.8. Adding these together, we get 7.3 + 2 = 9.3, and then 9.3 - 7.8 = 1.5. So, the combined constant term is 1.5.

Step 4: The Simplified Expression

Finally, we put the combined terms together to get our simplified expression:

4.4x + 1.5

And that's it! We've successfully simplified the expression (1.6x + 7.3) + (-0.6x + 2) - (7.8 - 3.4x) to 4.4x + 1.5.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

When simplifying algebraic expressions, there are a few common pitfalls that students often stumble into. Let's highlight these so you can steer clear of them:

  • Forgetting to Distribute the Negative Sign: This is a big one! As we saw in our step-by-step solution, when you have a minus sign in front of parentheses, you need to distribute it to every term inside. Failing to do so will change the signs incorrectly and lead to the wrong answer. Always double-check those negative signs!
  • Combining Unlike Terms: Remember, you can only combine like terms. That means terms with the same variable raised to the same power. You can't combine 'x' terms with constant terms, or 'xÂ²' terms with 'x' terms. Make sure you're only adding or subtracting terms that are truly alike.
  • Order of Operations: Just like with regular arithmetic, the order of operations matters in algebra too. While simplifying expressions, remember to handle parentheses first, then exponents, then multiplication and division (from left to right), and finally addition and subtraction (from left to right). Keeping this order in mind will help you avoid errors.
  • Careless Arithmetic: Sometimes, the mistake isn't in the algebra itself, but in the simple arithmetic. A small addition or subtraction error can throw off the whole solution. Take your time, double-check your calculations, and use a calculator if needed.
  • Rushing Through the Steps: Algebra problems can sometimes feel long and tedious, but it's important not to rush. Each step is crucial, and skipping steps or doing things in your head can increase the chances of making a mistake. Write out each step clearly and methodically.

By being aware of these common mistakes and taking the time to work carefully, you'll be well on your way to mastering algebraic expressions!

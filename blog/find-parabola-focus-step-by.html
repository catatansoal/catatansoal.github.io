Find Parabola Focus: Step-by-Step Guide

Understanding the Parabola and Its Focus

Before we jump into the calculations, let's establish a solid understanding of what a parabola is and what the focus represents. A parabola is a symmetrical, U-shaped curve. It's defined as the set of all points that are equidistant to a fixed point (the focus) and a fixed line (the directrix). Imagine a point source of light placed at the focus; the light rays would reflect off the parabola and form a parallel beam. This property makes parabolas incredibly useful in various applications, from satellite dishes to car headlights.

The focus is a point inside the curve of the parabola, while the directrix is a line outside the curve. The vertex of the parabola is the point that lies exactly halfway between the focus and the directrix. The axis of symmetry is the line that passes through the focus and the vertex, dividing the parabola into two symmetrical halves. The distance between the vertex and the focus (and also the distance between the vertex and the directrix) is often denoted by 'p'. This 'p' value plays a key role in determining the shape and dimensions of the parabola. Understanding these fundamental concepts is crucial before we tackle the algebraic manipulations needed to find the focus.

To really grasp the significance of the focus, consider its role in defining the parabola's shape. The closer the focus is to the vertex, the more 'narrow' or 'steep' the parabola will be. Conversely, the farther the focus is from the vertex, the 'wider' or 'flatter' the parabola becomes. The directrix acts as a kind of 'mirror' for the focus, influencing the curve's direction and spread. Visualizing these relationships can help you intuitively understand how changing the focus affects the overall shape of the parabola. Thinking about these visual aspects complements the algebraic methods we will use, providing a more complete picture of these important curves.

Transforming the Equation into Vertex Form

The given equation, y=18(x2−4x−12)y=\frac{1}{8}(x^2-4x-12)y=81​(x2−4x−12), is in a general form. To easily identify the vertex and subsequently the focus, we need to transform it into the vertex form. The vertex form of a parabola equation is given by: y=a(x−h)2+ky = a(x - h)^2 + ky=a(x−h)2+k, where (h, k) represents the coordinates of the vertex, and 'a' determines the parabola's direction and width. Our main goal here is to manipulate the given equation to match this beautiful and informative vertex form.

The key technique we'll employ is completing the square. This algebraic method allows us to rewrite a quadratic expression in a form that reveals a squared term, which is essential for achieving the vertex form. Identifying the Vertex and the Value of 'p'

This point is the cornerstone of our parabola, serving as a reference point for locating the focus and other key features. It's like the central hub from which all other measurements and distances radiate.

Now, let's determine the value of 'p', the distance between the vertex and the focus. We know that 'a' in the vertex form equation is related to 'p' by the equation a=14pa = \frac{1}{4p}a=4p1​. This seemingly small number, p = 2, holds significant information about the parabola's shape and the location of its focus. It's the crucial link between the algebraic representation and the geometric properties of the curve.

The value of 'p' not only tells us the distance between the vertex and focus but also helps us understand the 'tightness' or 'wideness' of the parabola. A larger 'p' value means the parabola is wider, while a smaller 'p' value indicates a narrower parabola. This geometric interpretation adds another layer of understanding to the algebraic manipulations we perform. Calculating the Focus of the Parabola

We've found the vertex (2, -2) and the value of p (p = 2). Now, we're in the home stretch! To calculate the coordinates of the focus, we need to consider the direction in which the parabola opens. Since the coefficient 'a' in the vertex form (y=18(x−2)2−2y = \frac{1}{8}(x - 2)^2 - 2y=81​(x−2)2−2) is positive (18\frac{1}{8}81​), the parabola opens upwards. This means the focus is located above the vertex.

If the parabola opens upwards, the focus is 'p' units above the vertex. So, we need to add 'p' to the y-coordinate of the vertex. The x-coordinate of the focus remains the same as the vertex, since the focus lies on the axis of symmetry, which is a vertical line passing through the vertex. Therefore, the coordinates of the focus are (2, -2 + 2), which simplifies to (2, 0). There we have it! We've successfully located the focus of the parabola.

Let's recap the process: we started with the general equation, transformed it into vertex form by completing the square, identified the vertex coordinates, calculated 'p', and finally, used the direction the parabola opens to find the focus. Each step builds upon the previous one, making the overall process logical and straightforward. Understanding the direction of the opening is crucial. Had the parabola opened downwards (if 'a' were negative), we would have subtracted 'p' from the y-coordinate of the vertex. Always remember to visualize the parabola's orientation; it's a powerful check on your calculations.

Perpendicular Line Equation: A Step-by-Step Guide

Aug 7, 2025 by ADMIN 50 views

Hey guys! Today, we're diving into a fun geometry problem where we'll find the equation of a line that passes through a specific point and is perpendicular to another line segment. Let's break it down step by step so you can conquer similar problems with confidence.

Problem Overview

We've got a triangle ABC defined by three points: A(3, 8), B(7, 5), and C(2, 3). Our mission, should we choose to accept it, is to find the equation of a line that goes through point A and is perpendicular to the line segment BC. Sounds exciting, right? Let's get started!

Step 1: Finding the Slope of BC

The first step in our quest is to figure out the slope of the line segment BC. Remember, the slope (often denoted as m) tells us how steep a line is. The formula for the slope between two points (x1, y1) and (x2, y2) is:

m = (y2 - y1) / (x2 - x1)

Let's plug in the coordinates of B(7, 5) and C(2, 3) into this formula:

m = (3 - 5) / (2 - 7) = -2 / -5 = 2/5

So, the slope of BC is 2/5. Keep this value handy; we'll need it soon!

Delving Deeper into Slope Calculation

Okay, guys, let’s really break down this slope thing. Imagine you’re hiking up a hill. The slope is how much you go up (the “rise”) for every step you take forward (the “run”). If the slope is a big number, the hill is steep! If it’s a small number, the hill is gentle. A negative slope means you’re walking downhill.

In our case, the slope of BC is 2/5. This means that for every 5 units we move horizontally (the “run”) along the line BC, we move 2 units vertically (the “rise”). The formula m = (y2 - y1) / (x2 - x1) is just a way to calculate this rise-over-run mathematically, no matter how the line is positioned on our graph. When we calculated the slope using the coordinates of points B and C, the negative signs canceled each other out, giving us a positive slope, which indicates that the line segment BC is sloping upwards from left to right. Remember, the order of the points matters in the subtraction, but as long as you subtract the y-coordinates and the x-coordinates in the same order, you’ll get the correct slope. For instance, we could have also calculated the slope as (5 - 3) / (7 - 2) = 2 / 5, arriving at the same result. Understanding the slope is absolutely crucial for tackling any problem involving lines, so make sure you’ve got this concept locked down! This foundation will help us later when we consider perpendicular lines and their unique slope relationship.

Step 2: Finding the Slope of the Perpendicular Line

Now, for the fun part! We need to find the slope of a line that's perpendicular to BC. Here's a key concept: perpendicular lines have slopes that are negative reciprocals of each other. This means if one line has a slope of m, a line perpendicular to it will have a slope of -1/m.

Since the slope of BC is 2/5, the slope of the perpendicular line is:

m_perpendicular = -1 / (2/5) = -5/2

So, the line we're trying to find has a slope of -5/2.

The Magic of Negative Reciprocals: Why Perpendicular Lines Behave This Way

Alright, let’s unravel the mystery behind why perpendicular lines have slopes that are negative reciprocals of each other. This might sound like a mathematical trick, but there’s a beautiful geometric reason why it works. Imagine two lines intersecting at a perfect 90-degree angle, forming that crisp right angle we all know and love. Now, think about the slopes of these lines. The slope, as we discussed earlier, tells us about the steepness and direction of a line.

If we have one line with a certain slope, the perpendicular line is essentially “tipping” the original line by 90 degrees. This tipping action has two key effects on the slope. First, it flips the rise and run. If the original line goes “up 2 and over 5,” the perpendicular line will go “over 2 and down 5” (or vice versa). This is where the reciprocal part comes in – we’re essentially inverting the fraction that represents the slope. Second, the perpendicular line changes the direction of the slope. If the original line is going uphill (positive slope), the perpendicular line will be going downhill (negative slope), and vice versa. This is where the negative sign comes in. The combination of flipping the rise and run (reciprocal) and changing the direction (negative sign) is what gives us the negative reciprocal relationship. You can even visualize this by drawing two perpendicular lines on a graph and carefully measuring their slopes. You’ll see this relationship play out right before your eyes! This understanding not only helps you solve problems but also deepens your appreciation for the elegant connections within geometry.

Step 3: Using the Point-Slope Form

We've got the slope of our perpendicular line (-5/2) and a point it passes through, A(3, 8). Now we can use the point-slope form of a linear equation:

y - y1 = m(x - x1)

where m is the slope and (x1, y1) is the point. Plugging in our values:

y - 8 = (-5/2)(x - 3)

This is a perfectly valid equation for our line, but let's clean it up and put it in slope-intercept form (y = mx + b), which is often preferred.

Point-Slope Form: Your Secret Weapon for Line Equations

Let's take a moment to appreciate the power of the point-slope form. This little equation is a true workhorse when it comes to finding the equation of a line. Why? Because it directly incorporates two pieces of information that we often have on hand: a point on the line and the slope of the line. The point-slope form, y - y1 = m(x - x1), might look a bit intimidating at first glance, but it’s actually quite intuitive. Let's break it down. The m, as we know, represents the slope of the line. The (x1, y1) represents the coordinates of a specific point that the line passes through. The x and y without subscripts are the variables that will appear in the final equation of the line, allowing us to plug in any x-value and find the corresponding y-value on the line. The beauty of this form lies in its directness. Once you have a point and a slope, you can simply plug them into the equation and you're well on your way to finding the line's equation. There's no need to calculate the y-intercept separately or juggle multiple steps. Think of it as a shortcut – a direct route from the information you have to the equation you need. Mastering the point-slope form will significantly speed up your problem-solving and give you a deeper understanding of how lines are represented mathematically. It's a fundamental tool in your mathematical arsenal!

Step 4: Converting to Slope-Intercept Form

To get to slope-intercept form (y = mx + b), we need to distribute the -5/2 and then isolate y:

y - 8 = (-5/2)x + (15/2)

Now, add 8 to both sides. Remember that 8 is the same as 16/2, so:

y = (-5/2)x + (15/2) + (16/2)

y = (-5/2)x + 31/2

Slope-Intercept Form: Unlocking the Secrets of a Line's Personality

Ah, the slope-intercept form: y = mx + b. This equation is like the DNA of a line, revealing its key characteristics at a single glance. Let’s decode what each part of this equation tells us. The m, as we've emphasized, is the slope. It tells us the steepness and direction of the line. A larger absolute value of m means a steeper line; a positive m means the line goes uphill from left to right, and a negative m means it goes downhill. But what about the b? This is the y-intercept, the point where the line crosses the y-axis. It's the value of y when x is 0. The y-intercept gives us a fixed point on the line, a starting point from which the line extends according to its slope. Together, the slope and y-intercept paint a complete picture of a line's position and orientation on the coordinate plane. Imagine you’re directing someone to draw a line. You could simply say, “Start at the point (0, b), and for every 1 unit you move to the right, move up (or down) m units.” That’s the essence of the slope-intercept form! Being able to quickly recognize and interpret the slope and y-intercept makes it incredibly easy to graph lines, compare lines, and understand their relationships. It’s a fundamental skill that unlocks a deeper understanding of linear equations and their applications. Think of slope-intercept form as the language in which lines communicate their essential properties.

Answer

So, the equation of the line passing through point A and perpendicular to BC is:

y = (-5/2)x + 31/2

In the requested format:

y = −5/2{-5/2}−5/2x + 31/2{31/2}31/2

Conclusion

There you have it! We successfully found the equation of the line perpendicular to BC and passing through A. Remember the key steps: find the slope of the original line, calculate the negative reciprocal for the perpendicular slope, use the point-slope form, and convert to slope-intercept form. Practice makes perfect, so try some similar problems to solidify your understanding. You got this!

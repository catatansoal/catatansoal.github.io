Calculating Distance On Inclined Plane A Physics Problem

Jul 18, 2025 by ADMIN 57 views

Hey guys! Let's dive into a classic physics problem: figuring out how far a ball rolls down an inclined plane during specific time intervals. We'll break it down step by step, making sure it's super clear and easy to understand. We will specifically focus on how to determine the distance the ball travels in the 1st second (from t=0 to t=1 second) and the 2nd second (from t=1 to t=2 seconds). This is a fundamental concept in kinematics, and mastering it will help you tackle a bunch of other physics problems.

Understanding the Physics Behind Inclined Plane Motion

Before we jump into the calculations, let's quickly recap the physics involved. When a ball rolls down an inclined plane, it experiences acceleration due to gravity. This acceleration isn't the full acceleration due to gravity (g ≈ 9.8 m/s²) because the inclined plane provides a supporting force, called the normal force. The component of gravity acting parallel to the inclined plane is what causes the ball to accelerate downwards.

Think of it this way: if the plane were perfectly vertical, the ball would fall straight down with acceleration g. But since the plane is tilted, only a portion of gravity pulls the ball down the slope. This component is gsin(θ), where θ is the angle of inclination of the plane. Understanding this is crucial because it gives us the constant acceleration (a) that we need for our calculations. In the real world, friction and the ball's moment of inertia also play roles, but for this problem, we're likely dealing with an idealized scenario where we can ignore these factors to simplify the calculations.

To calculate the distance the ball rolls, we'll use one of the fundamental equations of motion, often called the SUVAT equations. These equations relate displacement (s), initial velocity (u), final velocity (v), acceleration (a), and time (t). The specific equation we'll use is:

s = ut + (1/2)at²

This equation is perfect for our situation because it allows us to calculate the distance (s) if we know the initial velocity (u), acceleration (a), and time (t). Now, let's apply this to our specific time intervals.

(a) Distance in the 1st Second (t = 0 to t = 1 second)

Okay, let's figure out how far the ball rolls in the first second. This is a pretty straightforward application of the equation we just discussed.

First, let's identify what we know:

  • Initial time (t₀): 0 seconds
  • Final time (t): 1 second
  • Time interval (Δt): t - t₀ = 1 second
  • Initial velocity (u): We'll assume the ball starts from rest, so u = 0 cm/s (or m/s, depending on the units you want to use later).
  • Acceleration (a): This is where it gets slightly tricky. We know the acceleration is related to the component of gravity acting along the inclined plane, which is a = gsin(θ). However, the problem doesn't give us the angle θ or the acceleration a directly. We need this value to calculate the distance. Without knowing the acceleration a, we can't get a numerical answer for the distance. Let's assume for a moment that the acceleration a is given or has been previously calculated to be, say, 10 cm/s². This is just for illustration; in a real problem, you'd need to determine a from the angle of the incline or other provided information.

Now we have all the pieces we need for our equation:

s = ut + (1/2)at²

Plugging in our values:

s = (0 cm/s)(1 s) + (1/2)(10 cm/s²)(1 s)² s = 0 + (1/2)(10 cm/s²)(1 s²) s = 5 cm

So, if the acceleration is 10 cm/s², the ball rolls 5 cm in the first second. Remember, this result is entirely dependent on the value of the acceleration a. If a were different, the distance would also be different. In a real-world problem, you would need to calculate a first using a = gsin(θ) if you know the angle of inclination θ. If you have other information, like the final velocity after a certain time, you could use other kinematic equations to find a.

In summary, to accurately determine the distance in the first second, you absolutely need to know the acceleration. If you have the angle of the inclined plane, use a = gsin(θ). If you have other information, use the appropriate kinematic equation to solve for a first, and then plug it into our main equation s = ut + (1/2)at².

(b) Distance in the 2nd Second (t = 1 to t = 2 seconds)

Alright, let's tackle the second part: finding the distance the ball rolls during the 2nd second. This means we're looking at the time interval between t = 1 second and t = 2 seconds. This is slightly trickier than the first second because the ball already has some velocity at t = 1 second. We can't just assume the initial velocity is zero for this interval.

Here's how we'll approach it:

  1. Calculate the distance traveled from t = 0 to t = 2 seconds. We'll use the same equation s = ut + (1/2)at², but this time with t = 2 seconds.
  2. Subtract the distance traveled in the first second (which we already calculated in part (a)) from the distance traveled in the first two seconds. This will give us the distance traveled specifically during the 2nd second.

Let's break it down step-by-step.

Step 1: Distance from t = 0 to t = 2 seconds

  • Initial time (t₀): 0 seconds
  • Final time (t): 2 seconds
  • Time interval (Δt): 2 seconds
  • Initial velocity (u): Still assuming the ball starts from rest, u = 0 cm/s.
  • Acceleration (a): We'll use the same assumed acceleration as before for illustrative purposes: a = 10 cm/s². Remember, in a real problem, you need to determine a based on the given information.

Now, plug these values into our equation:

s = ut + (1/2)at² s = (0 cm/s)(2 s) + (1/2)(10 cm/s²)(2 s)² s = 0 + (1/2)(10 cm/s²)(4 s²) s = 20 cm

So, the ball rolls 20 cm in the first two seconds (from t = 0 to t = 2 seconds), assuming our acceleration of 10 cm/s².

Step 2: Subtract the Distance Traveled in the First Second

We already calculated that the ball travels 5 cm in the first second (part (a)). Now we subtract this from the total distance traveled in the first two seconds:

Distance in 2nd second = Distance (t=0 to t=2) - Distance (t=0 to t=1) Distance in 2nd second = 20 cm - 5 cm Distance in 2nd second = 15 cm

Therefore, the ball rolls 15 cm during the 2nd second, again assuming an acceleration of 10 cm/s². This makes sense, right? Since the ball is constantly accelerating, it should cover more distance in the 2nd second compared to the 1st second.

Alternative Method: Using the Initial Velocity at t = 1 second

There's another way to approach this, which can be helpful for reinforcing your understanding of these concepts. We can calculate the velocity of the ball at t = 1 second and then use that as the initial velocity for the 2nd second.

First, let's find the velocity at t = 1 second using another kinematic equation:

v = u + at

Where:

  • v = final velocity
  • u = initial velocity (0 cm/s)
  • a = acceleration (10 cm/s²)
  • t = time (1 s)

v = 0 cm/s + (10 cm/s²)(1 s) v = 10 cm/s

So, the ball's velocity at t = 1 second is 10 cm/s. Now, we can use this as our u for calculating the distance in the 2nd second.

  • Initial velocity (u) for the 2nd second: 10 cm/s
  • Time (t): 1 second (the duration of the 2nd second)
  • Acceleration (a): 10 cm/s²

Plugging these into s = ut + (1/2)at²:

s = (10 cm/s)(1 s) + (1/2)(10 cm/s²)(1 s)² s = 10 cm + 5 cm s = 15 cm

We get the same answer, 15 cm, which confirms our previous calculation. This alternative method highlights how the final velocity of one interval becomes the initial velocity of the next, a crucial concept in understanding motion with constant acceleration.

Key Takeaways and Final Thoughts

Whew! We've covered a lot in this explanation. The key takeaway here is that calculating the distance a ball rolls on an inclined plane during specific time intervals requires a solid understanding of kinematics, particularly the equations of motion. Remember these crucial points:

  • Acceleration is key: You must know or be able to calculate the acceleration of the ball down the plane. This is often related to the angle of the incline (a = gsin(θ)).
  • Initial velocity matters: For intervals beyond the first second, the initial velocity is not zero. You need to calculate it based on the previous motion.
  • Choose the right equation: The equation s = ut + (1/2)at² is your best friend for these types of problems.
  • Units, units, units: Make sure you're consistent with your units. If you're using cm/s² for acceleration, make sure your distances are in centimeters and your times are in seconds.

By breaking the problem down into smaller steps and carefully considering the physics involved, you can confidently solve these types of inclined plane problems. Keep practicing, guys, and you'll be physics pros in no time! Remember to always look for the given information and think about which equations will help you connect those pieces to what you're trying to find. Good luck, and happy problem-solving!

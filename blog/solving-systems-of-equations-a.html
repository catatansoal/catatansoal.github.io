Method 2: Substitution â€“ The Art of Swapping Variables

The substitution method is another fantastic way to solve systems of equations. In this method, we solve one equation for one variable and then substitute that expression into the other equation. This effectively turns our two-variable problem into a single-variable problem, which is much easier to handle. The key to success with the substitution method is choosing the right variable to isolate and substitute. Ideally, we want to pick a variable that has a simple coefficient (like 1 or -1) in one of the equations, as this will minimize the chances of dealing with messy fractions. The substitution method is like a mathematical chameleon, adapting the equations to suit our needs. It allows us to reshape the problem, transforming it into a more manageable form. Think of it like carefully rearranging pieces in a puzzle to create a clearer picture. By isolating one variable and substituting its equivalent expression into the other equation, we're essentially streamlining the problem, eliminating one unknown and focusing on the remaining one. This approach not only simplifies the algebra but also provides a different perspective on the relationships between the variables. So, let's explore how we can apply this method to our system of equations, carefully selecting our target variable and preparing for the substitution magic to unfold.

Looking at our equations:

12x + 15y = 34
-6x + 5y = 3

The second equation seems easier to manipulate. Let's solve it for y:

5y = 6x + 3
y = (6x + 3) / 5

Excellent! We've successfully isolated y in terms of x. This is a pivotal step in the substitution method, as we've now expressed one variable as a function of the other. Think of this as creating a mathematical bridge, connecting y to x in a clear and concise way. The expression y = (6x + 3) / 5 is our key to unlocking the solution, as it allows us to replace y in the first equation with an equivalent expression involving only x. This substitution will transform our two-variable problem into a single-variable equation, making it much easier to solve. The beauty of this approach lies in its elegance and efficiency. By carefully manipulating the equations, we've created a path towards simplification, streamlining the problem and focusing our efforts on a single variable. So, with our expression for y in hand, let's proceed to the next step: substituting it into the first equation and watching the substitution magic unfold. This is where the power of algebra truly shines, as we transform complex equations into manageable solutions.

Now, substitute this expression for y into the first equation:

12x + 15 * ((6x + 3) / 5) = 34

This might look a bit intimidating, but don't worry, we'll simplify it step by step. The key here is to carefully distribute and combine like terms. This is where our algebraic skills come into play, as we navigate the equation and reshape it into a more manageable form. Think of this process as untangling a knot, carefully teasing apart the strands until the solution becomes clear. The substitution of y = (6x + 3) / 5 into the first equation is a crucial step, as it transforms our two-variable problem into a single-variable equation. This is the essence of the substitution method: reducing complexity by eliminating one variable and focusing on the other. The equation we've obtained might look a bit daunting at first, but with careful manipulation, we'll soon reveal its hidden solution. So, let's roll up our sleeves and tackle this simplification, using our knowledge of algebraic operations to unlock the value of x. Each step we take brings us closer to the final answer, and the satisfaction of solving a challenging equation is well worth the effort.

Simplifying, we get:

12x + 3 * (6x + 3) = 34
12x + 18x + 9 = 34
30x = 25
x = 25 / 30 = 5 / 6

Fantastic! We've found x using the substitution method! It's like discovering a hidden treasure, the numerical value that satisfies our equation. This is a testament to the power of algebraic manipulation and the elegance of the substitution method. The journey might have involved a few twists and turns, but the result is well worth the effort. With x = 5/6 in hand, we're one step closer to solving our system of equations completely. Now that we know x, we can use it to find y, completing our solution and bringing harmony to our system of equations. So, with x securely in our grasp, let's move on to the final stage: substituting it back into our expression for y and revealing the value that completes our mathematical masterpiece.

Narayana Numbers: Peaks, High Peaks, And Dyck Paths

Aug 7, 2025 by ADMIN 52 views
Iklan Headers

Hey there, math enthusiasts! Ever stumbled upon a number that seems to pop up in the most unexpected places? Well, let's talk about the Narayana numbers, those fascinating little integers that keep showing up in the world of combinatorics. Today, we're diving deep into a specific, super cool property: the fact that Narayana numbers count both peaks and "high peaks" in Dyck paths. Buckle up, because it's going to be a fun ride!

What are Narayana Numbers?

First things first, what exactly are Narayana numbers? The Narayana number Nn,kN_{n,k}Nn,k​ is defined as:

Nn,k=1n(nk)(nk−1)N_{n,k} = \frac{1}{n} \binom{n}{k} \binom{n}{k-1}Nn,k​=n1​(kn​)(k−1n​)

Where:

  • nnn and kkk are positive integers
  • (nk)\binom{n}{k}(kn​) is the binomial coefficient, read as "n choose k"

Okay, that's the formula. But what do they mean? Narayana numbers pop up as the answer to a surprising number of counting problems. For example, Nn,kN_{n,k}Nn,k​ counts:

  • Binary trees with n nodes and k leaves.
  • Dyck paths of semilength n with k peaks.
  • Certain types of ballot sequences.
  • And much more!

It's this versatility that makes Narayana numbers so intriguing. The formula is elegant, but it's the diverse interpretations that make them truly special. These numbers provide a fundamental connection between different combinatorial structures.

Delving into Dyck Paths and Their Peaks

Dyck paths are a fundamental concept needed to understand the problem. A Dyck path of semilength n is a path in the Cartesian plane from (0, 0) to (2n, 0), consisting of n up steps (U) and n down steps (D), while always staying above the x-axis. Think of them as staircases that never go below ground level. A peak in a Dyck path is simply an occurrence of an up step immediately followed by a down step (UD). So, you go up, and then immediately down. High peaks are those peaks that occur at a height strictly greater than one.

Now, the fun part! It turns out that Nn,kN_{n,k}Nn,k​ also counts Dyck paths of semilength n with k peaks. For instance, let's say n = 3. The Dyck paths of semilength 3 are:

  1. UUUDDD (2 peaks)
  2. UUDDUD (2 peaks)
  3. UUDUDD (2 peaks)
  4. UDUUDD (2 peaks)
  5. UDUDUD (3 peaks)

Let's calculate the Narayana numbers for n = 3:

  • N3,1=13(31)(30)=1N_{3,1} = \frac{1}{3} \binom{3}{1} \binom{3}{0} = 1N3,1​=31​(13​)(03​)=1
  • N3,2=13(32)(31)=3N_{3,2} = \frac{1}{3} \binom{3}{2} \binom{3}{1} = 3N3,2​=31​(23​)(13​)=3
  • N3,3=13(33)(32)=1N_{3,3} = \frac{1}{3} \binom{3}{3} \binom{3}{2} = 1N3,3​=31​(33​)(23​)=1

If we look at all Dyck Paths of length 3, we can observe that:

  • One Dyck path has 1 peak
  • Three Dyck paths have 2 peaks
  • One Dyck path has 3 peaks

The Bijection: Connecting Peaks and High Peaks

So, here's the million-dollar question: Is there a bijection that directly explains why the Narayana numbers count both peaks and high peaks? In other words, can we find a one-to-one correspondence between Dyck paths with k peaks and Dyck paths with k high peaks?

The answer is yes! While there isn't one single, universally agreed-upon "obvious" bijection, several clever constructions achieve this. Let's explore one such approach.

A Bijection Involving Valley Decomposition

One elegant bijection involves the concept of valley decomposition. A valley is an occurrence of a down step followed by an up step (DU). The depth of a valley is the y-coordinate at which it occurs.

The Bijection Idea:

  1. Transform Peaks to Valleys: Take a Dyck path with k peaks. For each peak at height 1 (a peak that's not a high peak), perform a local transformation to turn it into a valley at height 0.
  2. Adjust the Path: After this transformation, you'll no longer have a valid Dyck path (it might go below the x-axis). However, you can adjust the path by reflecting the portion below the x-axis about the x-axis. This will create a new Dyck path.
  3. Mapping to High Peaks: The number of high peaks in the original path now corresponds to the total number of peaks in this new path.

Example:

Let's say we have the Dyck path UDUUDD. This path has two peaks, one at height 1 and one at height 2. The high peak is the one at height 2. Since we have one peak at height 1, we will want to transform this path to have a lower height.

  • Original path: UDUUDD
  • Peaks: 2
  • High Peaks: 1

To turn the peak at height one to a valley at height zero:

  • Transformed Path: DDUUDD

To change this into a valid Dyck Path, flip the prefix DD to UU:

  • New Path: UUUUDD
  • Peaks: 1
  • High Peaks: 0

Notice how the one high peak in the original path corresponds to the one peak in the new path. This bijection helps map the number of high peaks to the number of peaks.

Why This Works

The beauty of this bijection lies in its reversibility. You can uniquely reconstruct the original Dyck path from the transformed one. The key is tracking which valleys were originally peaks at height 1. This ensures that the mapping is one-to-one.

Significance and Implications

This bijection isn't just a mathematical curiosity. It highlights a deeper connection between different combinatorial interpretations of the Narayana numbers. It tells us that, at a fundamental level, counting peaks and counting high peaks in Dyck paths are really the same problem, just seen through different lenses. In conclusion, the Narayana numbers are truly remarkable combinatorial objects.

Solving X/5 = 8/(x-3) A Step-by-Step Guide

This type of equation, involving fractions with variables in the denominator, might seem intimidating at first, but don't worry, we'll break it down step by step. Think of it as a puzzle – a mathematical puzzle! – where we need to find the value(s) of x that make the equation true. Understanding how to tackle these kinds of problems is super useful not just for math class, but also for everyday problem-solving. The core concepts we'll explore here, such as cross-multiplication and solving quadratic equations, are fundamental tools in algebra and beyond. We will cover every aspect of the equation and guide you through the process until you feel completely comfortable solving similar problems yourself. Remember, math isn't about memorizing formulas; it's about understanding the underlying logic and principles. By the end of this guide, you’ll not only know how to solve this specific equation but also have a solid foundation for tackling more complex algebraic challenges. We'll make sure to explain each step in detail, providing clear explanations and examples along the way. So, grab your pencil and paper, and let's begin our journey to unlock the secrets of this equation!

Understanding the Equation x/5 = 8/(x-3)

Before we jump into solving, let's make sure we understand what the equation x/5 = 8/(x-3) actually means. At its heart, this is a statement of equality between two fractions. The left side, x/5, represents a fraction where x is the numerator and 5 is the denominator. The right side, 8/(x-3), is another fraction with 8 as the numerator and (x-3) as the denominator. Our goal is to find the value(s) of x that make these two fractions equal. To make things clearer, let's think about what the expression (x-3) in the denominator means. It's simply saying that we're taking the value of x and subtracting 3 from it. This introduces an important consideration: we need to make sure that (x-3) doesn't equal zero, because division by zero is undefined in mathematics. So, right off the bat, we know that x cannot be 3. This is called a restriction on the variable, and it's crucial to keep in mind as we solve. Why is understanding this initial setup so important? Well, it lays the groundwork for our solution strategy. Recognizing the structure of the equation – two fractions set equal to each other – tells us that cross-multiplication is a good approach. And identifying the restriction on x ensures that we don't end up with a nonsensical answer. Furthermore, understanding the equation conceptually helps us avoid common mistakes. For instance, students sometimes try to add or subtract terms across the equals sign without properly handling the fractions. But by focusing on the fundamental meaning of equality between fractions, we can approach the problem with confidence and clarity. This initial understanding is the first step towards successfully solving the equation and mastering this type of algebraic problem. So, make sure you're comfortable with the concepts we've discussed before moving on to the next step!

Step-by-Step Solution: Solving x/5 = 8/(x-3)

Okay, guys, let's get down to business and solve the equation x/5 = 8/(x-3) step by step. The most common and efficient way to tackle this kind of equation is by using a technique called cross-multiplication. Cross-multiplication is a nifty shortcut that works when you have two fractions set equal to each other. Basically, you multiply the numerator of the first fraction by the denominator of the second fraction, and vice versa. In our case, this means multiplying x by (x-3) and 5 by 8. This gives us the equation: x(x-3) = 5 * 8. See how we've transformed the original equation with fractions into a simpler equation without fractions? That's the magic of cross-multiplication! Now, let's simplify this new equation. First, we'll distribute the x on the left side: x^2 - 3x = 40. Next, we want to set the equation equal to zero because that's the standard form for a quadratic equation. So, we subtract 40 from both sides: x^2 - 3x - 40 = 0. Ta-da! We've arrived at a quadratic equation. But what exactly is a quadratic equation? It's an equation of the form ax^2 + bx + c = 0, where a, b, and c are constants. And luckily, there are several methods for solving quadratic equations, which we'll discuss in the next section. But for now, let's just appreciate how far we've come. We started with a fractional equation, used cross-multiplication to eliminate the fractions, and ended up with a quadratic equation. This is a classic example of how algebraic techniques can simplify complex problems into more manageable ones. So, remember the power of cross-multiplication when you encounter equations involving fractions! It's a valuable tool in your math toolkit.

Solving the Quadratic Equation: x² - 3x - 40 = 0

Now that we've arrived at the quadratic equation x² - 3x - 40 = 0, it's time to roll up our sleeves and find the values of x that satisfy this equation. There are a couple of popular methods for solving quadratic equations: factoring and the quadratic formula. Let's start by trying to factor the quadratic. Factoring involves breaking down the quadratic expression into two binomials (expressions with two terms). We're looking for two numbers that multiply to -40 (the constant term) and add up to -3 (the coefficient of the x term). After a bit of thinking, we can see that -8 and 5 fit the bill perfectly: (-8) * 5 = -40 and (-8) + 5 = -3. So, we can factor the quadratic as follows: (x - 8)(x + 5) = 0. Now, here comes a crucial principle: if the product of two factors is zero, then at least one of the factors must be zero. This is known as the zero-product property. Applying this to our factored equation, we get two possibilities: x - 8 = 0 or x + 5 = 0. Solving each of these linear equations gives us our solutions: x = 8 or x = -5. Great! We've found two potential solutions for x. But before we celebrate, we need to remember the restriction we identified earlier: x cannot be 3. Fortunately, neither of our solutions is 3, so we're in the clear. However, it's always a good practice to check our solutions by plugging them back into the original equation. This ensures that we haven't made any mistakes along the way. Let's try x = 8 first: 8/5 = 8/(8-3) => 8/5 = 8/5. This is true! Now let's try x = -5: -5/5 = 8/(-5-3) => -1 = 8/-8 => -1 = -1. This is also true! So, both x = 8 and x = -5 are valid solutions to our equation. If we couldn't find factors easily or prefer a more general method, we could also use the quadratic formula. The quadratic formula is a powerful tool that can solve any quadratic equation, regardless of whether it can be factored easily. It's a bit more involved, but it always works. Understanding both factoring and the quadratic formula gives you flexibility in solving quadratic equations. So, mastering these techniques is a valuable investment in your math skills!

Checking the Solutions and the Importance of Verification

Alright, we've found our solutions for x in the equation x/5 = 8/(x-3), which are x = 8 and x = -5. But hold on a second! We're not quite done yet. In mathematics, especially when dealing with equations that involve fractions or radicals, it's super important to check our solutions. Why? Because sometimes, we might end up with solutions that don't actually work when plugged back into the original equation. These are called extraneous solutions. They arise from the algebraic manipulations we perform to solve the equation, but they don't satisfy the original problem. In our case, we need to plug both x = 8 and x = -5 back into the original equation, x/5 = 8/(x-3), to make sure they hold true. We already did this in the previous section, but let's recap. For x = 8: 8/5 = 8/(8-3) => 8/5 = 8/5. This checks out! For x = -5: -5/5 = 8/(-5-3) => -1 = 8/-8 => -1 = -1. This also checks out! So, we can confidently say that both x = 8 and x = -5 are valid solutions. But what if one of our solutions didn't work? Well, that would mean it's an extraneous solution, and we would discard it. The importance of checking solutions can't be overstated. It's a crucial step in the problem-solving process that ensures the accuracy of our results. It also helps us catch any potential errors we might have made along the way. Think of it as a final quality control check before declaring victory! Furthermore, the act of verifying our solutions deepens our understanding of the equation and the solution process. It reinforces the connection between the algebraic manipulations and the original problem. So, always make checking your solutions a habit, especially when dealing with fractional equations, radical equations, or any situation where extraneous solutions might arise. It's a small investment of time that can save you from making big mistakes!

Alternative Methods and Further Exploration

While we've successfully solved the equation x/5 = 8/(x-3) using cross-multiplication and factoring, it's always beneficial to explore alternative methods and delve deeper into the concepts involved. This not only broadens our problem-solving toolkit but also enhances our understanding of the underlying mathematical principles. For instance, instead of factoring the quadratic equation x² - 3x - 40 = 0, we could have used the quadratic formula. The quadratic formula is a powerful tool that can solve any quadratic equation, regardless of whether it can be factored easily. It's given by: x = [-b ± √(b² - 4ac)] / 2a, where a, b, and c are the coefficients of the quadratic equation ax² + bx + c = 0. In our case, a = 1, b = -3, and c = -40. Plugging these values into the quadratic formula, we would arrive at the same solutions: x = 8 and x = -5. Using the quadratic formula provides a valuable backup method and is particularly useful when factoring is difficult or impossible. Another avenue for exploration is to consider the graphical interpretation of the equation. The equation x/5 = 8/(x-3) can be viewed as the intersection of two functions: y = x/5 and y = 8/(x-3). Graphing these functions would visually show us the points where the two curves intersect, and the x-coordinates of these points would be our solutions. This graphical approach provides a different perspective on the problem and can be especially helpful for visualizing solutions and understanding the behavior of functions. Furthermore, we can explore variations of this type of equation. What if the denominators were more complex expressions? What if there were more terms involved? By tackling similar but slightly different problems, we can solidify our understanding and develop our problem-solving skills even further. This type of exploration also encourages us to think critically about the steps involved in solving these equations and to identify the most efficient strategies.

Differentiating Y=ln(x)/x² A Step-by-Step Guide

Jul 18, 2025 by ADMIN 48 views

Hey guys! Today, we're diving into a classic calculus problem: differentiating the function y = ln(x)/x². This might seem a bit intimidating at first, but don't worry, we'll break it down step-by-step. We'll use the quotient rule, a fundamental concept in differential calculus, to solve this. By the end of this guide, you'll not only know how to differentiate this specific function but also understand the underlying principles that can be applied to a wide range of similar problems. So, let's get started and conquer this derivative together!

Understanding the Quotient Rule

Before we jump into differentiating y = ln(x)/x², let's quickly recap the quotient rule. This rule is essential for finding the derivative of a function that is expressed as a ratio of two other functions. In simpler terms, if you have a function like y = u(x) / v(x), where both u(x) and v(x) are differentiable functions, then the derivative of y with respect to x, denoted as dy/dx, can be found using the following formula:

dy/dx = [v(x) * u'(x) - u(x) * v'(x)] / [v(x)]²

Where:

  • u'(x) represents the derivative of u(x) with respect to x.
  • v'(x) represents the derivative of v(x) with respect to x.

This formula might look a bit complex, but it's actually quite straightforward once you understand its components. The numerator involves the denominator multiplied by the derivative of the numerator, minus the numerator multiplied by the derivative of the denominator. The entire expression is then divided by the square of the original denominator. Mastering this rule is crucial for tackling many calculus problems, and it's a tool you'll use frequently. Now that we've refreshed our understanding of the quotient rule, we're ready to apply it to our specific problem: differentiating y = ln(x)/x².

Applying the Quotient Rule to y = ln(x)/x²

Alright, let's get to the fun part: applying the quotient rule to our function, y = ln(x)/x². To do this, we first need to identify our u(x) and v(x). Looking at our function, it's clear that:

  • u(x) = ln(x) (the natural logarithm of x)
  • v(x) = x² (x squared)

Now that we've identified u(x) and v(x), the next step is to find their respective derivatives, u'(x) and v'(x). This is where our knowledge of basic differentiation rules comes into play. Remember, the derivative of ln(x) is 1/x, and the derivative of x² is 2x. Therefore:

  • u'(x) = 1/x
  • v'(x) = 2x

With u(x), v(x), u'(x), and v'(x) all identified, we can now plug these into the quotient rule formula we discussed earlier:

dy/dx = [v(x) * u'(x) - u(x) * v'(x)] / [v(x)]²

Substituting the values, we get:

dy/dx = [x² * (1/x) - ln(x) * (2x)] / [x²]²

This is the initial application of the quotient rule. The next step is to simplify this expression, which will lead us to our final answer. Stay with me, and we'll simplify this expression step-by-step.

Simplifying the Expression

Now that we've applied the quotient rule, we have the expression:

dy/dx = [x² * (1/x) - ln(x) * (2x)] / [x²]²

The next step is to simplify this expression to make it more manageable. Let's start by simplifying the numerator. We have two terms in the numerator: x² * (1/x) and ln(x) * (2x). Let's simplify each of these individually.

The first term, x² * (1/x), can be simplified by canceling out one of the x's in the numerator with the x in the denominator. This leaves us with just x.

The second term, ln(x) * (2x), can be rewritten as 2x * ln(x). There's not much more we can simplify here, so we'll leave it as is for now.

Now, let's rewrite the numerator with these simplified terms:

Numerator = x - 2x * ln(x)

Next, let's simplify the denominator. We have [x²]², which means x² raised to the power of 2. Using the power of a power rule, we multiply the exponents, giving us x⁴.

Denominator = x⁴

Now we can rewrite our entire expression with the simplified numerator and denominator:

dy/dx = [x - 2x * ln(x)] / x⁴

We're not quite done yet! We can further simplify this expression by factoring out an x from the numerator and then canceling it with one of the x's in the denominator. This will give us our final simplified form of the derivative.

Final Simplification and the Answer

Let's continue simplifying our expression:

dy/dx = [x - 2x * ln(x)] / x⁴

As we discussed, we can factor out an x from the numerator. This gives us:

dy/dx = x * [1 - 2 * ln(x)] / x⁴

Now, we can cancel out one x from the numerator and one x from the denominator. This leaves us with x³ in the denominator:

dy/dx = [1 - 2 * ln(x)] / x³

And there you have it! This is the fully simplified derivative of y = ln(x)/x². We've successfully applied the quotient rule, simplified the expression, and arrived at our final answer. This result tells us how the function y changes with respect to changes in x. It's a powerful piece of information that can be used for various applications, such as finding the slope of the tangent line at a specific point on the curve or determining the function's increasing and decreasing intervals.

Therefore, the derivative of y = ln(x)/x² is dy/dx = [1 - 2 * ln(x)] / x³.

Practical Applications of Differentiation

Now that we've successfully differentiated y = ln(x)/x², you might be wondering, "What's the point of all this?" Well, differentiation is a fundamental concept in calculus with a wide range of practical applications in various fields. Let's explore some of these applications to see why understanding differentiation is so important.

One of the most common applications of differentiation is in finding the slope of a tangent line to a curve at a specific point. The derivative of a function at a particular point gives us the instantaneous rate of change of the function at that point, which is precisely the slope of the tangent line. This is incredibly useful in fields like physics and engineering, where we often need to analyze the rate of change of various quantities.

Differentiation is also crucial in optimization problems. These problems involve finding the maximum or minimum value of a function, such as maximizing profit or minimizing cost. By finding the critical points of a function (where the derivative is zero or undefined), we can determine the points where the function reaches its maximum or minimum values. This is widely used in economics, business, and engineering.

In physics, differentiation is used extensively to describe motion. For example, the derivative of a position function gives us the velocity function, and the derivative of the velocity function gives us the acceleration function. Understanding these relationships is essential for analyzing the motion of objects and solving problems in mechanics.

Differentiation also plays a vital role in related rates problems. These problems involve finding the rate of change of one quantity in terms of the rate of change of another quantity. For example, we might want to find how the volume of a balloon is changing as we inflate it, given the rate at which the radius is increasing. Differentiation provides the tools we need to solve these types of problems.

These are just a few examples of the many practical applications of differentiation. From physics and engineering to economics and computer science, differentiation is a powerful tool that helps us understand and model the world around us. By mastering the concepts and techniques of differentiation, you'll be well-equipped to tackle a wide range of problems in various fields.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

When differentiating functions, especially those involving the quotient rule, it's easy to make mistakes if you're not careful. Let's go over some common mistakes that students often make and how to avoid them. By being aware of these pitfalls, you can increase your accuracy and confidence in solving differentiation problems.

One of the most common mistakes is misapplying the quotient rule itself. Remember, the quotient rule states that if y = u(x) / v(x), then dy/dx = [v(x) * u'(x) - u(x) * v'(x)] / [v(x)]². It's crucial to get the order of the terms in the numerator correct. A common mistake is to subtract the terms in the wrong order, resulting in an incorrect answer. Always double-check that you're subtracting u(x) * v'(x) from v(x) * u'(x), not the other way around.

Another frequent mistake is incorrectly finding the derivatives of u(x) and v(x). For example, students might forget the derivative of ln(x) is 1/x or make errors when differentiating polynomial terms. It's essential to have a solid understanding of basic differentiation rules and to practice them regularly. Keep a list of common derivatives handy and refer to it as needed.

Simplifying the expression after applying the quotient rule can also be a source of errors. Students might make mistakes when factoring, canceling terms, or applying algebraic rules. Take your time when simplifying and double-check each step. It's often helpful to break down the simplification process into smaller, more manageable steps to reduce the chance of errors.

Forgetting to apply the chain rule when differentiating composite functions is another common mistake. If you have a function within a function, such as ln(x²), you need to use the chain rule to differentiate it correctly. Remember, the chain rule states that the derivative of a composite function is the derivative of the outer function evaluated at the inner function, multiplied by the derivative of the inner function.

Finally, carelessness with notation and signs can lead to errors. Make sure to write your derivatives clearly and use parentheses to avoid ambiguity. Pay close attention to signs, as a simple sign error can completely change the answer. It's always a good idea to review your work carefully and check for any potential errors before submitting your answer.

By being aware of these common mistakes and taking steps to avoid them, you can significantly improve your accuracy and confidence in differentiating functions. Remember to practice regularly, double-check your work, and don't hesitate to ask for help if you're struggling.

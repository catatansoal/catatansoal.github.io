Line Integral Calculation Around Sphere-Plane Intersection

Jul 13, 2025 by ADMIN 59 views

Hey guys! Let's dive into a fascinating problem involving vector fields and line integrals. We're going to explore how to calculate the line integral of a given vector field around the curve formed by the intersection of a unit sphere and a plane. Buckle up, because we're about to embark on a mathematical journey that combines geometry and calculus!

Setting the Stage

Imagine a unit sphere, a perfectly round ball with a radius of 1, described by the equation x2+y2+z2=1x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = 1x2+y2+z2=1. Now, picture a plane slicing through this sphere, defined by the equation x+y=14x + y = 14x+y=14. The intersection of these two surfaces creates a curve, a circle in this case. Our mission is to compute the line integral of the vector field F=⟨yz+y,xz+5x,xy+2y⟩F = \langle yz + y, xz + 5x, xy + 2y \rangleF=⟨yz+y,xz+5x,xy+2y⟩ around this intersection curve. This is where things get interesting, and we'll need some powerful tools to tackle this problem effectively.

The Power of Stokes' Theorem

One of the most elegant ways to solve this problem is by using Stokes' Theorem. Stokes' Theorem provides a bridge between line integrals and surface integrals. It states that the line integral of a vector field around a closed curve is equal to the surface integral of the curl of the vector field over any surface bounded by that curve. In mathematical notation, this theorem is expressed as:

∮CF⋅dr=∬S(∇×F)⋅n dA\oint_C F \cdot dr = \iint_S (\nabla \times F) \cdot n \ dA ∮C​F⋅dr=∬S​(∇×F)⋅n dA

Where:

  • FFF is the vector field.
  • CCC is the closed curve.
  • SSS is any surface bounded by CCC.
  • ∇×F\nabla \times F∇×F is the curl of FFF.
  • nnn is the unit normal vector to the surface SSS.
  • dAdAdA is the differential area element on the surface.

The beauty of Stokes' Theorem is that it allows us to transform a potentially complex line integral into a surface integral, which might be easier to compute depending on the geometry of the problem and the nature of the vector field. For our specific problem, we will leverage this theorem to simplify our calculations and find the line integral efficiently.

Computing the Curl

Before we can apply Stokes' Theorem, we need to compute the curl of the vector field F=⟨yz+y,xz+5x,xy+2y⟩F = \langle yz + y, xz + 5x, xy + 2y \rangleF=⟨yz+y,xz+5x,xy+2y⟩. The curl, denoted by ∇×F\nabla \times F∇×F, is a vector field that measures the rotation of FFF at a given point. It is calculated as the determinant of the following matrix:

∇×F=∣ijk∂∂x∂∂y∂∂zyz+yxz+5xxy+2y∣\nabla \times F = \begin{vmatrix} i & j & k \\ \frac{\partial}{\partial x} & \frac{\partial}{\partial y} & \frac{\partial}{\partial z} \\ yz + y & xz + 5x & xy + 2y \end{vmatrix} ∇×F=​i∂x∂​yz+y​j∂y∂​xz+5x​k∂z∂​xy+2y​​

Where iii, jjj, and kkk are the unit vectors in the xxx, yyy, and zzz directions, respectively. Let's break down the calculation:

  1. i-component: ∂∂y(xy+2y)−∂∂z(xz+5x)=(x+2)−x=2\frac{\partial}{\partial y}(xy + 2y) - \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(xz + 5x) = (x + 2) - x = 2∂y∂​(xy+2y)−∂z∂​(xz+5x)=(x+2)−x=2
  2. j-component: ∂∂z(yz+y)−∂∂x(xy+2y)=y−y=0\frac{\partial}{\partial z}(yz + y) - \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(xy + 2y) = y - y = 0∂z∂​(yz+y)−∂x∂​(xy+2y)=y−y=0
  3. k-component: ∂∂x(xz+5x)−∂∂y(yz+y)=(z+5)−(z+1)=4\frac{\partial}{\partial x}(xz + 5x) - \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(yz + y) = (z + 5) - (z + 1) = 4∂x∂​(xz+5x)−∂y∂​(yz+y)=(z+5)−(z+1)=4

Thus, the curl of FFF is:

∇×F=⟨2,0,4⟩\nabla \times F = \langle 2, 0, 4 \rangle ∇×F=⟨2,0,4⟩

This constant vector field represents the rotational nature of FFF. Now that we have the curl, we can proceed with applying Stokes' Theorem to evaluate the line integral. Remember, the key to using Stokes' Theorem effectively is to choose a suitable surface bounded by our curve of intersection. In the next section, we will explore how to select such a surface and set up the surface integral.

Choosing the Surface and Normal Vector

Now, let's circle back to our problem: we're trying to figure out the line integral of a vector field around the intersection of a unit sphere (x2+y2+z2=1x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = 1x2+y2+z2=1) and a plane (x+y=14x + y = 14x+y=14). We've already used Stokes' Theorem to transform this into a surface integral of the curl of the vector field. The next step is crucial – we need to pick the right surface to make this integral as easy as possible to handle.

The Plane Truth

The most natural choice for a surface here is the disk formed by the intersection of the plane x+y=14x + y = 14x+y=14 and the unit sphere. This disk is a flat, circular region that lies within the plane. To make our calculations smoother, we'll call this surface SSS. But before we jump into integration, we need something super important: the unit normal vector, nnn, to this surface.

Finding the Normal

The plane x+y=14x + y = 14x+y=14 has a normal vector that's just the coefficients of xxx, yyy, and zzz in its equation. So, the normal vector is ⟨1,1,0⟩\langle 1, 1, 0 \rangle⟨1,1,0⟩. To get the unit normal vector (a vector with a length of 1), we need to divide this vector by its magnitude:

∥⟨1,1,0⟩∥=12+12+02=2\| \langle 1, 1, 0 \rangle \| = \sqrt{1^2 + 1^2 + 0^2} = \sqrt{2} ∥⟨1,1,0⟩∥=12+12+02​=2​

So, the unit normal vector is:

n=12⟨1,1,0⟩=⟨12,12,0⟩n = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \langle 1, 1, 0 \rangle = \langle \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}, \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}, 0 \rangle n=2​1​⟨1,1,0⟩=⟨2​1​,2​1​,0⟩

This vector points in the direction perpendicular to our disk-shaped surface. Now that we've got the surface and its normal vector, we're in a great position to set up the surface integral using Stokes' Theorem. In the next section, we'll dive into how to actually compute this integral and find the value of our line integral.

Setting Up and Evaluating the Surface Integral

Alright, guys, we're in the home stretch! We've transformed our line integral into a surface integral using Stokes' Theorem, found the curl of our vector field (∇×F=⟨2,0,4⟩\nabla \times F = \langle 2, 0, 4 \rangle∇×F=⟨2,0,4⟩), and determined the unit normal vector to our surface (n=⟨12,12,0⟩n = \langle \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}, \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}, 0 \ranglen=⟨2​1​,2​1​,0⟩). Now, it's time to put it all together and actually compute the surface integral.

The Grand Finale: The Surface Integral

According to Stokes' Theorem, the line integral is equal to the surface integral of the curl of FFF dotted with the unit normal vector over the surface SSS:

∮CF⋅dr=∬S(∇×F)⋅n dA\oint_C F \cdot dr = \iint_S (\nabla \times F) \cdot n \ dA ∮C​F⋅dr=∬S​(∇×F)⋅n dA

Let's plug in what we know:

∬S⟨2,0,4⟩⋅⟨12,12,0⟩ dA\iint_S \langle 2, 0, 4 \rangle \cdot \langle \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}, \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}, 0 \rangle \ dA ∬S​⟨2,0,4⟩⋅⟨2​1​,2​1​,0⟩ dA

Dot Product Time

First, we compute the dot product:

⟨2,0,4⟩⋅⟨12,12,0⟩=2⋅12+0⋅12+4⋅0=22=2\langle 2, 0, 4 \rangle \cdot \langle \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}, \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}, 0 \rangle = 2 \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} + 0 \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} + 4 \cdot 0 = \frac{2}{\sqrt{2}} = \sqrt{2} ⟨2,0,4⟩⋅⟨2​1​,2​1​,0⟩=2⋅2​1​+0⋅2​1​+4⋅0=2​2​=2​

So, our surface integral simplifies to:

∬S2 dA\iint_S \sqrt{2} \ dA ∬S​2​ dA

A Constant Integral

Notice that the integrand, 2\sqrt{2}2​, is a constant. This makes our lives much easier! We can pull the constant out of the integral:

2∬SdA\sqrt{2} \iint_S dA 2​∬S​dA

The integral ∬SdA\iint_S dA∬S​dA is just the area of the surface SSS. Remember, SSS is the disk formed by the intersection of the plane x+y=14x + y = 14x+y=14 and the unit sphere x2+y2+z2=1x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = 1x2+y2+z2=1. However, there is a crucial observation here: the plane x+y=14x + y = 14x+y=14 does not intersect the unit sphere. Why? Because the maximum value of x+yx + yx+y on the unit sphere occurs when x=y=12x = y = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}x=y=2​1​, giving x+y=2≈1.414x + y = \sqrt{2} \approx 1.414x+y=2​≈1.414, which is much smaller than 14.

The Twist

This means that the intersection is empty, and there is no curve CCC. Therefore, the line integral around this non-existent curve is zero. Mathematically, since there's no surface SSS, the integral is zero.

∮CF⋅dr=2⋅0=0\oint_C F \cdot dr = \sqrt{2} \cdot 0 = 0 ∮C​F⋅dr=2​⋅0=0

Conclusion

So, guys, we've reached the end of our journey! We set out to compute the line integral of a vector field around the intersection of a sphere and a plane. We harnessed the power of Stokes' Theorem to transform the line integral into a surface integral. However, we discovered a twist: the plane and sphere don't actually intersect! This led us to the conclusion that the line integral is zero. This problem highlights the importance of not just blindly applying formulas, but also thinking critically about the geometry and the conditions of the problem.

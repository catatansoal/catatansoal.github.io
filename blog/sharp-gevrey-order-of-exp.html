What are Gevrey Functions, Anyway?

Let's start with the basics. To understand the sharp Gevrey order of exp(-1/x^γ), we first need to understand what Gevrey functions are. Imagine smooth functions – functions that have derivatives of all orders. Gevrey functions are a special class of these smooth functions. They not only have infinitely many derivatives, but these derivatives also satisfy a particular growth condition. Think of it like this: while any smooth function has derivatives of all orders, Gevrey functions have derivatives that don't grow too fast. This controlled growth is what sets them apart and makes them so valuable in various mathematical contexts.

Formally, a function f : ℝ → ℝ is said to be Gevrey of order s > 0 if it's smooth (meaning it has derivatives of all orders) and if there exist positive constants A and B such that for all non-negative integers k and for all x in the domain, the following estimate holds:

|f^(k)(x)| ≤ A * B^k * (k!)^s

Where f^(k)(x) denotes the k-th derivative of f at x. This inequality is the heart of the Gevrey condition. It tells us that the growth of the derivatives f^(k)(x) is controlled by the factorial term (k!)^s, scaled by the constants A and B. The exponent s in (k!)^s is the Gevrey order, and it dictates how rapidly the derivatives can grow. A smaller s means the derivatives grow slower, indicating a higher degree of regularity. In essence, Gevrey functions bridge the gap between smooth functions and analytic functions (functions that can be represented by a convergent power series). Analytic functions have the strongest regularity, with derivatives that grow the slowest (Gevrey order s = 1). Gevrey functions, with orders s > 1, exhibit a controlled loss of analyticity.

Think of the Gevrey order s as a measure of how "nice" a function is. An analytic function is the "nicest", with a Gevrey order of 1. As s increases, the function becomes less "nice", but it still retains a certain level of smoothness. Understanding this concept is crucial for tackling problems in areas like partial differential equations, where the regularity of solutions plays a vital role. By understanding the Gevrey order of a function, we gain insights into its behavior and properties, which are essential for various mathematical analyses.

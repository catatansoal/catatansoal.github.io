Reflecting And Dilating Vectors A Comprehensive Guide

Jul 25, 2025 by ADMIN 54 views

Hey guys! Today, we're diving into a cool problem involving vectors, reflections, and dilations. It might sound a bit intimidating at first, but trust me, we'll break it down step by step. We have this vector, <6, -5>, and we're going to reflect it across the line y = x. Then, we're going to dilate the resulting vector by a scale factor of -1.5. The goal is to find the final vector [a, b] and determine the values of a and b. So, let's put on our thinking caps and get started!

When we talk about reflecting a vector across the line y = x, we're essentially swapping the x and y components of the vector. Think of it as flipping the vector over this diagonal line. This is a fundamental transformation in linear algebra, and it's super useful in various applications, from computer graphics to physics simulations. The reflection transformation is a linear transformation, and it can be represented by a matrix. The matrix that represents reflection across the line y = x is given by:

[ 0  1 ]
[ 1  0 ]

This matrix might look simple, but it packs a punch! When you multiply this matrix by a vector, it effectively swaps the components of the vector. For example, if we have a vector <x, y>, multiplying it by this matrix will give us the vector <y, x>. In our case, we have the vector <6, -5>. So, to reflect this vector across the line y = x, we simply swap the components. This gives us the reflected vector <-5, 6>. It's like magic, but it's just math! This initial step is crucial because it sets the stage for the next transformation, which is dilation. Without understanding reflection, we can't accurately predict how dilation will affect our vector. So, make sure you've got this concept down before moving on. We're building the foundation for solving the problem, and a solid understanding of reflection is key to our success. Remember, math is like building blocks; each concept builds upon the previous one.

Alright, now that we've got the theory down, let's apply it to our specific vector. We have the vector <6, -5>, and we want to reflect it across the line y = x. As we discussed, this means we simply swap the x and y components. So, 6 becomes the y-component, and -5 becomes the x-component. This gives us the reflected vector <-5, 6>. To make it crystal clear, let's write it out:

Original vector: <6, -5> Reflected vector: <-5, 6>

See? It's as easy as flipping a switch! This step is crucial because it sets the stage for the next transformation, which is dilation. Now, let's think about what this means geometrically. Imagine our vector <6, -5> as an arrow pointing from the origin (0, 0) to the point (6, -5) on a graph. When we reflect it across the line y = x, we're essentially creating a mirror image of this arrow on the other side of the line. The reflected vector <-5, 6> now points from the origin to the point (-5, 6). This visual representation can be super helpful in understanding what's happening. You can even sketch it out on a piece of paper to get a better feel for it. The line y = x acts like a mirror, and our vector is bouncing off it. This geometric intuition is valuable because it helps us visualize transformations and understand their effects. It's not just about manipulating numbers; it's about understanding the underlying geometric principles. So, let's keep this visual in mind as we move on to the next step: dilation.

Next up, we have dilation. Dilation is basically scaling a vector. We're making it bigger or smaller, depending on the scale factor. If the scale factor is greater than 1, the vector gets stretched. If it's between 0 and 1, the vector gets compressed. And if the scale factor is negative, things get even more interesting! A negative scale factor not only changes the size of the vector but also flips its direction. This is crucial to remember because it affects the final result. In our case, we have a scale factor of -1.5. This means we're going to make our vector 1.5 times longer, and we're going to flip it 180 degrees. So, if our vector is pointing in a certain direction, dilation by -1.5 will make it point in the opposite direction. To perform dilation, we simply multiply each component of the vector by the scale factor. It's a straightforward process, but it's essential to understand the underlying concept. Dilation is another fundamental transformation in linear algebra, and it's used extensively in various fields. Think about resizing images in graphic design, or scaling objects in 3D modeling. These are all applications of dilation. Understanding how dilation works is crucial for manipulating vectors and understanding their behavior under transformations. So, let's make sure we've got this concept down before we move on to applying it to our problem. We're building our toolkit of transformations, and dilation is a key tool in that kit.

Now that we understand dilation, let's apply it to our reflected vector. We have the reflected vector <-5, 6>, and we want to dilate it by a scale factor of -1.5. This means we multiply each component of the vector by -1.5. Let's do the math:

-1. 5 * -5 = 7.5 -1. 5 * 6 = -9

So, the dilated vector is <7.5, -9>. See how the negative scale factor flipped the signs of the components? This is a key characteristic of dilation with a negative scale factor. The vector not only changes in size but also in direction. Now, let's think about what this means geometrically. Our reflected vector <-5, 6> was pointing in a certain direction. When we dilate it by -1.5, we're not only making it longer but also flipping it 180 degrees. The dilated vector <7.5, -9> now points in the opposite direction. This geometric interpretation is crucial for understanding the overall effect of the transformations. We started with a vector, reflected it, and then dilated it. Each transformation changed the vector's position and direction in space. Visualizing these changes helps us grasp the bigger picture. You can think of dilation as a zoom lens on a camera. A positive scale factor zooms in or out, while a negative scale factor also flips the image. This analogy can be helpful in remembering the effect of dilation. So, let's keep this geometric picture in mind as we move on to the final step: determining the values of a and b.

Okay, we're almost there! We've reflected the vector <6, -5> across the line y = x, and we've dilated the resulting vector by a scale factor of -1.5. The final vector we obtained is <7.5, -9>. Now, the problem asks us to express this vector in the form [a, b] and determine the values of a and b. This is a simple step, but it's important to make the connection between the vector notation and the component values. In our case, the vector <7.5, -9> is equivalent to the vector [7.5, -9]. So, we can directly read off the values of a and b:

a = 7.5 b = -9

And there you have it! We've successfully solved the problem. We started with a vector, applied two transformations, and determined the final vector. This process demonstrates the power of linear transformations in manipulating vectors. These transformations are fundamental in various fields, from computer graphics to physics simulations. Understanding how they work is crucial for solving problems in these areas. Now, let's take a step back and recap what we've done. We started by understanding the concept of reflection across the line y = x, which involves swapping the components of the vector. Then, we learned about dilation, which involves scaling the vector by a scale factor. We applied these transformations step by step, and we arrived at the final vector. This step-by-step approach is key to solving complex problems. By breaking them down into smaller, manageable steps, we can tackle even the most challenging problems.

So, to wrap it all up, we found that:

a = 7.5 b = -9

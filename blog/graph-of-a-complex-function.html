Graph Of A Complex Function How Does It Change As N Increases

Hey math enthusiasts! Let's dive into the fascinating world of complex functions and explore how their graphs behave as the value of n increases. Today, we're going to focus on a specific function and unravel its graphical representation as n grows, starting from n = 1. So, buckle up and get ready for a mathematical journey!

The Function at Hand

Our function of interest is defined as follows:

f(n)=(47+45i)nf(n)=\left(\frac{4}{7}+\frac{4}{5} i\right)^n f(n)=(74​+54​i)n

This function takes an integer n as input and raises the complex number (4/7 + (4/5)i) to the power of n. To truly understand the behavior of this function, we need to delve into the realm of complex numbers and their graphical representations. Remember, complex numbers have a real part and an imaginary part, which allows us to plot them on a complex plane. The real part corresponds to the x-axis, and the imaginary part corresponds to the y-axis. This opens up a whole new dimension for visualizing mathematical functions!

Understanding the Complex Number

The heart of our function lies in the complex number (4/7 + (4/5)i). Let's break this down. The real part is 4/7, and the imaginary part is 4/5. This means that if we were to plot this number on the complex plane, we would move 4/7 units along the real axis and 4/5 units along the imaginary axis. But there's more to this complex number than just its coordinates. We also need to consider its magnitude and argument.

Magnitude of a Complex Number

The magnitude of a complex number, often denoted as |z|, represents its distance from the origin (0, 0) in the complex plane. It's like the length of a vector pointing from the origin to the point representing the complex number. The formula for calculating the magnitude of a complex number a + bi is:

∣a+bi∣=a2+b2| a + b i|=\sqrt{ a ^2+ b ^2} ∣a+bi∣=a2+b2​

In our case, the magnitude of (4/7 + (4/5)i) is:

∣47+45i∣=(47)2+(45)2=1649+1625=11361225=47135≈1.07|\frac{4}{7} + \frac{4}{5}i| = \sqrt{(\frac{4}{7})^2 + (\frac{4}{5})^2} = \sqrt{\frac{16}{49} + \frac{16}{25}} = \sqrt{\frac{1136}{1225}} = \frac{4\sqrt{71}}{35} \approx 1.07 ∣74​+54​i∣=(74​)2+(54​)2​=4916​+2516​​=12251136​​=35471​​≈1.07

This tells us that the complex number (4/7 + (4/5)i) is approximately 1.07 units away from the origin.

Argument of a Complex Number

The argument of a complex number, often denoted as arg(z), represents the angle formed between the positive real axis and the line segment connecting the origin to the complex number in the complex plane. It's essentially the direction of the vector. We can calculate the argument using the arctangent function:

arg(a+bi)=arctan(ba)arg(a + bi) = arctan(\frac{b}{a}) arg(a+bi)=arctan(ab​)

For our complex number (4/7 + (4/5)i), the argument is:

This means that the complex number (4/7 + (4/5)i) makes an angle of approximately 0.95 radians (or about 54.46 degrees) with the positive real axis.

The Power of n

Now, let's consider what happens when we raise this complex number to the power of n. This is where things get interesting! When we raise a complex number in polar form (magnitude and argument) to a power, we use De Moivre's Theorem. De Moivre's Theorem states that for any complex number z = r(cos θ + i sin θ) and any integer n:

zn=rn(cos(nθ)+isin(nθ))z^n = r^n(cos(nθ) + i sin(nθ)) zn=rn(cos(nθ)+isin(nθ))

In simpler terms, when we raise a complex number to the power of n, we raise its magnitude to the power of n and multiply its argument by n. This has a significant impact on the graphical representation of the function.

Visualizing the Graph as n Increases

As n increases, the magnitude of f(n) changes, and the argument of f(n) changes. Since the magnitude of our base complex number (4/7 + (4/5)i) is approximately 1.07 (slightly greater than 1), raising it to increasing powers of n will cause the magnitude of f(n) to increase gradually. This means that the points representing f(n) will move further away from the origin as n increases.

The argument of f(n), on the other hand, is multiplied by n. This means that as n increases, the points representing f(n) will rotate around the origin in the complex plane. The angle of rotation increases proportionally with n, leading to a spiral-like pattern.

Starting from n = 1

When n = 1, f(1) is simply the complex number (4/7 + (4/5)i), which we've already analyzed. As n increases to 2, 3, 4, and so on, the points will start to spiral outwards from the origin, tracing a path that resembles a widening spiral. The magnitude will gradually increase, and the points will rotate further and further around the origin.

Graphical Representation

To truly visualize this behavior, it's best to plot the points f(n) on the complex plane for various values of n. You'll observe a spiral pattern emerging as n increases. The points will gradually move away from the origin while simultaneously rotating around it. This graphical representation provides a powerful way to understand the behavior of complex functions.

In conclusion, understanding the magnitude and argument of a complex number, along with De Moivre's Theorem, allows us to predict and visualize the behavior of functions like f(n) = (4/7 + (4/5)i)^n* as n increases. The graph will exhibit a spiral pattern, gradually moving away from the origin and rotating around it. Remember the key takeaways, the magnitude and argument are crucial, and De Moivre's Theorem is your friend. Math is cool, guys!

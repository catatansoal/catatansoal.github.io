Equilibrium Charge Placement: A Physics Problem

Aug 4, 2025 by ADMIN 48 views
Iklan Headers

Hey everyone! Let's dive into a classic electrostatics problem where we need to figure out where to place a third charge so that it experiences no net force. This is all about balancing forces, and it's a super important concept in physics. So, let’s get started!

The Problem Setup

So, we've got three charges hanging out on the X-axis. We have a positive charge, let's call it q1, which is 10.0 microcoulombs and it's chilling at the x = 1.00 meter mark. Then, there's a negative charge, q2, that's -2.00 microcoulombs, and it's right at the origin (x = 0). Now, the big question is: where do we need to put a third positive charge, q3, on the X-axis so that it doesn't feel any net force? In other words, all the electrical forces on q3 have to cancel each other out. This is our mission, should we choose to accept it, and I think we should!

Breaking Down the Forces

First off, let's remember Coulomb's Law, because it's the star of the show here. Coulomb's Law tells us that the force between two point charges is directly proportional to the product of the magnitudes of the charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. Mathematically, it looks like this: F = k * (|q1 * q2| / r^2), where F is the force, k is Coulomb's constant (about 8.99 x 10^9 Nm²/C²), q1 and q2 are the charges, and r is the distance between them. This law is fundamental to understanding how charges interact.

Now, let's think about the forces acting on q3. Since q1 is positive and q3 is positive, they're going to repel each other. That means q1 will be pushing q3 away. On the flip side, q2 is negative and q3 is positive, so they're going to attract each other. q2 will be pulling q3 towards it. For q3 to experience no net force, these two forces need to be equal in magnitude but opposite in direction. This is where the balancing act comes in.

The Key Insight: Finding the Right Spot

So, where do we even start looking for this magical spot? A little intuition goes a long way here. We know that the force gets weaker as the distance increases. Since q1 is larger in magnitude than q2, we're going to need to place q3 closer to q2 than to q1 to balance things out. If we put q3 to the right of q1 (at x > 1.00 m), both q1 and q2 would be pulling or pushing q3 in the same direction, so there's no way the forces could cancel. Similarly, if q3 were between q1 and q2 (0 < x < 1.00 m), the forces would also be in the same direction. That leaves us with one option: q3 must be to the left of q2 (x < 0). This is the sweet spot where the forces can actually balance.

Setting Up the Equations

Alright, time to get a little mathematical. Let's call the position of q3 x. The distance between q1 and q3 is going to be |1.00 - x|, and the distance between q2 and q3 is |x|. Remember, since x is negative (because we're to the left of the origin), |x| will just be the positive distance. Now, we can write out the forces using Coulomb's Law.

The force exerted on q3 by q1, which we'll call F13, is:

F13 = k * (|q1 * q3| / (1.00 - x)^2)

And the force exerted on q3 by q2, which we'll call F23, is:

F23 = k * (|q2 * q3| / x^2)

For q3 to be at equilibrium, the magnitudes of these forces must be equal: |F13| = |F23|. So, we set them equal to each other:

k * (|q1 * q3| / (1.00 - x)^2) = k * (|q2 * q3| / x^2)

Notice that Coulomb's constant k and the magnitude of q3 appear on both sides, so we can cancel them out. This simplifies our equation quite a bit, which is always a win!

Solving for the Position of q3

After canceling out k and |q3|, we're left with:

|q1| / (1.00 - x)^2 = |q2| / x^2

Now, we plug in the values for q1 (10.0 μC) and q2 (2.00 μC):

10.0 / (1.00 - x)^2 = 2.00 / x^2

To make things a bit easier, let's cross-multiply:

  1. 0 * x^2 = 2.00 * (1.00 - x)^2

Now, expand that squared term:

  1. 0 * x^2 = 2.00 * (1.00 - 2.00x + x^2)

Distribute the 2.00:

  1. 0 * x^2 = 2.00 - 4.00x + 2.00x^2

Let's rearrange everything to get a quadratic equation:

  1. 00x^2 + 4.00x - 2.00 = 0

We can simplify this a bit by dividing everything by 2:

  1. 00x^2 + 2.00x - 1.00 = 0

Now, we've got a classic quadratic equation in the form ax^2 + bx + c = 0. Time to break out the quadratic formula! For those who need a refresher, the quadratic formula is:

x = (-b ± √(b^2 - 4ac)) / (2a)

In our case, a = 4.00, b = 2.00, and c = -1.00. Plugging these values in, we get:

x = (-2.00 ± √(2.00^2 - 4 * 4.00 * -1.00)) / (2 * 4.00)

Let's simplify:

x = (-2.00 ± √(4.00 + 16.00)) / 8.00

x = (-2.00 ± √20.00) / 8.00

x = (-2.00 ± 4.47) / 8.00

This gives us two possible solutions:

x1 = (-2.00 + 4.47) / 8.00 = 0.31 x2 = (-2.00 - 4.47) / 8.00 = -0.81

Choosing the Right Solution

Okay, we've got two possible positions for q3, but only one of them makes sense in our physical situation. Remember, we figured out earlier that q3 needs to be to the left of q2, which means x must be negative. So, the correct solution is:

x = -0.81 meters

The Final Answer

So, to make q3 feel no net force, you've got to place it at x = -0.81 meters. That's about 81 centimeters to the left of the origin. Pretty cool, huh? We used Coulomb's Law, a bit of intuition, and some algebra to solve this problem. It's a great example of how physics can help us understand the world around us.

Visualizing the Solution

It might be helpful to visualize this. Imagine the charges on a number line. q2 is at 0, q1 is at +1.00 m, and q3 is at -0.81 m. The negative charge q2 is pulling q3 towards the origin, while the positive charge q1 is pushing q3 away. At x = -0.81 m, these forces perfectly balance, creating a point of equilibrium. This visual representation can make the concept much clearer and easier to remember.

Real-World Applications

You might be wondering, "Okay, this is a cool problem, but does it have any real-world applications?" The answer is a resounding yes! Understanding how to balance electrostatic forces is crucial in many areas of technology. For example, in inkjet printers, tiny droplets of ink are charged and then precisely steered by electric fields to create the images you see on paper. The principles we've discussed here are at play in controlling the movement of those ink droplets. Similarly, electrostatic precipitators, used in power plants and factories to remove particulate matter from exhaust gases, rely on these principles to trap pollutants. Even in the design of sensitive scientific instruments, like mass spectrometers, careful management of electrostatic forces is essential for accurate measurements.

Tips for Solving Similar Problems

If you're tackling similar problems in the future, here are a few tips that might help:

  1. Draw a Diagram: Always start by drawing a clear diagram of the situation. Label the charges, their positions, and the distances between them. This visual representation can make the problem much easier to understand.
  2. Identify the Forces: Determine the direction of the forces acting on the charge you're interested in. Remember that like charges repel, and opposite charges attract.
  3. Apply Coulomb's Law: Use Coulomb's Law to write out the equations for the forces. Make sure to keep track of the signs of the charges and the distances between them.
  4. Set Up Equilibrium Conditions: If the problem asks for the position where the net force is zero, set the magnitudes of the opposing forces equal to each other.
  5. Solve the Equations: Solve the resulting equations for the unknown variable (usually a position or a charge).
  6. Check Your Answer: Make sure your answer makes sense in the context of the problem. For example, if you calculated a negative distance when it should be positive, you've likely made a mistake.

By following these steps and practicing regularly, you'll become a pro at solving electrostatics problems in no time!

Common Mistakes to Avoid

When working with electrostatics problems, it's easy to make a few common mistakes. Here are some pitfalls to watch out for:

  • Forgetting the Signs of Charges: The signs of the charges are crucial in determining the direction of the forces. Always remember to include the signs in your calculations.
  • Incorrectly Calculating Distances: Double-check your distance calculations, especially when dealing with charges that are not located at the origin. It's easy to make a sign error or forget to take the absolute value.
  • Not Considering the Direction of Forces: Forces are vectors, so they have both magnitude and direction. Make sure you're adding the forces vectorially, not just arithmetically.
  • Algebra Errors: Be careful with your algebra, especially when solving quadratic equations. A small mistake can lead to a completely wrong answer.
  • Not Checking Units: Always make sure your units are consistent throughout the problem. If you're working with microcoulombs, convert them to coulombs before plugging them into Coulomb's Law.

By being aware of these common mistakes, you can avoid them and improve your problem-solving accuracy.

Solving Linear Equations By Elimination: A Step-by-Step Guide

Aug 3, 2025 by ADMIN 62 views

Hey everyone! Let's dive into the world of solving systems of linear equations using the elimination method. You know, sometimes you're faced with a set of equations, and you need to find the values of the variables that satisfy all of them simultaneously. It's like solving a puzzle, and the elimination method is one of our awesome tools! In this article, we'll walk through how to use the elimination method to solve systems of linear equations, step by step, with an example. Get ready to flex those math muscles!

What are Linear Equations?

Before we get started with the elimination method, let's make sure we're all on the same page about what linear equations are. Linear equations are mathematical equations that represent a straight line when graphed on a coordinate plane. They typically involve variables raised to the power of 1 (no squares, cubes, or anything fancy!). A system of linear equations is just a set of two or more linear equations that we're trying to solve together. The solution to the system is the point (or points) where all the lines intersect. If the lines are parallel, then there is no solution, since parallel lines never intersect. For two variables such as x and y, the solution is usually an ordered pair (x, y).

Think of it like this: each equation is a rule, and the solution is the values that satisfy all the rules at the same time. That's the goal here, guys! We're trying to find those special x and y values that make both equations true.

So, when we talk about linear equations, we're looking at equations like y = 2x + 1 or 3x - y = 5. These are all linear equations because the variables, x and y, are only raised to the power of 1. They create lines when you graph them. Systems of linear equations are collections of such equations, and they are solved by finding the points (or points) where all the lines intersect. Ready to get started?

The Elimination Method Explained

Alright, let's get down to the nitty-gritty and discuss the elimination method! It's all about manipulating the equations to eliminate one of the variables. The main idea is to add or subtract the equations in a way that causes one of the variables to disappear. This simplifies the system into a single equation with a single variable, which is super easy to solve. Once you've found the value of that variable, you can substitute it back into one of the original equations to find the value of the other variable. Easy peasy, right?

Here are the general steps for the elimination method:

  1. Make a Choice: Examine your equations and decide which variable you want to eliminate. Look for coefficients (the numbers in front of the variables) that are the same or opposites. If they're not, you might have to multiply one or both equations by a constant to make them match.
  2. Adjust the Equations (if needed): Multiply one or both equations by a constant to make the coefficients of the variable you want to eliminate the same (or opposites). The goal is that when you add or subtract the equations, one of the variables cancels out.
  3. Add or Subtract: Add or subtract the equations to eliminate one variable. If the coefficients of the variable you're eliminating have the same sign, subtract the equations. If the coefficients have opposite signs, add the equations.
  4. Solve for the Remaining Variable: You should now have a single equation with one variable. Solve for this variable.
  5. Substitute Back: Substitute the value you found in step 4 into one of the original equations. Solve for the other variable.
  6. Write the Solution: Write your solution as an ordered pair (x, y).

That's it! Remember that with the elimination method, the goal is to make sure that adding or subtracting the equations will make one of the variables disappear. We need to be strategic in our choices and calculations!

Example: Solving a System of Equations by Elimination

Let's walk through an example to show you how to use the elimination method to solve a system of linear equations. We will use the example given to us. Let's find the solution to the system of linear equations using elimination.

\~\~\~\ \left\{\begin{aligned} \t-4 x-4 y & =-32 \\ \t-4 x+5 y & =4 \end{aligned}\right.

Alright, here's our system of equations. Let's get to work:

  1. Our choice: We have a choice here, but let's eliminate the x-variable. Notice that both equations have -4x, so we will subtract them.
  2. Adjust the Equations: In this case, we don't need to adjust anything because the coefficients of x are already the same. However, we do need to subtract the second equation from the first equation.
  3. Subtract:

\~\~\~\ \left\{\begin{aligned} \t-4 x-4 y & =-32 \\ \t-4 x+5 y & =4 \end{aligned}\right.

(−4x−4y)−(−4x+5y)=−32−4(-4x - 4y) - (-4x + 5y) = -32 - 4(−4x−4y)−(−4x+5y)=−32−4 simplifies to −9y=−36-9y = -36−9y=−36

  1. Solve for the Remaining Variable: Divide both sides of −9y=−36-9y = -36−9y=−36 by -9. Thus, y = 4.
  2. Substitute Back: Substitute y = 4 into any of the original equations. Let's use the second equation, −4x+5y=4-4x + 5y = 4−4x+5y=4. Thus, −4x+5(4)=4-4x + 5(4) = 4−4x+5(4)=4, or −4x+20=4-4x + 20 = 4−4x+20=4. Subtracting 20 from both sides gives −4x=−16-4x = -16−4x=−16. Dividing both sides by -4, we get x = 4.
  3. Write the Solution: The solution to the system of equations is the ordered pair (4, 4).

So, the solution to the system is (4, 4). We did it! High five!

Tips for Success

Here are a few tips to help you on your journey of solving systems of linear equations using the elimination method:

  • Double-Check Your Work: Always, always, always double-check your calculations, especially when multiplying and adding/subtracting. A small mistake can lead to the wrong answer.
  • Be Organized: Keep your work neat and organized. Writing things down clearly will help you avoid mistakes and make it easier to spot errors if you need to.
  • Practice, Practice, Practice: The more you practice, the better you'll become! Solve as many problems as you can to build your confidence and become comfortable with the method.
  • Don't Be Afraid to Ask for Help: If you're stuck, don't hesitate to ask your teacher, classmates, or use online resources for help. Math can be challenging, but you don't have to do it alone.
  • Know Your Goal: Keep in mind that the goal is to eliminate a variable. Make strategic choices about which variable to eliminate based on the coefficients. Remember, in the elimination method, we add or subtract the equations to eliminate one variable. We need to be strategic in our choices and calculations! We need to be smart!

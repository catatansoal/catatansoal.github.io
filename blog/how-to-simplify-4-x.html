How To Simplify 4(x-3)-2x+9 A Step-by-Step Guide

Understanding the Basics

Before we jump into simplifying 4(x-3)-2x+9, let's make sure we're all on the same page with some fundamental concepts. The first key concept is the distributive property. What exactly is the distributive property? In simple terms, it's a way to multiply a single term by multiple terms inside parentheses. For example, a(b+c) is the same as ab + ac. You're essentially 'distributing' the 'a' to both 'b' and 'c'. This is crucial because it allows us to remove parentheses and start simplifying. Think of it like this: you're making sure 'a' gets to both 'b' and 'c' equally. Without the distributive property, we'd be stuck with expressions that are much harder to work with. It’s like having a locked door – the distributive property is the key that unlocks the expression, allowing us to simplify it further. Another important concept is combining like terms. What are like terms? They are terms that have the same variable raised to the same power. For instance, 3x and -2x are like terms because they both have 'x' raised to the power of 1. Similarly, constants like 5 and -3 are like terms. We can add or subtract like terms to simplify an expression. Imagine you have a basket of apples and oranges. You can count the apples together and the oranges together, but you can't combine an apple and an orange into a single category. Like terms are the same – you can combine the 'x' terms and the constant terms separately. These two concepts, the distributive property and combining like terms, are the building blocks for simplifying algebraic expressions, and they're exactly what we need to tackle our problem today.

Step-by-Step Solution

Now that we've covered the basics, let’s walk through the solution to simplify 4(x-3)-2x+9 step by step. This will make the process clear and easy to follow. Each step has a specific purpose, and understanding why we do what we do is just as important as knowing how to do it. Let’s break it down: The first step is to apply the distributive property to the term 4(x-3). Remember, the distributive property means we multiply the term outside the parentheses (which is 4 in this case) by each term inside the parentheses. So, we multiply 4 by x, which gives us 4x, and then we multiply 4 by -3, which gives us -12. This transforms our expression from 4(x-3)-2x+9 to 4x - 12 - 2x + 9. See how we’ve eliminated the parentheses? That's a big step forward! The distributive property is like opening a door, allowing us to access and rearrange the terms inside. Next, we need to identify and combine like terms. Looking at our expression 4x - 12 - 2x + 9, we can see two types of terms: terms with 'x' and constant terms (numbers without variables). The terms with 'x' are 4x and -2x. The constant terms are -12 and +9. Now, let's combine them. We combine 4x and -2x by adding their coefficients (the numbers in front of the 'x'). So, 4x - 2x equals 2x. We then combine the constant terms, -12 and +9. Adding these gives us -3. So, -12 + 9 equals -3. Putting it all together, we have simplified 4x - 12 - 2x + 9 to 2x - 3. This is our final simplified expression! We’ve taken a more complex expression and boiled it down to its simplest form. By following these steps, we’ve transformed a potentially confusing problem into a straightforward solution.

Detailed Explanation of Each Step

To really nail this down, let's dive deeper into why each step in simplifying 4(x-3)-2x+9 is crucial. Understanding the 'why' behind the steps makes the 'how' much easier to remember and apply in different scenarios. Firstly, let’s revisit applying the distributive property. When we multiply 4 by both x and -3, we're not just following a rule – we're ensuring that the multiplication applies to the entire quantity inside the parentheses. Think of it as fairly distributing a resource to everyone in a group. If we didn't distribute, we'd be leaving out a part of the expression, which would lead to an incorrect simplification. The distributive property allows us to break down the expression into manageable pieces that we can then rearrange and combine. It’s a foundational step because it eliminates the parentheses, which are like roadblocks in our simplification journey. Without this step, we couldn't move forward to the next phase of combining like terms. Next, let’s discuss the importance of combining like terms. When we combine 4x and -2x to get 2x, and -12 and +9 to get -3, we're making the expression more concise and easier to understand. Imagine if you had a long grocery list with similar items scattered throughout. Combining like items would make the list shorter and clearer. Common Mistakes to Avoid

Alright, let's talk about some common pitfalls that students often encounter when simplifying expressions like 4(x-3)-2x+9. Knowing these mistakes can help you steer clear of them and boost your accuracy. One frequent error is incorrectly applying the distributive property. Remember, the distributive property means multiplying the term outside the parentheses by each term inside. A common mistake is to only multiply by the first term and forget the second. For example, some might incorrectly calculate 4(x-3) as 4x - 3, forgetting to multiply the 4 by the -3. This is a big no-no! Always double-check that you've distributed to all terms within the parentheses. It’s like making sure everyone gets a piece of the pie – don't leave anyone out! Another common mistake is not paying attention to signs. When distributing or combining like terms, signs matter a lot. A negative sign in front of a term can easily be overlooked, leading to an incorrect answer. For example, when combining -12 + 9, forgetting the negative sign on the 12 can lead to the wrong result. Always take a moment to double-check the signs before you perform any operation. Think of signs as crucial clues – missing one can lead you down the wrong path. A further mistake is combining unlike terms. As we discussed earlier, you can only combine terms that have the same variable raised to the same power. A common error is to try and combine terms like 2x and -3, which are not like terms. Remember, it's like trying to add apples and oranges – they're different things and can't be combined into a single category. Finally, another pitfall is skipping steps. It might be tempting to rush through the simplification process, but skipping steps can increase the likelihood of making a mistake. Each step has a purpose, and carefully working through each one ensures accuracy. It's better to go slow and get it right than to rush and make a mistake. By being aware of these common mistakes, you can be more careful and methodical in your approach, ultimately leading to more accurate simplifications.

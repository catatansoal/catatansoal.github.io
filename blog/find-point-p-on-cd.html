Find Point P On CD: A Step-by-Step Geometry Guide

Aug 3, 2025 by ADMIN 50 views

Hey guys! Today, we're diving into a classic geometry problem: finding a point on a line segment that's a certain fraction of the way from one endpoint. Specifically, we'll tackle the question: If the endpoints of CD are C (-8,4) and D (6,-6), what are the coordinates of point P on CD such that P is 5/8 the length of the line segment from D? Sounds a bit tricky, right? But don't worry, we'll break it down step by step so it becomes super clear.

Understanding the Problem

Before we jump into the calculations, let's make sure we understand what the problem is asking. We've got a line segment, CD, with two defined endpoints: C at (-8, 4) and D at (6, -6). Our mission is to find a point, P, that lies on this line segment. The key here is that P isn't just anywhere on the line; it's specifically located 5/8 of the distance from point D. Think of it like this: if you were walking from D to C, point P is where you'd be after covering 5/8 of the total distance. This concept is super important, as we're dealing with a ratio of distances along the line segment, and this ratio will guide our calculations.

The problem requires us to find the coordinates of point P. Remember, coordinates are just the (x, y) values that tell us exactly where a point is located on a coordinate plane. So, our goal is to figure out the x and y coordinates for point P. To do this, we'll use a clever approach that involves thinking about the changes in x and y as we move along the line segment. We'll determine the total change in x and y from D to C, and then use the fraction 5/8 to find the change in x and y from D to P. Finally, we'll add these changes to the coordinates of point D to find the coordinates of point P. Sounds like a plan? Let's dive into the math!

Calculating the Change in X and Y

Now for the fun part ‚Äì the calculations! Our first step is to figure out the total change in the x-coordinates and the y-coordinates as we move from point D to point C. This will give us a sense of the overall ‚Äúdirection‚Äù and ‚Äúdistance‚Äù of the line segment. Remember, point D has coordinates (6, -6), and point C has coordinates (-8, 4).

Let's start with the x-coordinate. To find the change in x, we subtract the x-coordinate of the starting point (D) from the x-coordinate of the ending point (C). So, the change in x is -8 - 6 = -14. This means that as we move from D to C, the x-coordinate decreases by 14 units. It‚Äôs a negative change because we're moving to the left on the coordinate plane.

Next up, the y-coordinate. We do the same thing: subtract the y-coordinate of D from the y-coordinate of C. The change in y is 4 - (-6) = 4 + 6 = 10. This means that as we move from D to C, the y-coordinate increases by 10 units. It's a positive change because we're moving upwards on the coordinate plane.

So, to recap, the total change in x from D to C is -14, and the total change in y from D to C is 10. These values represent the overall ‚Äúlegs‚Äù of our journey along the line segment. Now, we need to figure out how much of these changes apply to the segment DP, which is 5/8 of the total distance. We're getting closer to finding point P!

Finding the Change in X and Y for Point P

Okay, we've calculated the total change in x and y from point D to point C. Now, we need to figure out the change in x and y specifically for the segment DP, which is 5/8 of the total length of CD. This is where that fraction comes into play!

To find the change in x from D to P, we simply multiply the total change in x (from D to C) by the fraction 5/8. So, the change in x from D to P is (5/8) * (-14). Let's do the math: (5/8) * (-14) = -70/8 = -35/4 = -8.75. This means that the x-coordinate of point P will be 8.75 units less than the x-coordinate of point D.

Similarly, to find the change in y from D to P, we multiply the total change in y (from D to C) by 5/8. So, the change in y from D to P is (5/8) * (10). Let's calculate: (5/8) * (10) = 50/8 = 25/4 = 6.25. This means that the y-coordinate of point P will be 6.25 units more than the y-coordinate of point D.

So, we've found the changes in x and y that correspond to the segment DP. The x-coordinate decreases by 8.75 units, and the y-coordinate increases by 6.25 units as we move from D to P. Now, the final step is to apply these changes to the coordinates of point D to find the coordinates of point P. We're almost there!

Calculating the Coordinates of Point P

We've done the heavy lifting ‚Äì calculating the total changes in x and y and then finding the fractional changes for segment DP. Now, the moment we've been waiting for: finding the coordinates of point P! Remember, point D has coordinates (6, -6), and we've calculated that the change in x from D to P is -8.75, and the change in y from D to P is 6.25.

To find the x-coordinate of point P, we simply add the change in x to the x-coordinate of point D. So, the x-coordinate of P is 6 + (-8.75) = 6 - 8.75 = -2.75.

Similarly, to find the y-coordinate of point P, we add the change in y to the y-coordinate of point D. So, the y-coordinate of P is -6 + 6.25 = 0.25.

And there you have it! The coordinates of point P are (-2.75, 0.25). We've successfully found the point on line segment CD that is 5/8 the distance from point D. Woohoo!

Final Answer

So, after all our calculations, we've arrived at the final answer. The coordinates of point P on line segment CD, such that P is 5/8 the length of the line segment from D, are (-2.75, 0.25).

Key Takeaways

This problem might have seemed intimidating at first, but we conquered it by breaking it down into smaller, manageable steps. Here are the key takeaways:

  • Understanding the Problem: Always start by making sure you truly understand what the problem is asking. Visualizing the problem can be super helpful.
  • Calculating Total Changes: Find the total change in x and y between the two endpoints of the line segment. This gives you the overall ‚Äúdirection‚Äù of the line.
  • Fractional Changes: Multiply the total changes in x and y by the given fraction to find the changes specific to the segment you're interested in.
  • Adding Changes to the Starting Point: Add the fractional changes in x and y to the coordinates of the starting point to find the coordinates of the desired point.

By following these steps, you can tackle similar problems with confidence. Keep practicing, and you'll become a pro at finding points on line segments!

Understanding the Basic Function: p(x) = -1/x + 4

At its heart, the function p(x) = -1/x + 4 is a rational function, which basically means it's a fraction where the numerator and denominator are polynomials. The core element here is the term -1/x. Think of it like this: if 1/x gives you a curve in the first and third quadrants, then -1/x will give you a curve in the second and fourth quadrants. Now, let's talk about the '+ 4' part. This is a vertical shift. It takes the entire graph of -1/x and moves it upwards by 4 units. This shift is key because it changes the asymptotes of the function. Asymptotes are imaginary lines that the graph gets closer and closer to but never actually touches. In the case of -1/x, the asymptotes are the x and y axes (x=0 and y=0). But with the '+ 4', the horizontal asymptote shifts up to y=4. This means our function will approach, but never reach, the line y=4 as x gets really big or really small. So, to sum it up, p(x) = -1/x + 4 is a hyperbola flipped over the x-axis and shifted upwards by 4 units. We've got two branches, vertical asymptote at x=0, and a horizontal asymptote at y=4.

Increasing Interval Of F(x)=-x^2+3x+8 Explained

Jul 17, 2025 by ADMIN 48 views

Hey guys! Today, let's dive into a super common question in algebra: figuring out where a parabola is increasing. We've got the function f(x) = -x² + 3x + 8, and our mission is to pinpoint the interval where the graph is climbing upwards. This is all about understanding the behavior of quadratic functions, and I'm here to make it crystal clear. So, buckle up, and let's get started!

Understanding the Parabola

To determine over which interval the graph of f(x) = -x² + 3x + 8 is increasing, we first need to understand the basic shape and properties of a parabola. Since the coefficient of the x² term is negative (-1), we know this parabola opens downwards. Think of it like a hill – it goes up, reaches a peak, and then goes down. That peak is super important because it's the vertex of the parabola. The vertex is the point where the parabola changes direction. It's the maximum point in our case, as the parabola opens downwards. Finding the vertex is the key to solving our problem.

The x-coordinate of the vertex is where the function transitions from increasing to decreasing. Before the vertex, the graph is going uphill (increasing), and after the vertex, it's going downhill (decreasing). The vertex can be found using the formula x = -b / 2a, where a and b are the coefficients in the quadratic equation ax² + bx + c. For our function, f(x) = -x² + 3x + 8, a is -1 and b is 3. Plugging these values into the formula, we get:

x = -3 / (2 * -1) = -3 / -2 = 1.5

So, the x-coordinate of the vertex is 1.5. This is a crucial point because it tells us exactly where the parabola switches from increasing to decreasing. Now, to find the y-coordinate of the vertex, we plug this x-value back into the original function:

f(1.5) = -(1.5)² + 3(1.5) + 8 = -2.25 + 4.5 + 8 = 10.25

Therefore, the vertex of the parabola is at the point (1.5, 10.25). Knowing the vertex helps us visualize the graph. Imagine the highest point of our hill is at x = 1.5. To the left of this point, the hill is going up, meaning the function is increasing. To the right, it's going down, so the function is decreasing. Our focus is on the increasing part, which is before the vertex.

In summary, to really nail this concept, remember that the sign of the x² coefficient tells you whether the parabola opens upwards or downwards. The vertex is the turning point, and its x-coordinate is what we need to determine the interval of increase or decrease. Using the formula x = -b / 2a is your best friend for finding the vertex. Once you have it, the rest is smooth sailing!

Determining the Increasing Interval

Now, let's pinpoint the interval where the graph of f(x) = -x² + 3x + 8 is increasing. As we discussed, since the coefficient of the x² term is negative, the parabola opens downwards. This means the graph increases until it reaches its vertex and then decreases. We've already found that the x-coordinate of the vertex is 1.5. Therefore, the function is increasing for all x-values less than 1.5.

In interval notation, this is represented as (-∞, 1.5]. The parenthesis on the left side indicates that the interval extends indefinitely towards negative infinity. The square bracket on the right side includes 1.5 in the interval. We include 1.5 because at that point, the function is at its maximum and is considered to be neither increasing nor decreasing at the exact vertex, but the interval up to that point is increasing. The y-coordinate of the vertex, 10.25, is the maximum value of the function, but it doesn't define the interval where the function is increasing. The increasing interval is determined solely by the x-values.

So, the graph is increasing from negative infinity up to x = 1.5. This makes intuitive sense if you visualize the parabola. Imagine starting from the left side of the graph, way down at negative infinity. As you move towards the right, the graph climbs higher and higher until it reaches the peak at x = 1.5. After that, it starts to descend. Understanding this visual representation can really solidify the concept.

In conclusion, when you're asked to find the interval where a parabola is increasing, focus on finding the vertex. The x-coordinate of the vertex is your key. If the parabola opens downwards, the function increases up to the x-coordinate of the vertex. If it opens upwards, the function decreases until the x-coordinate of the vertex and then increases. Remember to use interval notation correctly, paying attention to whether to use parentheses or square brackets. This detailed understanding will help you tackle similar problems with confidence!

Why Other Options are Incorrect

Let's take a moment, guys, to dissect why the other options aren't the right fit for our question about the increasing interval of f(x) = -x² + 3x + 8. This is super important because understanding why something is wrong is just as crucial as knowing why something is right. It helps you avoid common pitfalls and solidifies your understanding of the core concepts.

We've established that the graph of our function is a parabola opening downwards, with its vertex at (1.5, 10.25). The function increases up to the x-coordinate of the vertex, which is 1.5. Therefore, the correct interval is (-∞, 1.5]. Knowing this, let's look at the incorrect options and see why they don't work:

  • B. (-∞, 10.25]: This option is incorrect because it uses the y-coordinate of the vertex (10.25) instead of the x-coordinate (1.5). Remember, the interval of increase is defined by the x-values, not the y-values. The y-coordinate tells us the maximum value of the function, but it doesn't tell us over what interval the function is increasing. So, while 10.25 is a significant value related to the parabola, it's not relevant to the increasing interval.
  • C. (1.5, ∞]: This option represents the interval where the function is decreasing, not increasing. After x = 1.5, the parabola starts to descend, so the function values are getting smaller as x increases. This interval is the exact opposite of what we're looking for. It's a common mistake to confuse the increasing and decreasing intervals, so always double-check which one the question is asking for.
  • D. (10.25, ∞]: This option is also incorrect because it uses the y-coordinate of the vertex and represents an interval where the function is neither increasing nor part of the domain. The function never takes on x-values greater than 1.5 in the context of its increasing behavior. It's crucial to keep the x and y values distinct in your mind – the x-values define the interval along the x-axis, and the y-values define the function's value at those points.

By understanding why these options are wrong, you're reinforcing your grasp of the concepts. It's not just about picking the right answer; it's about knowing why the other answers don't fit. This kind of critical thinking is what will make you a pro at solving these types of problems!

Steps to solve similar problems

Alright, let's break down a foolproof strategy, guys, for tackling similar problems like this one. You know, the kind where you're asked to find the interval over which a quadratic function is increasing or decreasing. These steps will help you approach any quadratic function question with confidence. Let’s make sure you have a solid plan of action!

  1. Identify the Type of Function: The first thing you want to do is recognize that you're dealing with a quadratic function. These functions are in the form f(x) = ax² + bx + c. Spotting this form immediately sets the stage for the rest of your approach. You'll know you're dealing with a parabola, which has a distinctive U-shape (or an upside-down U-shape).
  2. Determine the Direction of the Parabola: Next up, figure out whether the parabola opens upwards or downwards. This is super easy: just look at the coefficient of the x² term (that's a in our general form). If a is positive, the parabola opens upwards (like a smiley face). If a is negative, the parabola opens downwards (like a frowny face). This direction tells you a lot about where the function is increasing or decreasing. If it opens upwards, it decreases until the vertex and then increases. If it opens downwards, it increases until the vertex and then decreases.
  3. Find the Vertex: The vertex is the turning point of the parabola, and it's the key to finding the intervals of increase and decrease. Remember the vertex formula? It’s x = -b / 2a. This formula gives you the x-coordinate of the vertex. Once you have that, plug it back into the original function f(x) to find the y-coordinate. The vertex is the point (x, f(x)). This is your pivot point!
  4. Determine the Intervals of Increase and Decrease: Now you can determine the intervals. If the parabola opens upwards (a > 0), the function decreases from (-∞, x-coordinate of vertex] and increases from [x-coordinate of vertex, ∞). If the parabola opens downwards (a < 0), the function increases from (-∞, x-coordinate of vertex] and decreases from [x-coordinate of vertex, ∞). Pay close attention to those brackets and parentheses! We include the x-coordinate of the vertex because, at that exact point, the function is at its minimum or maximum, and the interval up to that point is still increasing or decreasing.
  5. Express the Answer in Interval Notation: Finally, write down your answer using interval notation. This notation is a way of expressing intervals on the number line. Remember to use parentheses for intervals that don't include the endpoint (like infinity) and square brackets for intervals that do include the endpoint. Make sure your notation matches the intervals you determined in the previous step.

By following these steps, you'll be well-equipped to handle any similar problem. Practice makes perfect, so try a few examples to get the hang of it. You'll be a quadratic function whiz in no time!

We also walked through why the other options were incorrect, reinforcing the importance of understanding the underlying concepts.

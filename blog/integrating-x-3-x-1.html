Integrating (x+3)/(x-1) Dx A Step-by-Step Solution

Jul 14, 2025 by ADMIN 51 views
Iklan Headers

Hey guys! Today, we're diving into a classic calculus problem: integrating the function (x+3)/(x-1). This is a fantastic example that showcases a couple of important techniques you'll want in your integration toolkit. We'll break it down step-by-step, making sure everyone, from calculus newbies to seasoned pros, can follow along. So, let's get started and unravel this integral together!

Understanding the Integral

Before we jump into the solution, let's quickly understand what we're dealing with. We're given the integral ∫(x+3)/(x-1) dx. This means we need to find a function whose derivative is (x+3)/(x-1). Now, looking at this fraction, we can see that the degree of the numerator (x+3) is the same as the degree of the denominator (x-1). This is a clue that we can simplify things using a little algebraic manipulation before we actually start integrating. Recognizing this initial setup is crucial, as it guides our strategy for solving the problem effectively.

The Strategy: Algebraic Manipulation

The key to tackling this integral lies in simplifying the fraction. Since the degree of the numerator and denominator are the same, we can perform polynomial long division or use a clever algebraic trick. We'll opt for the latter, as it's often quicker and more intuitive. The goal here is to rewrite (x+3)/(x-1) in a form that's easier to integrate. Specifically, we want to separate it into a constant term and a simpler fraction. This is a common strategy when dealing with rational functions in integration, and mastering it will make your calculus life much easier. So, let's see how this algebraic magic works!

Step-by-Step Algebraic Manipulation

Here's how we can rewrite the fraction (x+3)/(x-1):

  1. Rewrite the numerator: We want to express the numerator in terms of the denominator. Notice that x+3 can be written as (x-1) + 4. This is the crucial step! We're essentially forcing the numerator to have a term that matches the denominator.
  2. Separate the fraction: Now we have ((x-1) + 4) / (x-1). We can split this into two fractions: (x-1)/(x-1) + 4/(x-1).
  3. Simplify: The first fraction, (x-1)/(x-1), simplifies to 1. So, our expression becomes 1 + 4/(x-1).

Boom! We've successfully rewritten our original fraction into a much simpler form. Instead of integrating (x+3)/(x-1), we can now integrate 1 + 4/(x-1). This might seem like a small change, but it makes a world of difference when it comes to actually performing the integration. The algebraic manipulation has transformed a potentially tricky integral into a straightforward one. This is a powerful technique to keep in mind for similar problems.

Integrating the Simplified Expression

Now that we've massaged our expression into a friendlier form, integrating it becomes a breeze. We're dealing with the integral of 1 + 4/(x-1). Remember, the integral of a sum is the sum of the integrals, so we can break this down into two separate integrals:

∫(1 + 4/(x-1)) dx = ∫1 dx + ∫4/(x-1) dx

Let's tackle each of these integrals individually:

Integrating ∫1 dx

This is the most basic integral. The integral of a constant (in this case, 1) is simply the constant times the variable of integration (which is x). So,

∫1 dx = x + C₁

Where C₁ is the constant of integration. Don't forget the constant of integration! It's a crucial part of indefinite integrals.

Integrating ∫4/(x-1) dx

This integral is a classic example of a situation where a simple u-substitution comes in handy. Let's see how it works:

  1. Choose a substitution: Let u = x-1. This choice is strategic because the derivative of u is simply du/dx = 1, which is a constant. This will simplify the integral.
  2. Find du: Since u = x-1, then du = dx. This is a straightforward derivative.
  3. Substitute: Now we can substitute u and du into our integral: ∫4/(x-1) dx becomes ∫4/u du.
  4. Integrate: We can pull the constant 4 out of the integral: 4∫(1/u) du. The integral of 1/u is the natural logarithm of the absolute value of u: 4∫(1/u) du = 4ln|u| + C₂
  5. Substitute back: Finally, we need to substitute back our original variable, x. Since u = x-1, we have 4ln|u| + C₂ = 4ln|x-1| + C₂

So, ∫4/(x-1) dx = 4ln|x-1| + C₂ , where C₂ is the constant of integration.

The Final Result

We've integrated both parts of our expression. Now, let's put it all together to get the final answer:

∫(x+3)/(x-1) dx = ∫1 dx + ∫4/(x-1) dx

= x + C₁ + 4ln|x-1| + C₂

Since C₁ and C₂ are both arbitrary constants, we can combine them into a single constant, C. This gives us our final, elegant answer:

∫(x+3)/(x-1) dx = x + 4ln|x-1| + C

There you have it! We've successfully integrated (x+3)/(x-1). This problem demonstrates the power of algebraic manipulation and u-substitution in simplifying integrals. Remember, guys, the key is to break down complex problems into smaller, manageable steps. This approach not only makes the problem less intimidating but also helps you understand the underlying concepts more deeply.

Key Takeaways and Further Practice

This example illustrates several important concepts in integration. Let's recap the key takeaways:

  • Algebraic Manipulation: When dealing with rational functions where the degree of the numerator is greater than or equal to the degree of the denominator, algebraic manipulation (like long division or rewriting the numerator) is often the first crucial step. This simplifies the integral and makes it easier to solve.
  • U-Substitution: U-substitution is a powerful technique for simplifying integrals by changing the variable of integration. It's particularly useful when you have a function and its derivative (or a constant multiple of its derivative) within the integral.
  • The Importance of the Constant of Integration: Never forget to add the constant of integration, C, to indefinite integrals. It represents the family of functions that have the same derivative.

To solidify your understanding, try practicing similar problems. Look for integrals of rational functions where you might need to use algebraic manipulation or u-substitution. The more you practice, the more comfortable you'll become with these techniques, and the better you'll get at tackling integration challenges.

Conclusion

Integrating (x+3)/(x-1) might have seemed daunting at first, but by breaking it down into smaller steps and using techniques like algebraic manipulation and u-substitution, we were able to conquer it.

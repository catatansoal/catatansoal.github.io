Solving √(3v + 12) + 4 = 1 A Step By Step Guide

Jul 13, 2025 by ADMIN 48 views

Hey everyone! Today, we're diving into an interesting math problem where we need to solve for the variable v in the equation √(3v + 12) + 4 = 1. This might seem a bit daunting at first, but don't worry, we'll break it down step-by-step so you can easily follow along. We'll explore the process of isolating the square root, squaring both sides, and checking for extraneous solutions. So, let's get started and learn how to tackle this kind of problem like pros!

Understanding the Problem

Before we jump into the solution, let's understand the problem at hand. We have the equation √(3v + 12) + 4 = 1, and our mission is to find the value(s) of v that make this equation true. The presence of the square root adds a little twist, but it's nothing we can't handle. The key here is to remember the order of operations and how to reverse them to isolate the variable v. We need to carefully manipulate the equation, ensuring we perform the same operations on both sides to maintain the balance. This process involves several steps, each crucial for arriving at the correct solution. Let's delve deeper into why each step is important. First, isolating the square root is essential because it allows us to eliminate it by squaring both sides. Then, squaring both sides gets rid of the square root, making the equation simpler to solve. Finally, checking for extraneous solutions is vital because squaring both sides can sometimes introduce solutions that don't actually work in the original equation. So, paying attention to each step is key to solving the equation accurately. We will go through each of these steps in detail to ensure we understand the underlying logic and can apply it to similar problems in the future. So, let's begin our step-by-step journey to solving this equation!

Step 1: Isolate the Square Root

The first crucial step in solving for v is to isolate the square root term. This means we need to get the √(3v + 12) part all by itself on one side of the equation. Currently, we have a '+ 4' hanging out on the same side, so we need to get rid of it. How do we do that? Simple! We subtract 4 from both sides of the equation. Remember, whatever we do to one side, we must do to the other to keep the equation balanced. This is a fundamental principle in algebra, ensuring that the equality remains valid throughout our manipulations. Subtracting 4 from both sides gives us: √(3v + 12) + 4 - 4 = 1 - 4, which simplifies to √(3v + 12) = -3. Now, we have the square root term nicely isolated. This isolation is a significant milestone because it sets the stage for the next step: eliminating the square root. By isolating the square root, we've effectively simplified the equation, making it easier to work with. This is a common strategy in solving equations involving radicals – isolate the radical first, then deal with it. So, with the square root isolated, we're well on our way to finding the value of v. Let's move on to the next step and see how we can get rid of that square root sign!

Step 2: Square Both Sides

Now that we've isolated the square root, it's time to get rid of it! The magic trick here is to square both sides of the equation. Remember, squaring is the inverse operation of taking the square root, so they effectively cancel each other out. This is a fundamental algebraic technique used to eliminate radicals from equations. When we square the left side, (√(3v + 12))^2, the square root disappears, leaving us with just 3v + 12. On the right side, we have (-3)^2, which equals 9. So, our equation now looks like this: 3v + 12 = 9. See how much simpler it looks without the square root? This is the power of squaring both sides – it transforms a more complex equation into a more manageable one. However, it's important to remember that squaring both sides can sometimes introduce extraneous solutions, which we'll need to check later. But for now, we've made significant progress in solving for v. We've successfully eliminated the square root and simplified the equation. The next step involves isolating v even further by performing basic algebraic operations. So, let's move on and see how we can isolate v completely and find its potential value(s).

Step 3: Solve for v

With the square root gone, we now have a simple linear equation: 3v + 12 = 9. To solve for v, we need to isolate it on one side of the equation. First, let's get rid of the '+ 12' by subtracting 12 from both sides. This gives us 3v + 12 - 12 = 9 - 12, which simplifies to 3v = -3. We're getting closer! Now, v is being multiplied by 3, so to isolate v, we need to do the inverse operation: divide both sides by 3. This gives us (3v)/3 = (-3)/3, which simplifies to v = -1. Fantastic! We've found a potential solution: v = -1. However, we're not done just yet. Remember, we squared both sides of the equation earlier, which means we might have introduced an extraneous solution. An extraneous solution is a value that satisfies the transformed equation but doesn't work in the original equation. So, we absolutely must check our solution in the original equation to make sure it's a valid answer. This is a crucial step in solving radical equations, and it's one that should never be skipped. So, let's move on to the final step and verify whether v = -1 is indeed a solution.

Step 4: Check for Extraneous Solutions

This is a critical step! We need to check our potential solution (v = -1) in the original equation to make sure it actually works. This is because squaring both sides of an equation can sometimes introduce solutions that aren't valid in the original context. These are called extraneous solutions, and we want to make sure we don't include them in our final answer. So, let's plug v = -1 back into the original equation: √(3v + 12) + 4 = 1. Substituting v = -1, we get √(3(-1) + 12) + 4 = 1. Now, let's simplify: √( -3 + 12) + 4 = 1, which becomes √9 + 4 = 1. We know that √9 is 3, so we have 3 + 4 = 1. This simplifies to 7 = 1. Uh oh! This is definitely not true. This tells us that v = -1 is an extraneous solution. It doesn't satisfy the original equation, even though it satisfied the equation we got after squaring both sides. This highlights the importance of this checking step. So, what does this mean for our problem? It means that there is no real number v that satisfies the original equation. The equation has no solution. This is a perfectly valid outcome in mathematics. Sometimes, equations just don't have solutions, and that's okay. The key is to go through the steps carefully and arrive at the correct conclusion. So, in this case, our final answer is that there is no solution.

Final Answer: No Solution

After carefully working through the steps and checking our potential solution, we've arrived at the final answer: there is no solution to the equation √(3v + 12) + 4 = 1. We started by isolating the square root, then squared both sides, solved for v, and finally, checked for extraneous solutions. The check revealed that our potential solution, v = -1, did not satisfy the original equation. This means that there is no real number value for v that makes the equation true. It's important to remember that this is a perfectly valid outcome in mathematics. Not all equations have solutions, and it's crucial to go through the process of solving and checking to arrive at the correct conclusion. This problem serves as a good reminder of the importance of checking for extraneous solutions when dealing with radical equations. It's a step that should never be skipped, as it can prevent us from incorrectly identifying a value as a solution. So, with that, we've successfully tackled this problem and learned a valuable lesson about solving radical equations. Keep practicing, and you'll become a pro at solving these types of problems!

In conclusion, solving radical equations requires a systematic approach. We must isolate the radical, eliminate it by using inverse operations, solve the resulting equation, and most importantly, check for extraneous solutions. This process ensures that we arrive at the correct solution, or correctly identify when no solution exists. Remember to always be meticulous and careful with each step, and you'll be well-equipped to tackle any radical equation that comes your way!

Calculating Phosphorous Acid Yield From Diphosphorus Trioxide A Stoichiometry Problem

Introduction: Stoichiometry and Chemical Reactions

Hey guys! Today, we're diving into the fascinating world of stoichiometry, which is basically the study of the quantitative relationships between reactants and products in chemical reactions. Think of it as the math behind chemistry! We're going to tackle a problem where we need to figure out how much of a product, specifically phosphorous acid (H3PO3H_3PO_3H3​PO3​), is formed when a certain amount of diphosphorus trioxide (P2O3P_2O_3P2​O3​) reacts with water. This kind of problem is super common in chemistry, so understanding how to solve it is crucial. In this article, we'll break down the steps involved, making sure you grasp the concepts along the way. So, buckle up, and let's get started!

Stoichiometry, at its core, is about understanding the proportions in which chemical reactions occur. It allows chemists to predict how much of a product will be formed from a given amount of reactants, or how much of a reactant is needed to produce a specific quantity of a product. This is incredibly important in various fields, from industrial chemistry (where optimizing production processes is key) to research labs (where precise measurements are necessary for accurate results).

The problem we're tackling today involves a balanced chemical equation, which is the foundation of any stoichiometric calculation. The balanced equation tells us the mole ratio in which the reactants and products interact. In our case, the reaction between P2O3P_2O_3P2​O3​ and H2OH_2OH2​O to form H3PO3H_3PO_3H3​PO3​ is represented by the equation: P2O3+3H2Oightarrow2H3PO3P_2O_3 + 3H_2O ightarrow 2H_3PO_3P2​O3​+3H2​Oightarrow2H3​PO3​. This equation tells us that one mole of P2O3P_2O_3P2​O3​ reacts with three moles of H2OH_2OH2​O to produce two moles of H3PO3H_3PO_3H3​PO3​. This mole ratio is the key to unlocking the solution to our problem. Remember, stoichiometry isn't just about crunching numbers; it's about understanding the fundamental principles that govern chemical reactions. By mastering these principles, you'll be well-equipped to tackle a wide range of chemical problems.

Problem Statement: How Much Phosphorous Acid?

Okay, let's get down to the specifics. The question we're trying to answer is: How many grams of phosphorous acid (H3PO3H_3PO_3H3​PO3​) are formed when 92.3 grams of diphosphorus trioxide (P2O3P_2O_3P2​O3​) react with water? This is a classic stoichiometry problem where we're given the mass of a reactant and asked to find the mass of a product. To solve this, we'll need to use the balanced chemical equation and the concept of molar mass to convert between grams and moles. Remember, the balanced equation provides the mole ratio, which is the bridge between the amounts of different substances in a reaction. The molar mass, on the other hand, is the conversion factor between grams and moles for a specific substance.

So, 92.3 grams of P2O3P_2O_3P2​O3​ is our starting point. This is the given information in the problem, and it's crucial for setting up our calculations. We know that P2O3P_2O_3P2​O3​ will react with water (H2OH_2OH2​O) to produce H3PO3H_3PO_3H3​PO3​. The balanced chemical equation, P2O3+3H2Oightarrow2H3PO3P_2O_3 + 3H_2O ightarrow 2H_3PO_3P2​O3​+3H2​Oightarrow2H3​PO3​, tells us the exact mole relationship between these substances. For every one mole of P2O3P_2O_3P2​O3​ that reacts, two moles of H3PO3H_3PO_3H3​PO3​ are formed. This 1:2 mole ratio is the cornerstone of our calculation. We're not just dealing with moles here; we need to find the mass of H3PO3H_3PO_3H3​PO3​ in grams. This means we'll need to use the molar mass of both P2O3P_2O_3P2​O3​ and H3PO3H_3PO_3H3​PO3​ to convert between grams and moles. The molar mass is the mass of one mole of a substance, and it's calculated by adding up the atomic masses of all the atoms in the chemical formula.

Before we jump into the calculations, let's think about the big picture. We're starting with a mass of P2O3P_2O_3P2​O3​, we'll convert it to moles using its molar mass, then use the mole ratio from the balanced equation to find the moles of H3PO3H_3PO_3H3​PO3​ produced, and finally, we'll convert the moles of H3PO3H_3PO_3H3​PO3​ back to grams using its molar mass. It might seem like a lot of steps, but each step is a simple conversion that builds upon the previous one. Keep this roadmap in mind as we work through the problem, and you'll see how stoichiometry helps us connect the dots between reactants and products.

When we read the problem, we see the question: "How many grams of H3PO3H_3PO_3H3​PO3​ form from 92.3 g P2O3P_2O_3P2​O3​?". The key piece of information here is the 92.3 g of P2O3P_2O_3P2​O3​. This is a mass measurement, and it tells us how much of the diphosphorus trioxide is available for the reaction.

Why is identifying the given so crucial? Well, in stoichiometry, we're essentially following a recipe. We need to know how much of each ingredient (reactant) we're starting with to figure out how much of the final product we'll get. The given information is like the list of ingredients you have in your pantry. Without knowing how much of each ingredient you have, you can't accurately predict how many cookies you can bake! We'll use this information, along with the balanced chemical equation, to figure out how much H3PO3H_3PO_3H3​PO3​ we can make.

Detailed Solution Steps

Now that we've identified the given (92.3 g of P2O3P_2O_3P2​O3​), let's map out the steps we'll take to solve the problem. This is like creating a roadmap for our calculation journey. We're starting with grams of P2O3P_2O_3P2​O3​ and we want to end up with grams of H3PO3H_3PO_3H3​PO3​. To get there, we'll need to go through a few conversions:

  1. Convert grams of P2O3P_2O_3P2​O3​ to moles of P2O3P_2O_3P2​O3​: We'll use the molar mass of P2O3P_2O_3P2​O3​ for this conversion. The molar mass is the mass of one mole of a substance, and we can calculate it by adding up the atomic masses of each element in the compound from the periodic table.
  2. Convert moles of P2O3P_2O_3P2​O3​ to moles of H3PO3H_3PO_3H3​PO3​: This is where the balanced chemical equation comes in!   3. Convert moles of H3PO3H_3PO_3H3​PO3​ to grams of H3PO3H_3PO_3H3​PO3​: Just like in step 1, we'll use the molar mass, but this time the molar mass of H3PO3H_3PO_3H3​PO3​, to convert from moles back to grams.

By carefully stringing these conversions together, we can move from the given information (grams of P2O3P_2O_3P2​O3​) to the desired answer (grams of H3PO3H_3PO_3H3​PO3​).

Detailed Calculation and Answer

Okay, let's crunch some numbers! We'll start by calculating the molar masses of P2O3P_2O_3P2​O3​ and H3PO3H_3PO_3H3​PO3​. Remember, the molar mass is the sum of the atomic masses of all the atoms in the molecule, which we can find on the periodic table.

Now that we have the molar masses, we can start our conversions. We'll use dimensional analysis, which is a fancy way of saying we'll track our units to make sure they cancel out correctly.

  1. Convert grams of P2O3P_2O_3P2​O3​ to moles of P2O3P_2O_3P2​O3​:

  2. Convert moles of P2O3P_2O_3P2​O3​ to moles of H3PO3H_3PO_3H3​PO3​:

    We'll use the mole ratio from the balanced equation: P2O3+3H2Oightarrow2H3PO3P_2O_3 + 3H_2O ightarrow 2H_3PO_3P2​O3​+3H2​Oightarrow2H3​PO3​. This tells us that 1 mol of P2O3P_2O_3P2​O3​ produces 2 mol of H3PO3H_3PO_3H3​PO3​.

  3. Convert moles of H3PO3H_3PO_3H3​PO3​ to grams of H3PO3H_3PO_3H3​PO3​:

    680 mol H3PO3H_3PO_3H3​PO3​ * (82.00 g H3PO3H_3PO_3H3​PO3​ / 1 mol H3PO3H_3PO_3H3​PO3​) = 137.76 g H3PO3H_3PO_3H3​PO3​ (approximately)

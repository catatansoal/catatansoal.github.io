Subtracting Linear Functions: P(x) - Q(x) Explained

Aug 7, 2025 by ADMIN 52 views

Hey guys! Today, let's dive into the world of linear functions and tackle a fun little problem. We're given two functions, p(x) and q(x), and our mission is to find the difference between them, which is p(x) - q(x). Don't worry, it's not as scary as it sounds! We'll break it down step by step, so you'll be a pro in no time.

Understanding Linear Functions

Before we jump into the calculation, let's quickly recap what linear functions are all about. A linear function is basically a function that, when graphed, forms a straight line. The general form of a linear function is f(x) = mx + b, where m represents the slope (the steepness of the line) and b represents the y-intercept (the point where the line crosses the y-axis). Our functions p(x) and q(x) fit this form perfectly, so we know we're dealing with linear functions.

In our case, we have p(x) = 6x - 2. Here, the slope is 6, and the y-intercept is -2. This means that for every 1 unit increase in x, the value of p(x) increases by 6, and the line crosses the y-axis at the point (0, -2). Similarly, for q(x) = 3x + 5, the slope is 3, and the y-intercept is 5. So, for every 1 unit increase in x, the value of q(x) increases by 3, and the line crosses the y-axis at the point (0, 5). Understanding these individual functions is the first step towards finding the difference between them. Now that we have a solid grasp of what linear functions are, we can confidently move on to the next step: setting up the subtraction.

Setting up the Subtraction: p(x) - q(x)

The key to solving this problem is to remember that we're subtracting the entire function q(x) from p(x). This means we need to be careful with our signs, especially when dealing with terms inside parentheses. Let's write out the expression for p(x) - q(x) by substituting the given functions:

p(x) - q(x) = (6x - 2) - (3x + 5)

Notice the parentheses around both functions. They're crucial because they remind us that we need to distribute the negative sign in front of the second set of parentheses. It's a common mistake to only subtract the first term of q(x), but we need to subtract the entire expression. Think of it like this: we're not just subtracting 3x, we're subtracting the whole package, which is 3x + 5. So, the negative sign needs to affect both terms inside the parentheses. This careful setup ensures that we don't make any silly mistakes later on. Now that we have the expression set up correctly, the next step is to distribute the negative sign and simplify the expression. This involves a bit of algebraic manipulation, but nothing too complicated. We'll carefully walk through each step to make sure you're following along. Remember, the goal is to combine like terms and arrive at a simplified expression for p(x) - q(x).

Distributing and Simplifying

Alright, let's get rid of those parentheses! To do that, we need to distribute the negative sign in front of the second set of parentheses. This means we multiply each term inside the parentheses by -1. So, (3x + 5) becomes -3x - 5. Our expression now looks like this:

p(x) - q(x) = 6x - 2 - 3x - 5

See how the positive 3x became negative, and the positive 5 also became negative? That's the magic of distributing the negative sign! Now that we've successfully removed the parentheses, the next step is to combine like terms. Like terms are terms that have the same variable raised to the same power. In our expression, we have two terms with x (6x and -3x) and two constant terms (-2 and -5). We can add or subtract like terms to simplify the expression. Let's start with the x terms: 6x - 3x = 3x. And now the constant terms: -2 - 5 = -7. Putting it all together, we get:

p(x) - q(x) = 3x - 7

And there you have it! We've successfully simplified the expression. This is the final form of p(x) - q(x). It's a new linear function with a slope of 3 and a y-intercept of -7. We took the original two functions, p(x) and q(x), and by carefully subtracting and simplifying, we've arrived at a single, concise expression that represents their difference. This is a common operation in algebra, and mastering it is crucial for understanding more complex mathematical concepts.

The Final Result: p(x) - q(x) = 3x - 7

So, after all that careful work, we've arrived at the final answer: p(x) - q(x) = 3x - 7. This is the linear function that represents the difference between p(x) and q(x). It tells us that for any value of x, we can plug it into this function to find the difference between the values of p(x) and q(x) at that same x. For example, if we wanted to find the difference when x is 0, we would plug in 0 into our result: 3(0) - 7 = -7. This means that when x is 0, p(x) - q(x) is -7. Similarly, we can find the difference for any other value of x. This result is not just a number; it's a function in itself! It has its own slope and y-intercept, just like the original functions. The slope of our result is 3, and the y-intercept is -7. This means the line representing p(x) - q(x) is less steep than p(x) but has the same steepness as q(x), and it crosses the y-axis at the point (0, -7). Understanding the final result in this way gives us a deeper understanding of what we've accomplished. We haven't just found a single number; we've found a whole new function that describes the relationship between p(x) and q(x).

Key Takeaways and Tips

Okay, let's recap the key things we learned today and throw in a few tips to help you ace similar problems in the future. First, remember the importance of parentheses when subtracting functions. They ensure you distribute the negative sign correctly. It's a small step, but it makes a huge difference in the final answer. Second, always combine like terms after distributing. This simplifies the expression and makes it easier to understand. Third, don't be afraid to break down the problem into smaller, more manageable steps. We started by understanding what linear functions are, then we set up the subtraction, distributed, simplified, and finally arrived at the answer. Each step is straightforward on its own, and when you put them together, you can solve even complex problems. Fourth, double-check your work, especially the signs. A small sign error can throw off the whole calculation. Fifth, practice makes perfect! The more you work with linear functions, the more comfortable you'll become with these types of problems. Try working through similar examples with different functions to solidify your understanding. And finally, remember that math is not just about finding the right answer; it's about understanding the process. So, focus on the steps, the reasoning, and the underlying concepts, and you'll be well on your way to mastering linear functions!

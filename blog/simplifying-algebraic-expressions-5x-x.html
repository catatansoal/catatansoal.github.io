Simplifying Algebraic Expressions 5x(x^(2/3)y^(1/3)) + 2(x^(5/3)y^(1/3)) A Step By Step Guide

Dissecting the Expression: A Closer Look

Before we jump into the simplification process, let's take a moment to truly understand what we're dealing with. Our expression, 5x(x(2/3)y(1/3)) + 2(x(5/3)y(1/3)), is a sum of two terms. Each term involves variables x and y raised to fractional exponents, which might seem a bit scary at first. But remember, fractional exponents are just another way of representing radicals or roots. For instance, x^(2/3) is the same as the cube root of x squared (∛(x²)), and y^(1/3) is the cube root of y (∛y). Understanding this connection between fractional exponents and radicals is the key to unlocking the simplification process.

The expression also involves coefficients – the numbers that multiply the variable terms. We have 5 and 2 as our coefficients, which will play a crucial role in the final simplified form. The presence of both x and y in the expression tells us that we're working in two dimensions, and the relationship between these variables is defined by the exponents and coefficients. So, keep your focus, and let’s begin the journey to simplify this expression together, making sure every step is crystal clear and easy to follow. We're not just doing math; we're uncovering a beautiful pattern hidden within the symbols.

The Power of Exponent Rules: Our Mathematical Toolkit

To simplify this expression effectively, we're going to lean heavily on the rules of exponents. Think of these rules as our trusty mathematical toolkit – they provide us with the necessary tools to manipulate and simplify expressions involving exponents. One of the most crucial rules we'll be using is the product of powers rule: when multiplying terms with the same base, you add the exponents. Mathematically, this is expressed as x^m * x^n = x^(m+n). This rule will be instrumental in combining the x terms in our expression.

Another important concept to keep in mind is how to handle coefficients. Remember, coefficients are simply multipliers, so we can multiply them directly. For example, 5 * 2 is just 10. When simplifying, we'll also be looking for common factors within the terms. Identifying common factors allows us to factor out these terms, making the expression more concise and easier to work with. Understanding the nuances of exponent rules and coefficient manipulation will transform what seems like a complex problem into a series of manageable steps. So, let’s keep these rules in mind as we move forward, and watch how they help us unravel the expression's hidden simplicity.

Step-by-Step Simplification: Let's Get to Work!

Okay, guys, let’s roll up our sleeves and start simplifying our expression 5x(x(2/3)y(1/3)) + 2(x(5/3)y(1/3)). Remember, the key here is to take it one step at a time, applying our exponent rules and keeping things organized. First, let’s focus on the first term: 5x(x(2/3)y(1/3)). Notice that we have an x term outside the parentheses and an x^(2/3) term inside. This is where our product of powers rule comes into play. We can rewrite x as x^1, so we have 5 * x^1 * x^(2/3) * y^(1/3). Now, we add the exponents of the x terms: 1 + 2/3. To add these, we need a common denominator, so we rewrite 1 as 3/3. Thus, 3/3 + 2/3 equals 5/3. This means our first term simplifies to 5x(5/3)y(1/3).

Now, let’s look at the second term: 2(x(5/3)y(1/3)). There's nothing to simplify within this term – it's already in a pretty neat form. So, now we have our simplified terms: 5x(5/3)y(1/3) and 2x(5/3)y(1/3). Notice anything special? Both terms have the same variable parts: x(5/3)y(1/3). This means we can combine these terms just like we combine like terms in any algebraic expression. We simply add the coefficients: 5 + 2 = 7. And that gives us our final simplified expression: 7x(5/3)y(1/3). Wasn't that satisfying? By carefully applying the rules of exponents and breaking the problem down into smaller steps, we've transformed a seemingly complex expression into a much simpler form. Let's celebrate this small victory and move on to discuss the significance of our simplified expression and its potential applications.

The Simplified Expression: 7x(5/3)y(1/3) and Its Significance

So, after our mathematical journey, we've arrived at the simplified expression: 7x(5/3)y(1/3). But what does this actually mean? Why is this simplified form significant? Well, for starters, it's much easier to work with! Imagine trying to substitute values for x and y in the original expression versus plugging them into our simplified version. The simplified form reduces the number of calculations we need to perform, making it less prone to errors.

Furthermore, the simplified expression provides a clearer picture of the relationship between x and y. The exponents tell us how the variables contribute to the overall value of the expression. The coefficient, 7, acts as a scaling factor. This form is particularly useful in calculus and other advanced mathematical fields where manipulating expressions is a common task. For example, if we needed to find the derivative or integral of this expression, the simplified form would make the process much smoother. Moreover, the simplified form allows us to quickly identify the degree of the expression and its behavior as x and y change. In essence, simplifying algebraic expressions isn't just about making them look prettier; it's about gaining a deeper understanding of the underlying mathematical relationships.

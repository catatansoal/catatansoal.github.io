Graph (x+4)(x-7) ≥ 0: Number Line Solution

Aug 7, 2025 by ADMIN 43 views

Have you ever stared at an inequality like (x+4)(x-7) ≥ 0 and wondered how to visualize its solution? Don't worry, you're not alone! Many students find graphing inequalities on a number line a bit tricky, but with a step-by-step approach, it becomes much clearer. This guide will walk you through the process, ensuring you understand not just the how, but also the why behind each step. So, let's dive in and master this essential mathematical skill, guys!

Understanding the Inequality

Before we jump into graphing, let's break down what the inequality (x+4)(x-7) ≥ 0 actually means. In simple terms, we're looking for all the values of x that, when plugged into the expression (x+4)(x-7), result in a value that is either greater than or equal to zero. This means we're interested in the regions where the expression is positive or zero.

The expression (x+4)(x-7) is a quadratic expression, and it represents a parabola when graphed on a coordinate plane. However, for our purposes, we're focusing on the number line, which represents all possible values of x. The points where the expression equals zero are crucial because they divide the number line into intervals where the expression is either positive or negative. These points are called critical points or zeros.

To find these critical points, we set each factor in the expression equal to zero:

  • x + 4 = 0 => x = -4
  • x - 7 = 0 => x = 7

So, our critical points are x = -4 and x = 7. These points are the key to unlocking the solution to our inequality. They divide the number line into three intervals: (-∞, -4), (-4, 7), and (7, ∞). Now, we need to determine whether the expression (x+4)(x-7) is positive, negative, or zero within each of these intervals. This is where the concept of test intervals comes into play.

Identifying Critical Points

Critical points are the values of x where the expression (x+4)(x-7) equals zero. As we found earlier, these points are x = -4 and x = 7. These points are crucial because they mark the boundaries where the expression can change its sign (from positive to negative or vice versa). Think of them as the "turning points" of our inequality.

Graphically, these critical points represent the x-intercepts of the parabola represented by the equation y = (x+4)(x-7). At these points, the parabola crosses the x-axis, which means the y-value (or the value of the expression) is zero. To plot these points on the number line, we simply draw closed circles (or filled-in dots) at x = -4 and x = 7. We use closed circles because the inequality includes "or equal to" (≥), meaning these points are part of the solution.

The number line now looks like this:

-----●-----|-----●-----
    -4          7

Here, the filled circles at -4 and 7 indicate that these points are included in the solution set. The number line is now divided into three distinct intervals, each of which we'll analyze in the next step. Remember, identifying these critical points is the first and most crucial step in solving and graphing inequalities like this. Without them, we wouldn't know where to start testing the intervals.

Using Test Intervals

With our critical points identified, the next step is to use test intervals to determine the sign of the expression (x+4)(x-7) in each of the intervals created by these points. As we discussed earlier, our critical points x = -4 and x = 7 divide the number line into three intervals: (-∞, -4), (-4, 7), and (7, ∞). To figure out whether the expression is positive or negative in each interval, we pick a test value within each interval and plug it into the expression.

Let's start with the first interval, (-∞, -4). A convenient test value here would be x = -5. Plugging this into our expression, we get:

  • (-5 + 4)(-5 - 7) = (-1)(-12) = 12

Since 12 is positive, the expression (x+4)(x-7) is positive in the interval (-∞, -4). This means all values of x in this interval satisfy the inequality (x+4)(x-7) ≥ 0.

Next, let's consider the interval (-4, 7). A good test value here would be x = 0. Plugging this into our expression, we get:

  • (0 + 4)(0 - 7) = (4)(-7) = -28

Since -28 is negative, the expression (x+4)(x-7) is negative in the interval (-4, 7). This means no values of x in this interval satisfy our inequality.

Finally, let's look at the interval (7, ∞). A convenient test value here would be x = 8. Plugging this into our expression, we get:

  • (8 + 4)(8 - 7) = (12)(1) = 12

Since 12 is positive, the expression (x+4)(x-7) is positive in the interval (7, ∞). This means all values of x in this interval satisfy the inequality.

By using test intervals, we've successfully determined the sign of the expression in each interval. This information is crucial for graphing the solution on the number line.

Graphing the Solution

Now that we know where the expression (x+4)(x-7) is greater than or equal to zero, we can graph the solution on the number line. Remember, we found that the expression is positive in the intervals (-∞, -4) and (7, ∞), and it's equal to zero at x = -4 and x = 7. These are the regions and points that satisfy our inequality (x+4)(x-7) ≥ 0.

To graph the solution, we shade the intervals (-∞, -4) and (7, ∞) on the number line. Shading indicates that all the values within these intervals are part of the solution. Since the inequality includes "or equal to," we also include the critical points x = -4 and x = 7 in our solution. We've already marked these points with closed circles.

The graph of the solution looks like this:

<------------------●-----|-----●------------------>
(-∞)             -4          7             (∞)

The shaded regions extending to negative infinity and positive infinity, along with the closed circles at -4 and 7, visually represent the solution to the inequality. This graph tells us that any value of x less than or equal to -4 or greater than or equal to 7 will make the expression (x+4)(x-7) greater than or equal to zero.

In interval notation, we can write the solution as (-∞, -4] ∪ [7, ∞). The square brackets indicate that -4 and 7 are included in the solution set, while the parentheses indicate that negative and positive infinity are not (since infinity is not a number).

Graphing the solution on the number line provides a clear and intuitive way to understand the range of values that satisfy the inequality. It's a powerful tool for visualizing solutions and communicating them effectively.

Writing the Solution in Interval Notation

After graphing the solution on the number line, it's often helpful to express the solution set using interval notation. Interval notation is a concise way to represent a set of numbers using intervals and symbols. It's a standard way to communicate solutions in mathematics, guys.

Looking at our graph, we have two shaded regions: one extending from negative infinity to -4, and another extending from 7 to positive infinity. In interval notation, we represent these regions as follows:

  • The interval from negative infinity to -4 is written as (-∞, -4]. The parenthesis next to negative infinity indicates that infinity is not included in the interval (since it's not a number), while the square bracket next to -4 indicates that -4 is included in the interval because our inequality includes "or equal to."
  • The interval from 7 to positive infinity is written as [7, ∞). Similarly, the square bracket next to 7 indicates that 7 is included in the interval, and the parenthesis next to positive infinity indicates that infinity is not included.

Since we have two separate intervals in our solution, we use the union symbol (∪) to combine them. The union symbol means "or," so the solution includes all numbers in either interval.

Therefore, the solution to the inequality (x+4)(x-7) ≥ 0 in interval notation is:

(-∞, -4] ∪ [7, ∞)

This notation clearly and concisely represents all the values of x that satisfy the inequality. It's a powerful tool for communicating solutions in a mathematical context.

Conclusion

Graphing the solution to the inequality (x+4)(x-7) ≥ 0 on the number line involves several key steps: identifying critical points, using test intervals, graphing the solution, and writing the solution in interval notation. By following these steps, you can confidently solve and visualize inequalities of this type. Remember, the critical points are the "turning points," the test intervals help determine the sign of the expression, and the graph provides a visual representation of the solution. And finally, interval notation gives us a concise way to express the solution set.

Mastering these techniques will not only help you in your mathematics courses but also provide a solid foundation for more advanced mathematical concepts. Keep practicing, and you'll become a pro at graphing inequalities in no time, guys! If you get stuck, just remember to break it down step by step, and you'll get there. Happy graphing!

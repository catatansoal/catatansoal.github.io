Oxygen From Aluminum Oxide: A Mole Ratio Calculation

Aug 5, 2025 by ADMIN 53 views

Hey there, chemistry enthusiasts! Let's dive into a fascinating chemical reaction and explore the world of stoichiometry. Today, we're tackling the decomposition of aluminum oxide (Al2O3Al_2O_3Al2​O3​) and figuring out just how much oxygen (O2O_2O2​) we can get from it. This is a classic example of how we use balanced chemical equations to predict the quantities of reactants and products in a reaction. So, grab your calculators and let's get started!

The Reaction: A Quick Look

The reaction we're focusing on is:

2Al2O3ightarrow4Al+3O22 Al_2 O_3 ightarrow 4 Al + 3 O_22Al2​O3​ightarrow4Al+3O2​

This equation tells us a story. It says that for every 2 moles of aluminum oxide that decompose, we get 4 moles of aluminum and 3 moles of oxygen. The coefficients in front of each chemical formula are super important because they give us the mole ratios. These ratios are the key to solving stoichiometry problems. Think of it like a recipe: if you double the ingredients, you'll double the output, right? It's the same principle here.

Stoichiometry: The Math Behind the Magic

Now, let's talk about stoichiometry. It's a fancy word, but all it really means is the study of the quantitative relationships between reactants and products in chemical reactions. In simpler terms, it's the math that helps us figure out how much of something we need or how much of something we'll get in a chemical reaction. And the mole is our best friend in this calculation game. A mole is just a unit of measurement, like a dozen, but for atoms and molecules. One mole contains Avogadro's number (6.022imes10236.022 imes 10^{23}6.022imes1023) of particles. So, when we talk about moles, we're talking about a specific number of molecules or atoms.

To solve stoichiometry problems, we usually follow these steps:

  1. Make sure the equation is balanced: This is crucial! A balanced equation tells us the correct mole ratios.
  2. Convert given quantities to moles: If we're given grams, liters, or anything else, we need to convert it to moles first.
  3. Use the mole ratio from the balanced equation: This is the heart of the calculation. We use the ratio to convert moles of one substance to moles of another.
  4. Convert back to the desired units: If the question asks for grams, we convert back from moles to grams.

Cracking the Problem: How Much Oxygen?

Our main question is: How many moles of oxygen are produced when 26.5 moles of aluminum oxide decompose? We've already got our balanced equation, which is a great start.

2Al2O3ightarrow4Al+3O22 Al_2 O_3 ightarrow 4 Al + 3 O_22Al2​O3​ightarrow4Al+3O2​

We're given 26.5 moles of Al2O3Al_2O_3Al2​O3​. Now, let's use the mole ratio from the balanced equation. The equation tells us that 2 moles of Al2O3Al_2O_3Al2​O3​ produce 3 moles of O2O_2O2​. This gives us a mole ratio of 3 moles O2O_2O2​ / 2 moles Al2O3Al_2O_3Al2​O3​.

We can use this ratio as a conversion factor:

Moles of O2O_2O2​ = 26.5 moles Al2O3Al_2O_3Al2​O3​ × (3 moles O2O_2O2​ / 2 moles Al2O3Al_2O_3Al2​O3​)

Notice how the "moles Al2O3Al_2O_3Al2​O3​" units cancel out, leaving us with moles of O2O_2O2​, which is exactly what we want. Now, let's do the math:

Moles of O2O_2O2​ = 26.5 × (3 / 2) = 26.5 × 1.5 = 39.75 moles

So, when 26.5 moles of aluminum oxide decompose, we produce 39.75 moles of oxygen. Looking at our answer choices, the closest one is:

C. 39.8 mol

Why Mole Ratios are Key

The mole ratio is the cornerstone of stoichiometric calculations. It allows us to bridge the gap between different substances in a chemical reaction. Think of it as a recipe again. If you know the ratio of flour to sugar in a cake, you can easily scale the recipe up or down. Similarly, the mole ratio tells us how the amounts of different substances in a reaction are related. Mastering mole ratios is essential for success in chemistry!

Real-World Applications of Stoichiometry

Stoichiometry isn't just an academic exercise; it has tons of real-world applications. Here are a few examples:

  • Chemical Industry: Stoichiometry is used to calculate the amounts of reactants needed to produce specific quantities of products. This is crucial for optimizing chemical processes and minimizing waste.
  • Pharmaceuticals: Drug manufacturers use stoichiometry to ensure that they're using the correct amounts of ingredients in their medications. This is vital for safety and efficacy.
  • Environmental Science: Stoichiometry is used to study air and water pollution. For example, it can be used to calculate the amount of a pollutant produced by a certain activity.
  • Cooking and Baking: Yes, even cooking and baking involve stoichiometry! Recipes are essentially stoichiometric ratios. If you want to double a recipe, you need to double all the ingredients to maintain the correct proportions.

Common Stoichiometry Mistakes to Avoid

Stoichiometry can be tricky, and it's easy to make mistakes if you're not careful. Here are some common pitfalls to watch out for:

  1. Not balancing the equation: This is the most common mistake. If your equation isn't balanced, your mole ratios will be wrong, and your calculations will be off.
  2. Using the wrong mole ratio: Make sure you're using the correct ratio from the balanced equation. Double-check which substances you're comparing.
  3. Not converting to moles: Remember, the mole is the central unit in stoichiometry. If you're given grams or liters, you need to convert to moles before using the mole ratio.
  4. Incorrectly rounding: Rounding too early in the calculation can lead to significant errors in the final answer. It's best to round at the very end.
  5. Forgetting units: Always include units in your calculations. This helps you keep track of what you're doing and ensures that your answer has the correct units.

Practice Makes Perfect: More Stoichiometry Problems

The best way to master stoichiometry is to practice, practice, practice! Let's try a couple more examples to solidify our understanding.

Example 1:

Consider the reaction:

N2+3H2ightarrow2NH3N_2 + 3H_2 ightarrow 2NH_3N2​+3H2​ightarrow2NH3​

How many moles of ammonia (NH3NH_3NH3​) are produced when 6 moles of hydrogen (H2H_2H2​) react completely?

Solution:

  1. The equation is already balanced.
  2. We're given 6 moles of H2H_2H2​.
  3. The mole ratio of NH3NH_3NH3​ to H2H_2H2​ is 2:3.
  4. Moles of NH3NH_3NH3​ = 6 moles H2H_2H2​ × (2 moles NH3NH_3NH3​ / 3 moles H2H_2H2​) = 4 moles NH3NH_3NH3​

So, 4 moles of ammonia are produced.

Example 2:

Consider the reaction:

2KClO3ightarrow2KCl+3O22KClO_3 ightarrow 2KCl + 3O_22KClO3​ightarrow2KCl+3O2​

If 12.25 grams of KClO3KClO_3KClO3​ are decomposed, how many grams of O2O_2O2​ are produced? (Molar mass of KClO3KClO_3KClO3​ = 122.55 g/mol, molar mass of O2O_2O2​ = 32.00 g/mol)

Solution:

  1. The equation is already balanced.
  2. Convert grams of KClO3KClO_3KClO3​ to moles: 12.25 g / 122.55 g/mol = 0.1 moles
  3. The mole ratio of O2O_2O2​ to KClO3KClO_3KClO3​ is 3:2.
  4. Moles of O2O_2O2​ = 0.1 moles KClO3KClO_3KClO3​ × (3 moles O2O_2O2​ / 2 moles KClO3KClO_3KClO3​) = 0.15 moles
  5. Convert moles of O2O_2O2​ to grams: 0.15 moles × 32.00 g/mol = 4.8 grams

So, 4.8 grams of oxygen are produced.

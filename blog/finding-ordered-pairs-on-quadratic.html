Finding Ordered Pairs On Quadratic Function F(x) = -1/4x² - 1

Jul 13, 2025 by ADMIN 62 views

Hey there, math enthusiasts! Today, we're diving deep into the world of quadratic functions and ordered pairs. Our mission? To figure out which ordered pair, from a set of options, actually sits pretty on the graph of the function f(x) = -1/4x² - 1. Sounds like a fun puzzle, right? Let's get started!

Understanding the Function f(x) = -1/4x² - 1

First, let's break down this function. The given function is f(x) = -1/4x² - 1. This is a quadratic function, which means it's in the form of f(x) = ax² + bx + c. In our case, a is -1/4, b is 0 (since there's no x term), and c is -1. The x² term tells us it's a parabola, and the negative coefficient (-1/4) means the parabola opens downwards – like a sad face, not a smiley one. Understanding the nature of the quadratic function is crucial. The constant term, -1, shifts the entire graph down by one unit on the y-axis. This is something we need to keep in mind as we evaluate our ordered pairs. A quadratic function is essentially a polynomial function with one or more variables in which the highest-degree term is second degree. This typically involves terms like x², and it defines the shape of a parabola when graphed on a coordinate plane. The function's characteristics, such as whether it opens upwards or downwards and its vertical shift, are determined by its coefficients and constants. To fully grasp the behavior of the quadratic function f(x) = -1/4x² - 1, it's also crucial to understand the impact of each term on the parabola's shape and position. The coefficient of the x² term dictates whether the parabola opens upwards or downwards, as well as how wide or narrow it is. In this case, the negative coefficient indicates a downward-opening parabola. Moreover, the constant term affects the vertical shift of the parabola. Understanding these nuances helps in predicting the graph's behavior and identifying ordered pairs that lie on it. Remember, the graph of a quadratic function is a symmetrical U-shaped curve called a parabola. The symmetry is around a vertical line known as the axis of symmetry, which passes through the vertex of the parabola. The vertex is the point where the parabola changes direction – either the highest point (maximum) for a downward-opening parabola or the lowest point (minimum) for an upward-opening parabola. Recognizing these features can assist in visually confirming the function's nature and validating potential solutions. When dealing with quadratic functions, it's also essential to be aware of the various forms in which they can be expressed, such as standard form, vertex form, and factored form. Each form provides different insights into the function's characteristics. For instance, the standard form makes it easy to identify the coefficients, while the vertex form directly reveals the coordinates of the vertex. Familiarizing yourself with these forms enhances your ability to analyze and solve problems involving quadratic functions. Visualizing the graph of the quadratic function can also be a valuable tool in understanding its behavior. By plotting a few points and connecting them, you can see the shape of the parabola and how it relates to the function's equation. This visual representation can provide additional context and intuition when solving problems involving ordered pairs and function evaluations. Furthermore, understanding the concept of zeros or roots of a quadratic function is crucial in many mathematical contexts. The zeros are the x-values for which the function equals zero, and they correspond to the points where the parabola intersects the x-axis. Finding the zeros involves solving the quadratic equation, which can be done through various methods such as factoring, completing the square, or using the quadratic formula.

What are Ordered Pairs?

Now, what about ordered pairs? An ordered pair is simply a pair of numbers, written as (x, y), that represents a point on a coordinate plane. The first number, x, tells us how far to move along the horizontal axis (the x-axis), and the second number, y, tells us how far to move along the vertical axis (the y-axis). To determine if an ordered pair lies on the function's graph, we substitute the x-value into the function and see if the result matches the y-value. If it does, bingo! The point is on the graph. An ordered pair is a fundamental concept in mathematics, particularly in the context of coordinate geometry. It consists of two values, conventionally referred to as the x-coordinate and the y-coordinate, enclosed in parentheses and separated by a comma. The order of these values is crucial because it determines the location of the point on the coordinate plane. The x-coordinate represents the horizontal position, indicating how far to move along the x-axis from the origin, while the y-coordinate represents the vertical position, indicating how far to move along the y-axis from the origin. Understanding the role of ordered pairs is essential for graphing functions and analyzing relationships between variables. Each point on the coordinate plane corresponds to a unique ordered pair, and vice versa. This correspondence allows us to visually represent mathematical functions and equations, making it easier to understand their behavior and properties. For example, when plotting a graph of a function, we typically generate a set of ordered pairs by evaluating the function at different x-values. These ordered pairs then serve as the coordinates of points on the graph. By connecting these points, we can visualize the function's curve and observe its patterns and trends. Furthermore, ordered pairs are also used extensively in various other areas of mathematics, such as linear algebra, calculus, and statistics. In linear algebra, ordered pairs can represent vectors or points in space, while in calculus, they are used to define curves and surfaces. In statistics, ordered pairs can represent data points, allowing for the analysis of relationships between variables. The concept of ordered pairs extends beyond two-dimensional space as well. In three-dimensional space, for instance, we use ordered triples (x, y, z) to represent points, where the z-coordinate indicates the position along the third axis. Similarly, in higher dimensions, we can use ordered n-tuples to represent points or vectors. These extensions of the ordered pair concept are crucial in various advanced mathematical and scientific applications. When working with functions, ordered pairs provide a powerful tool for verifying whether a particular point lies on the function's graph. To do this, we substitute the x-coordinate of the ordered pair into the function and check if the result matches the y-coordinate. If it does, then the point is on the graph; otherwise, it is not. This method is widely used in algebra and calculus to verify solutions and analyze function behavior.

Testing the Options

Alright, let's put our detective hats on and test each ordered pair:

A) (2, 2)

Plug in x = 2 into our function:

f(2) = -1/4(2)² - 1 = -1/4(4) - 1 = -1 - 1 = -2

So, when x is 2, f(x) is -2. This means the ordered pair should be (2, -2), not (2, 2). Option A is a no-go.

B) (-2, 2)

Let's try x = -2:

f(-2) = -1/4(-2)² - 1 = -1/4(4) - 1 = -1 - 1 = -2

Again, when x is -2, f(x) is -2. The ordered pair should be (-2, -2), not (-2, 2). Option B is out.

C) (2, -2)

We actually already calculated this one in option A! When x is 2, f(x) is indeed -2. So, the ordered pair (2, -2) does lie on the function. We have a contender!

D) (0, -2)

Let's give this one a shot:

f(0) = -1/4(0)² - 1 = -1/4(0) - 1 = 0 - 1 = -1

When x is 0, f(x) is -1, not -2. So, (0, -2) is not on the function.

Option A Explained

Starting with Option A, we were given the ordered pair (2, 2). To determine if this ordered pair lies on the quadratic function f(x) = -1/4x² - 1, we substituted x = 2 into the function. This yields f(2) = -1/4(2)² - 1. Evaluating this expression, we first square 2, which gives us 4. Then, we multiply -1/4 by 4, resulting in -1. Finally, we subtract 1 from -1, which gives us -2. Thus, f(2) = -2. This indicates that when x is 2, the corresponding y-value should be -2. However, the given ordered pair in Option A has a y-value of 2, not -2. Therefore, the ordered pair (2, 2) does not lie on the graph of the function. It's essential to follow the order of operations correctly to obtain the accurate result. First, perform exponentiation, then multiplication, and finally, addition or subtraction. This ensures that the function is evaluated correctly and the correct y-value is obtained for the given x-value. In this case, the error would have been made if the order of operations was not followed correctly. If we had incorrectly subtracted 1 from -1/4 before squaring 2, we would have obtained an incorrect y-value, leading to a wrong conclusion about whether the ordered pair lies on the function's graph. For instance, if we subtracted 1 from -1/4 first, we would get -5/4. Then, if we squared 2 and multiplied it by -5/4, we would get -5, which is not the correct y-value. This highlights the importance of adhering to the correct order of operations when evaluating quadratic functions or any mathematical expressions. Furthermore, it's helpful to visualize the graph of the quadratic function to gain a better understanding of why the ordered pair (2, 2) does not lie on it. The function f(x) = -1/4x² - 1 represents a parabola that opens downwards, with its vertex at (0, -1). This means that the function's values decrease as we move away from the vertex in either direction along the x-axis. At x = 2, the function's value is f(2) = -2, which is lower than the y-value of 2 in the given ordered pair. This indicates that the point (2, 2) is located above the parabola and, therefore, does not lie on the graph of the quadratic function. Additionally, understanding the symmetry of parabolas can be helpful in verifying results. Parabolas are symmetrical about a vertical line that passes through the vertex. In this case, the axis of symmetry is the y-axis (x = 0). This means that for any x-value, there is a corresponding x-value on the opposite side of the axis of symmetry that yields the same y-value. For example, since f(2) = -2, we can expect that f(-2) will also be equal to -2. This symmetry can be used to double-check calculations and identify potential errors. Visualizing the parabola and its symmetry can provide a more intuitive understanding of why certain ordered pairs lie on the graph while others do not. By considering the shape, direction, and position of the parabola, we can make informed judgments about the validity of ordered pairs.

Option B Explained

Now, let's delve into why Option B, which presents the ordered pair (-2, 2), does not lie on the quadratic function f(x) = -1/4x² - 1. Similar to Option A, our approach here is to substitute the x-value from the ordered pair into the function and then compare the resulting y-value with the y-value given in the ordered pair. When we substitute x = -2 into the function, we get f(-2) = -1/4(-2)² - 1. Evaluating this expression, we first square -2, which results in 4. Next, we multiply -1/4 by 4, which gives us -1. Finally, we subtract 1 from -1, leading to f(-2) = -2. This result indicates that the corresponding y-value for x = -2 should be -2, not 2 as given in Option B. Therefore, the ordered pair (-2, 2) does not lie on the graph of the quadratic function. One crucial aspect to note here is the importance of correctly handling negative numbers when performing mathematical operations. Squaring a negative number always results in a positive number. In this case, (-2)² = 4, which is a positive value. If the squaring operation were performed incorrectly, it could lead to an erroneous result, potentially causing a wrong conclusion about whether the ordered pair lies on the function's graph. For instance, if we incorrectly thought that (-2)² = -4, our subsequent calculations would be incorrect, and we would likely arrive at the wrong y-value for the ordered pair. Additionally, it's worth highlighting the symmetry property of quadratic functions. As mentioned earlier, parabolas are symmetrical about a vertical line that passes through the vertex. In this specific function, the axis of symmetry is the y-axis, which means that if an ordered pair (x, y) lies on the graph, then the ordered pair (-x, y) will also lie on the graph, provided that the x values are not at the vertex. In this case, we found that f(-2) = -2, which means that the ordered pair (-2, -2) lies on the graph. Since x = -2 is not the vertex, we would expect that f(2) should also equal -2, confirming that (2, -2) also lies on the graph. This symmetry can be a useful tool for verifying results and gaining a deeper understanding of the function's behavior. As we move away from the vertex along the x-axis in either direction, the y-values of the function decrease. At x = -2, the function's value is f(-2) = -2, which is lower than the y-value of 2 in the ordered pair. This suggests that the point (-2, 2) is located above the parabola and, therefore, does not lie on the graph of the function. Understanding the shape and position of the parabola in relation to the given ordered pair can help in making informed decisions about whether the ordered pair satisfies the function.

Following our established method, we substitute x = 0 into the function, which yields f(0) = -1/4(0)² - 1. Evaluating this expression, we first square 0, which results in 0. Then, we multiply -1/4 by 0, which also equals 0. Finally, we subtract 1 from 0, leading to f(0) = -1. This result indicates that when x = 0, the corresponding y-value should be -1, not -2 as given in Option D. Therefore, we can conclude that the ordered pair (0, -2) does not lie on the graph of the quadratic function. The point where x = 0 is particularly significant because it corresponds to the y-intercept of the function's graph. The y-intercept is the point where the parabola intersects the y-axis, and it is found by setting x to 0 in the function's equation. In this case, we found that f(0) = -1, which means that the y-intercept of the parabola is at the point (0, -1). This is also the vertex of the downward-opening parabola, as the vertex is the point where the parabola changes direction. The fact that the ordered pair (0, -2) has the same x-value as the y-intercept but a different y-value confirms that it cannot lie on the function's graph. Visualizing the graph of the quadratic function can further illustrate this point. In this case, since the parabola opens downwards, the vertex represents the maximum point of the function. This means that the y-values of all other points on the parabola will be less than or equal to the y-value of the vertex, which is -1. The ordered pair (0, -2) has a y-value of -2, which is less than the y-value of the vertex. However, since the vertex is the maximum point, any point with a y-value less than the y-value of the vertex cannot lie on the parabola. This concept provides another way to understand why Option D is incorrect.

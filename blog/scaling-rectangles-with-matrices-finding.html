Scaling Rectangles With Matrices Finding New Vertices

Jul 13, 2025 by ADMIN 54 views

Hey guys! Today, we're diving into a cool math problem that combines linear algebra and geometry. We're going to explore how matrices can represent geometric shapes and how scaling a matrix affects the shape's vertices. Specifically, we'll be looking at a rectangle defined by the columns of a matrix and how multiplying the matrix by a scalar changes its vertices. Let's get started!

Understanding the Problem

Our problem involves a matrix, which is essentially a grid of numbers. In this case, we have the matrix:

A = 
$
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 6 & 6 & 1 \\
1 & 1 & 5 & 5
\end{bmatrix}
$

Each column of this matrix represents a vertex (a corner point) of a rectangle on a coordinate plane. Think of it like this: the first column [1, 1] represents the point (1, 1), the second column [6, 1] represents the point (6, 1), and so on. These four points define our rectangle. The question asks us what happens to these vertices when we scale the rectangle. Scaling in this context means multiplying the entire matrix by a constant factor, which in our case is 4. So, we need to find 4A and determine the new vertices of the scaled rectangle.

Representing Geometric Shapes with Matrices

Before we jump into the calculations, let's take a moment to appreciate the elegance of using matrices to represent geometric shapes. Each column in a matrix can be interpreted as a coordinate pair (x, y) that defines a point in the plane. When we connect these points in the order they appear in the matrix, we can form various shapes like lines, triangles, squares, and, in our case, a rectangle. This representation allows us to perform transformations on the shape, such as scaling, rotation, and translation, by performing mathematical operations on the matrix itself. This is a fundamental concept in computer graphics and linear algebra, making it a powerful tool for various applications.

The Concept of Scaling

Scaling a geometric shape involves changing its size while maintaining its overall form. In the context of matrices, scaling is achieved by multiplying the matrix by a scalar (a constant number). This scalar acts as a scaling factor, uniformly stretching or shrinking the shape along both the x and y axes. If the scalar is greater than 1, the shape expands; if it's between 0 and 1, the shape shrinks; and if it's negative, the shape also reflects across the origin. Understanding scaling is crucial in many fields, including image processing, computer-aided design (CAD), and even video game development, where objects need to be resized dynamically.

Why Scaling Matters

Scaling is a fundamental transformation in geometry and has numerous practical applications. For example, in computer graphics, scaling is used to zoom in or out of an image or a scene. In architecture and engineering, scaling is used to create blueprints and models that are proportional to the actual structures. In data analysis, scaling is often used to normalize data, making it easier to compare and analyze different datasets. By understanding how scaling works with matrices, we gain a powerful tool for manipulating geometric shapes and data in a consistent and predictable way. It's a cornerstone of many technologies we use every day, from the displays on our smartphones to the simulations used in scientific research.

Calculating the Scaled Matrix

Okay, let's get our hands dirty with some calculations! To find the scaled matrix 4A, we simply multiply each element of matrix A by the scalar 4. This is a straightforward process, but it's crucial to get it right to determine the new vertices accurately.

Remember our original matrix:

A = 
$
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 6 & 6 & 1 \\
1 & 1 & 5 & 5
\end{bmatrix}
$

Now, let's multiply each element by 4:

4A = 4 * 
$
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 6 & 6 & 1 \\
1 & 1 & 5 & 5
\end{bmatrix}
$ = 
$
\begin{bmatrix}
4*1 & 4*6 & 4*6 & 4*1 \\
4*1 & 4*1 & 4*5 & 4*5
\end{bmatrix}
$ = 
$
\begin{bmatrix}
4 & 24 & 24 & 4 \\
4 & 4 & 20 & 20
\end{bmatrix}
$

So, our scaled matrix 4A is:

$
\begin{bmatrix}
4 & 24 & 24 & 4 \\
4 & 4 & 20 & 20
\end{bmatrix}
$

Step-by-Step Multiplication

Let's break down the multiplication process step-by-step to ensure clarity. We're essentially applying the distributive property of multiplication over addition to each element of the matrix. This means that the scalar 4 is multiplied by each individual number within the matrix. For instance, the top-left element, which was 1 in the original matrix, becomes 4 * 1 = 4 in the scaled matrix. Similarly, the element in the first row and second column, which was 6, becomes 4 * 6 = 24. We repeat this process for every element in the matrix. This method ensures that the scaling is applied uniformly across the entire shape, maintaining its proportions.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

While the scalar multiplication itself is relatively simple, there are a few common mistakes that students sometimes make. One is forgetting to multiply every element of the matrix by the scalar. It's crucial to ensure that no element is left untouched. Another mistake is performing the multiplication incorrectly, especially when dealing with larger matrices or more complex numbers. Double-checking your calculations is always a good idea. Additionally, it's important to remember that scalar multiplication is different from matrix multiplication, which involves a more intricate row-by-column operation. Keeping these distinctions in mind will help you avoid errors and master matrix operations more effectively.

Verification and Sanity Checks

After performing any matrix operation, it's wise to do a quick sanity check to ensure that the result makes sense in the context of the problem. In this case, we expect the scaled rectangle to be larger than the original rectangle since we multiplied by a scalar greater than 1. By looking at the elements of the scaled matrix, we can see that the coordinates are indeed larger than the original coordinates, which gives us confidence that our calculation is correct. This kind of verification step is invaluable in catching errors and solidifying your understanding of the underlying concepts. It also helps you develop a better intuition for how matrix operations affect geometric shapes.

Identifying the New Vertices

Now that we have the scaled matrix 4A, we can easily identify the new vertices of the rectangle. Remember, each column of the matrix represents a vertex on the coordinate plane. So, the columns of 4A will give us the coordinates of the scaled rectangle's vertices.

The scaled matrix is:

4A = 
$
\begin{bmatrix}
4 & 24 & 24 & 4 \\
4 & 4 & 20 & 20
\end{bmatrix}
$

Therefore, the new vertices are:

  • (4, 4)
  • (24, 4)
  • (24, 20)
  • (4, 20)

Mapping Matrix Columns to Coordinates

The key to understanding this step is recognizing the direct correspondence between the columns of the matrix and the coordinates of the vertices. Each column provides the x and y coordinates of a single point. The first element in the column represents the x-coordinate, and the second element represents the y-coordinate. This simple mapping allows us to translate matrix representation into geometric interpretation. It's a powerful connection that allows us to use the tools of linear algebra to solve geometric problems, and vice versa. By visualizing this correspondence, we can better grasp the relationship between algebraic operations on matrices and geometric transformations in the plane.

Visualizing the Scaled Rectangle

To further solidify our understanding, it's helpful to visualize the original and scaled rectangles on a coordinate plane. The original rectangle had vertices at (1, 1), (6, 1), (6, 5), and (1, 5). The scaled rectangle has vertices at (4, 4), (24, 4), (24, 20), and (4, 20). If you were to plot these points, you would see that the scaled rectangle is a larger version of the original rectangle, with each side being four times longer. This visual representation reinforces the concept of scaling and helps us appreciate how scalar multiplication affects the size and position of geometric shapes. It's a great way to connect the abstract world of matrices and numbers to the concrete world of geometry.

Practical Implications of Vertex Identification

Identifying vertices from a matrix has significant practical implications in various fields. In computer graphics, for instance, objects are often represented as a collection of vertices connected by lines or surfaces. Matrices are used to store and manipulate these vertices, allowing for transformations like scaling, rotation, and translation. In geographic information systems (GIS), vertices can represent locations on a map, and matrix operations can be used to analyze spatial relationships. In robotics, vertices can define the shape of a robot's workspace, and matrix transformations can be used to plan robot movements. By mastering the skill of extracting vertex information from matrices, you're equipping yourself with a valuable tool that can be applied in a wide range of real-world scenarios.

Conclusion

So, there you have it! By scaling the matrix A by a factor of 4, we found the new vertices of the rectangle to be (4, 4), (24, 4), (24, 20), and (4, 20). This problem beautifully illustrates how matrices can be used to represent and manipulate geometric shapes. We've seen how scalar multiplication scales a shape, changing its size while preserving its fundamental form.

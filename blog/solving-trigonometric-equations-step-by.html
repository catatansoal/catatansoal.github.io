Solving Trigonometric Equations Step-by-Step Example

Hey guys! Trigonometric equations can seem daunting, but don't worry, we're going to break it down and make it super easy to understand. In this article, we'll tackle a common scenario: solving a trigonometric equation over a specific interval. We'll walk through an example step-by-step, ensuring you grasp the concepts and can confidently solve similar problems. Let's dive in!

Understanding the Problem

First, let's understand what we're dealing with. We're given an equation where, after some initial steps, we've arrived at the solutions for 2x within a certain range. Specifically, we have:

2x = \frac{2\pi}{3}, 2\pi, \frac{8\pi}{3}

Our mission, should we choose to accept it, is to find the corresponding values of x. We're looking for solutions within the interval [0, 2π). This means we want all the angles x that fall between 0 (inclusive) and 2π (exclusive). Essentially, we're looking for solutions within one full revolution around the unit circle.

The key here is that we've solved for 2x, not x. This means we've effectively compressed our interval. To find the values of x, we need to account for this compression. Think of it like squeezing a spring – when you release it, it stretches back out. We'll do something similar mathematically.

Step-by-Step Solution

1. Isolate x

The most direct way to find x is to divide all the given solutions for 2x by 2. This will "stretch" our interval back to the original scale. So, let's do that:

x = \frac{2\pi}{3} \div 2, 2\pi \div 2, \frac{8\pi}{3} \div 2

Simplifying each term, we get:

x = \frac{\pi}{3}, \pi, \frac{4\pi}{3}

2. Check the Interval

Now, we have our candidate solutions for x: π/3, π, and 4π/3. We need to make sure these solutions fall within our specified interval of [0, 2π). Let's check each one:

  • π/3 is approximately 1.047 radians, which is clearly within the interval [0, 2π).
  • π is approximately 3.142 radians, which also falls within the interval [0, 2π).
  • 4π/3 is approximately 4.189 radians, which is also within the interval [0, 2π).

So far, so good! All our solutions appear to be valid.

3. Account for the Compression

Here's the tricky part. Because we were initially solving for 2x, we need to consider that we might have missed some solutions. When we divide by 2, we effectively halve the angles, which means angles that were outside the original [0, 2π) interval for 2x might now fall inside the interval for x.

To account for this, we need to consider solutions for 2x that are within the interval [2π, 4π). Why this interval? Because when we divide these solutions by 2, they'll fall within our target interval of [π, 2π), which could give us additional solutions for x.

Let's find these extra solutions. We'll add 2π to our initial solutions for 2x:

2x = \frac{2\pi}{3} + 2\pi, 2\pi + 2\pi, \frac{8\pi}{3} + 2\pi

Simplifying, we get:

2x = \frac{8\pi}{3}, 4\pi, \frac{14\pi}{3}

Now, we divide these new solutions by 2 to find the corresponding x values:

x = \frac{4\pi}{3}, 2\pi, \frac{7\pi}{3}

4. Recheck the Interval (Again!)

We've got a new batch of potential solutions. Let's check if they fall within our interval [0, 2π):

  • 4π/3 (approximately 4.189 radians) – We already had this one, and it's valid.
  • 2π (approximately 6.283 radians) – This is not in our interval because the interval is exclusive at 2π (i.e., it does not include 2π).
  • 7π/3 (approximately 7.330 radians) – This is also not in our interval. It's greater than 2π.

So, we only get one new valid solution from this process: 4π/3, which we already had.

Final Answer

Therefore, the corresponding values of x within the interval [0, 2π) are:

x = \frac{\pi}{3}, \pi, \frac{4\pi}{3}

These are our final answers! We've successfully navigated the trigonometric terrain and emerged victorious.

Key Takeaways

  • Isolate the variable: Always get x (or whatever variable you're solving for) by itself.
  • Consider the interval: Make sure your solutions fall within the given range.
  • Account for compression/expansion: When solving for multiples of x (like 2x or 3x), remember to check for additional solutions that might arise due to the change in the interval.
  • Be systematic: Work through the problem step-by-step to avoid errors.

Practice Makes Perfect

The best way to master trigonometric equations is to practice. Try solving similar problems with different intervals and different multiples of x. The more you practice, the more comfortable you'll become with these types of problems.

Solving Trigonometric Equations A Detailed Example

In the realm of trigonometry, solving trigonometric equations is a fundamental skill. These equations often involve finding angles that satisfy certain relationships involving trigonometric functions like sine, cosine, and tangent. When tasked with solving such equations over a specific interval, such as [0, 2π), it's essential to follow a systematic approach to ensure accurate and complete solutions. Let's break down the process with a comprehensive example, focusing on the critical steps and concepts involved.

Problem Setup

Imagine you're faced with a trigonometric equation where, after some initial manipulation, the solutions for 2x are determined to be 2π/3, 2π, and 8π/3. The objective is to find the corresponding values of x that fall within the interval [0, 2π). This interval represents one full revolution around the unit circle, starting from 0 and going up to (but not including) 2π radians. Understanding this interval is crucial because it defines the range of solutions we're seeking.

The challenge here is that we've solved for 2x, which means the interval has been effectively compressed. To find the solutions for x, we need to reverse this compression while ensuring that all possible solutions within the original interval are accounted for. This involves dividing the solutions for 2x by 2 and considering additional solutions that might arise due to the compression effect.

Step 1 Isolating x

The first and most straightforward step is to isolate x by dividing all the given solutions for 2x by 2. This effectively "stretches" the interval back to its original scale, allowing us to find the corresponding values of x. The calculations are as follows:

x = \frac{2\pi}{3} \div 2, 2\pi \div 2, \frac{8\pi}{3} \div 2

Simplifying each term, we obtain:

x = \frac{\pi}{3}, \pi, \frac{4\pi}{3}

These are our initial candidate solutions for x. However, we're not done yet. We need to verify that these solutions fall within the specified interval and consider the potential for additional solutions due to the initial compression.

Step 2 Checking the Interval

Now, let's ensure that our candidate solutions lie within the interval [0, 2π). This is a crucial step because any solution outside this interval is not valid for our problem.

  • π/3 (approximately 1.047 radians) is clearly within the interval [0, 2π). It's a positive angle less than 2π.
  • π (approximately 3.142 radians) also falls within the interval [0, 2π). It represents half a revolution around the unit circle.
  • 4π/3 (approximately 4.189 radians) is within the interval [0, 2π) as well. It's an angle in the third quadrant.

So far, all our initial solutions appear to be valid. However, we must remember the initial compression caused by solving for 2x. This compression might have hidden some solutions that we need to uncover.

Step 3 Accounting for Compression

This is where the problem becomes more nuanced. Because we initially solved for 2x, we need to consider that we might have missed some solutions. The compression effect means that angles outside the original [0, 2π) interval for 2x could potentially fall inside the interval for x when divided by 2. This is analogous to finding all the possible "pre-images" of our solutions under the transformation 2x.

To account for this, we need to consider solutions for 2x that are within the interval [2π, 4π). Why this interval? Because when we divide these solutions by 2, they will fall within our target interval of [π, 2π), which could yield additional solutions for x. Essentially, we're looking for angles that, when doubled, fall within the second revolution around the unit circle.

To find these extra solutions, we add 2π to our initial solutions for 2x:

2x = \frac{2\pi}{3} + 2\pi, 2\pi + 2\pi, \frac{8\pi}{3} + 2\pi

Simplifying, we get:

2x = \frac{8\pi}{3}, 4\pi, \frac{14\pi}{3}

Now, we divide these new solutions by 2 to find the corresponding x values:

x = \frac{4\pi}{3}, 2\pi, \frac{7\pi}{3}

Step 4 Rechecking the Interval (Again!)

We've generated a new set of potential solutions, and we need to check if they fall within our interval [0, 2π). This step is crucial for eliminating extraneous solutions and ensuring we only include valid angles.

  • 4π/3 (approximately 4.189 radians) – We already had this solution, and it's still valid as it falls within [0, 2π). This reinforces its correctness.
  • 2π (approximately 6.283 radians) – This value is not in our interval because the interval [0, 2π) is exclusive at 2π. It's a boundary point but not included in the solution set.
  • 7π/3 (approximately 7.330 radians) – This value is also not in our interval. It's greater than 2π, indicating it's more than one full revolution around the unit circle.

From this additional check, we only retain one new valid solution: 4π/3, which we already had. This reinforces the importance of systematically checking the interval at each stage of the solution process.

Step 5 Final Answer

After carefully considering all possible solutions and verifying them against the given interval, we arrive at the final answer. The corresponding values of x within the interval [0, 2π) are:

x = \frac{\pi}{3}, \pi, \frac{4\pi}{3}

These are the complete set of solutions for the given trigonometric equation within the specified interval. We've successfully navigated the complexities of solving for 2x and accounted for the potential compression effects.

Key Concepts Revisited

Let's recap the key concepts that were instrumental in solving this problem. These concepts are fundamental to solving trigonometric equations in general:

  1. Isolating the variable: Always aim to isolate the variable (x in this case) to find its possible values. This often involves algebraic manipulation and the application of inverse trigonometric functions.
  2. Considering the interval: The given interval defines the range of solutions you're seeking. It's crucial to check that your solutions fall within this range and to exclude any extraneous solutions.
  3. Accounting for compression/expansion: When solving for multiples of x (like 2x, 3x, etc.), remember to consider the compression or expansion of the interval. This often requires checking for additional solutions by adding multiples of 2π to the initial solutions.
  4. Systematic approach: A step-by-step approach is essential to avoid errors and ensure you've considered all possibilities. This includes isolating the variable, checking the interval, accounting for compression/expansion, and rechecking the interval as needed.

The Importance of Practice

Common Mistakes to Avoid

When solving trigonometric equations, it's easy to stumble upon common pitfalls that can lead to incorrect answers. By being aware of these mistakes, you can avoid them and ensure you arrive at the correct solutions. Let's explore some frequent errors and how to sidestep them.

1 Forgetting the Interval

One of the most prevalent mistakes is neglecting to consider the specified interval. Trigonometric functions are periodic, meaning they repeat their values over regular intervals. This implies that there are infinitely many solutions to a trigonometric equation. However, when you're given an interval, you're only interested in the solutions that fall within that specific range. For example, if the interval is [0, 2π), you should only include solutions between 0 and 2π (excluding 2π itself).

How to Avoid It: Always keep the interval in mind throughout the solution process. After finding potential solutions, check each one to ensure it lies within the given interval. Discard any solutions that fall outside the range.

2 Ignoring the Periodicity

As mentioned earlier, trigonometric functions are periodic. This means that if you find one solution to an equation, there are infinitely many other solutions that can be obtained by adding or subtracting multiples of the function's period. For sine and cosine, the period is 2π, while for tangent, it's π. Ignoring this periodicity can lead to missing solutions within the given interval.

How to Avoid It: When solving for x, consider the periodicity of the trigonometric function involved. If you find a solution x, remember that x + 2πn (for sine and cosine) or x + πn (for tangent) are also solutions, where n is an integer. Check if any of these additional solutions fall within the interval.

3 Incorrectly Applying Inverse Functions

Inverse trigonometric functions (like arcsin, arccos, and arctan) are used to find angles corresponding to specific trigonometric values. However, these functions have restricted ranges, which can lead to missing solutions if not handled carefully. For instance, arcsin has a range of [-π/2, π/2], arccos has a range of [0, π], and arctan has a range of (-π/2, π/2). This means that the inverse functions only return angles within these specific ranges, even though there might be other angles outside these ranges that satisfy the equation.

How to Avoid It: When using inverse trigonometric functions, remember their restricted ranges. After finding an initial solution, consider the symmetry and periodicity of the trigonometric functions to find any additional solutions within the given interval. For example, if x is a solution to sin(x) = a, then π - x is also a solution.

4 Dividing by a Trigonometric Function

Dividing both sides of an equation by a trigonometric function can be tempting, but it's a dangerous move because it can lead to losing solutions. Trigonometric functions can be equal to zero for certain angles, and dividing by zero is undefined. If you divide by a trigonometric function that could be zero, you're effectively eliminating those angles as potential solutions.

How to Avoid It: Instead of dividing by a trigonometric function, rearrange the equation so that all terms are on one side, and then factorize. This way, you can find all the solutions, including those where the trigonometric function is equal to zero.

5 Squaring Both Sides Incorrectly

Squaring both sides of an equation is a common algebraic technique, but it can introduce extraneous solutions, which are solutions that satisfy the squared equation but not the original equation. This is because squaring can eliminate the sign information (positive or negative) of the terms involved.

How to Avoid It: If you square both sides of an equation, always check your solutions in the original equation to eliminate any extraneous solutions. Substitute each potential solution back into the original equation and verify that it satisfies the equation.

6 Making Sign Errors

Trigonometric functions have specific signs in different quadrants of the unit circle. Sine is positive in the first and second quadrants, cosine is positive in the first and fourth quadrants, and tangent is positive in the first and third quadrants. Making sign errors when determining solutions can lead to incorrect answers.

How to Avoid It: Use the unit circle or the CAST rule (or ASTC rule) to determine the signs of trigonometric functions in different quadrants. Be careful when finding angles using inverse trigonometric functions and ensure that the angles are in the correct quadrants.

7 Incorrectly Simplifying Expressions

Trigonometric expressions can be complex, and simplifying them incorrectly can lead to errors. This includes mistakes in algebraic manipulations, such as expanding brackets, combining like terms, or applying trigonometric identities.

How to Avoid It: Double-check your algebraic manipulations and simplifications. Use trigonometric identities correctly and remember the order of operations. If possible, use a calculator or a computer algebra system to verify your simplifications.

By being mindful of these common mistakes and taking steps to avoid them, you can significantly improve your accuracy and confidence in solving trigonometric equations. Remember to always check your solutions and use a systematic approach to ensure you haven't missed any potential solutions.

Conclusion

In conclusion, solving trigonometric equations requires a blend of algebraic manipulation, trigonometric knowledge, and careful consideration of intervals and periodicity. By following a systematic approach, accounting for potential compression or expansion of intervals, and avoiding common mistakes, you can confidently navigate the world of trigonometric equations and arrive at accurate solutions. Remember, practice is key to mastering these concepts, so keep exploring and solving different types of problems to enhance your skills.

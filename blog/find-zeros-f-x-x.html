Understanding Zeros of Rational Functions

Before we jump into solving the problem, it's crucial to understand what we mean by the zeros of a rational function. Simply put, the zeros are the x-values that make the function equal to zero. In other words, they are the solutions to the equation f(x) = 0. Graphically, these zeros represent the points where the function's graph intersects the x-axis. Finding these zeros is a fundamental concept in algebra and calculus, as it helps us understand the behavior and properties of the function. For rational functions, which are fractions where the numerator and denominator are polynomials, finding zeros involves a slightly different approach than dealing with simple polynomials.

For a rational function to be zero, the numerator must be equal to zero, while the denominator must not be equal to zero. This makes sense because if the numerator is zero, the entire fraction becomes zero (0 divided by anything is 0). However, if the denominator is zero, the function is undefined (division by zero is a big no-no in mathematics). So, to find the zeros, we primarily focus on the numerator. We set the numerator equal to zero and solve for x. However, we also need to make sure that the solutions we find don't make the denominator zero, because those values would be vertical asymptotes, not zeros. This crucial step helps us distinguish between the true zeros of the function and the points where the function is undefined.

Moreover, the multiplicity of a zero plays a significant role in how the graph of the function behaves at that point. The multiplicity of a zero is the number of times the corresponding factor appears in the factored form of the numerator. For instance, in our function f(x)f(x)f(x), the factor (x−1)(x-1)(x−1) appears twice, so the zero x=1x=1x=1 has a multiplicity of 2. If a zero has an odd multiplicity, the graph will cross the x-axis at that point. If a zero has an even multiplicity, the graph will touch the x-axis and bounce back, without crossing it. This behavior gives us valuable insights into sketching the graph of the rational function and understanding its overall characteristics. In summary, identifying the zeros of a rational function is a multi-faceted process that requires understanding the relationship between the numerator, denominator, and the multiplicity of each zero.

Setting the Numerator to Zero

The heart of finding the zeros of our rational function lies in the numerator. Remember, our function is:

f(x)=x(x−1)2(x+3)3f(x) = \frac{x(x-1)^2}{(x+3)^3}f(x)=(x+3)3x(x−1)2​

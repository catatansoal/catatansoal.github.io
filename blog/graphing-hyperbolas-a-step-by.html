Graphing Hyperbolas A Step-by-Step Guide

Understanding the Hyperbola Equation

First things first, let's understand what the standard form of a hyperbola equation tells us. The general form for a hyperbola centered at (h,k)(h, k)(h,k) is:

(x−h)2a2−(y−k)2b2=1\frac{(x-h)^2}{a^2} - \frac{(y-k)^2}{b^2} = 1a2(x−h)2​−b2(y−k)2​=1 (for a horizontal hyperbola)

(y−k)2a2−(x−h)2b2=1\frac{(y-k)^2}{a^2} - \frac{(x-h)^2}{b^2} = 1a2(y−k)2​−b2(x−h)2​=1 (for a vertical hyperbola)

In our case, the equation is (x−23)2−(y+24)2=1\left(\frac{x-2}{3}\right)^2-\left(\frac{y+2}{4}\right)^2=1(3x−2​)2−(4y+2​)2=1. Comparing this to the standard form, we can immediately identify some key parameters.

  • Center: The center of the hyperbola is given by the (h,k)(h, k)(h,k) values. In our equation, we have (x−2)(x - 2)(x−2) and (y+2)(y + 2)(y+2), which means h=2h = 2h=2 and k=−2k = -2k=−2. So, the center of our hyperbola is at the point (2,−2)(2, -2)(2,−2). This is our starting point for graphing. Think of the center as the heart of the hyperbola, the point around which everything else is built. It's where the asymptotes intersect and serves as a crucial reference point for sketching the curve.
  • a and b Values: The values of aaa and bbb determine the shape and size of the hyperbola. In our equation, we have a2=32=9a^2 = 3^2 = 9a2=32=9 and b2=42=16b^2 = 4^2 = 16b2=42=16. Therefore, a=3a = 3a=3 and b=4b = 4b=4. These values tell us how far to move from the center along the hyperbola's axes to find important points called vertices and co-vertices. The aaa value is associated with the variable that comes first in the equation (in this case, xxx), and it represents the distance from the center to the vertices along the transverse axis. The bbb value is associated with the other variable (in this case, yyy), and it represents the distance from the center to the co-vertices along the conjugate axis. Understanding aaa and bbb is crucial for accurately plotting the hyperbola's branches and asymptotes.
  • Orientation: Because the x2x^2x2 term comes first and is positive, this hyperbola opens horizontally. This means the hyperbola's branches will extend to the left and right of the center. If the y2y^2y2 term were first and positive, the hyperbola would open vertically, with branches extending upward and downward. Knowing the orientation helps us visualize the hyperbola's shape and direction before we even start plotting points. It's like having a mental roadmap that guides our graphing process and prevents us from drawing the hyperbola in the wrong orientation.

Finding the Vertices

The vertices are the points where the hyperbola intersects its transverse axis (the axis that passes through the center and the foci). For a horizontal hyperbola, the vertices are located at (h±a,k)(h ± a, k)(h±a,k).

In our case, with a center at (2,−2)(2, -2)(2,−2) and a=3a = 3a=3, the vertices are:

  • (2−3,−2)=(−1,−2)(2 - 3, -2) = (-1, -2)(2−3,−2)=(−1,−2)

These two points are crucial because they define the endpoints of the hyperbola's main branches. They tell us exactly where the hyperbola starts to curve away from the center. Imagine the vertices as the anchor points of the hyperbola, firmly planted on the transverse axis and guiding the shape of the curve. Connecting the vertices with a smooth, U-shaped curve will give us the basic form of the hyperbola's branches. The distance between the vertices is called the length of the transverse axis, which is equal to 2a2a2a. This length provides a further measure of the hyperbola's overall size and shape.

Finding the Co-vertices

The co-vertices are the points that define the width of the hyperbola. They are located on the conjugate axis (the axis perpendicular to the transverse axis) at (h,k±b)(h, k ± b)(h,k±b).

With our center at (2,−2)(2, -2)(2,−2) and b=4b = 4b=4, the co-vertices are:

  • (2,−2+4)=(2,2)(2, -2 + 4) = (2, 2)(2,−2+4)=(2,2)
  • (2,−2−4)=(2,−6)(2, -2 - 4) = (2, -6)(2,−2−4)=(2,−6)

Unlike the vertices, the co-vertices are not points on the hyperbola itself. Instead, they help us construct a rectangle that guides the placement of the asymptotes. Think of the co-vertices as the guardians of the hyperbola's width, preventing it from becoming too narrow or too wide. The distance between the co-vertices is called the length of the conjugate axis, which is equal to 2b2b2b. This length provides another important dimension to the hyperbola's overall shape and helps determine the slope of the asymptotes.

Drawing the Asymptotes

Asymptotes are lines that the hyperbola approaches as it extends towards infinity. They are essential for accurately sketching the hyperbola's curves. To find the asymptotes, we first create a rectangle using the vertices and co-vertices as guides. The corners of this rectangle are at:

The asymptotes are the lines that pass through the center of the hyperbola and the corners of this rectangle. They act like guide rails, showing the paths that the hyperbola's branches will follow as they move away from the center. Imagine the asymptotes as the hyperbola's invisible boundaries, preventing it from straying too far in any direction. The slopes of the asymptotes are determined by the ratio of bbb to aaa. For a horizontal hyperbola, the slopes are ±ba\pm \frac{b}{a}±ab​, and for a vertical hyperbola, the slopes are ±ab\pm \frac{a}{b}±ba​. Sketching the Hyperbola

Now, we have all the information we need to sketch the hyperbola:

  1. Plot the center at (2,−2)(2, -2)(2,−2).
  2. Plot the vertices at (5,−2)(5, -2)(5,−2) and (−1,−2)(-1, -2)(−1,−2).
  3. Plot the co-vertices at (2,2)(2, 2)(2,2) and (2,−6)(2, -6)(2,−6).
  4. Draw the rectangle using the vertices and co-vertices.
  5. Draw the asymptotes through the corners of the rectangle and the center.
  6. Sketch the hyperbola branches, starting at the vertices and approaching the asymptotes.

The hyperbola will open horizontally, with each branch curving away from the center and getting closer and closer to the asymptotes without ever actually touching them. The vertices are the closest points of the hyperbola to the center, and the asymptotes act as guidelines for the curves as they extend outward. Remember to draw smooth, U-shaped curves that gradually approach the asymptotes, avoiding any sharp corners or abrupt changes in direction. The final sketch should be a pair of symmetrical branches that reflect across both the transverse and conjugate axes.

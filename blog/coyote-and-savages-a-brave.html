Coyote & Savages: A Brave New World Relocation

Hey guys, let's dive into a seriously obscure, yet fascinating, science fiction story that's been rattling around in my memory for over two decades! I'm on a quest to unearth a tale where a character resembling Wile E. Coyote finds himself unexpectedly tasked with assisting in the relocation of a community of "Savages" (in the sense of Aldous Huxley's Brave New World). This one's a bit hazy, so bear with me as we piece it together.

The Premise: A Coyote's Conundrum

So, picture this: a story, possibly a novella, that throws together the iconic, perpetually-failing-yet-persistent Wile E. Coyote archetype with the dystopian societal structure of Brave New World. The core concept, as I recall, centers around a community of "Savages". These aren't your typical, bloodthirsty brutes; rather, they represent a group of individuals who, unlike the conditioned citizens of the Brave New World, still possess emotions, individuality, and a connection to the natural world. They are seen as "undesirables" or "anachronisms" by the controlling society.

Now, enter our Coyote-esque protagonist. He might not be the actual cartoon character, but he shares the same physical characteristics: the lean, lanky build, the long snout, perhaps even the comically oversized eyes. More importantly, he shares the same spirit of tenacity, the refusal to give up, even in the face of overwhelming odds. This character, for reasons I can't quite remember (the fog of time, you know?), finds himself involved in the plight of the "Savages". This could be due to a sense of empathy, a personal connection, or even a contractual obligation imposed upon him by the ruling powers. Whatever the reason, he's stuck with a mission: to help relocate this community to a new, hopefully more hospitable environment.

What makes this premise so intriguing is the juxtaposition of the cartoonish, almost slapstick nature of the Coyote character with the serious, thought-provoking themes of Brave New World. The story likely explores themes of social control, the value of individuality, and the conflict between nature and technology. It's a recipe for a truly unique and memorable science fiction experience, and I am eager to rediscover it. I am also curious to explore what kind of tech and strategy this Coyote-like character would have.

The Coyote's Role: More Than Meets the Eye

Let's unpack the Coyote's specific role. He's not just a bystander; he's actively involved in the relocation process. Given his archetype, it's reasonable to assume that his methods are, shall we say, unconventional. Picture this: elaborate, yet ultimately flawed, plans. Perhaps he relies on gadgets and contraptions that backfire hilariously, leading to unexpected outcomes and, hopefully, some genuine problem-solving. It's important to remember that Wile E. Coyote's failure, despite his grand designs, is also a testament to his resilience and resourcefulness. In this story, that could be a key element.

The challenge for our Coyote character is not simply to move the "Savages" from point A to point B. The real challenge is to do so while outwitting a controlling society, avoiding their surveillance, and protecting the "Savages" from their machinations. This could involve navigating treacherous landscapes, outsmarting technologically advanced opponents, and maybe even employing the very technology that the ruling society uses against them. The Coyote's arsenal, so to speak, might include anything from makeshift vehicles to camouflage techniques. He might even use clever psychological ploys, playing on the arrogance or predictability of his adversaries.

Furthermore, the Coyote's character arc could evolve as the story progresses. He might start out as a reluctant participant, forced into the situation, but eventually grow to understand and even admire the values of the "Savages". This could lead to a deeper understanding of his own place in the world, and a rejection of the societal norms that have dictated his own existence. Potential Obstacles and Conflicts

  • The Society: The ruling elite of the Brave New World-esque society would undoubtedly be the primary antagonists. They would see the relocation as a threat to their control and would employ any means necessary to stop it. This could involve technological surveillance, psychological manipulation, and even outright violence.
  • The Environment: The new environment that the "Savages" are being relocated to could present its own set of challenges. Decoding the Literary DNA: Key Themes and Influences

This story is a tapestry of literary and cultural influences. Obviously, Brave New World by Aldous Huxley is the backbone. The dystopian setting, the social engineering, and the control of individuals would set the stage for the conflict. The "Savages", representing a longing for freedom and authenticity, are the antithesis of the conditioned citizens of Huxley's world.

The Wile E. Coyote character brings in the element of cartoonish humor, slapstick, and the indomitable spirit of perseverance. He is also a master of improvisation, constantly coming up with new plans, even when they fail spectacularly. In this story, the Coyote character wouldn't be just about laughter; his persistence and ability to think outside the box would be critical to the success of the mission.

Moreover, the story probably grapples with the theme of individuality versus conformity. It explores the conflict between those who embrace freedom of thought, emotion, and choice, and those who are molded by society to fit a specific mold. This idea becomes particularly crucial as the Coyote and the "Savages" try to live the life they choose.

Possible Story Arcs and Climax

  • The Escape: The story could focus on a daring escape from the clutches of the controlling society. The Coyote's inventive schemes and the "Savages'" knowledge of the natural world would be essential to their freedom.
  • The Rebellion: The story could depict the "Savages" revolting against the society, with the Coyote helping them navigate the conflict. This could involve acts of sabotage, guerilla warfare, and the spreading of subversive ideas.
  • The Sanctuary: The story could focus on finding a new, safe haven for the "Savages", where they can live in peace and freedom. The Coyote's task would be to help them find a location and defend it against those who seek to control them.

The climax could involve a final confrontation with the society, testing the Coyote's ingenuity, the "Savages'" resilience, and the value of their way of life. It is likely that the story would conclude with a message of hope and a celebration of the human spirit, along with the idea that the true "savages" are those who would deny freedom.

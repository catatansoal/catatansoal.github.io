Graphing The Inequality Y < (2/3)x + 1 True Statements Explained

Jul 23, 2025 by ADMIN 65 views

Hey guys! Let's dive into the world of inequalities and graphs, specifically focusing on the inequality y < (2/3)x + 1. This might seem a bit daunting at first, but trust me, we'll break it down into bite-sized pieces so everyone can understand. We're going to explore what each part of this inequality tells us about its graph, and by the end, you'll be able to confidently identify the characteristics of this graph and solve related problems. So, buckle up and let's get started!

Understanding Linear Inequalities

Before we jump into the specifics of our inequality, y < (2/3)x + 1, let's take a moment to understand the basics of linear inequalities. Linear inequalities are mathematical statements that compare two expressions using inequality symbols like < (less than), > (greater than), ≤ (less than or equal to), and ≥ (greater than or equal to). Unlike linear equations, which represent a straight line, linear inequalities represent a region on the coordinate plane. This region consists of all the points that satisfy the inequality. When we graph a linear inequality, we're essentially visualizing all the possible solutions to that inequality.

The graph of a linear inequality is defined by a boundary line and a shaded region. The boundary line is the graph of the related linear equation (obtained by replacing the inequality symbol with an equals sign). For instance, in our case, the related linear equation is y = (2/3)x + 1. The boundary line divides the coordinate plane into two regions. One of these regions represents the solutions to the inequality, and we shade this region to indicate that all the points within it satisfy the inequality. The type of line (solid or dashed) and the direction of shading depend on the inequality symbol used. If the inequality is strict (using < or >), the boundary line is dashed to indicate that the points on the line are not included in the solution set. If the inequality includes equality (using ≤ or ≥), the boundary line is solid, meaning the points on the line are part of the solution set. The shaded region is determined by testing a point (usually (0,0) if it's not on the line) in the original inequality. If the point satisfies the inequality, we shade the region containing that point; otherwise, we shade the other region.

Key Components of the Inequality y < (2/3)x + 1

Now, let's dissect our inequality, y < (2/3)x + 1, and identify its key components. This will give us a solid foundation for understanding its graph. The inequality is in slope-intercept form, which is written as y = mx + b, where 'm' represents the slope of the line and 'b' represents the y-intercept. By understanding these components, we can quickly sketch the graph of the line and then determine the shaded region based on the inequality symbol.

In our inequality, y < (2/3)x + 1, the coefficient of x, which is 2/3, represents the slope (m) of the line. The slope tells us how steep the line is and whether it rises or falls as we move from left to right. A positive slope, like 2/3, indicates that the line rises from left to right. Specifically, a slope of 2/3 means that for every 3 units we move to the right along the x-axis, we move 2 units up along the y-axis. The constant term, which is 1, represents the y-intercept (b). The y-intercept is the point where the line crosses the y-axis. In this case, the y-intercept is 1, which means the line crosses the y-axis at the point (0, 1). These two pieces of information, the slope and the y-intercept, are crucial for accurately graphing the line. Once we have the line, we can then consider the inequality symbol (<) to determine whether the line should be dashed or solid and which region should be shaded.

Analyzing the Statements

Okay, guys, let's get to the heart of the matter! We need to evaluate the following statements about the graph of y < (2/3)x + 1 and determine which ones are true. This is where our understanding of linear inequalities and their graphs will really shine. We'll go through each statement one by one, carefully considering its validity based on the information we've already discussed.

  1. The slope of the line is 1.

    This statement is false. As we identified earlier, the slope of the line is determined by the coefficient of x in the inequality. In our case, the coefficient of x is 2/3, not 1. A slope of 1 would mean that for every 1 unit we move to the right along the x-axis, we move 1 unit up along the y-axis. However, the line represented by y = (2/3)x + 1 is less steep than a line with a slope of 1. It rises 2 units for every 3 units we move to the right. So, this statement is incorrect, and we can confidently cross it off our list.

  2. The line is solid.

    This statement is also false. The type of line (solid or dashed) depends on the inequality symbol used. When the inequality symbol is strict (either < or >), the boundary line is dashed to indicate that the points on the line are not included in the solution set. This is because the inequality only holds true for points that are strictly less than or strictly greater than the expression on the other side. In our case, the inequality symbol is < (less than), which is a strict inequality. Therefore, the line should be dashed, not solid. A solid line would imply that the points on the line are also solutions to the inequality, which is not the case here. So, this statement is incorrect as well.

  3. The area below the line is shaded.

    This statement is true. To determine which region to shade, we can test a point that is not on the line. The easiest point to test is usually the origin (0, 0), provided it doesn't lie on the line itself. Let's substitute x = 0 and y = 0 into our inequality, y < (2/3)x + 1: 0 < (2/3)(0) + 1. This simplifies to 0 < 1, which is a true statement. Since the point (0, 0) satisfies the inequality, we shade the region that contains the point (0, 0). This region is the area below the line. If the inequality were y > (2/3)x + 1, we would shade the area above the line. So, this statement is correct and a key characteristic of the graph.

  4. A solution to the inequality is (2, 3).

    This statement is false. To check if (2, 3) is a solution, we substitute x = 2 and y = 3 into the inequality y < (2/3)x + 1: 3 < (2/3)(2) + 1. This simplifies to 3 < (4/3) + 1, which further simplifies to 3 < 7/3. Since 7/3 is approximately 2.33, the inequality becomes 3 < 2.33, which is a false statement. Therefore, the point (2, 3) does not satisfy the inequality and is not a solution. If we were to plot the point (2, 3) on the graph, it would fall above the dashed line, in the unshaded region. So, this statement is incorrect.

Conclusion

So, guys, we've thoroughly analyzed the inequality y < (2/3)x + 1 and evaluated the given statements. We found that only one statement is true: the area below the line is shaded. The other statements regarding the slope, the type of line, and the solution point (2, 3) are false. By breaking down the inequality into its components and understanding the concepts of slope, y-intercept, dashed lines, and shaded regions, we were able to confidently determine the characteristics of the graph.

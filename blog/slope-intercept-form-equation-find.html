Slope-Intercept Form Equation Find With M = -9/8 And Point (32,-23)

Hey guys! Let's dive into the fascinating world of linear equations, specifically the slope-intercept form. This form is a super handy way to represent the equation of a line, and once you get the hang of it, you'll be solving these problems like a pro. So, let's break down the challenge: We need to find the equation of a line in slope-intercept form, given its slope and a point it passes through. Sounds like fun, right?

Understanding Slope-Intercept Form: The Key to Linear Equations

First things first, what exactly is the slope-intercept form? It's a way of writing linear equations that makes it incredibly easy to identify the slope and the y-intercept of the line. The general form looks like this:

y = mx + b

Where:

  • y is the dependent variable (usually plotted on the vertical axis).
  • x is the independent variable (usually plotted on the horizontal axis).
  • m is the slope of the line, which tells us how steep the line is and whether it's going uphill or downhill.
  • b is the y-intercept, which is the point where the line crosses the y-axis. It's the value of y when x is 0.

Think of the slope as the line's 'rise over run' ‚Äì how much the y value changes for every unit change in the x value. A positive slope means the line goes upwards from left to right, while a negative slope means it goes downwards. The y-intercept, on the other hand, is our starting point on the y-axis. It's where the line begins its journey across the graph. Knowing these two key pieces of information ‚Äì the slope and the y-intercept ‚Äì allows us to completely define a straight line.

Now, why is this form so useful? Well, imagine you're looking at a graph and you want to quickly understand the line's behavior. With the slope-intercept form, you can instantly see the slope and the y-intercept. This makes it super easy to visualize the line and understand its characteristics. Plus, it's a great starting point for solving various problems related to linear equations, like finding the equation of a line given two points, or determining if two lines are parallel or perpendicular. So, understanding the slope-intercept form is like having a secret weapon in your math arsenal!

The Point-Slope Formula: Our Bridge to Slope-Intercept Form

Okay, so we know what slope-intercept form is, but how do we get there when we're given a slope and a point? That's where the point-slope formula comes in! This formula is like a bridge that helps us connect the information we have (a point and a slope) to the equation of the line.

The point-slope formula looks like this:

Where:

  • m is the slope of the line (same as in slope-intercept form).
  • (x‚ÇÅ, y‚ÇÅ) is a known point on the line.

Notice how this formula cleverly uses the slope and a point to define the line. It's based on the idea that the slope between any two points on a line is constant. So, if we know the slope and one point, we can use this formula to find the relationship between x and y for all points on the line.

Think of it this way: The point-slope formula is like a customizable equation. You plug in the slope and the coordinates of the point you know, and it gives you an equation that represents the line. But, this equation isn't in slope-intercept form yet. It's in a slightly different format that's designed to use the given point and slope directly. To get to slope-intercept form, we'll need to do a little bit of algebraic rearranging. But don't worry, it's not too complicated! We'll use the distributive property and some simple addition or subtraction to isolate y on one side of the equation. Once we do that, we'll have the equation in the familiar y = mx + b form, and we'll be able to easily identify the slope and the y-intercept.

So, the point-slope formula is a crucial stepping stone in our journey. It allows us to take the given information and transform it into an equation that we can then manipulate into the slope-intercept form. It's a powerful tool, and once you master it, you'll be able to tackle a wide range of linear equation problems!

Applying the Formulas: A Step-by-Step Solution

Alright, let's get down to business and solve the problem! We're given a slope, m = -9/8, and a point, (32, -23). Our mission is to find the equation of the line in slope-intercept form.

Here's the breakdown:

Step 1: Plug the values into the point-slope formula.

Remember the point-slope formula? It's y - y‚ÇÅ = m(x - x‚ÇÅ). We'll substitute our given values into this formula. We have m = -9/8, x‚ÇÅ = 32, and y‚ÇÅ = -23. Plugging these in, we get:

y - (-23) = (-9/8)(x - 32)

Notice the double negative on the left side? We'll simplify that in the next step.

Step 2: Simplify the equation.

Let's simplify the equation we got in step 1. First, we'll deal with the double negative: y - (-23) becomes y + 23. So our equation now looks like this:

y + 23 = (-9/8)(x - 32)

Next, we'll use the distributive property to multiply (-9/8) by both terms inside the parentheses: (-9/8) * x is (-9/8)x, and (-9/8) * (-32) is 36. Remember, a negative times a negative is a positive. So our equation becomes:

y + 23 = (-9/8)x + 36

Step 3: Isolate y to get slope-intercept form.

Our final step is to get the equation into the y = mx + b form. To do this, we need to isolate y on one side of the equation. Currently, we have y + 23 on the left side. To get y by itself, we'll subtract 23 from both sides of the equation:

y + 23 - 23 = (-9/8)x + 36 - 23

This simplifies to:

y = (-9/8)x + 13

And there you have it! We've successfully transformed the equation into slope-intercept form.

The Grand Finale: Decoding the Slope-Intercept Equation

So, what does our final equation, y = (-9/8)x + 13, tell us? Well, it gives us a clear picture of the line's behavior.

  • The slope, m, is -9/8. This means that for every 8 units we move to the right on the graph (the 'run'), the line goes down 9 units (the 'rise'). The negative sign indicates that the line is sloping downwards from left to right.
  • The y-intercept, b, is 13. This tells us that the line crosses the y-axis at the point (0, 13). It's our starting point on the vertical axis.

By simply looking at the equation in slope-intercept form, we can immediately grasp the line's direction and its position on the coordinate plane. This is the power of this form ‚Äì it makes linear equations incredibly easy to understand and visualize.

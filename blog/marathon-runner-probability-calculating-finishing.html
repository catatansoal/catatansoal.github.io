Marathon Runner Probability Calculating Finishing Time Under 3 Hours 20 Minutes

Jul 14, 2025 by ADMIN 80 views

Introduction

Hey guys! Today, we're diving into a fun little probability problem involving marathon runners. We're going to explore the world of normal distributions and see how we can calculate the likelihood of a runner finishing a marathon within a certain time. This is a classic example of how statistics can be applied to real-world scenarios, and it's super useful for understanding data in general. So, let's get started and break down this problem step by step!

Problem Statement

In this marathon scenario, we're told that the times of the runners are normally distributed. This is a key piece of information because it allows us to use the properties of the normal distribution to solve the problem. We know that the average finishing time, or the mean, is 3 hours and 50 minutes. We also know that the spread of the data, or the standard deviation, is 30 minutes. The question we're trying to answer is: What is the probability that a randomly selected runner finishes the marathon in 3 hours and 20 minutes or less? This is a common type of question in statistics, and we're going to use the concept of z-scores to find the solution. Understanding this problem involves a few key concepts, so let's break them down. Firstly, we need to understand what a normal distribution is. Think of it as a bell-shaped curve â€“ most of the data clusters around the average, with fewer data points further away from the average. In our case, most runners will finish around the 3 hour 50 minute mark, with fewer runners finishing much faster or much slower. Secondly, we need to understand the mean and standard deviation. The mean is just the average, the central point of our distribution. The standard deviation tells us how spread out the data is. A smaller standard deviation means the data is clustered more tightly around the mean, while a larger standard deviation means the data is more spread out. In our case, a standard deviation of 30 minutes tells us how much the finishing times typically vary from the average. Finally, we need to figure out how to calculate the probability of a runner finishing within a certain time. This is where z-scores come in, but before we jump into that, let's make sure we're all on the same page with the basics. This problem isn't just about numbers; it's about understanding how data behaves in the real world. Normal distributions are everywhere, from test scores to heights, so grasping this concept is super valuable. Now, let's get into the nitty-gritty and start crunching some numbers!

Converting Time to Minutes

Alright, first things first, let's make sure we're working with the same units. Since our standard deviation is given in minutes, it's a smart move to convert all our times into minutes. This will make the calculations much smoother and reduce the risk of making mistakes. So, let's break it down. Our mean time is 3 hours and 50 minutes. To convert this to minutes, we multiply the hours by 60 (since there are 60 minutes in an hour) and then add the remaining minutes. So, 3 hours * 60 minutes/hour = 180 minutes. Then, we add the 50 minutes, giving us a total of 180 + 50 = 230 minutes. Easy peasy, right? Now, let's do the same for the time we're interested in, which is 3 hours and 20 minutes. Again, we multiply the hours by 60: 3 hours * 60 minutes/hour = 180 minutes. Then, we add the 20 minutes, giving us 180 + 20 = 200 minutes. So, we've successfully converted both times into minutes. Our mean time is 230 minutes, and we want to find the probability of a runner finishing in 200 minutes or less. This conversion step is crucial because it ensures that all our values are in the same unit, which is essential for accurate calculations. Imagine trying to add apples and oranges â€“ it just doesn't work! Similarly, mixing hours and minutes in our calculations would lead to incorrect results. By converting everything to minutes, we're setting ourselves up for success. This might seem like a small step, but it's a fundamental part of problem-solving in statistics. Always double-check your units and make sure they're consistent. It can save you a lot of headaches down the road. Now that we've got our times in minutes, we're ready to move on to the next step: calculating the z-score. This is where things get a little more interesting, so buckle up and let's dive in!

Calculating the Z-score

Okay, now for the fun part: calculating the z-score! The z-score is a super handy tool in statistics. It tells us how many standard deviations a particular data point is away from the mean. In our case, it will tell us how many standard deviations 3 hours and 20 minutes (or 200 minutes) is away from the average finishing time of 3 hours and 50 minutes (or 230 minutes). The formula for the z-score is pretty straightforward: z = (X - Î¼) / Ïƒ, where X is the data point we're interested in (200 minutes in our case), Î¼ is the mean (230 minutes), and Ïƒ is the standard deviation (30 minutes). So, let's plug in the numbers: z = (200 - 230) / 30. This simplifies to z = -30 / 30, which gives us a z-score of -1. This z-score of -1 is super meaningful. It tells us that a finishing time of 200 minutes is exactly one standard deviation below the mean of 230 minutes. Remember, a negative z-score means the data point is below the mean, while a positive z-score means it's above the mean. A z-score of 0 would mean the data point is exactly at the mean. Understanding z-scores is crucial because they allow us to compare data points from different normal distributions. Imagine you have two different marathons with different average finishing times and standard deviations. You can't directly compare a finishing time from one marathon to a finishing time from the other. But, if you convert both finishing times to z-scores, you can easily compare them. The z-score essentially standardizes the data, allowing for meaningful comparisons. In our case, a z-score of -1 tells us that a runner finishing in 200 minutes is performing one standard deviation better than the average runner. This is a pretty good performance! But, we're not quite done yet. We need to use this z-score to find the probability of a runner finishing in 200 minutes or less. That's where the z-table comes in, so let's head there next!

Using the Z-table

Alright, we've calculated our z-score, which is -1. Now, the next step is to use a z-table (also known as a standard normal table) to find the probability associated with this z-score. The z-table is a fantastic tool that gives us the cumulative probability of a standard normal distribution up to a certain z-score. In simpler terms, it tells us the probability of a value being less than or equal to a given z-score. You can easily find z-tables online with a quick search. They usually have z-scores listed in the first column and the corresponding probabilities in the body of the table. To use the z-table, we need to find the row corresponding to our z-score. In our case, we're looking for -1.0. The table will typically have z-scores listed to one decimal place in the first column, and the second decimal place in the top row. Since our z-score is exactly -1.0, we just need to find the value in the row for -1.0 and the column for .00 (since there's no second decimal place). When you look up -1.0 in the z-table, you'll find a value of approximately 0.1587. This is the probability that a randomly selected runner will have a finishing time less than or equal to 200 minutes. So, what does this 0.1587 actually mean? It means that there's about a 15.87% chance that a runner will finish the marathon in 3 hours and 20 minutes or less. This is a pretty cool result! It shows us how we can use statistics to make predictions about real-world events. Using the z-table might seem a little daunting at first, but it's actually quite straightforward once you get the hang of it. The key is to understand what the table is telling you â€“ it's giving you the probability of a value falling below a certain point in a normal distribution. This is a fundamental concept in statistics, and it's used in a wide variety of applications. Now that we've found the probability, let's take a step back and think about what we've done. We started with a real-world problem, converted it into a statistical problem, and then used our knowledge of normal distributions and z-scores to find a solution. Conclusion

Alright guys, let's wrap things up and talk about what we've discovered. We started with the question: What is the probability that a randomly selected runner has a marathon time less than or equal to 3 hours and 20 minutes, given that the times are normally distributed with a mean of 3 hours and 50 minutes and a standard deviation of 30 minutes? We tackled this problem step by step, and here's a quick recap of our journey.

First, we converted the times to minutes to ensure we were working with consistent units. This gave us a mean of 230 minutes and a target time of 200 minutes. Then, we calculated the z-score, which told us how many standard deviations our target time was away from the mean. We found a z-score of -1, indicating that 200 minutes is one standard deviation below the average finishing time. Next, we used the z-table to find the probability associated with our z-score. The z-table gave us a probability of approximately 0.1587. So, what does this all mean? Our final answer is that there is approximately a 15.87% probability that a randomly selected runner will finish the marathon in 3 hours and 20 minutes or less. This is a pretty insightful result. It tells us that only a relatively small percentage of runners are expected to finish that quickly. This kind of information could be useful for a variety of purposes. For example, race organizers might use this data to plan the timing of awards ceremonies or to estimate the number of runners who will require medical attention at different points in the race. Runners themselves might use this information to set realistic goals and to compare their performance to the overall distribution of finishing times. But beyond this specific problem, what's the bigger takeaway here? This exercise has shown us how we can use the power of statistics to analyze real-world data and make meaningful predictions.

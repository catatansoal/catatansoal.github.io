Solve Math Problems Using Properties: A Step-by-Step Guide

Hey guys! Today, we're going to break down a math problem that might look a little intimidating at first, but trust me, it's totally manageable once we use the right properties and approach. We'll be tackling this expression: $\frac{-1}{6} \times \frac{4}{7}+\frac{1}{2}-\frac{3}{7} \times \frac{1}{6}$

This problem involves fractions, multiplication, addition, and subtraction. It's a perfect example of how using properties like the commutative and distributive properties can simplify things and make the calculation smoother. So, let's dive in and learn how to solve this step-by-step!

Understanding the Problem

Before we start crunching numbers, it's super important to understand what the problem is asking. We have a series of operations involving fractions. The key here is to remember the order of operations (PEMDAS/BODMAS) and how properties can help us rearrange and group terms to make the calculations easier. Our main keywords here are understanding the problem, order of operations, and properties of fractions. These concepts will guide us through the solution, ensuring we don’t miss any crucial steps. By focusing on these keywords, we lay a strong foundation for solving more complex problems in the future. It’s not just about getting the right answer; it’s about learning the process and applying it to various scenarios. This approach is what truly builds mathematical confidence and problem-solving skills. Remember, math isn’t just about memorizing formulas; it’s about understanding the logic behind them and how they can be used in different situations. So, let's keep these keywords in mind as we move forward, and you'll see how they help us tackle this problem effectively!

Identifying Key Operations and Numbers

The first thing we need to do is identify the key operations and numbers involved. We've got multiplication, addition, and subtraction, all working with fractions. The fractions are -1/6, 4/7, 1/2, 3/7, and 1/6. Recognizing these elements is crucial because it sets the stage for applying the correct order of operations and mathematical properties. Our main focus should be on identifying key operations and numbers to lay the groundwork for solving the problem. This involves pinpointing which mathematical operations (multiplication, addition, subtraction) are present and noting the specific numbers involved, which in this case are fractions. Recalling Order of Operations (PEMDAS/BODMAS)

Now, let's quickly recap the order of operations, which you might know as PEMDAS (Parentheses, Exponents, Multiplication and Division, Addition and Subtraction) or BODMAS (Brackets, Orders, Division and Multiplication, Addition and Subtraction). This is our golden rule for solving expressions! Remembering the order of operations, often known by the acronyms PEMDAS or BODMAS, is crucial. It serves as a golden rule, guiding us on which operations to perform first in a mathematical expression. PEMDAS stands for Parentheses, Exponents, Multiplication and Division (from left to right), and Addition and Subtraction (from left to right). Similarly, BODMAS represents Brackets, Orders (powers and square roots), Division and Multiplication (from left to right), and Addition and Subtraction (from left to right). This order ensures that we solve mathematical problems consistently and accurately. Skipping or changing the order can lead to incorrect answers. Think of it as a recipe; you need to follow the steps in the right sequence to achieve the desired outcome. By adhering to PEMDAS/BODMAS, we can confidently navigate complex expressions and arrive at the correct solution. So, before we dive into the problem, let’s make sure we have this fundamental principle firmly in place.

Applying Properties to Simplify

This is where things get interesting! We can use mathematical properties to make our lives easier. In this case, the commutative and distributive properties are our best friends. Applying properties to simplify the expression is a game-changer, making the problem much easier to handle. We’re particularly going to lean on the commutative and distributive properties, which are like secret weapons in our mathematical arsenal. The commutative property allows us to change the order of numbers in addition or multiplication without affecting the result. For example, a + b = b + a and a × b = b × a. This can be incredibly helpful when rearranging terms to group similar fractions together. The distributive property, on the other hand, helps us simplify expressions involving multiplication and addition/subtraction. It states that a × (b + c) = a × b + a × c. This property is super useful for breaking down complex expressions into simpler parts. By using these properties, we can transform the original problem into a more manageable form, setting us up for a smoother calculation process. So, let’s roll up our sleeves and see how we can strategically apply these properties to unravel this mathematical puzzle.

Using the Commutative Property

The commutative property lets us change the order of numbers when adding or multiplying. So, a + b = b + a and a * b = b * a. Let's rearrange our expression to group the multiplication terms together: $\frac{-1}{6} \times \frac{4}{7} - \frac{3}{7} \times \frac{1}{6} + \frac{1}{2}$ The commutative property, a fundamental concept in mathematics, is like a magic wand that allows us to rearrange numbers without altering the outcome. This property is especially handy when we’re dealing with addition or multiplication, as it states that the order in which we add or multiply numbers doesn’t change the result. Mathematically, this means that a + b = b + a and a × b = b × a. For instance, 2 + 3 is the same as 3 + 2, and 4 × 5 is the same as 5 × 4. In the context of our problem, the commutative property is our secret weapon for grouping similar terms together. By strategically rearranging the expression, we can bring fractions with common denominators or similar operations closer to each other. This makes the subsequent calculations much more manageable and less prone to errors. So, let’s wield this property wisely and set ourselves up for a smoother problem-solving journey.

Applying the Distributive Property

Now comes the distributive property, which is a * real game-changer *. It lets us factor out common terms. Notice that both the first and second terms have 1/6 in them? Let's factor it out: $\frac{1}{6} \times (\frac{-4}{7} - \frac{3}{7}) + \frac{1}{2}$ The distributive property is a powerful tool in mathematics, acting like a mathematical Swiss Army knife. It allows us to simplify expressions by either expanding or factoring terms. At its core, the distributive property states that a × (b + c) = a × b + a × c. This means we can multiply a single term by a group of terms inside parentheses, or conversely, factor out a common term from a group of terms. In the context of our problem, the distributive property shines when we notice a common factor in multiple terms. By factoring out this common factor, we can consolidate the expression and make it easier to handle. It’s like gathering similar ingredients before starting to cook; it streamlines the process and makes the final dish (or solution) much more cohesive. By strategically applying the distributive property, we transform a potentially complex expression into a more manageable and solvable form. So, let’s harness this property and watch how it simplifies our mathematical journey!

Now that we've simplified the expression, it's time to roll up our sleeves and solve it! We'll follow the order of operations and work through each step carefully. Solving the simplified expression is the moment we’ve been working towards. With the expression neatly organized and simplified using the properties, we’re now in a prime position to crunch the numbers and find the solution. It’s like the final lap of a race, where all the preparation and strategy come together. We’ll proceed step by step, meticulously following the order of operations to ensure accuracy. Simplifying Inside the Parentheses

First, let's tackle what's inside the parentheses: $\frac-4}{7} - \frac{3}{7} = \frac{-4-3}{7} = \frac{-7}{7} = -1$ So our expression becomes $\frac{1{6} \times (-1) + \frac{1}{2}$ Simplifying inside the parentheses is like decluttering a workspace before starting a project. It’s about focusing on one part of the problem at a time, making it easier to manage and reducing the risk of errors. Parentheses act like containers, grouping terms that need to be addressed before anything else. In our case, we have the expression (-4/7) - (3/7) inside the parentheses. Since the fractions have a common denominator, we can simply subtract the numerators. This step is crucial because it condenses the expression within the parentheses into a single, manageable value. It’s like taking a deep breath and centering yourself before tackling a challenge. By simplifying within the parentheses, we create a clearer path forward, allowing us to proceed with the rest of the calculation with confidence. So, let’s tidy up those parentheses and pave the way for a smooth solution!

Performing Multiplication

Next up, we've got multiplication: $\frac1}{6} \times (-1) = \frac{-1}{6}$ Now our expression looks like this $ rac{-1{6} + \frac{1}{2}$ Performing the multiplication is like connecting the dots in a puzzle. It’s the step where we take the simplified terms and combine them according to the order of operations. In our expression, we have (1/6) × (-1), which is a straightforward multiplication of a fraction by a whole number. Remembering the rules of fraction multiplication, we simply multiply the numerator by the whole number, keeping the denominator the same. This step transforms the expression further, bringing us closer to the final answer. It’s a crucial bridge between simplification and the ultimate solution. By carefully executing the multiplication, we maintain the integrity of the expression and set ourselves up for the final addition or subtraction. So, let’s confidently multiply and continue our journey towards the solution!

Adding the Fractions

Finally, let's add the fractions. To do this, we need a common denominator. The least common multiple of 6 and 2 is 6. So, we rewrite 1/2 as 3/6: $\frac{-1}{6} + \frac{3}{6} = \frac{-1+3}{6} = \frac{2}{6}$ And we can simplify 2/6 to 1/3. Adding the fractions is like the grand finale of our mathematical performance. It’s where we bring together the simplified terms and combine them to reveal the final answer. However, before we can add fractions, they need to have a common denominator. This is like making sure everyone is speaking the same language before starting a conversation. In our case, we need to find the least common multiple (LCM) of the denominators 6 and 2, which is 6. Once we have the common denominator, we can rewrite the fractions and add their numerators. This step requires attention to detail to ensure we’re adding the correct values and maintaining the integrity of the fractions. It’s a satisfying moment when the fractions align, and we can perform the final addition. By carefully adding the fractions, we arrive at the solution that we’ve been working towards throughout the entire problem-solving process.

So, the final answer is 1/3!

Solving Systems Of Equations With Quadratic And Linear Functions A Comprehensive Guide

Jul 13, 2025 by ADMIN 87 views

Hey guys! Let's dive into a super interesting problem today: finding the solutions to a system of equations where we've got a quadratic function and a linear function hanging out together. It might sound intimidating, but trust me, it's totally manageable. We're going to break it down step by step, so you'll be a pro at this in no time!

Understanding the Functions

First, let's make sure we're all on the same page about what these functions actually are. We're given a quadratic function f(x) = 5x² + x + 3. Now, remember, a quadratic function is one where the highest power of x is 2. These functions create a U-shaped curve when you graph them, which we call a parabola. The coefficients in this equation (5, 1, and 3) determine the exact shape and position of the parabola. The coefficient '5' stretches the parabola vertically and it opens upwards, the '+ x' shifts it slightly, and the '+ 3' moves it up the y-axis. Visualizing this helps a lot in understanding how it interacts with other functions.

Next up, we've got a linear function g(x) defined by a table of values. A linear function, as the name suggests, forms a straight line when graphed. The table gives us a bunch of points (x, g(x)) that lie on this line: (-2, 3), (-1, 5), (0, 7), (1, 9), and (2, 11). What we need to recognize here is the pattern. As x increases by 1, g(x) increases by 2. This consistent rate of change is the slope of the line. We can use any two points from this table to calculate the slope (rise over run). Once we have the slope, and any point, we can easily find the equation of the line in slope-intercept form (y = mx + b), where m is the slope and b is the y-intercept.

The cool thing is, once you understand the basic forms and characteristics of these functions, tackling problems like this becomes way less daunting. We're just dealing with shapes and lines interacting on a graph, and algebra helps us find exactly where they meet.

Finding the Equation of the Linear Function

Alright, let's dive a bit deeper into our linear function g(x). We've got this awesome table of values, and our mission now is to transform that data into the equation of a line. Remember, the general form of a linear equation is g(x) = mx + b, where m is the slope and b is the y-intercept. Finding these two values is the key to unlocking the equation.

To find the slope (m), we can use the formula: m = (y₂ - y₁) / (x₂ - x₁). Let's pick two points from our table. How about (-2, 3) and (-1, 5)? Plugging these into our formula, we get m = (5 - 3) / (-1 - (-2)) = 2 / 1 = 2. So, our slope is 2, which means for every 1 unit we move to the right on the graph, the line goes up 2 units. Makes sense, right?

Now, let's tackle the y-intercept (b). This is the point where the line crosses the y-axis, which occurs when x = 0. Lucky for us, our table already gives us this point! When x = 0, g(x) = 7. So, b = 7. If we didn't have the y-intercept directly in the table, we could pick any point and the slope we just calculated, plug them into the equation g(x) = mx + b, and solve for b. But hey, we got lucky this time!

So, putting it all together, the equation of our linear function is g(x) = 2x + 7. We've successfully turned a table of values into a neat, usable equation. This is a crucial step because now we can algebraically compare this function to our quadratic function and find their points of intersection.

Solving the System of Equations

Okay, the real fun begins now! We've got our quadratic function, f(x) = 5x² + x + 3, and our linear function, g(x) = 2x + 7. To find the solutions to this system of equations, we need to figure out where these two functions intersect. In other words, we're looking for the x values where f(x) = g(x). This is like finding the meeting point of two different paths.

So, let's set those equations equal to each other: 5x² + x + 3 = 2x + 7. Now we've got a quadratic equation to solve. To make things easier, we'll want to rearrange it so it's in the standard quadratic form: ax² + bx + c = 0. Subtracting 2x and 7 from both sides, we get 5x² + x - 2x + 3 - 7 = 0, which simplifies to 5x² - x - 4 = 0. Great, now we're talking!

There are a couple of ways we can solve this. We could try to factor the quadratic, but sometimes that can be tricky. The other option, and a reliable one, is to use the quadratic formula. Remember that gem? It's x = (-b ± √(b² - 4ac)) / (2a). In our equation, a = 5, b = -1, and c = -4. Plugging these values in, we get:

x = (1 ± √((-1)² - 4 * 5 * -4)) / (2 * 5) x = (1 ± √(1 + 80)) / 10 x = (1 ± √81) / 10 x = (1 ± 9) / 10

So, we have two possible solutions for x:

x₁ = (1 + 9) / 10 = 10 / 10 = 1 x₂ = (1 - 9) / 10 = -8 / 10 = -0.8

We've found the x-coordinates where the parabola and the line intersect! But wait, we're not quite done yet. We need to find the corresponding y values as well. That's where we plug these x values back into either f(x) or g(x). The linear function g(x) is typically easier to work with, so let's use that.

Finding the Intersection Points

Alright, we've crunched the numbers and found the x-values where our quadratic and linear functions meet. We've got x = 1 and x = -0.8. But a solution to a system of equations isn't just an x-value; it's a pair of coordinates (x, y) that satisfy both equations. So, our next step is to find the corresponding y-values for each of these x-values.

We'll use our linear function, g(x) = 2x + 7, because it's usually simpler to plug values into a linear equation than a quadratic one. For x = 1, we have g(1) = 2(1) + 7 = 2 + 7 = 9. So, one of our intersection points is (1, 9). Feels good to find a match, doesn't it?

Now, let's tackle x = -0.8. Plugging that into our linear function, we get g(-0.8) = 2(-0.8) + 7 = -1.6 + 7 = 5.4. So, our other intersection point is (-0.8, 5.4). This point might seem a little less “clean” than (1, 9), but hey, that's math for you – not everything comes out as whole numbers!

So, what does this mean? It means that our quadratic function and our linear function intersect at two points on the graph: (1, 9) and (-0.8, 5.4). If you were to graph these functions, you'd see the parabola and the line crossing paths at these exact spots. These are the solutions to our system of equations.

Let's quickly recap what we did. We set the two functions equal to each other, solved for x using the quadratic formula, and then plugged those x values back into the linear function to find the corresponding y values. Voila! We've conquered the system of equations.

Verifying the Solution

Okay, we've done the hard work of solving for our intersection points, but a good mathematician (or student!) always double-checks their work. It's like proofreading a paper before you turn it in – a crucial step that can save you from making silly mistakes. We found two possible solutions: (1, 9) and (-0.8, 5.4). To verify these, we need to make sure they work in both equations: f(x) = 5x² + x + 3 and g(x) = 2x + 7.

Let's start with the point (1, 9). For the quadratic function, f(1) = 5(1)² + 1 + 3 = 5 + 1 + 3 = 9. That matches our y-value, so far so good! For the linear function, g(1) = 2(1) + 7 = 2 + 7 = 9. Bingo! It works in both equations. This gives us a good feeling that (1, 9) is indeed a correct solution.

Now, let's put (-0.8, 5.4) to the test. This one might be a little trickier because we're dealing with decimals, but we've got this! For the quadratic function, f(-0.8) = 5(-0.8)² + (-0.8) + 3 = 5(0.64) - 0.8 + 3 = 3.2 - 0.8 + 3 = 5.4. Awesome, it checks out! For the linear function, g(-0.8) = 2(-0.8) + 7 = -1.6 + 7 = 5.4. Double bingo! It works in both equations as well.

By verifying our solutions, we've gained confidence that we've not only found the right answers but also understood the process thoroughly. It’s a small step that makes a big difference in ensuring accuracy and solidifying your understanding. Plus, it's super satisfying to see everything come together perfectly.

So, what did we learn? Solving systems of equations involving quadratic and linear functions might seem complex at first, but by breaking it down step by step, using the right tools (like the quadratic formula), and verifying our solutions, we can tackle these problems like champs! Keep practicing, and you'll be a pro in no time.

The Correct Answer

Looking back at the original problem, we were given a specific multiple-choice option: A. (-1, 7). After our detailed analysis and calculations, we found our solutions to be (1, 9) and (-0.8, 5.4). So, let's see if option A matches any of our solutions. Nope! (-1, 7) is not one of the points where our quadratic and linear functions intersect.

This highlights an important point: it's crucial to actually solve the problem rather than just guessing or relying on intuition. While (-1, 7) might seem like a plausible answer at first glance, our systematic approach allowed us to find the actual solutions and confidently say that option A is incorrect.

This also underscores the value of verifying our solutions. If we had mistakenly chosen (-1, 7) without checking, we would have missed the correct answer. Verification is like the final seal of approval on our work, ensuring that our solutions are not only mathematically sound but also accurate within the context of the problem.

In this case, the correct answer would likely be a different option that includes one of our calculated intersection points or a statement indicating that none of the provided options are correct. The key takeaway here is the process – understanding how to set up the equations, solve for the variables, and verify the results. With these skills in your toolkit, you'll be well-equipped to tackle any system of equations problem that comes your way!

So, there you have it, guys! We've journeyed through the process of solving a system of equations involving a quadratic function and a linear function. We started by understanding what these functions represent graphically, then we found the equation of the linear function from a table of values. We set the two functions equal to each other, used the quadratic formula to find the x-coordinates of the intersection points, and finally, we calculated the corresponding y-coordinates to identify the actual solutions. And, of course, we emphasized the importance of verifying our solutions to ensure accuracy.

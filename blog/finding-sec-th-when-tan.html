Finding Sec Θ When Tan²θ Equals 3/8 A Trigonometric Exploration

Jul 16, 2025 by ADMIN 64 views

Hey there, math enthusiasts! Today, we're diving into a trigonometric problem that might seem a bit daunting at first, but trust me, we'll break it down together step by step. We're tasked with finding the value of secant (sec θ) given that the square of tangent (tan²θ) is equal to 3/8. Sounds like fun, right? So, let's put on our thinking caps and get started!

Understanding the Trigonometric Identities

Before we jump into solving the problem directly, it's crucial to establish a solid foundation by understanding the fundamental trigonometric identities that will serve as our guiding principles throughout this journey. Think of these identities as the secret sauce that will help us unlock the solution. These identities are the bedrock upon which trigonometric problem-solving is built, and mastering them is the key to navigating the often-intricate world of angles, ratios, and functions.

The first identity that we absolutely must have in our toolkit is the Pythagorean identity. This identity is like the superhero of trigonometry, always there to save the day. It states a profound relationship between the sine, cosine, and the number 1. In its most basic form, the Pythagorean identity is expressed as sin²θ + cos²θ = 1. This equation tells us that for any angle θ, the sum of the squares of its sine and cosine is always equal to 1. It's a beautiful and powerful relationship that stems directly from the Pythagorean theorem applied to the unit circle.

But wait, there's more! The Pythagorean identity is not just a standalone equation; it's a versatile tool that can be manipulated and transformed to suit our needs. By dividing the entire equation by either cos²θ or sin²θ, we can derive two more incredibly useful identities. When we divide by cos²θ, we get a new identity that relates tangent and secant: 1 + tan²θ = sec²θ. This is the identity that will play a starring role in today's problem, so keep it locked in your memory! On the other hand, if we divide the original Pythagorean identity by sin²θ, we arrive at yet another valuable relationship: cot²θ + 1 = csc²θ, connecting cotangent and cosecant.

These three identities – sin²θ + cos²θ = 1, 1 + tan²θ = sec²θ, and cot²θ + 1 = csc²θ – are like the trinity of trigonometric identities. They are interconnected and can be used interchangeably to solve a wide range of problems. Understanding their derivations and how they relate to each other is a game-changer in trigonometry.

Beyond the Pythagorean identities, there are other crucial relationships that we need to keep in mind. Reciprocal identities are particularly important. These identities define the reciprocal trigonometric functions: cosecant (csc θ), secant (sec θ), and cotangent (cot θ). Cosecant is the reciprocal of sine (csc θ = 1/sin θ), secant is the reciprocal of cosine (sec θ = 1/cos θ), and cotangent is the reciprocal of tangent (cot θ = 1/tan θ). Knowing these reciprocal relationships allows us to easily switch between different trigonometric functions and simplify expressions.

Quotient identities are another set of fundamental relationships. These identities express tangent and cotangent in terms of sine and cosine. Tangent is defined as the ratio of sine to cosine (tan θ = sin θ/cos θ), and cotangent is defined as the ratio of cosine to sine (cot θ = cos θ/sin θ). These identities provide a direct link between the primary trigonometric functions (sine and cosine) and the secondary functions (tangent and cotangent), making them invaluable tools in trigonometric manipulations.

In essence, mastering these trigonometric identities is like learning a new language. Once you understand the grammar and vocabulary, you can express yourself fluently and tackle even the most complex problems with confidence. So, let's embrace these identities, practice using them, and watch as the world of trigonometry unfolds before our eyes!

Applying the Identity to Find sec θ

Okay, now that we've brushed up on our trigonometric identity knowledge – specifically, the relationship between tangent and secant (1 + tan²θ = sec²θ) – let's roll up our sleeves and tackle the problem head-on. Remember, we're given that tan²θ = 3/8, and our mission is to find the value of sec θ. It's like we have a treasure map (the identity) and a clue (tan²θ), and we're on the hunt for the hidden treasure (sec θ).

So, how do we use this identity to our advantage? Well, the equation 1 + tan²θ = sec²θ directly connects the square of tangent (tan²θ) to the square of secant (sec²θ). This is exactly what we need! We already know the value of tan²θ, so we can simply plug it into the equation and see what happens. It's like fitting a puzzle piece into its correct place, and the picture starts to become clearer.

Let's do it step by step. We start with the identity: 1 + tan²θ = sec²θ. Now, we substitute the given value of tan²θ, which is 3/8, into the equation. This gives us: 1 + (3/8) = sec²θ. It's a simple substitution, but it's a crucial step in the process. We've now transformed the problem into a more manageable form.

Next, we need to simplify the left side of the equation. We have 1 + (3/8), which is essentially adding a whole number to a fraction. To do this, we need to express 1 as a fraction with the same denominator as 3/8. We can write 1 as 8/8. So, the equation becomes: (8/8) + (3/8) = sec²θ. Now, we can easily add the fractions since they have the same denominator. Adding the numerators, we get (8 + 3)/8, which simplifies to 11/8. Therefore, our equation now reads: 11/8 = sec²θ. We're getting closer to our treasure!

At this point, we have found the value of sec²θ, which is 11/8. However, remember that our goal is to find the value of sec θ, not sec²θ. So, we need to take one more step. To get sec θ by itself, we need to undo the squaring operation. The inverse operation of squaring is taking the square root. So, we take the square root of both sides of the equation. This gives us: √(11/8) = √(sec²θ).

On the right side of the equation, the square root and the square cancel each other out, leaving us with sec θ. On the left side, we have √(11/8). To simplify this, we can use the property of square roots that says the square root of a fraction is equal to the square root of the numerator divided by the square root of the denominator. So, √(11/8) becomes √11 / √8. We're almost there, guys!

Now, we can simplify √8 further. We can express 8 as 4 * 2, and since √4 = 2, we have √8 = √(4 * 2) = √4 * √2 = 2√2. So, our expression becomes √11 / (2√2). To rationalize the denominator (that is, to get rid of the square root in the denominator), we multiply both the numerator and the denominator by √2. This gives us (√11 * √2) / (2√2 * √2). Multiplying the square roots in the numerator, we get √(11 * 2) = √22. In the denominator, we have 2 * (√2 * √2) = 2 * 2 = 4. So, our final simplified expression for sec θ is √22 / 4.

But hold on a second! When we take the square root, we need to remember that there are actually two possible solutions: a positive solution and a negative solution. This is because both a positive number and its negative counterpart, when squared, will give the same positive result. For example, both 2² and (-2)² are equal to 4. So, when we take the square root of 4, we get both +2 and -2 as possible answers. Similarly, when we took the square root of sec²θ to get sec θ, we need to consider both the positive and the negative square roots.

Therefore, the value of sec θ could be either positive √22 / 4 or negative √22 / 4. We write this as sec θ = ±√22 / 4. This means there are two possible values for sec θ that satisfy the given condition tan²θ = 3/8. Depending on the quadrant in which the angle θ lies, sec θ could be positive (in the first and fourth quadrants) or negative (in the second and third quadrants).

So, there you have it! We've successfully navigated the problem using the trigonometric identity 1 + tan²θ = sec²θ, along with some algebraic manipulation and a careful consideration of the sign of the square root. It's like we've pieced together all the clues and finally uncovered the treasure. Remember, the key to success in trigonometry is a solid understanding of the identities, a willingness to apply them creatively, and a keen eye for detail. Keep practicing, and you'll be a trig whiz in no time!

Considering the Sign of sec θ

Alright, so we've arrived at the solution sec θ = ±√22 / 4, which is fantastic! But hold on, the trigonometric journey isn't quite over yet. We've got a crucial detail to explore: the sign of sec θ. In the world of trigonometry, signs matter. The sign of a trigonometric function tells us in which quadrants the angle θ can lie. It's like a secret code that unlocks the full picture of the problem.

Remember the unit circle? It's our trusty map for navigating trigonometric functions. The unit circle is a circle with a radius of 1 centered at the origin of the coordinate plane. Angles are measured counterclockwise from the positive x-axis, and the coordinates of points on the unit circle are directly related to the sine and cosine of the angle. The x-coordinate corresponds to the cosine of the angle (cos θ), and the y-coordinate corresponds to the sine of the angle (sin θ).

Now, let's bring in secant. Secant, as we know, is the reciprocal of cosine: sec θ = 1/cos θ. This means that the sign of sec θ is directly determined by the sign of cos θ. If cos θ is positive, then sec θ will also be positive. If cos θ is negative, then sec θ will be negative. It's a simple but powerful relationship.

So, where is cos θ positive, and where is it negative? This is where the quadrants come into play. The coordinate plane is divided into four quadrants, numbered I through IV, starting from the upper right and moving counterclockwise. In the first quadrant (I), both x and y coordinates are positive. This means that both cos θ and sin θ are positive in the first quadrant. Consequently, sec θ (which is 1/cos θ) is also positive in the first quadrant.

In the second quadrant (II), the x-coordinate is negative, and the y-coordinate is positive. This means that cos θ is negative, and sin θ is positive. Therefore, sec θ (being the reciprocal of cos θ) is negative in the second quadrant.

In the third quadrant (III), both x and y coordinates are negative. This means that both cos θ and sin θ are negative. So, sec θ (1/cos θ) is also negative in the third quadrant. It's like the third quadrant is a double-negative zone for cosine and secant.

Finally, in the fourth quadrant (IV), the x-coordinate is positive, and the y-coordinate is negative. This means that cos θ is positive, and sin θ is negative. As a result, sec θ (1/cos θ) is positive in the fourth quadrant.

To summarize, sec θ is positive in the first and fourth quadrants and negative in the second and third quadrants. This is a crucial piece of information that helps us narrow down the possible solutions to trigonometric problems.

In our specific problem, we found that sec θ = ±√22 / 4. This means that sec θ can be either positive or negative. Without additional information about the angle θ, we cannot determine the exact sign of sec θ. If we knew, for example, that θ lies in the first quadrant, we could confidently say that sec θ is positive (√22 / 4). If we knew that θ lies in the second quadrant, we would conclude that sec θ is negative (-√22 / 4).

But wait, there's more! We were given that tan²θ = 3/8. This piece of information can actually help us narrow down the possibilities. Remember that tan θ = sin θ / cos θ. Since tan²θ is positive (3/8), this means that tan θ itself can be either positive or negative. If tan θ is positive, then either both sin θ and cos θ are positive (first quadrant) or both sin θ and cos θ are negative (third quadrant). If tan θ is negative, then either sin θ is positive and cos θ is negative (second quadrant) or sin θ is negative and cos θ is positive (fourth quadrant).

Now, let's connect this back to sec θ. We know that sec θ has the same sign as cos θ. So, if tan θ is positive (first or third quadrant), sec θ can be either positive (first quadrant) or negative (third quadrant). If tan θ is negative (second or fourth quadrant), sec θ can be either negative (second quadrant) or positive (fourth quadrant).

However, since we are given tan² θ = 3/8, we know that tan θ is either positive √3/√8 or negative -√3/√8. This tells us that θ can lie in any of the four quadrants. So, without further information, we cannot definitively determine the sign of sec θ. It could be either positive or negative.

Therefore, the final answer remains sec θ = ±√22 / 4. We've considered all the possibilities and taken into account the sign of sec θ in different quadrants. It's like we've solved a trigonometric mystery, carefully analyzing the clues and considering all the angles (pun intended!).

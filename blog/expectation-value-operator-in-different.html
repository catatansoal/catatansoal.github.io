Expectation Value: Operator In Different Eigenbasis

Aug 7, 2025 by ADMIN 52 views
Iklan Headers

Hey everyone! Today, let's dive into a fascinating topic in quantum mechanics: figuring out the expectation value of an operator when we're working in the eigenbasis of another operator. It might sound a bit complex, but we'll break it down step by step so it becomes crystal clear. This is super important in quantum mechanics because it helps us predict the outcomes of measurements. So, grab your quantum hats, and let's get started!

Understanding the Basics

Before we jump into the nitty-gritty, let's make sure we're all on the same page with the fundamental concepts. We're talking about quantum mechanics, so things can get a little abstract, but bear with me. Think of it like this: in the quantum world, particles don't have definite properties until we measure them. Instead, they exist in a superposition of states, described by a wavefunction. Now, to understand how to calculate expectation values, we need to discuss operators, eigenbases, and state vectors.

Operators in Quantum Mechanics

In quantum mechanics, operators are mathematical entities that act on quantum states to produce another quantum state. Think of them as instructions or actions we perform on a system. These operators represent physical observables – things we can measure, like position, momentum, or energy. For instance, the momentum operator tells us about a particle's momentum, and the Hamiltonian operator is related to the total energy of the system. Mathematically, an operator is often represented by a matrix or a differential operator. These operators are crucial because they bridge the gap between the abstract quantum world and our measurable reality.

Eigenbases and Eigenvalues

Now, let's talk about eigenbases and eigenvalues. When an operator acts on a special state vector, called an eigenvector, it simply scales that vector by a constant factor. This constant factor is the eigenvalue. In simpler terms, if you have an operator AAA and an eigenvector ∣a′⟩\vert a' \rangle∣a′⟩, then A∣a′⟩=a′∣a′⟩A \vert a' \rangle = a' \vert a' \rangleA∣a′⟩=a′∣a′⟩, where a′a'a′ is the eigenvalue. The set of all eigenvectors of an operator forms the eigenbasis. This basis is super useful because it provides a natural coordinate system for describing the quantum system. Each eigenvector corresponds to a definite value of the physical observable represented by the operator. This means that if you measure the observable when the system is in an eigenstate, you'll get the corresponding eigenvalue with certainty. Understanding eigenbases is crucial for predicting measurement outcomes in quantum mechanics.

State Vectors and Wavefunctions

The state vector, often denoted as ∣Ψ⟩\vert \Psi \rangle∣Ψ⟩, is a mathematical representation of the quantum state of a system. It lives in a special kind of space called Hilbert space. The state vector contains all the information we can possibly know about the system. Think of it as a complete description of the particle's properties, but in a quantum way. The state vector can be expressed as a linear combination of basis vectors, like the eigenvectors we talked about earlier. This means we can write the state vector as a sum of contributions from different eigenstates. The coefficients in this sum tell us the probability amplitudes for finding the system in each eigenstate. When we square the absolute value of these amplitudes, we get the probabilities of measuring the corresponding eigenvalues. So, the state vector is like a quantum recipe, telling us the likelihood of different measurement outcomes.

Wavefunctions

Closely related to the state vector is the wavefunction, often written as Ψ(x)\Psi(x)Ψ(x). The wavefunction is the state vector expressed in position space. It tells us the probability amplitude of finding a particle at a particular position. The absolute square of the wavefunction gives us the probability density, which is the probability per unit volume of finding the particle at a given location. Wavefunctions are solutions to the Schrödinger equation, which governs the time evolution of quantum systems. They are the cornerstone of quantum mechanics, providing a complete description of a particle's behavior. By understanding the wavefunction, we can predict how a particle will move, interact, and evolve over time.

Expectation Value: What is it?

Now that we've got the basics down, let's talk about the expectation value. In quantum mechanics, the expectation value of an operator represents the average value we'd expect to get if we made many measurements of the corresponding physical observable on identical systems. It's not necessarily a value that you'll get in a single measurement, but rather the average result over many trials. The expectation value is a crucial concept because it bridges the gap between the abstract quantum world and our experimental observations. To calculate the expectation value, we use the following formula:

⟨A⟩=⟨Ψ∣A∣Ψ⟩\langle A \rangle = \langle \Psi \vert A \vert \Psi \rangle ⟨A⟩=⟨Ψ∣A∣Ψ⟩

Where:

  • ⟨A⟩\langle A \rangle⟨A⟩ is the expectation value of the operator AAA.
  • ∣Ψ⟩\vert \Psi \rangle∣Ψ⟩ is the state vector of the system.
  • AAA is the operator representing the physical observable.
  • ⟨Ψ∣\langle \Psi \vert⟨Ψ∣ is the bra vector, which is the conjugate transpose of the ket vector ∣Ψ⟩\vert \Psi \rangle∣Ψ⟩.

This formula tells us to sandwich the operator AAA between the bra and ket vectors representing the state of the system. The result is a complex number, and its real part gives us the expectation value. Think of it like this: we're projecting the effect of the operator onto the state vector, giving us the average result we'd expect to see in our measurements. Calculating the expectation value is a fundamental skill in quantum mechanics, allowing us to make predictions about the outcomes of experiments.

Finding the Expectation Value in a Different Eigenbasis

Okay, here's where things get interesting! We want to find the expectation value of an operator AAA, but we're working in the eigenbasis of a different operator, let's call it BBB. This means we have a set of eigenvectors {∣b′⟩}\{\vert b' \rangle\}{∣b′⟩} that satisfy the eigenvalue equation B∣b′⟩=b′∣b′⟩B \vert b' \rangle = b' \vert b' \rangleB∣b′⟩=b′∣b′⟩, where b′b'b′ are the eigenvalues of BBB. So, how do we calculate ⟨A⟩\langle A \rangle⟨A⟩ when we have the eigenstates of BBB?

The Identity Operator Trick

The key to solving this problem is using the identity operator. The identity operator, denoted by III, doesn't change the state vector when it acts on it. It's like multiplying by 1 in regular algebra. In any complete basis, we can write the identity operator as a sum over the outer products of the basis vectors. Specifically, in the eigenbasis of BBB, we can write:

I=∑b′∣b′⟩⟨b′∣I = \sum_{b'} \vert b' \rangle \langle b' \vert I=b′∑​∣b′⟩⟨b′∣

This equation tells us that the sum over all possible eigenstates of BBB, projected onto themselves, equals the identity. This is a powerful tool because it allows us to insert this identity operator into our expectation value formula without changing the value. So, let's see how we can use this.

Inserting the Identity Operator

Let's go back to our expectation value formula:

⟨A⟩=⟨Ψ∣A∣Ψ⟩\langle A \rangle = \langle \Psi \vert A \vert \Psi \rangle ⟨A⟩=⟨Ψ∣A∣Ψ⟩

Now, we're going to insert the identity operator twice in this equation – once to the left of AAA and once to the right. Remember, inserting the identity operator doesn't change the value, so we're just rewriting the equation in a more convenient form:

⟨A⟩=⟨Ψ∣IAI∣Ψ⟩\langle A \rangle = \langle \Psi \vert I A I \vert \Psi \rangle ⟨A⟩=⟨Ψ∣IAI∣Ψ⟩

Now, let's substitute the expression for the identity operator in the eigenbasis of BBB:

⟨A⟩=⟨Ψ∣(∑b′∣b′⟩⟨b′∣)A(∑b′′∣b′′⟩⟨b′′∣)∣Ψ⟩\langle A \rangle = \langle \Psi \vert \left( \sum_{b'} \vert b' \rangle \langle b' \vert \right) A \left( \sum_{b''} \vert b'' \rangle \langle b'' \vert \right) \vert \Psi \rangle ⟨A⟩=⟨Ψ∣(b′∑​∣b′⟩⟨b′∣)A(b′′∑​∣b′′⟩⟨b′′∣)∣Ψ⟩

Here, we've used two different labels for the eigenvalues, b′b'b′ and b′′b''b′′, to keep things clear in the summations. This is just a mathematical trick to help us keep track of the different terms. Now, we can rearrange the terms a bit:

⟨A⟩=∑b′∑b′′⟨Ψ∣b′⟩⟨b′∣A∣b′′⟩⟨b′′∣Ψ⟩\langle A \rangle = \sum_{b'} \sum_{b''} \langle \Psi \vert b' \rangle \langle b' \vert A \vert b'' \rangle \langle b'' \vert \Psi \rangle ⟨A⟩=b′∑​b′′∑​⟨Ψ∣b′⟩⟨b′∣A∣b′′⟩⟨b′′∣Ψ⟩

The Final Formula

This is where the magic happens! Let's break down what this equation means. We have a double sum over all possible eigenvalues of BBB. Inside the sum, we have a series of terms: ⟨Ψ∣b′⟩\langle \Psi \vert b' \rangle⟨Ψ∣b′⟩, ⟨b′∣A∣b′′⟩\langle b' \vert A \vert b'' \rangle⟨b′∣A∣b′′⟩, and ⟨b′′∣Ψ⟩\langle b'' \vert \Psi \rangle⟨b′′∣Ψ⟩. Each of these terms has a specific meaning:

  • ⟨Ψ∣b′⟩\langle \Psi \vert b' \rangle⟨Ψ∣b′⟩: This is the projection of the state vector ∣Ψ⟩\vert \Psi \rangle∣Ψ⟩ onto the eigenstate ∣b′⟩\vert b' \rangle∣b′⟩. It tells us the probability amplitude of finding the system in the eigenstate ∣b′⟩\vert b' \rangle∣b′⟩. We often write this as cb′c_{b'}cb′​, where cb′c_{b'}cb′​ is the coefficient of the eigenstate ∣b′⟩\vert b' \rangle∣b′⟩ in the expansion of ∣Ψ⟩\vert \Psi \rangle∣Ψ⟩.
  • ⟨b′′∣Ψ⟩\langle b'' \vert \Psi \rangle⟨b′′∣Ψ⟩: This is the projection of the state vector ∣Ψ⟩\vert \Psi \rangle∣Ψ⟩ onto the eigenstate ∣b′′⟩\vert b'' \rangle∣b′′⟩. Similarly, we can write this as cb′′c_{b''}cb′′​.
  • ⟨b′∣A∣b′′⟩\langle b' \vert A \vert b'' \rangle⟨b′∣A∣b′′⟩: This is the matrix element of the operator AAA in the eigenbasis of BBB. It tells us how the operator AAA connects the eigenstates ∣b′⟩\vert b' \rangle∣b′⟩ and ∣b′′⟩\vert b'' \rangle∣b′′⟩. This term is crucial because it incorporates the action of the operator AAA within the eigenbasis of BBB.

So, we can rewrite our equation as:

⟨A⟩=∑b′∑b′′cb′∗⟨b′∣A∣b′′⟩cb′′\langle A \rangle = \sum_{b'} \sum_{b''} c_{b'}^* \langle b' \vert A \vert b'' \rangle c_{b''} ⟨A⟩=b′∑​b′′∑​cb′∗​⟨b′∣A∣b′′⟩cb′′​

Where cb′∗c_{b'}^*cb′∗​ is the complex conjugate of cb′c_{b'}cb′​. This is our final formula for the expectation value of AAA in the eigenbasis of BBB! Guys, this is a pretty powerful result. It tells us that to find the expectation value of an operator in a different eigenbasis, we need to know the coefficients of the state vector in that basis and the matrix elements of the operator in that basis.

Example Time!

Let's make things even clearer with a simple example. Suppose we have a spin-1/2 particle, and we want to find the expectation value of the spin operator in the x-direction (SxS_xSx​) when the particle is in an eigenstate of the spin operator in the z-direction (SzS_zSz​). This is a classic problem in quantum mechanics, and it's a great way to see our formula in action.

Defining the Operators and Eigenstates

First, let's define our operators and eigenstates. For a spin-1/2 particle, the spin operators are given by the Pauli matrices:

Sx=ℏ2(0110),Sz=ℏ2(100−1)S_x = \frac{\hbar}{2} \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad S_z = \frac{\hbar}{2} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix} Sx​=2ℏ​(01​10​),Sz​=2ℏ​(10​0−1​)

Where ℏ\hbarℏ is the reduced Planck constant. The eigenstates of SzS_zSz​ are:

∣+⟩=(10),∣−⟩=(01)\vert + \rangle = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \vert - \rangle = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} ∣+⟩=(10​),∣−⟩=(01​)

These correspond to the spin being aligned along the positive and negative z-directions, respectively. The eigenvalues are +ℏ2+\frac{\hbar}{2}+2ℏ​ and −ℏ2-\frac{\hbar}{2}−2ℏ​.

Setting Up the Problem

Now, let's say our state vector ∣Ψ⟩\vert \Psi \rangle∣Ψ⟩ is the eigenstate ∣+⟩\vert + \rangle∣+⟩ of SzS_zSz​. This means the particle has its spin aligned along the positive z-direction. We want to find the expectation value of SxS_xSx​ in this state:

⟨Sx⟩=⟨Ψ∣Sx∣Ψ⟩=⟨+∣Sx∣+⟩\langle S_x \rangle = \langle \Psi \vert S_x \vert \Psi \rangle = \langle + \vert S_x \vert + \rangle ⟨Sx​⟩=⟨Ψ∣Sx​∣Ψ⟩=⟨+∣Sx​∣+⟩

But, we want to use our formula for finding the expectation value in the eigenbasis of a different operator. In this case, we'll use the eigenbasis of SzS_zSz​, which we already have ({∣+⟩,∣−⟩}\{\vert + \rangle, \vert - \rangle\}{∣+⟩,∣−⟩}).

Applying the Formula

Let's apply our formula:

⟨Sx⟩=∑sz′∑sz′′csz′∗⟨sz′∣Sx∣sz′′⟩csz′′\langle S_x \rangle = \sum_{s_z'} \sum_{s_z''} c_{s_z'}^* \langle s_z' \vert S_x \vert s_z'' \rangle c_{s_z''} ⟨Sx​⟩=sz′​∑​sz′′​∑​csz′​∗​⟨sz′​∣Sx​∣sz′′​⟩csz′′​​

Where sz′s_z'sz′​ and sz′′s_z''sz′′​ are the eigenvalues of SzS_zSz​, and csz′c_{s_z'}csz′​​ and csz′′c_{s_z''}csz′′​​ are the coefficients of the eigenstates in the expansion of ∣Ψ⟩\vert \Psi \rangle∣Ψ⟩. In our case, the eigenstates are ∣+⟩\vert + \rangle∣+⟩ and ∣−⟩\vert - \rangle∣−⟩, so we can rewrite the sum as:

⟨Sx⟩=c+∗⟨+∣Sx∣+⟩c++c+∗⟨+∣Sx∣−⟩c−+c−∗⟨−∣Sx∣+⟩c++c−∗⟨−∣Sx∣−⟩c−\langle S_x \rangle = c_+^* \langle + \vert S_x \vert + \rangle c_+ + c_+^* \langle + \vert S_x \vert - \rangle c_- + c_-^* \langle - \vert S_x \vert + \rangle c_+ + c_-^* \langle - \vert S_x \vert - \rangle c_- ⟨Sx​⟩=c+∗​⟨+∣Sx​∣+⟩c+​+c+∗​⟨+∣Sx​∣−⟩c−​+c−∗​⟨−∣Sx​∣+⟩c+​+c−∗​⟨−∣Sx​∣−⟩c−​

Since our state vector is ∣Ψ⟩=∣+⟩\vert \Psi \rangle = \vert + \rangle∣Ψ⟩=∣+⟩, the coefficients are c+=1c_+ = 1c+​=1 and c−=0c_- = 0c−​=0. This simplifies the equation to:

⟨Sx⟩=⟨+∣Sx∣+⟩\langle S_x \rangle = \langle + \vert S_x \vert + \rangle ⟨Sx​⟩=⟨+∣Sx​∣+⟩

Calculating the Matrix Element

Now, we need to calculate the matrix element ⟨+∣Sx∣+⟩\langle + \vert S_x \vert + \rangle⟨+∣Sx​∣+⟩. We can do this by plugging in the matrix representations of SxS_xSx​ and the eigenstates:

⟨+∣Sx∣+⟩=(10)ℏ2(0110)(10)\langle + \vert S_x \vert + \rangle = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \frac{\hbar}{2} \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix} ⟨+∣Sx​∣+⟩=(1​0​)2ℏ​(01​10​)(10​)

Multiplying these matrices, we get:

⟨+∣Sx∣+⟩=ℏ2(10)(01)=0\langle + \vert S_x \vert + \rangle = \frac{\hbar}{2} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} = 0 ⟨+∣Sx​∣+⟩=2ℏ​(1​0​)(01​)=0

The Result

So, the expectation value of SxS_xSx​ in the state ∣+⟩\vert + \rangle∣+⟩ is:

⟨Sx⟩=0\langle S_x \rangle = 0 ⟨Sx​⟩=0

This result tells us that if we measure the spin of the particle in the x-direction, we'll get an average value of zero. This makes sense because the spin is aligned along the z-direction, so there's no preferred direction in the x-y plane. This example demonstrates how our formula works in practice and how we can use it to make predictions about measurement outcomes in quantum mechanics.

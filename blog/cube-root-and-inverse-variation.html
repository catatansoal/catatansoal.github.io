Cube Root & Inverse Variation: Find The Equations!

Aug 6, 2025 by ADMIN 51 views

Hey guys! Let's dive into a fascinating mathematical concept: inverse variation, specifically when cube roots and squares are involved. This type of problem might seem tricky at first, but with a clear understanding of the principles, you'll be able to tackle it with confidence. We're going to break down a problem where the cube root of one variable varies inversely with the square of another. Get ready to explore the world of equations and relationships!

Understanding Inverse Variation

Before we jump into the specific problem, let's quickly recap what inverse variation means. In simple terms, two quantities vary inversely if, as one quantity increases, the other quantity decreases, and vice versa. This relationship can be represented mathematically using the following general form: y = k/x, where y and x are the two variables, and k is a constant of variation. This constant, k, is the key to understanding the proportional link between x and y. It dictates the strength and direction of the inverse relationship. The larger the value of k, the greater the impact that changes in x have on y, and vice versa. Understanding this fundamental relationship is crucial for tackling the problem at hand. When you see the phrase “varies inversely,” think of this equation as your starting point. This equation tells us that the product of x and y is always equal to the constant k, which is a really helpful thing to know when setting up and solving problems involving inverse variation. Remember, this relationship implies a balancing act: as x goes up, y must come down to keep their product constant, and vice versa. This concept is not just confined to the realm of abstract mathematics; it has real-world applications in physics, engineering, and economics, where understanding how different quantities influence each other is critical.

The Problem: Cube Root and Square

Now, let's tackle the specific problem we're faced with. The problem states: "The cube root of r varies inversely with the square of s. Which two equations model this relationship?" The core of this problem lies in translating the given statement into mathematical equations. Remember, the key phrase here is “varies inversely.” We know from our previous discussion that this phrase indicates a relationship where the product of two related expressions is a constant. The first step in solving this problem is identifying the two expressions that are varying inversely. In this case, the expressions are “the cube root of r” and “the square of s.” Mathematically, these expressions can be written as r^(1/3) and s^2, respectively. These are the building blocks we’ll use to construct our equations. The next step is to apply the concept of inverse variation. Since r^(1/3) varies inversely with s^2, their product must be equal to a constant, which we'll denote as k. This allows us to write the fundamental equation representing this inverse relationship. From this foundation, we can explore different ways to express the same relationship, which will lead us to the correct answer choices.

Constructing the Equations

Based on our understanding of inverse variation and the given information, we can construct the initial equation. Since the cube root of r (which is r^(1/3)) varies inversely with the square of s (which is s^2), we can write the equation as: r^(1/3) * s^2 = k. This equation is the most direct translation of the problem statement into mathematical form. It clearly shows that the product of the cube root of r and the square of s is equal to the constant k. This means that for any valid pair of r and s values that satisfy the inverse relationship, their cube root and square, respectively, will always multiply to the same constant value k. This is the essence of inverse variation. Now, let's analyze the given options to see if they match this fundamental equation or can be derived from it. We'll start by examining each option individually, and manipulating them algebraically if necessary, to see if they are equivalent to our core equation r^(1/3) * s^2 = k. This process of manipulation is a critical skill in mathematics, as it allows us to recognize different forms of the same equation.

Analyzing the Options

Now, let's dissect the options provided in the problem. We need to identify which of them correctly model the inverse variation relationship we've established. The first option is: s^2 * r^(1/3) = k. Guys, look closely! This equation is actually identical to the one we derived: r^(1/3) * s^2 = k. Remember that multiplication is commutative, which means the order of the factors doesn't change the result. So, s^2 * r^(1/3) is the same as r^(1/3) * s^2. Therefore, this option is definitely a correct representation of the inverse variation. The second option is: √[3]r = k/3^2. To analyze this, let's rewrite it as r^(1/3) = k/9. This equation seems to suggest that the cube root of r is a constant value (k/9), which contradicts the idea that it varies inversely with the square of s. In an inverse variation, the cube root of r should change as the square of s changes, not remain constant. Therefore, this option is incorrect. Moving on, the third option is: (s * √7)/3^2 = k. This can be rewritten as s * √7 = 9k. This equation suggests a direct variation between s and a constant, not an inverse variation between the cube root of r and the square of s. There's no r term present, indicating that this equation does not model the relationship we're interested in. Thus, this option is also incorrect. Finally, the fourth option is: s^(1/2). This option is incomplete. It doesn't provide a full equation and doesn't relate s to r or any constant. Without a complete equation, we can't determine if it models the inverse variation. Therefore, this option is not a valid representation of the relationship.

The Correct Answers

After carefully analyzing each option, we've identified the equations that correctly model the given relationship. The first equation, s^2 * r^(1/3) = k, is a direct representation of the inverse variation between the cube root of r and the square of s. It simply states that the product of these two quantities is equal to a constant, k, which is the defining characteristic of inverse variation. The other options either contradict this fundamental principle or do not provide a complete relationship between the variables. Therefore, the first equation stands as a correct model.

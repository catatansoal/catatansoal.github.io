Adding Rational Expressions A Step-by-Step Guide

It might sound a little intimidating at first, but trust me, we'll break it down into manageable steps so that you'll be a pro in no time. We're going to specifically look at an example: adding 415n2b6\frac{4}{15 n^2 b^6}15n2b64​ and 56n6b2\frac{5}{6 n^6 b^2}6n6b25​. So, grab your pencils, and let's get started!

Understanding Rational Expressions

Before we jump into the nitty-gritty of adding these fractions, let's make sure we're all on the same page about what rational expressions actually are. Essentially, a rational expression is just a fancy way of saying a fraction where the numerator and the denominator are polynomials. Think of it like this: instead of dealing with plain old numbers, we're now dealing with expressions that involve variables and exponents. These expressions can represent a wide range of mathematical relationships, and being able to manipulate them is a crucial skill in algebra and beyond. So, when you see something like 415n2b6\frac{4}{15 n^2 b^6}15n2b64​ or 56n6b2\frac{5}{6 n^6 b^2}6n6b25​, remember that these are just fractions with a little algebraic twist! Now that we've got that down, we can move on to the challenge of adding them together.

When we add rational expressions, the key challenge arises when the denominators are different, just like in our example. You might remember from basic arithmetic that you can't directly add fractions unless they share a common denominator. For instance, you can't directly add 12\frac{1}{2}21​ and 13\frac{1}{3}31​ without finding a common denominator. The same principle applies to rational expressions. We need to find a common denominator that both fractions can be expressed with before we can combine them. This common denominator will allow us to rewrite the fractions with equivalent forms, making the addition process straightforward. It's like speaking the same language – once both fractions are expressed in terms of the same denominator, we can easily add their numerators and simplify the result. This step of finding a common denominator is crucial for accurately adding rational expressions and is the foundation of the entire process.

Finding the Least Common Denominator (LCD)

The first crucial step in adding rational expressions with different denominators is finding the least common denominator (LCD). The LCD is the smallest expression that is divisible by both denominators. It's like finding the smallest common multiple, but for algebraic expressions. In our example, we have the denominators 15n2b615 n^2 b^615n2b6 and 6n6b26 n^6 b^26n6b2. To find the LCD, we need to consider both the numerical coefficients and the variable terms. For the numerical coefficients, 15 and 6, the least common multiple is 30. Think of it as the smallest number that both 15 and 6 divide into evenly. For the variable terms, we take the highest power of each variable that appears in either denominator. So, for n, we have n2n^2n2 and n6n^6n6, and the highest power is n6n^6n6. For b, we have b6b^6b6 and b2b^2b2, and the highest power is b6b^6b6. Therefore, the LCD is 30n6b630 n^6 b^630n6b6. This is the expression we'll use to rewrite both fractions with a common denominator, making them ready for addition. Mastering the art of finding the LCD is a fundamental skill in working with rational expressions, so let's make sure we've got it down!

Once we've identified the least common denominator (LCD), the next step is to rewrite each rational expression so that it has the LCD as its denominator. This involves multiplying both the numerator and the denominator of each fraction by a carefully chosen expression. The goal here is to transform the original fractions into equivalent fractions that share the same denominator, which is the LCD we just calculated. Let's break it down for our example. For the first fraction, 415n2b6\frac{4}{15 n^2 b^6}15n2b64​, we need to figure out what to multiply the denominator 15n2b615 n^2 b^615n2b6 by to get our LCD, 30n6b630 n^6 b^630n6b6. We can see that we need to multiply by 2n42 n^42n4 (since 15×2=3015 \times 2 = 3015×2=30, n2×n4=n6n^2 \times n^4 = n^6n2×n4=n6, and b6b^6b6 is already present). So, we multiply both the numerator and the denominator of the first fraction by 2n42 n^42n4. Similarly, for the second fraction, 56n6b2\frac{5}{6 n^6 b^2}6n6b25​, we need to multiply the denominator 6n6b26 n^6 b^26n6b2 by 5b45 b^45b4 to get our LCD (since 6×5=306 \times 5 = 306×5=30, n6n^6n6 is already present, and b2×b4=b6b^2 \times b^4 = b^6b2×b4=b6). Thus, we multiply both the numerator and the denominator of the second fraction by 5b45 b^45b4. This process ensures that we are creating equivalent fractions, meaning we're not changing the value of the expressions, just their form. This rewriting step is crucial for setting up the addition process and ensuring accurate results.

Adding the Rational Expressions

Now that we've successfully rewritten our rational expressions with the least common denominator (LCD), the fun part begins: adding them together! This step is actually quite straightforward once you've done the groundwork of finding the LCD and rewriting the fractions. Remember, we're dealing with fractions that now have the same denominator, which means we can simply add their numerators and keep the common denominator. It's just like adding regular fractions, but with algebraic expressions involved. In our example, after rewriting the fractions, we'll have something like expression 130n6b6+expression 230n6b6\frac{\text{expression 1}}{30 n^6 b^6} + \frac{\text{expression 2}}{30 n^6 b^6}30n6b6expression 1​+30n6b6expression 2​. To add these, we add the numerators (expression 1 + expression 2) and keep the denominator as 30n6b630 n^6 b^630n6b6. The result will be a new rational expression that represents the sum of the original two expressions. This is a key step in simplifying complex algebraic expressions and is a fundamental skill in algebra. So, let's see how it plays out in our specific example!

Let's apply this to our example: 415n2b6+56n6b2\frac{4}{15 n^2 b^6}+\frac{5}{6 n^6 b^2}15n2b64​+6n6b25​. We found the LCD to be 30n6b630n^6b^630n6b6. Now we rewrite each fraction with this denominator:

  • For the first fraction, 415n2b6\frac{4}{15 n^2 b^6}15n2b64​, we multiply the numerator and denominator by 2n42n^42n4:

    4×2n415n2b6×2n4=8n430n6b6\frac{4 \times 2n^4}{15 n^2 b^6 \times 2n^4} = \frac{8n^4}{30 n^6 b^6} 15n2b6×2n44×2n4​=30n6b68n4​

  • For the second fraction, 56n6b2\frac{5}{6 n^6 b^2}6n6b25​, we multiply the numerator and denominator by 5b45b^45b4:

    5×5b46n6b2×5b4=25b430n6b6\frac{5 \times 5b^4}{6 n^6 b^2 \times 5b^4} = \frac{25b^4}{30 n^6 b^6} 6n6b2×5b45×5b4​=30n6b625b4​

Now that both fractions have the same denominator, we can add them:

8n430n6b6+25b430n6b6=8n4+25b430n6b6\frac{8n^4}{30 n^6 b^6} + \frac{25b^4}{30 n^6 b^6} = \frac{8n^4 + 25b^4}{30 n^6 b^6} 30n6b68n4​+30n6b625b4​=30n6b68n4+25b4​

So, there you have it! We've successfully added the two rational expressions. The result is 8n4+25b430n6b6\frac{8n^4 + 25b^4}{30 n^6 b^6}30n6b68n4+25b4​.

Simplifying the Result (If Possible)

After adding rational expressions, it's always a good practice to check if the resulting expression can be simplified further. Simplification is like putting the final touch on your work – it ensures that your answer is in its most concise and elegant form. To simplify a rational expression, we look for common factors in the numerator and the denominator that can be canceled out. This might involve factoring both the numerator and the denominator and then identifying any shared factors. If we find common factors, we can divide both the numerator and the denominator by those factors, which reduces the expression to its simplest form. However, it's important to remember that we can only cancel out factors, not terms. In our example, 8n4+25b430n6b6\frac{8n^4 + 25b^4}{30 n^6 b^6}30n6b68n4+25b4​, we need to carefully examine the numerator and the denominator to see if there are any common factors. If there aren't any, then our result is already in its simplest form. But if we do find common factors, simplifying the expression will give us the most accurate and refined answer. So, let's take a look at our result and see if we can simplify it!

In our case, let's examine the result we obtained: 8n4+25b430n6b6\frac{8n^4 + 25b^4}{30 n^6 b^6}30n6b68n4+25b4​. To determine if we can simplify this expression, we need to look for common factors in the numerator and the denominator. The numerator is 8n4+25b48n^4 + 25b^48n4+25b4, and the denominator is 30n6b630 n^6 b^630n6b6. In the denominator, we have 30n6b630 n^6 b^630n6b6, which can be factored into its prime factors and variable components, but none of these factors are present in the numerator as a common factor for the entire expression. Since there are no common factors between the numerator and the denominator, the expression is already in its simplest form. Therefore, our final simplified answer is 8n4+25b430n6b6\frac{8n^4 + 25b^4}{30 n^6 b^6}30n6b68n4+25b4​.

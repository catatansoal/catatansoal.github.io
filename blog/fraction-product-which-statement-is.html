Fraction Product: Which Statement Is True?

Aug 5, 2025 by ADMIN 43 views

Hey there, math enthusiasts! Today, we're diving into a question that might seem tricky at first glance but is super fun to explore: "Which of the following statements is true?"

Let's break down the options and get a solid understanding of how fraction multiplication works. We'll tackle each statement step by step, making sure we're crystal clear on the concepts involved. By the end of this article, you'll not only know the answer but also why it's the answer. So, grab your thinking caps, and let's get started!

Decoding the Statements

Before we jump into calculations, let's take a closer look at the statements themselves. Understanding what they're asking is half the battle, right? The core concept here revolves around the effect of multiplying fractions. Specifically, we want to know if multiplying a fraction by another fraction always results in a smaller number. Is that always the case? Or are there situations where the product might be larger than the original fraction?

The statements present us with specific scenarios involving the multiplication of fractions. Our mission is to determine which statement accurately reflects the outcome of the multiplication. We'll need to perform the multiplications and then compare the results to the original fractions. This comparison will reveal whether the product is indeed less than the fraction it was multiplied by. It's like a little detective work with numbers, and we're the detectives!

It's important to remember that fractions represent parts of a whole. When we multiply fractions, we're essentially taking a "part of a part." This can sometimes lead to a smaller result, but not always. The key lies in the specific values of the fractions involved. To solve this, we'll need to brush up on our fraction multiplication rules and pay close attention to the numerators and denominators.

Statement A: The Product of $ rac{3}{4}$ and $ rac{8}{9}$

Let's dissect the first statement: "The product of $ rac{3}{4}$ and $ rac{8}{9}$ is less than $ rac{8}{9}$." To verify this, our first step is to actually calculate the product of $ rac{3}{4}$ and $ rac{8}{9}$. Remember, when we multiply fractions, we multiply the numerators (the top numbers) together and the denominators (the bottom numbers) together. So, we have:

rac{3}{4} imes rac{8}{9} = rac{3 imes 8}{4 imes 9} = rac{24}{36}

Now, we've got a new fraction, $ rac{24}{36}$. To make things easier to compare, we should simplify this fraction. Both 24 and 36 are divisible by 12, so we can reduce the fraction:

rac{24}{36} = rac{24 ext{ ÷ } 12}{36 ext{ ÷ } 12} = rac{2}{3}

Okay, so the product of $ rac3}{4}$ and $ rac{8}{9}$ is $ rac{2}{3}$. Now, the critical part is $ rac{2{3}$ less than $ rac{8}{9}$? This is where we need to compare the two fractions directly. To do this effectively, we need to find a common denominator. The least common multiple of 3 and 9 is 9, so let's convert $ rac{2}{3}$ to an equivalent fraction with a denominator of 9:

rac{2}{3} = rac{2 imes 3}{3 imes 3} = rac{6}{9}

Now we can easily compare: Is $ rac{6}{9}$ less than $ rac{8}{9}$? Yes, it is! 6 is less than 8. Therefore, the product of $ rac{3}{4}$ and $ rac{8}{9}$ is indeed less than $ rac{8}{9}$. So, based on our analysis, statement A appears to be true. But remember, we need to examine all the statements before we declare a winner.

Statement B: The Product of $1 rac{1}{4}$ and $ rac{2}{11}$

Now, let's tackle statement B: "The product of $1 rac{1}{4}$ and $ rac{2}{11}$ is less than $ rac{2}{11}$." This statement involves a mixed number, $1 rac{1}{4}$, so our first order of business is to convert it into an improper fraction. This will make the multiplication process smoother. To convert a mixed number to an improper fraction, we multiply the whole number by the denominator and then add the numerator. This result becomes the new numerator, and we keep the same denominator.

So, for $1 rac{1}{4}$, we have:

(1imes4)+1=5(1 imes 4) + 1 = 5 (1imes4)+1=5

This gives us the improper fraction $ rac{5}{4}$. Now we can multiply $ rac{5}{4}$ by $ rac{2}{11}$:

rac{5}{4} imes rac{2}{11} = rac{5 imes 2}{4 imes 11} = rac{10}{44}

We have a product of $ rac{10}{44}$. Let's simplify this fraction by dividing both the numerator and the denominator by their greatest common divisor, which is 2:

rac{10}{44} = rac{10 ext{ ÷ } 2}{44 ext{ ÷ } 2} = rac{5}{22}

Okay, the product is $ rac5}{22}$. Now, the crucial question is $ rac{5{22}$ less than $ rac{2}{11}$? To compare these fractions, we need a common denominator. The least common multiple of 22 and 11 is 22. So, let's convert $ rac{2}{11}$ to an equivalent fraction with a denominator of 22:

rac{2}{11} = rac{2 imes 2}{11 imes 2} = rac{4}{22}

Now we can compare easily: Is $ rac{5}{22}$ less than $ rac{4}{22}$? No, it is not! 5 is greater than 4. Therefore, the product of $1 rac{1}{4}$ and $ rac{2}{11}$ is not less than $ rac{2}{11}$. This means statement B is false.

The Verdict: Which Statement Holds True?

We've meticulously analyzed both statements, and the results are in! Statement A, "The product of $ rac{3}{4}$ and $ rac{8}{9}$ is less than $ rac{8}{9}$," proved to be true. We calculated the product, simplified the resulting fraction, and compared it to the original fraction, confirming that the product was indeed smaller.

On the other hand, statement B, "The product of $1 rac{1}{4}$ and $ rac{2}{11}$ is less than $ rac{2}{11}$," turned out to be false. Our calculations showed that the product was actually greater than the fraction it was compared to.

Therefore, the answer to our question is statement A.

Key Takeaways and Fraction Multiplication Mastery

Wow, we've done some serious fraction detective work today! We've not only solved the problem but also reinforced some crucial concepts about fraction multiplication and comparison. Let's recap the key takeaways to solidify our understanding:

  • Multiplying fractions: We multiply the numerators and the denominators. It's a straightforward process, but accuracy is key.
  • Simplifying fractions: Reducing fractions to their simplest form makes comparisons much easier. Always look for common factors to simplify.
  • Comparing fractions: To compare fractions effectively, we need a common denominator. This allows us to directly compare the numerators.
  • Mixed numbers and improper fractions: Converting mixed numbers to improper fractions is essential for multiplication. It streamlines the calculation process and reduces the chance of errors.

This exercise highlights a crucial point about fraction multiplication: multiplying a fraction by another fraction doesn't always result in a smaller number. It depends on the value of the fractions involved. If you multiply a fraction by a fraction less than 1, the product will be smaller. But if you multiply by a fraction greater than 1 (like our mixed number in statement B), the product will be larger.

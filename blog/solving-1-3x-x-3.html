Solving √1-3x = X+3: A Step-by-Step Guide

Aug 1, 2025 by ADMIN 42 views

Hey guys! Today, we're diving into the world of algebra to tackle a fun little equation: √1-3x = x+3. This equation involves a square root, which might seem intimidating at first, but don't worry! We'll break it down step by step, making sure everyone can follow along. Our main goal here is to find the value(s) of 'x' that make this equation true. Think of it like a puzzle where we need to find the missing piece – in this case, the correct value for 'x'. So, buckle up, grab your thinking caps, and let's get started!

Understanding the Equation

Before we jump into solving, let's make sure we fully understand what the equation √1-3x = x+3 is telling us. On the left side, we have a square root. The expression inside the square root, 1-3x, is called the radicand. Remember, the square root of a number is a value that, when multiplied by itself, gives you the original number. For example, the square root of 9 is 3 because 3 * 3 = 9. The key thing to remember about square roots in the context of real numbers is that we can only take the square root of non-negative numbers (numbers greater than or equal to zero). This is because there's no real number that, when multiplied by itself, gives you a negative number. This little rule is going to be crucial later when we check our solutions. On the right side of the equation, we have a simple linear expression, x+3. This represents a straight line when graphed. Our goal is to find the value(s) of 'x' where the square root expression on the left side equals the linear expression on the right side. In simpler terms, we're looking for the x-values where these two expressions 'meet' or have the same value. Now that we have a clear understanding of the equation, let's move on to the first step in solving it: squaring both sides.

Step 1: Squaring Both Sides

The most common approach to eliminate a square root in an equation is to square both sides. This is based on the principle that if two things are equal (in this case, √1-3x and x+3), then squaring them will also result in equal values. So, when we square both sides of the equation √1-3x = x+3, we get: (√1-3x)² = (x+3)². On the left side, the square root and the square cancel each other out, leaving us with just the radicand: 1-3x. On the right side, we need to expand the square of the binomial (x+3)². Remember, this means multiplying (x+3) by itself: (x+3) (x+3). Using the FOIL method (First, Outer, Inner, Last) or the distributive property, we get: x² + 3x + 3x + 9, which simplifies to x² + 6x + 9. So, our equation now looks like this: 1-3x = x² + 6x + 9. We've successfully eliminated the square root, but now we have a quadratic equation. Don't worry, we'll handle that in the next step. The important thing to remember here is that squaring both sides can sometimes introduce extraneous solutions. These are solutions that satisfy the squared equation but not the original equation. This is why it's absolutely crucial to check our solutions at the end of the process. Now that we have a quadratic equation, let's move all the terms to one side and set the equation equal to zero.

Step 2: Rearranging into a Quadratic Equation

Now that we have 1-3x = x² + 6x + 9, our next step is to rearrange the equation into the standard form of a quadratic equation, which is ax² + bx + c = 0. This makes it easier to solve using various methods, such as factoring, completing the square, or the quadratic formula. To do this, we want to get all the terms on one side of the equation, leaving zero on the other side. Let's move all the terms from the left side to the right side. To do this, we'll add 3x to both sides and subtract 1 from both sides. This gives us: 0 = x² + 6x + 3x + 9 - 1. Simplifying this, we get: 0 = x² + 9x + 8. Now we have a quadratic equation in standard form: x² + 9x + 8 = 0. Here, a = 1, b = 9, and c = 8. We're now ready to solve this quadratic equation. The most common methods for solving quadratic equations are factoring and using the quadratic formula. Let's try factoring first, as it's often the quickest method if the quadratic equation is factorable. We're looking for two numbers that multiply to c (which is 8) and add up to b (which is 9). Can you think of what those numbers might be? Think of factors of 8 and see if any pair adds up to 9. Once we've found the factors, we can rewrite the quadratic equation in factored form. Let's move on to the next step and see how to factor this equation.

Step 3: Factoring the Quadratic Equation

Okay, so we have the quadratic equation x² + 9x + 8 = 0. To factor this, we need to find two numbers that multiply to 8 (the constant term) and add up to 9 (the coefficient of the x term). After a little thought, you might realize that the numbers 1 and 8 fit the bill! 1 * 8 = 8, and 1 + 8 = 9. Excellent! Now we can rewrite the quadratic equation in its factored form. Factoring involves expressing the quadratic as a product of two binomials. Since our numbers are 1 and 8, we can write the equation as: (x + 1) (x + 8) = 0. Think about it: if you were to multiply these two binomials using the FOIL method, you'd get back our original quadratic equation, x² + 9x + 8. Now, the beauty of factoring is that it allows us to use the zero-product property. This property states that if the product of two factors is zero, then at least one of the factors must be zero. In our case, this means that either (x + 1) = 0 or (x + 8) = 0. We can now set each factor equal to zero and solve for x. This will give us two potential solutions for our original equation. Let's do that in the next step. We're getting closer to finding the values of x that satisfy the equation!

Step 4: Solving for x

We've successfully factored our quadratic equation into (x + 1) (x + 8) = 0. Now, using the zero-product property, we set each factor equal to zero: x + 1 = 0 and x + 8 = 0. Solving the first equation, x + 1 = 0, we subtract 1 from both sides, giving us x = -1. Solving the second equation, x + 8 = 0, we subtract 8 from both sides, giving us x = -8. So, we have two potential solutions: x = -1 and x = -8. But remember, we squared both sides of our original equation, which means we might have introduced extraneous solutions. This is why it's super important to check our solutions in the original equation. We need to plug each of these values back into the original equation, √1-3x = x+3, to see if they actually work. If a solution doesn't make the original equation true, it's an extraneous solution, and we need to discard it. Let's start by checking x = -1. We'll substitute -1 for x in the original equation and see what happens. Get ready to do a little bit of arithmetic!

Step 5: Checking for Extraneous Solutions

Alright, we've got our potential solutions, x = -1 and x = -8. Now comes the crucial step: checking for extraneous solutions. We need to plug each value back into the original equation, √1-3x = x+3, and see if it holds true. Let's start with x = -1. Substituting x = -1 into the equation, we get: √1-3(-1) = -1+3. Simplifying the left side, we have: √1+3 = √4 = 2. Simplifying the right side, we have: -1+3 = 2. So, we have 2 = 2, which is a true statement! This means that x = -1 is a valid solution to our equation. Now, let's check our other potential solution, x = -8. Substituting x = -8 into the equation, we get: √1-3(-8) = -8+3. Simplifying the left side, we have: √1+24 = √25 = 5. Simplifying the right side, we have: -8+3 = -5. So, we have 5 = -5, which is definitely not a true statement! This means that x = -8 is an extraneous solution. It's a solution that we got during the solving process, but it doesn't actually satisfy the original equation. This highlights why checking solutions is so important when dealing with equations involving square roots (or any operation that can introduce extraneous solutions). So, we've checked both potential solutions, and we found that x = -1 is a valid solution, while x = -8 is an extraneous solution. This means that our final answer is just x = -1. We've successfully solved the equation! Let's wrap things up in the final section.

Final Solution

We've journeyed through the steps of solving the equation √1-3x = x+3, and after careful consideration and checking for extraneous solutions, we've arrived at our final answer. The only solution that satisfies the original equation is x = -1. Remember, it's always a good idea to double-check your work, especially when dealing with square roots and quadratic equations. Make sure you understand each step and why we performed it. Solving equations like this is a fundamental skill in algebra, and mastering it will open doors to more advanced mathematical concepts. We started by understanding the equation, then we squared both sides to eliminate the square root. This led us to a quadratic equation, which we rearranged and factored. We then used the zero-product property to find potential solutions, and finally, we checked those solutions in the original equation to eliminate any extraneous ones. This process is a great example of how mathematical problem-solving often involves a series of logical steps, each building upon the previous one. So, congratulations! You've successfully solved the equation √1-3x = x+3. Keep practicing, and you'll become a pro at solving these types of problems in no time!

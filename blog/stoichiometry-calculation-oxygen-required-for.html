Stoichiometry Calculation Oxygen Required For Acetylene Combustion

Jul 13, 2025 by ADMIN 67 views

Hey guys! Today, we're diving into a stoichiometry problem, which basically means we're using balanced chemical equations to figure out how much stuff we need in a reaction. Our mission, should we choose to accept it, is to calculate the grams of oxygen required to react completely with 859.0 grams of acetylene (C₂H₂). Sounds like a blast, right? Let's break it down step by step.

Understanding the Combustion Reaction of Acetylene

Combustion reactions are fascinating chemical processes where a substance reacts rapidly with an oxidant, usually oxygen, to produce heat and light. In our case, the combustion reaction involves acetylene (C₂H₂), a colorless gas widely used as a fuel in welding torches due to its high heat of combustion. The balanced chemical equation is our roadmap here:

2 C₂H₂ + 5 O₂ → 4 CO₂ + 2 H₂O

This equation tells us that two moles of acetylene react with five moles of oxygen to produce four moles of carbon dioxide and two moles of water. It's like a recipe – we need the right proportions of ingredients to get the desired outcome. This balanced equation is the cornerstone of our calculations, providing the molar ratios necessary to convert between the masses of reactants and products. To accurately perform these calculations, it's crucial to understand the stoichiometry of the reaction, which essentially means the quantitative relationship between reactants and products in a chemical reaction. This relationship allows us to predict the amount of reactants needed or products formed. In essence, stoichiometry is the language that allows us to interpret and apply chemical equations to real-world scenarios.

Stoichiometry is essential in various fields, including industrial chemistry, environmental science, and even cooking! In industrial chemistry, it helps optimize chemical processes to maximize product yield and minimize waste. In environmental science, it's used to calculate the amount of pollutants released into the atmosphere or water. And yes, even in cooking, understanding the ratios of ingredients can make the difference between a culinary masterpiece and a kitchen disaster. Therefore, mastering stoichiometric principles is not just for chemists; it’s a valuable skill applicable across many disciplines. So, let's get our stoichiometry hats on and dive deeper into this problem.

Key Stoichiometry Concepts

  • Moles: The mole is the SI unit for measuring the amount of a substance. One mole contains Avogadro's number (approximately 6.022 x 10²³) of particles (atoms, molecules, ions, etc.). Think of it as a chemist's dozen. The mole concept bridges the microscopic world of atoms and molecules with the macroscopic world of grams and liters that we can measure in the lab.
  • Molar Mass: The molar mass of a substance is the mass of one mole of that substance, usually expressed in grams per mole (g/mol). You can find the molar mass by adding up the atomic masses of all the atoms in the chemical formula, which is why our periodic table is our best friend in these calculations. Molar mass serves as the conversion factor between mass (in grams) and moles, allowing us to switch back and forth between these two fundamental units.
  • Mole Ratio: The mole ratio is derived from the coefficients in the balanced chemical equation. It represents the proportional relationship between the number of moles of any two substances involved in the reaction. In our equation, the mole ratio between acetylene and oxygen is 2:5, indicating that for every 2 moles of acetylene, we need 5 moles of oxygen. This ratio is the key to converting moles of one substance to moles of another.

Step-by-Step Calculation of Oxygen Required

Alright, let's roll up our sleeves and crunch some numbers! We're going to follow a classic stoichiometry roadmap to get our answer.

Step 1: Convert Grams of Acetylene to Moles

Our first task is to convert the given mass of acetylene (859.0 g) into moles. To do this, we need the molar mass of acetylene (C₂H₂). Grab your trusty periodic table! The atomic mass of carbon (C) is approximately 12.01 g/mol, and the atomic mass of hydrogen (H) is approximately 1.01 g/mol.

Molar mass of C₂H₂ = (2 × 12.01 g/mol) + (2 × 1.01 g/mol) = 24.02 g/mol + 2.02 g/mol = 26.04 g/mol

Now we can convert grams to moles using the molar mass as a conversion factor:

Moles of C₂H₂ = 859.0 g / 26.04 g/mol ≈ 33.00 mol

So, we have approximately 33.00 moles of acetylene. This conversion is the first critical step in our stoichiometric journey, transforming the macroscopic mass we can measure into the microscopic world of moles, which directly relates to the number of molecules involved in the reaction.

Step 2: Use the Mole Ratio to Find Moles of Oxygen

Here's where the balanced equation shines! We know from the equation that 2 moles of C₂H₂ react with 5 moles of O₂. This gives us the mole ratio:

Mole ratio: 5 mol O₂ / 2 mol C₂H₂

We can use this ratio to convert moles of C₂H₂ to moles of O₂:

Moles of O₂ = 33.00 mol C₂H₂ × (5 mol O₂ / 2 mol C₂H₂) = 82.50 mol O₂

This step is the heart of stoichiometry, where we use the mole ratio derived from the balanced chemical equation to translate the quantity of one substance into the corresponding quantity of another substance. It highlights the predictive power of stoichiometry, allowing us to determine the amount of oxygen needed based solely on the amount of acetylene we start with. The balanced equation acts as our guide, ensuring we maintain the correct proportions of reactants according to the reaction's stoichiometry.

Step 3: Convert Moles of Oxygen to Grams

We're almost there! Now we need to convert moles of O₂ to grams. We'll use the molar mass of oxygen (O₂). The atomic mass of oxygen (O) is approximately 16.00 g/mol.

Molar mass of O₂ = 2 × 16.00 g/mol = 32.00 g/mol

Now we convert moles to grams:

Grams of O₂ = 82.50 mol × 32.00 g/mol = 2640.0 g

And there you have it! We've calculated that 2640.0 grams of oxygen are required to react completely with 859.0 grams of acetylene. This final conversion brings us back to the macroscopic world, expressing the amount of oxygen in grams, a unit we can easily measure in the lab. By converting moles back to grams, we've completed the stoichiometric cycle, starting with a mass measurement, moving through the mole concept, and arriving at the mass of another substance involved in the reaction.

Final Answer

So, the answer is 2640.0 grams of oxygen are needed. Stoichiometry can seem daunting at first, but with practice, it becomes a powerful tool for understanding and predicting chemical reactions.

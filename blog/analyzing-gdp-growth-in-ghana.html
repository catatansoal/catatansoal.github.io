Analyzing GDP Growth In Ghana A Calculus Example

Jul 14, 2025 by ADMIN 49 views
Iklan Headers

In this article, we're diving into a fascinating example of how mathematical functions can be used to model economic growth. We'll be looking at the Gross Domestic Product (GDP) of a country, specifically Ghana, and how it changed over time. The example we'll be dissecting is represented by a quadratic function, which provides a great way to understand the rate of change and percentage rate of change in GDP. So, let's get started and explore the dynamics of Ghana's economic growth between 1998 and 2008!

Problem Statement

The gross domestic product (GDP) of Ghana is modeled by the function:

N(t)=t2+5t+106N(t) = t^2 + 5t + 106 N(t)=t2+5t+106

in billion Ghana cedis, where t represents the number of years after 1998.

We aim to determine two key aspects of Ghana's GDP during this period:

(a) Rate of Change in 2008: At what rate was the GDP changing with respect to time in 2008?

(b) Percentage Rate of Change in 2008: At what percentage rate was the GDP changing in 2008?

Solution

(a) Rate of Change in 2008

To figure out how fast the GDP was changing, we need to find the derivative of our GDP function, $N(t)$. Think of the derivative as the speedometer for our GDP – it tells us how quickly the GDP is increasing or decreasing at any given moment. Finding the derivative is like zooming in on the curve of the GDP function until it looks like a straight line, and the slope of that line is the rate of change.

First things first, let's find the derivative of N(t):

To calculate the derivative, we'll apply the power rule, which is a fundamental concept in calculus. The power rule states that if you have a term of the form $ax^n$, its derivative is $n*ax^{n-1}$. This rule helps us find the rate of change for each term in our GDP function. We'll apply it term by term, making sure we account for the constants and coefficients.

N′(t)=ddt(t2+5t+106)N'(t) = \frac{d}{dt} (t^2 + 5t + 106) N′(t)=dtd​(t2+5t+106)

Applying the power rule to each term:

  • For $t^2$, the derivative is $2t$.
  • For $5t$, the derivative is $5$.
  • For the constant $106$, the derivative is $0$ because constants don't change.

Combining these, we get:

N′(t)=2t+5N'(t) = 2t + 5 N′(t)=2t+5

This new function, $N'(t)$, tells us the instantaneous rate of change of the GDP at any time t. It's like having a formula that tells us how much the GDP is increasing or decreasing at any specific year after 1998.

Now, let's figure out the value of t for the year 2008:

Since t represents the number of years after 1998, for the year 2008, we have:

t=2008−1998=10t = 2008 - 1998 = 10 t=2008−1998=10

So, t = 10 corresponds to the year 2008. This means we'll plug 10 into our derivative function to find the rate of change in that specific year. It's like taking a snapshot of the GDP's speed at that particular moment in time.

Substitute t = 10 into N'(t):

To find the rate of change in 2008, we substitute t = 10 into the derivative function $N'(t)$: This gives us the rate at which the GDP was changing in 2008. It's a crucial step in understanding the economic dynamics of that year, providing a clear picture of whether the economy was accelerating or decelerating.

N′(10)=2(10)+5=25N'(10) = 2(10) + 5 = 25 N′(10)=2(10)+5=25

So, in 2008, the GDP was changing at a rate of 25 billion Ghana cedis per year.

This means that, in 2008, Ghana's GDP was increasing by 25 billion cedis each year. This is a significant piece of information, as it tells us not just the total GDP, but the speed at which it was growing. It's like knowing not just how far a car has traveled, but also how fast it's moving at a particular moment.

(b) Percentage Rate of Change in 2008

The percentage rate of change gives us a relative measure of how the GDP is changing. It tells us the rate of change as a percentage of the actual GDP. This is often more informative than the absolute rate of change because it puts the change in perspective relative to the size of the economy. A change of 25 billion cedis might seem large, but its significance depends on the overall size of the GDP.

To find the percentage rate of change, we use the formula:

Percentage Rate of Change=N′(t)N(t)×100\text{Percentage Rate of Change} = \frac{N'(t)}{N(t)} \times 100 Percentage Rate of Change=N(t)N′(t)​×100

This formula essentially compares the rate of change (N'(t)) to the GDP itself (N(t)), giving us a percentage that represents the relative growth or decline. It's like comparing the speed of a car to the distance it has already traveled – it tells us how quickly the car is covering ground relative to its current position.

We already know N'(10) = 25. Now, we need to find N(10):

To calculate N(10), we substitute t = 10 into the original GDP function, $N(t) = t^2 + 5t + 106$. This will give us the total GDP in the year 2008, which we need to calculate the percentage rate of change. It's like finding out the total distance traveled by the car, so we can compare it to its current speed.

N(10)=(10)2+5(10)+106N(10) = (10)^2 + 5(10) + 106 N(10)=(10)2+5(10)+106

N(10)=100+50+106N(10) = 100 + 50 + 106 N(10)=100+50+106

N(10)=256N(10) = 256 N(10)=256

So, in 2008, the GDP was 256 billion Ghana cedis. This is the baseline against which we'll measure the change. It's like knowing the total distance the car has traveled before we can assess how fast it's moving relative to that distance.

Now, we can calculate the percentage rate of change:

Using the formula for the percentage rate of change:

Percentage Rate of Change=N′(10)N(10)×100\text{Percentage Rate of Change} = \frac{N'(10)}{N(10)} \times 100 Percentage Rate of Change=N(10)N′(10)​×100

Percentage Rate of Change=25256×100\text{Percentage Rate of Change} = \frac{25}{256} \times 100 Percentage Rate of Change=25625​×100

Percentage Rate of Change≈9.77%\text{Percentage Rate of Change} \approx 9.77 \% Percentage Rate of Change≈9.77%

Therefore, in 2008, the GDP was changing at a percentage rate of approximately 9.77%.

This means that Ghana's economy grew by almost 10% in 2008, relative to its total size. This is a significant growth rate, indicating a healthy and expanding economy. The percentage rate of change gives us a clear picture of the economic momentum, showing how the economy is performing relative to its own scale.

Conclusion

In summary, by applying calculus to our GDP function, we've discovered some valuable insights into Ghana's economic performance between 1998 and 2008. We found that in 2008:

  • The GDP was increasing at a rate of 25 billion Ghana cedis per year.
  • The GDP was growing at a percentage rate of approximately 9.77%.

These figures paint a picture of a growing economy, with a substantial increase in GDP and a healthy growth rate. Understanding these rates of change is crucial for policymakers and economists, as it allows them to assess the effectiveness of economic policies and make informed decisions for the future. By analyzing these mathematical models, we can gain a deeper understanding of the economic forces shaping a country's development.

This example demonstrates how mathematical models can provide valuable insights into real-world phenomena like economic growth. By using calculus to analyze the rate of change and percentage rate of change, we can gain a more nuanced understanding of the dynamics at play. So, next time you hear about GDP figures, remember that there's a whole world of mathematical analysis behind those numbers!

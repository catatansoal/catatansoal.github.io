Calculate Determinant With Reduced Row Echelon Form

Jul 14, 2025 by ADMIN 52 views

Hey guys! Today, we're diving deep into the fascinating world of matrices, specifically focusing on how to use the reduced row echelon form to calculate determinants. We'll be tackling a problem involving a given matrix and exploring the properties of determinants when a matrix is scaled and inverted. So, buckle up and let's get started!

The Matrix at Hand

We're given the following matrix, which we'll call A:

A = 
\begin{bmatrix}
3 & 1 & -2 \\
2 & 1 & -1 \\
-1 & 0 & -2
\end{bmatrix}

Our mission, should we choose to accept it (and we do!), is to use the reduced row echelon form (RREF) to determine:

(a) det(-2/3 A)

(b) det((-2/3 A)^-1)

Let's break down each step and understand the concepts involved. Determinant of a matrix is a special number that can be computed from the elements of a square matrix. The determinant of a matrix A is often denoted as det(A) or |A|. It provides valuable information about the matrix, such as whether the matrix is invertible and the volume scaling factor of the linear transformation described by the matrix.

Reduced Row Echelon Form (RREF) Explained

Before we jump into the calculations, let's quickly recap what the reduced row echelon form is all about. The reduced row echelon form is a simplified form of a matrix obtained through a series of elementary row operations. A matrix is in RREF if it satisfies the following conditions:

  1. All rows consisting entirely of zeros are at the bottom.
  2. The leading entry (the first non-zero entry) of each non-zero row is 1.
  3. The leading 1 in each non-zero row is to the right of the leading 1 in the row above it.
  4. Each column containing a leading 1 has all other entries equal to 0.

The process of transforming a matrix into RREF involves elementary row operations, which include:

  • Swapping two rows.
  • Multiplying a row by a non-zero scalar.
  • Adding a multiple of one row to another row.

The RREF is crucial because it simplifies the matrix while preserving its essential properties, making it easier to solve systems of linear equations and, in our case, calculate determinants.

Finding the RREF of Matrix A

Okay, let's get our hands dirty and find the RREF of matrix A. We'll perform elementary row operations step-by-step:

  1. Start with the given matrix:

    A = 
\begin{bmatrix}
3 & 1 & -2 \\
2 & 1 & -1 \\
-1 & 0 & -2
\end{bmatrix}

  2. Swap row 1 and row 3 to get a -1 in the top-left position:

    \begin{bmatrix}
-1 & 0 & -2 \\
2 & 1 & -1 \\
3 & 1 & -2
\end{bmatrix}

  3. Multiply row 1 by -1 to get a leading 1:

    \begin{bmatrix}
1 & 0 & 2 \\
2 & 1 & -1 \\
3 & 1 & -2
\end{bmatrix}

  4. Subtract 2 times row 1 from row 2 to get a 0 below the leading 1 in row 1:

    \begin{bmatrix}
1 & 0 & 2 \\
0 & 1 & -5 \\
3 & 1 & -2
\end{bmatrix}

  5. Subtract 3 times row 1 from row 3 to get a 0 below the leading 1 in row 1:

    \begin{bmatrix}
1 & 0 & 2 \\
0 & 1 & -5 \\
0 & 1 & -8
\end{bmatrix}

  6. Subtract row 2 from row 3 to get a 0 below the leading 1 in row 2:

    \begin{bmatrix}
1 & 0 & 2 \\
0 & 1 & -5 \\
0 & 0 & -3
\end{bmatrix}

  7. Multiply row 3 by -1/3 to get a leading 1 in row 3:

    \begin{bmatrix}
1 & 0 & 2 \\
0 & 1 & -5 \\
0 & 0 & 1
\end{bmatrix}

  8. Add 5 times row 3 to row 2 to get a 0 above the leading 1 in row 3:

    \begin{bmatrix}
1 & 0 & 2 \\
0 & 1 & 0 \\
0 & 0 & 1
\end{bmatrix}

  9. Subtract 2 times row 3 from row 1 to get a 0 above the leading 1 in row 3:

    \begin{bmatrix}
1 & 0 & 0 \\
0 & 1 & 0 \\
0 & 0 & 1
\end{bmatrix}

So, the RREF of matrix A is the identity matrix:

I = 
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 0 & 0 \\
0 & 1 & 0 \\
0 & 0 & 1
\end{bmatrix}

Calculating det(A)

Now that we have the RREF, we can calculate the determinant of A. Remember that elementary row operations affect the determinant in specific ways:

  • Swapping two rows changes the sign of the determinant.
  • Multiplying a row by a scalar multiplies the determinant by the same scalar.
  • Adding a multiple of one row to another row does not change the determinant.

We performed one row swap (step 2) and multiplied one row by -1 (step 3) and another row by -1/3 (step 7). Therefore, to get the original determinant, we need to reverse these operations. The determinant of the identity matrix is 1. We have reversed one row swap so we changed the sign from positive to negative and have multiplied by -1, and then multiplied by -3. So:

det(A) = 1 * (-1) * (-1) * (-3) = -3

So, det(A) = -3.

Part (a): Calculating det(-2/3 A)

Now, let's tackle the first part of our mission: finding det(-2/3 A). We're scaling matrix A by -2/3. A crucial property of determinants comes into play here: If A is an n x n matrix and c is a scalar, then det(cA) = c^n det(A).

In our case, A is a 3 x 3 matrix, and c = -2/3. So,

det(-2/3 A) = (-2/3)^3 det(A)

We already know that det(A) = -3, so:

det(-2/3 A) = (-8/27) * (-3) = 8/9

Therefore, det(-2/3 A) = 8/9.

Part (b): Calculating det((-2/3 A)^-1)

Now for the final challenge: finding det((-2/3 A)^-1). This involves the determinant of the inverse of a scaled matrix. Another essential property of determinants comes to our aid: If A is an invertible matrix, then det(A^-1) = 1/det(A).

We already know that det(-2/3 A) = 8/9. Therefore,

det((-2/3 A)^-1) = 1 / det(-2/3 A) = 1 / (8/9) = 9/8

So, det((-2/3 A)^-1) = 9/8.

Mission Accomplished!

We've successfully navigated the world of matrices, RREF, and determinants. We calculated det(-2/3 A) and det((-2/3 A)^-1) using the properties of determinants and the RREF. Remember, guys, understanding these concepts opens doors to more advanced topics in linear algebra and its applications in various fields, from computer graphics to data analysis.

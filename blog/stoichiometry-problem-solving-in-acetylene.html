Stoichiometry Problem Solving In Acetylene Combustion Reaction

Jul 13, 2025 by ADMIN 63 views

Hey guys! Today, we're diving deep into a fascinating combustion reaction: the burning of acetylene (C2H2C_2H_2C2​H2​). This reaction is not only scientifically intriguing but also has practical applications, like in welding torches. We'll break down a stoichiometry problem step by step, making it super easy to understand. So, let's get started and unravel the secrets of this fiery process!

The Combustion Reaction of Acetylene

The balanced chemical equation for the combustion of acetylene is:

2C2H2(g)+5O2(g)→4CO2(g)+2H2O(g)2 C_2 H_2(g) + 5 O_2(g) \rightarrow 4 CO_2(g) + 2 H_2O(g)2C2​H2​(g)+5O2​(g)→4CO2​(g)+2H2​O(g)

This equation tells us that 2 moles of acetylene gas (C2H2C_2H_2C2​H2​) react with 5 moles of oxygen gas (O2O_2O2​) to produce 4 moles of carbon dioxide gas (CO2CO_2CO2​) and 2 moles of water vapor (H2OH_2OH2​O). Understanding this balanced equation is crucial because it provides the stoichiometric ratios needed to solve many problems related to this reaction. The coefficients in front of each chemical formula represent the molar ratios. For example, for every 2 moles of acetylene, we need 5 moles of oxygen for complete combustion. This precise ratio is critical; if we have too much or too little of either reactant, it will affect the amount of products formed. Moreover, the physical states of the reactants and products are specified in parentheses: (g) for gas, (l) for liquid, and (s) for solid. In this case, all reactants and products are in the gaseous state, which simplifies some calculations but is an important detail to note for a comprehensive understanding of the reaction. Remembering these fundamentals will help us tackle more complex stoichiometry questions later on.

Problem Statement

We have an acetylene tank containing 37.0 moles of C2H2C_2H_2C2​H2​ and an oxygen tank containing 81.0 moles of O2O_2O2​. The big question is: what's the limiting reactant in this scenario? And how much of each product (CO2CO_2CO2​ and H2OH_2OH2​O) will be formed? To nail this, we need to use the balanced equation and stoichiometry like true chemistry rockstars. It’s like baking a cake; if you don’t have enough of one ingredient, you can’t make the full recipe, right? The same principle applies here. The limiting reactant is the ingredient—or in this case, the chemical—that runs out first, dictating the maximum amount of product that can be formed. The other reactant is in excess, meaning there's more than enough to react completely with the limiting reactant. So, our mission is to figure out which tank, acetylene or oxygen, will be "empty" first and then calculate the yield of our products based on that.

Identifying the Limiting Reactant

Okay, let's roll up our sleeves and figure out the limiting reactant. There are a couple of ways we can tackle this, but we'll go with a method that's super clear and straightforward. First, we'll calculate how much oxygen we need to react completely with the acetylene we have. Then, we'll see if we actually have that much oxygen. If not, then acetylene is in excess, and oxygen is our limiting reactant. If we have more than enough oxygen, then acetylene is the limiting reactant. Using the balanced equation, we know that 2 moles of C2H2C_2H_2C2​H2​ react with 5 moles of O2O_2O2​. So, we can set up a ratio to find out how many moles of O2O_2O2​ are required for 37.0 moles of C2H2C_2H_2C2​H2​:

(37.0 mol C2H2)×(5 mol O22 mol C2H2)=92.5 mol O2(37.0 \text{ mol } C_2H_2) \times (\frac{5 \text{ mol } O_2}{2 \text{ mol } C_2H_2}) = 92.5 \text{ mol } O_2(37.0 mol C2​H2​)×(2 mol C2​H2​5 mol O2​​)=92.5 mol O2​

This calculation shows us that we need 92.5 moles of O2O_2O2​ to react completely with 37.0 moles of C2H2C_2H_2C2​H2​. But hold on! We only have 81.0 moles of O2O_2O2​ in our tank. This means we don't have enough oxygen to react with all the acetylene. So, drumroll, please... oxygen (O2O_2O2​) is our limiting reactant. Acetylene (C2H2C_2H_2C2​H2​) is in excess, which means some of it will be left over after the reaction is complete. Knowing this is crucial because the amount of products we can form is limited by the amount of oxygen we have, not the amount of acetylene.

Calculating the Amount of Products Formed

Now that we've pinpointed O2O_2O2​ as the limiting reactant, we can calculate how much CO2CO_2CO2​ and H2OH_2OH2​O will be produced. Remember, the amount of product formed is directly determined by the amount of the limiting reactant available. We’ll use the stoichiometric ratios from our balanced equation to convert moles of O2O_2O2​ to moles of each product.

First, let's calculate the moles of CO2CO_2CO2​ formed. According to the balanced equation, 5 moles of O2O_2O2​ produce 4 moles of CO2CO_2CO2​. We can set up the following conversion:

(81.0 mol O2)×(4 mol CO25 mol O2)=64.8 mol CO2(81.0 \text{ mol } O_2) \times (\frac{4 \text{ mol } CO_2}{5 \text{ mol } O_2}) = 64.8 \text{ mol } CO_2(81.0 mol O2​)×(5 mol O2​4 mol CO2​​)=64.8 mol CO2​

So, 81.0 moles of O2O_2O2​ will produce 64.8 moles of CO2CO_2CO2​. Next, we'll calculate the moles of H2OH_2OH2​O formed. From the balanced equation, 5 moles of O2O_2O2​ produce 2 moles of H2OH_2OH2​O. Here’s the calculation:

(81.0 mol O2)×(2 mol H2O5 mol O2)=32.4 mol H2O(81.0 \text{ mol } O_2) \times (\frac{2 \text{ mol } H_2O}{5 \text{ mol } O_2}) = 32.4 \text{ mol } H_2O(81.0 mol O2​)×(5 mol O2​2 mol H2​O​)=32.4 mol H2​O

This means 81.0 moles of O2O_2O2​ will produce 32.4 moles of H2OH_2OH2​O. We’ve successfully calculated the theoretical yield of both products, which is the maximum amount of product that can be formed from the given amounts of reactants. This is super useful information in many real-world scenarios, from industrial chemical production to laboratory experiments.

Summary of Results

Alright, let's recap what we've discovered in this stoichiometry adventure! We started with 37.0 moles of C2H2C_2H_2C2​H2​ and 81.0 moles of O2O_2O2​. After some savvy calculations, we identified that oxygen (O2O_2O2​) is the limiting reactant because we don't have enough of it to react with all the acetylene. This is a critical insight because it dictates the maximum amount of products we can form.

Based on the amount of oxygen available, we then calculated the theoretical yields of our products. We found that 81.0 moles of O2O_2O2​ will produce:

  • 64.8 moles of CO2CO_2CO2​
  • 32.4 moles of H2OH_2OH2​O

These values represent the maximum amount of carbon dioxide and water that can be formed in this reaction, given our initial conditions. Understanding the limiting reactant and theoretical yield is fundamental in chemistry because it allows us to predict and optimize the outcome of chemical reactions. Whether you’re in the lab or in an industrial setting, these calculations are essential for efficient and effective chemical processes.

Our main focus is on decoding these functions, so let's get started! Looking at the table, we can see how each function, namely f(x), g(x), and h(x), responds to different inputs of x. Unraveling f(x): A Linear Function

Let’s start with f(x), it's pretty straightforward. Unraveling this linear function is easier than you might think! The values in the table for f(x) are -14, -7, 0, 7, and 14. Observing these values, we can see a consistent pattern: for every increase of 1 in x, f(x) increases by 7. This consistent rate of change is a hallmark of linear functions. Linear functions are the simplest type of function, forming a straight line when graphed. They are characterized by their constant slope and y-intercept. The general form of a linear function is f(x) = mx + c, where m represents the slope (the rate of change) and c represents the y-intercept (the point where the line crosses the y-axis). To determine the specific equation for f(x), we need to calculate the slope and y-intercept. The slope (m) can be calculated using any two points from the table. Let's take the points (-1, -7) and (0, 0). The slope is calculated as the change in y divided by the change in x: m = (0 - (-7)) / (0 - (-1)) = 7 / 1 = 7. So, the slope of f(x) is 7. The y-intercept (c) is the value of f(x) when x is 0. From the table, we can see that when x is 0, f(x) is 0. Therefore, the y-intercept is 0. Now that we have both the slope (m = 7) and the y-intercept (c = 0), we can write the equation for f(x): f(x) = 7x + 0. Simplifying this, we get f(x) = 7x. This is a simple and elegant linear function. The function increases linearly with x, which means that for every unit increase in x, f(x) increases by 7 units. Linear functions are incredibly important because they model many real-world situations, such as the relationship between distance and time at a constant speed, or the cost of a service that charges a fixed rate per unit. In our case, f(x) could represent something like the total cost of an item if each unit costs $7. Understanding the slope and y-intercept allows us to quickly interpret and apply the function in various contexts. For example, if x represents the number of hours worked, then f(x) represents the total earnings at a rate of $7 per hour. So, unraveling f(x) has given us a clear picture of a simple yet powerful linear relationship. This understanding forms the foundation for exploring more complex functions and mathematical concepts. Keep this in mind as we move forward, and remember, linear functions are all about consistent change!

Cracking g(x): An Exponential Growth Story

Alright, let's move on to g(x). Cracking this exponential growth story is where things get interesting! The values for g(x) in the table are 1/49, 1/7, 1, 7, and 49. These values scream exponential growth, but let's break down exactly how this function works. Exponential functions are characterized by their rapid growth or decay. They have the general form g(x) = a^x, where a is the base and x is the exponent. The base a determines the rate of growth or decay. If a is greater than 1, the function represents exponential growth, and if a is between 0 and 1, it represents exponential decay. Looking at the values of g(x), we can see that as x increases, g(x) increases at an accelerating rate. This is a clear indication of exponential growth. To determine the base a for g(x), we can look for a consistent multiplicative factor between successive values. Let's take two consecutive points from the table: when x is -1, g(x) is 1/7, and when x is 0, g(x) is 1. The ratio between these values is 1 / (1/7) = 7. This suggests that the base a is 7. We can verify this by checking other points. When x is 1, g(x) is 7, and when x is 2, g(x) is 49. The ratio between these values is 49 / 7 = 7, confirming our suspicion. Therefore, the base of the exponential function is 7. Now we can write the equation for g(x) as g(x) = 7^x. This equation tells us that g(x) is 7 raised to the power of x. Exponential functions like this one are incredibly powerful in modeling real-world phenomena such as population growth, compound interest, and radioactive decay. For example, if we were modeling the growth of a bacterial colony that doubles every hour, we could use an exponential function to predict the population size at any given time. The rapid growth characteristic of exponential functions can lead to dramatic changes over time, which is why they are so important in understanding and predicting various trends. Cracking g(x) reveals a classic exponential growth pattern. This function is a great example of how simple mathematical expressions can describe complex and rapidly changing phenomena. Keep the power of exponents in mind as we explore the next function, and remember, exponential growth is all about rapid change!

Deciphering h(x): A Quadratic Mystery

Finally, let's tackle h(x). Deciphering this quadratic mystery requires a bit more finesse. The values for h(x) in the table are -28, -7, 0, -7, and -28. This set of values doesn't fit the linear or exponential pattern we've seen so far. The symmetry in the values—decreasing to a minimum and then increasing—hints at a quadratic function. Quadratic functions are characterized by their parabolic shape. They have the general form h(x) = ax^2 + bx + c, where a, b, and c are constants. The coefficient a determines whether the parabola opens upwards (if a is positive) or downwards (if a is negative). The values of b and c affect the position and shape of the parabola. Looking at the values of h(x), we notice that they decrease from -28 to -7, reach a minimum at 0, and then increase back to -7 and -28. This symmetry around x = 0 suggests that the vertex of the parabola is at the origin (0, 0). Additionally, the fact that the values are negative and decrease from both sides indicates that the parabola opens downwards, meaning that a is negative. To determine the specific equation for h(x), we can use the points from the table. Since the vertex is at (0, 0), the c term in the quadratic equation is 0. This simplifies our equation to h(x) = ax^2 + bx. Now, we can use another point from the table to find a and b. Let's use the point (1, -7). Plugging these values into our equation, we get -7 = a(1)^2 + b(1), which simplifies to -7 = a + b. Next, let’s use the point (-1, -7). Plugging these values into our equation, we get -7 = a(-1)^2 + b(-1), which simplifies to -7 = a - b. Now we have a system of two equations with two variables:

  1. -7 = a + b
  2. -7 = a - b

Adding these two equations, we get -14 = 2a, which gives us a = -7. Now, plugging a = -7 into the first equation, we get -7 = -7 + b, which gives us b = 0. So, we have a = -7 and b = 0. Plugging these values back into the general quadratic equation, we get h(x) = -7x^2 + 0x + 0, which simplifies to h(x) = -7x^2. This equation represents a parabola that opens downwards, with its vertex at the origin. The negative coefficient -7 indicates that the parabola is reflected across the x-axis and stretched vertically. Deciphering h(x) has revealed a classic quadratic relationship with symmetry and a downward-opening parabolic shape. Quadratic functions are essential in many areas of science and engineering, modeling phenomena such as projectile motion and the shape of suspension cables in bridges. Keep the characteristics of parabolas in mind as you encounter different functions, and remember, quadratic functions bring curves into the picture!

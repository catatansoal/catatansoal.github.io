Solving 5^x * 25^x = 1/5 A Step-by-Step Guide

Hey everyone! Today, we're diving into an exciting math problem that involves exponential equations. Don't worry if that sounds intimidating; we're going to break it down step-by-step so it's super easy to understand. We will take on the challenge to solve the equation 5^x * 25^x = 1/5. This is a fantastic example that combines exponent rules and algebraic manipulation. So, grab your thinking caps, and let's get started!

Understanding the Basics of Exponential Equations

Before we jump into the solution, let's quickly recap what exponential equations are all about. An exponential equation is essentially an equation where the variable appears in the exponent. For example, 2^x = 8 is an exponential equation. The key to solving these equations lies in manipulating them so that we can compare exponents directly. This often involves expressing all terms with the same base. This is one of the most important rules to keep in mind when approaching exponential equations. Identifying the common base early on can drastically simplify the problem. Moreover, understanding the properties of exponents, like the product of powers rule (a^(m) * a^(n) = a^(m+n)) and the power of a power rule ((a(m))(n) = a^(mn)), is crucial. These rules act as tools in our mathematical toolbox, enabling us to rewrite and simplify complex expressions. Recognizing these patterns and applying the appropriate rules will make solving exponential equations feel less like a chore and more like solving a puzzle. Keep practicing and these concepts will become second nature, allowing you to tackle more challenging problems with confidence. Don't be afraid to experiment with different approaches, and remember, the journey of solving a math problem is just as valuable as finding the solution itself. Embracing the challenge and developing your problem-solving skills will benefit you not only in mathematics but also in various aspects of life. So, let's continue to explore the world of exponential equations and unlock the beauty of mathematics together.

Rewriting the Equation with a Common Base

Our problem is 5^x * 25^x = 1/5. The first thing we need to do is rewrite the equation so that all terms have the same base. Notice that 25 can be expressed as 5 squared (5^2), and 1/5 can be written as 5 to the power of -1 (5^-1). This is a crucial step because having a common base allows us to directly compare the exponents. We know that 25 is 5^2, so 25^x becomes (52)x. Using the power of a power rule, which states that (am)n = a^(m*n), we can simplify this to 5^(2x). Now, let's tackle 1/5. This is a classic example of a negative exponent. Remember, a^(-n) is the same as 1/a^n. So, 1/5 is equivalent to 5^(-1). Rewriting the terms in this way sets the stage for simplifying the original equation. By expressing all terms with the base of 5, we've transformed the problem into a form where we can apply exponent rules to combine and compare terms effectively. This step is not just about rewriting; it's about strategically simplifying the problem to reveal its underlying structure, making it easier to solve. Keep an eye out for these opportunities to find common bases in exponential equations, as they are often the key to unlocking the solution. With this foundation in place, we can now move on to the next step: simplifying the equation and solving for x. So, let's keep pushing forward and see how this all comes together!

Applying Exponent Rules to Simplify

Now our equation looks like this: 5^x * 5^(2x) = 5^(-1). The next step is to simplify the left side of the equation. Remember the rule for multiplying exponents with the same base: a^m * a^n = a^(m+n). Applying this rule, we can combine 5^x and 5^(2x). When you multiply exponential terms with the same base, you add the exponents. This is a fundamental rule in dealing with exponents, and it's the key to simplifying expressions like this. Think of it as combining like terms, but instead of adding coefficients, we're adding exponents. So, 5^x * 5^(2x) becomes 5^(x + 2x), which simplifies further to 5^(3x). This step is crucial because it consolidates the terms on the left side of the equation into a single exponential expression. By using this exponent rule, we've transformed the multiplication of two exponential terms into a single term, making the equation much easier to handle. This simplification is not just about making the equation look cleaner; it's about changing its structure to reveal the underlying relationship between the variables. Now, our equation is much more manageable: 5^(3x) = 5^(-1). We've successfully reduced the complexity of the equation by applying a simple yet powerful exponent rule. With this streamlined equation, we're now in a prime position to solve for x. The next step involves a straightforward comparison of exponents, which will lead us directly to the solution. So, let's move forward and see how this simplified form helps us crack the code!

Solving for x

We've simplified the equation to 5^(3x) = 5^(-1). Here's the exciting part: If the bases are the same, then the exponents must be equal. This is a fundamental principle in solving exponential equations. If you have an equation in the form a^m = a^n, where 'a' is the same base, then it directly implies that m = n. This principle allows us to bypass the exponential part and focus solely on the exponents. In our case, the base is 5 on both sides of the equation. Therefore, we can confidently equate the exponents: 3x = -1. Now, we have a simple linear equation to solve for x. This is where our basic algebra skills come into play. To isolate x, we need to divide both sides of the equation by 3. So, we get x = -1/3. And there you have it! We've successfully solved for x. This step highlights the elegance of mathematics, where complex problems can be reduced to simple equations through strategic simplification and the application of key principles. The solution x = -1/3 is not just a number; it's the value that satisfies the original exponential equation, making both sides equal. This process of solving for x underscores the power of algebraic manipulation and the importance of understanding the properties of exponents. With this solution in hand, we've completed the puzzle. But the journey doesn't end here. It's essential to check our answer to ensure it's correct. So, let's move on to the final step and verify our solution.

Checking the Solution

To make sure our solution is correct, let's plug x = -1/3 back into the original equation: 5^x * 25^x = 1/5. Substituting x = -1/3, we get 5^(-1/3) * 25^(-1/3) = 1/5. Now, let's simplify the left side of the equation. Remember that 25 is 5^2, so 25^(-1/3) is (52)(-1/3), which simplifies to 5^(-2/3) using the power of a power rule. So, our equation now looks like this: 5^(-1/3) * 5^(-2/3) = 1/5. Now, we apply the rule for multiplying exponents with the same base: a^m * a^n = a^(m+n). Adding the exponents, we get 5^(-1/3 + -2/3) = 5^(-3/3), which simplifies to 5^(-1). And guess what? 5^(-1) is indeed equal to 1/5! This verification is a crucial step in the problem-solving process. It confirms that our solution is not just mathematically derived but also practically correct, satisfying the original conditions of the equation. By plugging the solution back into the original equation, we're essentially testing its validity, ensuring that it holds true under the given constraints. This step reinforces the importance of accuracy and attention to detail in mathematics. It's not enough to just find a solution; we must also verify that it's the correct one. This process of checking not only confirms the answer but also deepens our understanding of the equation and the concepts involved. So, with this final check, we can confidently say that x = -1/3 is the correct solution to the equation 5^x * 25^x = 1/5. We've successfully navigated through the problem, applying exponent rules, algebraic manipulation, and careful verification to arrive at the solution. Great job, everyone!

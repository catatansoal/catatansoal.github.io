Exponential Functions Analysis F(x) G(x) And H(x)

Hey guys! Let's dive into the fascinating world of exponential functions. We're going to break down three functions in particular and see what makes them tick. We'll be looking at their ranges, their relationships, and how they behave. So, buckle up and get ready for some mathematical fun!

Unveiling the Functions: f(x), g(x), and h(x)

Before we start dissecting, let's introduce our players. We have three exponential functions: f(x), g(x), and h(x). They're defined as follows:

  • f(x) = -6/11 * (11/2)^x
  • g(x) = 6/11 * (11/2)^(-x)
  • h(x) = -6/11 * (11/2)^(-x)

These functions might look a bit intimidating at first glance, but don't worry! We'll break them down piece by piece. The key thing to notice is that they all involve the exponential term (11/2) raised to some power of x. This is what makes them exponential functions. We also have some constants multiplying these exponential terms, which will affect the functions' behavior.

Now, when we analyze exponential functions, understanding the base is crucial. In our case, the base is 11/2, which is greater than 1. This means that as x increases, (11/2)^x will also increase, leading to exponential growth. Conversely, as x decreases (becomes more negative), (11/2)^x will approach zero. The negative exponents in g(x) and h(x) introduce a twist, as (11/2)^(-x) is the same as (2/11)^x. Since 2/11 is less than 1, these functions will exhibit exponential decay as x increases.

The constants multiplying the exponential terms also play a significant role. The 6/11 factor simply scales the function vertically. However, the negative signs in f(x) and h(x) have a more dramatic effect: they flip the functions over the x-axis. This means that if (11/2)^x is positive, -6/11 * (11/2)^x will be negative, and vice versa. Understanding these basic components allows us to predict the general behavior of each function. For instance, we can anticipate that f(x) will be a decreasing function (due to the negative sign and the base being greater than 1) and that its range will be negative. Similarly, g(x) will be a decreasing function approaching zero, and h(x) will be an increasing function approaching zero. These initial observations set the stage for a more in-depth exploration of their ranges and relationships.

Decoding the Range of Exponential Functions

The range of a function refers to the set of all possible output values (y-values). To determine the range of our exponential functions, we need to consider how they behave as x takes on different values. Let's start with f(x) = -6/11 * (11/2)^x. As we discussed earlier, (11/2)^x will always be positive for any real number x. When we multiply this positive value by the negative constant -6/11, we get a negative result. This tells us that f(x) will always be negative. Furthermore, as x becomes very large, (11/2)^x grows without bound, making f(x) infinitely negative. On the other hand, as x becomes very negative, (11/2)^x approaches zero, so f(x) approaches zero as well. However, f(x) will never actually reach zero because (11/2)^x will never be exactly zero. Therefore, the range of f(x) is all negative real numbers, which we can write as (-∞, 0).

Now, let's consider g(x) = 6/11 * (11/2)^(-x). As we noted before, (11/2)^(-x) is equivalent to (2/11)^x. Since 2/11 is between 0 and 1, (2/11)^x will decrease as x increases, approaching zero. However, it will always be positive. Multiplying this positive value by the positive constant 6/11 keeps the result positive. As x becomes very negative, (2/11)^x grows without bound, so g(x) also becomes infinitely large. Therefore, the range of g(x) is all positive real numbers, which we can write as (0, ∞).

Finally, let's analyze h(x) = -6/11 * (11/2)^(-x). We know that (11/2)^(-x), or (2/11)^x, is always positive. But we're multiplying it by the negative constant -6/11, so h(x) will always be negative. As x becomes very large, (2/11)^x approaches zero, and so does h(x). However, h(x) will never actually reach zero. As x becomes very negative, (2/11)^x grows without bound, making h(x) infinitely negative. Therefore, the range of h(x) is all negative real numbers, which we can write as (-∞, 0). Understanding how these individual components interact is essential for determining the range and overall behavior of these exponential functions.

Uncovering the Relationships Between f(x), g(x), and h(x)

Now that we've explored the ranges of our functions, let's dive into the connections between them. This is where things get really interesting! By carefully comparing the function definitions, we can uncover some fascinating relationships.

First, let's compare f(x) and h(x). We have:

  • f(x) = -6/11 * (11/2)^x
  • h(x) = -6/11 * (11/2)^(-x)

The key difference here is the exponent of (11/2). In f(x), it's x, and in h(x), it's -x. What does this mean? Well, remember that (11/2)^(-x) is the same as (2/11)^x. So, h(x) is essentially a reflection of f(x) across the y-axis. This is because replacing x with -x in a function's formula always results in a reflection about the y-axis. Furthermore, both f(x) and h(x) have the same constant factor -6/11, which means they share the same vertical stretch and direction (they're both negative).

Now, let's compare g(x) and h(x). We have:

  • g(x) = 6/11 * (11/2)^(-x)
  • h(x) = -6/11 * (11/2)^(-x)

Notice that the exponential term (11/2)^(-x) is the same in both functions. The only difference is the constant factor: 6/11 in g(x) and -6/11 in h(x). This negative sign is crucial! It tells us that h(x) is simply a reflection of g(x) across the x-axis. Multiplying a function by -1 always flips its graph vertically. In essence, the graph of h(x) is the mirror image of the graph of g(x) with the x-axis as the mirror.

Finally, let's consider the relationship between f(x) and g(x). We have:

  • f(x) = -6/11 * (11/2)^x
  • g(x) = 6/11 * (11/2)^(-x)

This is a bit more complex, but we can break it down. First, we know that g(x) has (11/2)^(-x), which is the same as (2/11)^x. So, g(x) represents exponential decay, while f(x) represents exponential growth (due to the (11/2)^x term). Additionally, f(x) is negative, while g(x) is positive. Combining these observations, we can see that f(x) and g(x) are reflections of each other across both the x-axis and the y-axis. If we reflect f(x) across the y-axis, we get a function with (11/2)^(-x), and if we then reflect that across the x-axis, we flip the sign, resulting in g(x).

Understanding these relationships between functions helps us visualize their graphs and predict their behavior. It's like having a secret code to unlock the mysteries of exponential functions!

Putting it All Together: A Comprehensive Comparison

Alright, guys, we've dissected each function individually and explored the relationships between them. Now, let's bring it all together and create a comprehensive comparison. This will help solidify our understanding and highlight the key differences and similarities.

To effectively compare these functions, we can consider several aspects:

  • Growth/Decay: We've already established that f(x) represents exponential decay and g(x) represents exponential growth. This is determined by whether the base of the exponential term is greater than 1 (growth) or between 0 and 1 (decay).
  • Range: We found that the ranges of f(x) and h(x) are both (-∞, 0), meaning they can take on any negative real value. The range of g(x) is (0, ∞), meaning it can take on any positive real value.
  • Reflections: We discovered that h(x) is a reflection of g(x) across the x-axis, and f(x) and h(x) are reflections of each other across the y-axis. Also, f(x) and g(x) are reflections of each other across both the x-axis and the y-axis.
  • Asymptotic Behavior: All three functions have a horizontal asymptote at y = 0. This means that as x approaches positive or negative infinity, the function values get closer and closer to 0 but never actually reach it. This is a characteristic feature of exponential functions.

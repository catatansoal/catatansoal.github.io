Understanding the Problem: A Cubic Function Adventure

The Significance of Zeros: Unveiling the Secrets of Functions

The zeros of a function tell us a lot about its behavior. For a cubic function, we can have up to three real zeros. This means the graph can cross the x-axis up to three times. The number and nature of these zeros (whether they are real or complex, distinct or repeated) can help us sketch the graph of the function, determine its intervals of increase and decrease, and identify its local maxima and minima. In practical applications, the zeros might represent equilibrium points in a physical system, break-even points in a business model, or critical values in an optimization problem. Understanding how to find these zeros empowers us to analyze and interpret a wide range of scenarios.

The Challenge of Cubics: Navigating the Complexity

Unlike quadratic equations, which have a straightforward formula (the quadratic formula) for finding their roots, cubic equations don't have such a simple solution. Instead, we often rely on a combination of techniques, including the Rational Root Theorem, synthetic division, and factoring, to find the zeros. The Rational Root Theorem: Our First Clue

The Rational Root Theorem is a powerful tool that helps us narrow down the possible rational roots of a polynomial equation. It states that if a polynomial equation with integer coefficients has a rational root p/q (where p and q are integers with no common factors other than 1), then p must be a factor of the constant term and q must be a factor of the leading coefficient. This might sound like a mouthful, but let's break it down and see how it applies to our specific problem.

In our case, the function is g(x)=x3+19x2+100x+82g(x) = x^3 + 19x^2 + 100x + 82g(x)=x3+19x2+100x+82. The constant term is 82, and the leading coefficient (the coefficient of the x3x^3x3 term) is 1. According to the Rational Root Theorem, any rational root of this equation must be of the form p/q, where p is a factor of 82 and q is a factor of 1.

The Power of Testing: Finding the Right Key

We can test these potential roots by directly substituting them into the function g(x)g(x)g(x) and seeing if the result is zero. However, there's a more efficient method called synthetic division. Synthetic division is a streamlined way to divide a polynomial by a linear factor of the form (x - c). If the remainder of the division is zero, then c is a root of the polynomial. This method not only tells us if a number is a root but also gives us the quotient polynomial, which can be helpful for finding the remaining roots.

Synthetic Division: A Shortcut to the Solution

Synthetic division is a fantastic technique that simplifies polynomial division, especially when dividing by a linear factor. It's a much faster and cleaner method than long division, and it's perfectly suited for our task of testing potential rational roots.

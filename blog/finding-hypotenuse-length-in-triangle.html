Finding Hypotenuse Length In Triangle QRS A Step-by-Step Guide

Jul 14, 2025 by ADMIN 63 views
Iklan Headers

Hey guys! Let's dive into the fascinating world of triangles, specifically triangle QRS. This is a crucial concept in mathematics, and we're going to break it down in a way that's super easy to understand. We'll tackle the challenge of finding the hypotenuse length, which is a common question in geometry and trigonometry. So, let's get started and unlock the secrets of this triangle!

Delving into Triangle QRS

Let's talk about triangle QRS. We know that it's a special type of triangle where the legs, those sides that form the right angle, each measure 10 units. This is key information! When we have a triangle with two sides of equal length, and one of the angles is 90 degrees, we're dealing with a right-angled isosceles triangle. These triangles have some really cool properties that make solving for the missing side (the hypotenuse) a breeze.

Before we jump into the calculations, let's make sure we're all on the same page with some basic triangle terminology. In a right-angled triangle, the two shorter sides that form the right angle are called the legs (or sometimes, the cathetus). The longest side, which is opposite the right angle, is the hypotenuse. The hypotenuse is always the longest side in a right triangle, and it has a special relationship with the legs – a relationship defined by the one and only Pythagorean theorem.

Now, why is this important? Because the information given directly points us towards using the Pythagorean theorem. The theorem states that in a right-angled triangle, the square of the hypotenuse (let's call it 'c') is equal to the sum of the squares of the other two sides (the legs, 'a' and 'b'). Mathematically, it's expressed as: a² + b² = c². This is the magical formula we'll use to find our missing hypotenuse length. Understanding this theorem is fundamental not just for solving this particular problem but for tackling a wide array of geometric challenges.

Applying the Pythagorean Theorem

Now, let's get our hands dirty and apply the Pythagorean Theorem to our triangle QRS. We know that the legs, 'a' and 'b', each have a length of 10 units. So, a = 10 and b = 10. We need to find the length of the hypotenuse, 'c'.

Let's plug these values into our equation: a² + b² = c². Substituting the values, we get 10² + 10² = c². This translates to 100 + 100 = c², which simplifies to 200 = c². We're getting closer to our answer! Remember, we're trying to find 'c', not 'c²'.

To find 'c', we need to take the square root of both sides of the equation. So, √(200) = √(c²). This means c = √200. Now, we could leave our answer like this, but it's often best practice to simplify radicals. Simplifying radicals makes them easier to work with and compare. In math, you often need to express your final answer in its simplest form. Think of it like reducing a fraction to its lowest terms – it's just a cleaner and more conventional way of presenting the result. So, how do we simplify √200?

Simplifying the Radical

Simplifying radicals might sound intimidating, but it's actually a pretty straightforward process. We're essentially looking for perfect square factors within the number under the square root. A perfect square is a number that can be obtained by squaring an integer (like 4, 9, 16, 25, and so on). So, when we look at √200, we're asking ourselves: what's the largest perfect square that divides evenly into 200?

We can break down 200 into its factors: 200 = 100 x 2. Aha! 100 is a perfect square (10 x 10 = 100). So, we can rewrite √200 as √(100 x 2). Now, here's where a handy property of square roots comes in: the square root of a product is equal to the product of the square roots. In other words, √(ab) = √a x √b. Applying this to our problem, we get √(100 x 2) = √100 x √2.

We know that √100 is 10. So, our expression simplifies to 10√2. And that's it! We've simplified the radical. This means that the length of the hypotenuse, c, is 10√2 units. This is a more elegant and simplified way of expressing the answer than just leaving it as √200. This final step of simplification is really important for clarity and precision in your math work.

The Hypotenuse Length: 10√2 Units

Alright, guys, we've cracked the code! After applying the Pythagorean Theorem and simplifying the radical, we've found that the length of the hypotenuse of triangle QRS is 10√2 units. This corresponds to option D in our list of answer choices. So, D is our correct answer!

This problem highlights a crucial concept in geometry: the relationship between the sides of a right-angled triangle. The Pythagorean Theorem is a powerful tool that allows us to calculate the length of any side if we know the lengths of the other two. Mastering this theorem is essential for success in geometry and trigonometry. And remember, simplifying radicals is a vital skill for presenting your answers in the clearest and most concise way.

Wrapping Up and Key Takeaways

So, to recap, we started with a triangle QRS where the legs each measured 10 units. We recognized that this was a right-angled isosceles triangle, which meant we could use the Pythagorean Theorem to find the hypotenuse. We applied the theorem (a² + b² = c²), plugged in our values, and found that c² = 200. Taking the square root of both sides gave us c = √200.

Then, we simplified the radical √200 to get 10√2. This is the length of the hypotenuse. It’s important to remember that simplifying radicals is a common practice in math and helps in expressing your answer in its simplest form. Finally, we identified the correct answer choice as D. 10√2 units.

Remember these key takeaways:

  • The Pythagorean Theorem (a² + b² = c²) is your best friend when dealing with right-angled triangles.
  • Simplifying radicals is an important skill for expressing answers clearly and concisely.
  • Right-angled isosceles triangles have special properties that make them easier to work with.

I hope this explanation has helped you understand how to find the hypotenuse of a triangle using the Pythagorean Theorem. Keep practicing, and you'll become a pro at solving these types of problems! You've got this!

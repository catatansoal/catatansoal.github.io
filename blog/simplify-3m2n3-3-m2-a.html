Understanding the Basics: Exponents and the Power of a Product Rule

Before we jump into the main problem, let's quickly refresh some fundamental concepts about exponents. Exponents are a shorthand way of representing repeated multiplication. For example, $x^3$ means $x \times x \times x$. The number being multiplied (in this case, x) is called the base, and the number indicating how many times the base is multiplied by itself (in this case, 3) is called the exponent or power. Understanding exponents is crucial because they govern how we manipulate and simplify algebraic expressions.

Now, let's talk about the power of a product rule. This rule states that when you have a product raised to a power, you can distribute the power to each factor within the product. Mathematically, it's expressed as: $(ab)^n = a^n b^n$. This rule is going to be our best friend in simplifying the numerator of our expression. Think of it like this: if you have a group of items (like 'a' and 'b') all raised to a certain power, it's the same as raising each item in the group to that power individually. For example, $(2x)^3$ can be simplified to $2^3 x^3$, which equals $8x^3$. This might seem like a small thing, but it's a powerful tool in our algebraic arsenal!

Another crucial rule we'll use is the power of a power rule, which states: $(am)n = a^{m \times n}$. This means that if you have a base raised to a power, and that entire expression is raised to another power, you can simply multiply the exponents. For instance, $(x2)3$ becomes $x^{2 \times 3} = x^6$. This rule helps us simplify expressions where exponents are nested within each other. To make it crystal clear, imagine you're building a tower of exponents. This rule tells you how to combine those layers into a single, simpler exponent. Mastering these exponent rules is like learning the secret language of algebra â€“ it unlocks the ability to decode and simplify complex expressions with ease!

Step-by-Step Simplification of the Expression

We need to distribute the exponent of 3 to each factor inside the parentheses. This means we'll apply the exponent to the constant 3, the variable $m^2$, and the variable $n^3$. Applying the rule, we get: $(3 m^2 n3)3 = 3^3 \times (m2)3 \times (n3)3$. Notice how the exponent '3' outside the parentheses now applies individually to each term inside. Step 2: Simplifying Further Using the Power of a Power Rule

Step 3: Incorporating the Denominator

To simplify this fraction, we need to remember the quotient of powers rule. This rule states that when dividing exponents with the same base, you subtract the exponents $\frac{a^ma^n} = a^{m-n}$. In our case, we have $m^6$ in the numerator and $m^2$ in the denominator. Applying the quotient of powers rule, we subtract the exponents $m^{6-2 = m^4$. The constant 27 and the term $n^9$ remain unchanged, as there are no corresponding terms in the denominator to simplify them with. Therefore, our simplified expression becomes: $27 m^4 n^9$. We've reached the summit! We've successfully simplified the entire expression using our knowledge of exponent rules. Common Mistakes to Avoid When Simplifying Expressions

Simplifying algebraic expressions can sometimes be tricky, and it's easy to stumble if you're not careful. Let's highlight some common pitfalls to help you avoid making mistakes. One frequent error is incorrectly applying the power of a product rule. Remember, the exponent outside the parentheses applies to every factor inside. A common mistake is to forget to apply the exponent to the constant term. For example, in $(3m2)2$, some might incorrectly simplify it to $3m^4$, forgetting to square the 3. The correct simplification is $9m^4$. Always double-check that you've distributed the exponent to each term within the parentheses.

Another common mistake arises when dealing with the quotient of powers rule. It's crucial to remember that you subtract the exponents when dividing terms with the same base. A frequent error is to divide the exponents instead of subtracting them. For instance, $\frac{x5}{x2}$ should be simplified to $x^{5-2} = x^3$, not $\frac{x{5}}{x{2}} = x^{5/2}$. Pay close attention to the operation â€“ subtraction for division, and addition for multiplication of exponents with the same base.

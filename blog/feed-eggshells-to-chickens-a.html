Feed Eggshells To Chickens: A Calcium Boost Guide

Aug 4, 2025 by ADMIN 50 views

Hey there, chicken keepers! Ever wondered if you could put those leftover eggshells to good use? Well, guess what? You totally can! Feeding eggshells back to your chickens is a fantastic way to boost their calcium intake, which is super important for strong eggs and healthy birds. It might sound a bit weird at first, but trust me, it's a natural and effective practice. In this guide, we'll dive deep into why and how to feed eggshells to your feathered friends, ensuring they get all the calcium they need in a safe and tasty way. Let's get cracking!

Why Feed Eggshells to Your Chickens?

So, you might be thinking, "Why bother feeding eggshells to chickens?" That’s a great question! The main reason is calcium. Calcium is an essential nutrient for laying hens because they need a lot of it to produce strong, healthy eggshells. In fact, an eggshell is almost entirely made of calcium carbonate. If your chickens aren't getting enough calcium in their diet, they might start laying eggs with thin or weak shells, or even stop laying altogether. Nobody wants that, right? Feeding them their own eggshells is a brilliant way to recycle this valuable resource and keep your flock in tip-top shape.

The Importance of Calcium for Laying Hens

Let's talk more about why calcium is so crucial. Laying hens require significantly more calcium than non-laying chickens. When a hen is producing eggs, her body pulls calcium from her bones to form the eggshell. If she doesn't have enough calcium in her diet, her body will deplete its own stores, leading to health problems like osteoporosis (weak bones) and reduced egg production. This is why it’s super important to ensure your girls are getting adequate calcium. Calcium deficiency can lead to a whole host of issues, including soft-shelled eggs, reduced egg production, and even behavioral problems like egg-eating. Egg-eating can become a habit if hens are trying to compensate for a calcium deficiency by eating their own eggs. This is definitely a habit you want to avoid, as it can be hard to break once it starts.

Eggshells are a natural and easily digestible form of calcium for chickens. They’re also a sustainable and cost-effective way to supplement your flock's diet. Instead of throwing away your eggshells, you’re essentially turning waste into a valuable resource. This not only benefits your chickens but also reduces your environmental footprint. Plus, it’s a really simple process once you get the hang of it. You’re basically giving your chickens a nutritional boost by recycling something you would otherwise discard. It’s a win-win situation!

Preventing Calcium Deficiency in Your Flock

Making sure your chickens get enough calcium isn't just about feeding them eggshells; it's about ensuring their overall diet is balanced. High-quality layer feed is formulated to provide the necessary nutrients, including calcium, for laying hens. However, supplementing with eggshells can be an extra boost, especially during peak laying seasons when their calcium demands are highest. Think of it as giving your chickens an extra dose of the good stuff when they need it most.

Another way to prevent calcium deficiency is to offer a separate source of calcium, like oyster shells. Oyster shells are another excellent source of calcium and can be provided in a separate feeder, allowing your chickens to eat them as needed. This is known as a “free-choice” feeding method, which means your chickens can regulate their calcium intake based on their individual needs. Some hens might need more calcium than others, so having a separate source available ensures everyone gets what they need. Remember, a happy and healthy flock starts with a balanced diet, and calcium is a key part of that balance.

How to Prepare Eggshells for Your Chickens

Okay, so now you know why feeding eggshells is a great idea. But how do you actually prepare them for your chickens? It's a pretty simple process, but there are a few important steps to follow to ensure you're doing it safely and effectively. The main goals are to sanitize the shells and make them unrecognizable so your chickens don't associate them with fresh eggs. The last thing you want is for your flock to develop a taste for their own eggs!

Step-by-Step Guide to Preparing Eggshells

  1. Collect Your Eggshells: Start by saving your eggshells after you use the eggs for cooking or baking. Rinse them lightly to remove any excess egg white or yolk. You don’t need to scrub them vigorously, just a quick rinse will do. This prevents any bacteria from growing and makes the drying process more effective. Think of it as giving the eggshells a little spa treatment before their transformation!
  2. Dry the Eggshells: Next, you need to dry the eggshells thoroughly. This is a crucial step because drying helps to kill any lingering bacteria and makes the shells brittle enough to crush easily. There are a few ways you can dry them:
    • Oven Method: The most common method is to bake the eggshells in the oven. Spread the rinsed eggshells in a single layer on a baking sheet. Bake them at 250°F (120°C) for about 10-15 minutes. Keep an eye on them to make sure they don't burn. The goal is to dry them out completely, not to cook them. Once they’re dry, they should be brittle and snap easily.
    • Air Dry: If you prefer a more energy-efficient method, you can air dry the eggshells. Place them in a well-ventilated area and let them dry for a few days. This method takes longer but is just as effective. Make sure to spread them out so they dry evenly. A sunny windowsill works great for this!
  3. Crush the Eggshells: Once the eggshells are completely dry, it's time to crush them. This is super important because you don't want your chickens to recognize the eggshells as eggs. If they do, they might start pecking at their own eggs, which is a habit you definitely want to avoid. There are a few ways to crush the eggshells:
    • By Hand: If you have a small flock, you can crush the eggshells by hand. Simply crumble them into small pieces. The smaller the pieces, the better. You want them to look like a coarse powder rather than recognizable shell fragments.
    • Food Processor or Blender: For larger quantities, a food processor or blender can be a lifesaver. Pulse the eggshells until they are finely crushed. Be careful not to over-process them into a fine powder, as this can make them less palatable for your chickens. You want a slightly coarse texture.
    • Mortar and Pestle: If you're feeling old-school, you can use a mortar and pestle to grind the eggshells. This method is a bit more labor-intensive but gives you a lot of control over the size of the pieces.
  4. Store the Crushed Eggshells: Store the crushed eggshells in an airtight container until you're ready to feed them to your chickens. This will keep them dry and prevent them from absorbing moisture. A plastic container or a resealable bag works perfectly.

Why Drying and Crushing are Essential

The drying process is essential for sanitizing the eggshells and killing any potential bacteria, such as Salmonella. Heating the shells in the oven or allowing them to air dry significantly reduces the risk of bacterial contamination. This is super important for the health and safety of your flock. You don’t want to inadvertently introduce any harmful pathogens into their diet.

Crushing the eggshells is equally important for behavioral reasons. If chickens recognize the eggshells as eggs, they may start pecking at and eating their own eggs, leading to a problematic habit that’s difficult to break. By crushing the shells into small, unrecognizable pieces, you’re preventing this behavior from developing. It’s all about making sure they don’t associate the crushed shells with the fresh eggs they lay. Think of it as disguising the eggshells so they don’t look so tempting!

How to Feed Eggshells to Your Chickens

Alright, you've got your eggshells dried and crushed – now what? Feeding them to your chickens is the easy part! There are a couple of ways you can incorporate crushed eggshells into your flock's diet, and we'll cover the most effective methods here. The key is to make sure they're getting the right amount without overdoing it. Too much of a good thing can sometimes be a bad thing, even with calcium!

Mixing Eggshells with Chicken Feed

One of the simplest ways to feed eggshells is by mixing them directly into your chickens' regular feed. This ensures that they're getting a consistent dose of calcium along with their other nutrients. It’s like adding a little calcium boost to their everyday meals. Here’s how you do it:

  1. Measure the Eggshells: A general guideline is to add about 5-10% crushed eggshells to their feed by weight. This means if you have 10 pounds of feed, you would add about 0.5 to 1 pound of crushed eggshells. You don't need to be super precise, but this gives you a good starting point.
  2. Mix Thoroughly: Make sure to mix the crushed eggshells evenly throughout the feed. This ensures that every chicken gets a fair share of the calcium. You can use a large bucket or container to mix it all together.
  3. Serve as Usual: Serve the mixed feed to your chickens as you normally would. They’ll happily peck away at it, getting their calcium fix along with their regular meal.

Mixing eggshells into the feed is a great way to ensure that all your chickens are getting the calcium they need. It’s especially useful if you have a mixed flock with varying calcium requirements. However, it’s important to remember that this method doesn’t allow individual chickens to self-regulate their calcium intake. This is where the next method comes in handy.

Offering Eggshells as a Free-Choice Supplement

Another excellent way to feed eggshells is by offering them as a free-choice supplement. This means providing the crushed eggshells in a separate container, allowing your chickens to eat them as they need them. This method is particularly beneficial because it allows each chicken to regulate their calcium intake based on their individual needs. Some hens may require more calcium than others, especially during peak laying times.

  1. Set Up a Separate Feeder: Use a small container or feeder specifically for the crushed eggshells. This could be a small dish, a spare feeder, or even a DIY container. Just make sure it’s clean and easily accessible for your chickens.
  2. Fill the Feeder with Crushed Eggshells: Fill the container with the crushed eggshells. Make sure the shells are still crushed into small, unrecognizable pieces to prevent egg-eating.
  3. Monitor Consumption: Keep an eye on how much the chickens are eating. If they’re consuming a lot, it could indicate a higher calcium demand. If they’re not eating much, it could mean their calcium needs are being met by their regular feed. This helps you gauge the overall calcium status of your flock.

Offering eggshells as a free-choice supplement is a fantastic way to empower your chickens to manage their own calcium intake. It’s like giving them the option to snack on calcium whenever they feel they need it. This is particularly useful in larger flocks where individual needs can vary greatly.

How Much Eggshell is Too Much?

While calcium is essential, it’s also possible to overdo it. Too much calcium can lead to health problems, such as kidney issues and decreased absorption of other essential nutrients. The key is to find the right balance. A general guideline is that chickens need about 4-5 grams of calcium per day, which is roughly equivalent to the calcium content of one eggshell. However, this can vary based on the hen's size, age, and laying status. If you're mixing eggshells into their feed, aim for that 5-10% ratio we discussed earlier. If you're offering them as a free-choice supplement, let your chickens regulate their own intake.

It's also important to consider the calcium content of your chicken feed. High-quality layer feed is formulated to provide adequate calcium, so you may not need to supplement as much. If you’re unsure, it’s always a good idea to consult with a veterinarian or poultry expert. They can help you assess your flock’s specific needs and develop a feeding plan that works best for them.

Other Sources of Calcium for Chickens

While eggshells are a fantastic source of calcium, they aren't the only option available. It's always good to have a few tricks up your sleeve, especially if you run out of eggshells or want to offer your flock some variety. Plus, having multiple sources of calcium can help ensure that your chickens are getting everything they need, especially during peak laying seasons.

Oyster Shells: A Classic Choice

Oyster shells are a popular and effective calcium supplement for chickens. They are almost pure calcium carbonate, making them an excellent alternative to eggshells. Oyster shells are also very affordable and readily available at most feed stores. They come in a granulated form, which is easy for chickens to eat. Think of them as the classic calcium supplement – reliable and effective.

Offering oyster shells is similar to offering eggshells as a free-choice supplement. You can put them in a separate feeder and let your chickens eat them as needed. This allows them to regulate their calcium intake based on their individual needs. It’s a simple and effective way to ensure your flock has access to plenty of calcium. Many chicken keepers swear by oyster shells as a staple in their flock's diet.

Limestone: Another Great Option

Limestone is another excellent source of calcium for chickens. It’s a naturally occurring sedimentary rock that is rich in calcium carbonate. Like oyster shells, limestone is readily available and relatively inexpensive. It’s often sold as ground limestone or limestone flour, which can be easily mixed into chicken feed.

Limestone is a great option if you prefer to mix calcium into your chickens' feed rather than offering it as a free-choice supplement. You can simply add the appropriate amount of ground limestone to their regular feed, ensuring they get a consistent dose of calcium. Just be sure to mix it thoroughly so that it’s evenly distributed. Limestone is a versatile option that can be used in various feeding strategies.

Commercial Calcium Supplements

If you’re looking for a more precise way to supplement calcium, there are also commercial calcium supplements available. These supplements come in various forms, such as powders, liquids, and pellets. They often contain other essential nutrients in addition to calcium, making them a convenient way to boost your flock's overall health.

Commercial calcium supplements can be particularly useful if you have a flock with specific calcium needs or if you’re dealing with a calcium deficiency. They allow you to administer a precise dose of calcium, ensuring your chickens get exactly what they need. However, they can be more expensive than other options like eggshells, oyster shells, and limestone. It’s important to weigh the cost and benefits to determine the best choice for your flock.

Troubleshooting Common Issues

Even with the best intentions, you might run into a few hiccups when feeding eggshells to your chickens. Don't worry, it's all part of the learning process! Here are some common issues you might encounter and how to troubleshoot them:

Chickens Not Eating Eggshells

Sometimes, chickens may be hesitant to eat crushed eggshells, especially if they’re not used to them. There are a few reasons why this might happen:

  • Shells Not Crushed Enough: If the eggshells are still in large pieces, your chickens might recognize them as eggs and avoid them. Make sure to crush the shells into small, unrecognizable pieces. This will help prevent them from associating the shells with fresh eggs.
  • Other Calcium Sources: If your chickens are getting enough calcium from their regular feed or other supplements, they might not feel the need to eat the eggshells. This is perfectly fine! It means their calcium needs are being met.
  • Palatability: Some chickens are just picky eaters! You can try mixing the crushed eggshells with a small amount of their favorite treat, like mealworms or scratch grains, to make them more appealing.

Chickens Eating Fresh Eggs

As we’ve mentioned before, one of the biggest concerns when feeding eggshells is the potential for chickens to start eating their own fresh eggs. This is a tricky habit to break once it starts, so prevention is key. Here are some tips to prevent egg-eating:

  • Crush Eggshells Thoroughly: Again, make sure the eggshells are crushed into small, unrecognizable pieces. This is the most important step in preventing egg-eating.
  • Collect Eggs Regularly: Collect eggs frequently throughout the day. This reduces the chances of chickens accidentally breaking an egg and developing a taste for it.
  • Provide a Comfortable Nesting Environment: Ensure your chickens have a comfortable and private nesting area. Stress and overcrowding can contribute to egg-eating behavior.
  • Provide Adequate Calcium: Make sure your chickens are getting enough calcium in their diet. Calcium deficiency can lead to egg-eating as hens try to compensate for the lack of calcium.

Soft-Shelled Eggs

If you’re still finding soft-shelled eggs despite feeding eggshells, it could indicate a more significant calcium deficiency or another underlying issue. Here are some steps to take:

  • Increase Calcium Intake: Try increasing the amount of calcium in your chickens' diet. You can offer more crushed eggshells, oyster shells, or a commercial calcium supplement.
  • Check Vitamin D Levels: Vitamin D is essential for calcium absorption. Make sure your chickens are getting enough sunlight or supplement their diet with vitamin D if necessary.
  • Rule Out Other Health Issues: Soft-shelled eggs can also be a sign of other health problems, such as a reproductive issue or an illness. If the problem persists, it’s best to consult with a veterinarian or poultry expert.

Conclusion: Happy Chickens, Healthy Eggs

Feeding eggshells to your chickens is a simple, sustainable, and effective way to boost their calcium intake and promote healthy egg production. By following the steps outlined in this guide, you can ensure your flock gets the calcium they need in a safe and natural way. Remember, drying and crushing the eggshells are crucial steps to prevent bacterial contamination and egg-eating behavior. Whether you mix the crushed shells into their feed or offer them as a free-choice supplement, your chickens will appreciate the extra calcium boost.

So, go ahead and start saving those eggshells! You'll be doing your chickens a favor and reducing waste at the same time. It's a win-win situation for everyone. Happy chicken keeping!

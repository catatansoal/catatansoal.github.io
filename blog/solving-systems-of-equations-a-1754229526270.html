Solving Systems Of Equations: A Complete Guide

Aug 3, 2025 by ADMIN 47 views

Choosing the correct solution(s) for a system of equations can sometimes feel like navigating a maze, but don't worry, we'll break it down step by step. This is all about finding the points where the graphs of the equations intersect. Let's get started, guys!

Diving into the Problem: The Equations

Our mission, should we choose to accept it, involves solving this system of equations. Here are the equations we're working with:

x2+y2=6x2−y=6\begin{array}{l} x^2+y^2=6 \\ x^2-y=6 \end{array}x2+y2=6x2−y=6​

The first equation, x2+y2=6x^2 + y^2 = 6x2+y2=6, represents a circle centered at the origin (0, 0) with a radius of 6\sqrt{6}6​. Remember, this is because the general form of a circle's equation is (x−h)2+(y−k)2=r2(x-h)^2 + (y-k)^2 = r^2(x−h)2+(y−k)2=r2, where (h, k) is the center, and r is the radius. Now, we can see that the center is (0,0) and r2=6r^2 = 6r2=6 therefore, r=6r = \sqrt{6}r=6​. The second equation, x2−y=6x^2 - y = 6x2−y=6, can be rearranged to y=x2−6y = x^2 - 6y=x2−6. This is a parabola opening upwards, with its vertex at (0, -6). To find the solution(s), we're looking for the points (x, y) that satisfy BOTH equations simultaneously. These points are where the circle and the parabola intersect.

We can solve this system through a method called substitution. It's like a mathematical detective story – we use one equation to learn something, then plug that into the other equation to find the solution. You'll see, it's super easy. The goal is to find the values of x and y that make both equations true. If you want to visualize this, imagine the circle and the parabola drawn on a graph. The solution(s) are the points where they actually touch each other. And that's it, we're on our way to find our solution, yay!

Solving the Equations: Unveiling the Solutions

Alright, let's get down to business and actually solve the equations, alright? From the second equation, we can see that x2=y+6x^2 = y + 6x2=y+6. Now, let's substitute this value of x2x^2x2 into the first equation. This gives us:

(y+6)+y2=6(y + 6) + y^2 = 6(y+6)+y2=6

Which simplifies to:

y2+y=0y^2 + y = 0y2+y=0

We can factor this to get:

y(y+1)=0y(y + 1) = 0y(y+1)=0

This gives us two possible values for y:

y=0y = 0y=0 or y=−1y = -1y=−1

Now, let's plug these values back into the equation x2=y+6x^2 = y + 6x2=y+6 to find the corresponding values of x.

If y=0y = 0y=0, then x2=0+6=6x^2 = 0 + 6 = 6x2=0+6=6, which means x=6x = \sqrt{6}x=6​ or x=−6x = -\sqrt{6}x=−6​. This gives us two possible solutions: (6,0)(\sqrt{6}, 0)(6​,0) and (−6,0)(-\sqrt{6}, 0)(−6​,0).

If y=−1y = -1y=−1, then x2=−1+6=5x^2 = -1 + 6 = 5x2=−1+6=5, which means x=5x = \sqrt{5}x=5​ or x=−5x = -\sqrt{5}x=−5​. This gives us two more possible solutions: (5,−1)(\sqrt{5}, -1)(5​,−1) and (−5,−1)(-\sqrt{5}, -1)(−5​,−1).

So, we've got a few potential solutions here. Now, we must determine which of these points satisfy BOTH equations. That's how you know they're real solutions and not just imposters. The process of solving systems of equations often involves algebraic manipulation, which can sometimes introduce extraneous solutions – solutions that appear to work but don't actually satisfy the original equations. That's why it's always a good idea to check your answers. Always double-check your answers, like, always. And that is the real path to solving the question. So, don't worry and just trust the process, you'll be fine!

Verification: Checking the Potential Solutions

Let's test each of our potential solutions in both of the original equations to make sure everything checks out, ok?

  1. Checking (6\sqrt{6}6​, 0):

    • In the first equation, (6)2+02=6+0=6(\sqrt{6})^2 + 0^2 = 6 + 0 = 6(6​)2+02=6+0=6. Check!
    • In the second equation, (6)2−0=6−0=6(\sqrt{6})^2 - 0 = 6 - 0 = 6(6​)2−0=6−0=6. Check!

    So, (6\sqrt{6}6​, 0) is a valid solution.

  2. Checking (−-−\sqrt{6}$, 0):

    • In the first equation, (−6)2+02=6+0=6(-\sqrt{6})^2 + 0^2 = 6 + 0 = 6(−6​)2+02=6+0=6. Check!
    • In the second equation, (−6)2−0=6−0=6(-\sqrt{6})^2 - 0 = 6 - 0 = 6(−6​)2−0=6−0=6. Check!

    So, (−-−\sqrt{6}$, 0) is also a valid solution.

  3. Checking (5\sqrt{5}5​, -1):

    • In the first equation, (5)2+(−1)2=5+1=6(\sqrt{5})^2 + (-1)^2 = 5 + 1 = 6(5​)2+(−1)2=5+1=6. Check!
    • In the second equation, (5)2−(−1)=5+1=6(\sqrt{5})^2 - (-1) = 5 + 1 = 6(5​)2−(−1)=5+1=6. Check!

    So, (5\sqrt{5}5​, -1) is a valid solution.

  4. Checking (−-−\sqrt{5}$, -1):

    • In the first equation, (−5)2+(−1)2=5+1=6(-\sqrt{5})^2 + (-1)^2 = 5 + 1 = 6(−5​)2+(−1)2=5+1=6. Check!
    • In the second equation, (−5)2−(−1)=5+1=6(-\sqrt{5})^2 - (-1) = 5 + 1 = 6(−5​)2−(−1)=5+1=6. Check!

    So, (−-−\sqrt{5}$, -1) is also a valid solution.

Wow, it seems like all the points we found are indeed solutions to the system of equations! This means all these points lie on both the circle and the parabola, like perfectly aligned.

The Solution: Identifying the Correct Options

Based on our verification, the following points are the solutions to the system of equations:

  • (6\sqrt{6}6​, 0)
  • (-6\sqrt{6}6​, 0)
  • (5\sqrt{5}5​, -1)
  • (-5\sqrt{5}5​, -1)

Now, let's check which of the given options match our solutions:

  • A. no solution - Incorrect, since we found solutions.
  • B. (6\sqrt{6}6​, 0) - Correct, this is one of our solutions.
  • C. (5\sqrt{5}5​, 1) - Incorrect, the y-value should be -1.
  • D. (5\sqrt{5}5​, -1) - Correct, this is one of our solutions.
  • E. infinitely many - Incorrect, the circle and parabola intersect at a finite number of points.

Therefore, the correct options are B and D.

Finding The Y-Coordinate Dividing A Line Segment In A 5:1 Ratio

Jul 13, 2025 by ADMIN 64 views

Hey guys! Today, we're diving into a fun geometry problem that involves finding the coordinates of a point that divides a line segment in a specific ratio. This is a classic problem that pops up in various math courses, from algebra to calculus, so it's definitely a concept worth mastering. We'll break down the formula, walk through the steps, and make sure you understand exactly how to tackle these types of questions.

Understanding the Section Formula

At the heart of this problem is the section formula. This formula helps us determine the coordinates of a point that divides a line segment joining two points in a given ratio. Let's say we have two points, J(x1,y1)J(x_1, y_1)J(x1​,y1​) and K(x2,y2)K(x_2, y_2)K(x2​,y2​), and we want to find a point P(x,y)P(x, y)P(x,y) that divides the line segment JKJKJK in the ratio m:nm:nm:n. The section formula states:

P(x,y)=(mx2+nx1m+n,my2+ny1m+n)P(x, y) = \left(\frac{mx_2 + nx_1}{m + n}, \frac{my_2 + ny_1}{m + n}\right)P(x,y)=(m+nmx2​+nx1​​,m+nmy2​+ny1​​)

Where:

  • (x,y)(x, y)(x,y) are the coordinates of the point dividing the line segment.
  • (x1,y1)(x_1, y_1)(x1​,y1​) are the coordinates of the first point, JJJ.
  • (x2,y2)(x_2, y_2)(x2​,y2​) are the coordinates of the second point, KKK.
  • mmm and nnn are the parts of the ratio in which the line is divided.

It's super important to remember that the order of the points and the ratio matters! Make sure you correctly identify which point is first and which is second, and that you assign the correct values to mmm and nnn based on the given ratio. A common mistake is to mix up mmm and nnn, which will lead to the wrong answer. So, pay close attention to the wording of the problem and double-check your values before plugging them into the formula.

Why does this formula work, you ask? Well, it's based on the concept of similar triangles. When you divide a line segment in a certain ratio, you're essentially creating two smaller triangles that are similar to the larger triangle formed by the original line segment and the coordinate axes. The ratios of corresponding sides in similar triangles are equal, and this relationship is what the section formula mathematically captures. You can actually derive the section formula using similar triangles, which is a cool exercise to try if you're interested in seeing the proof behind it!

Breaking Down the Formula for the yyy-coordinate

Since our question specifically asks for the yyy-coordinate, we can focus on just the second part of the section formula:

y=my2+ny1m+ny = \frac{my_2 + ny_1}{m + n}y=m+nmy2​+ny1​​

This formula tells us exactly how to calculate the yyy-coordinate of the point that divides the line segment. We simply multiply the yyy-coordinate of the second point (y2y_2y2​) by mmm, multiply the yyy-coordinate of the first point (y1y_1y1​) by nnn, add those two products together, and then divide by the sum of mmm and nnn. Easy peasy, right?

Now, let's get into the specific details of our problem and see how we can apply this formula to find the answer.

Applying the Formula to Our Problem

Okay, so our main goal is to figure out the yyy-coordinate of a point that splits a line segment from point JJJ to point KKK in a 5:15:15:1 ratio. Let's start by clearly defining what we know. We're given a ratio of 5:15:15:1, which means m=5m = 5m=5 and n=1n = 1n=1. Remember, mmm corresponds to the segment closer to point KKK, and nnn corresponds to the segment closer to point JJJ.

However, we're missing a crucial piece of information: the actual coordinates of points JJJ and KKK! Without knowing the yyy-coordinates (y1y_1y1​ and y2y_2y2​), we can't directly plug the values into our formula. This is a typical trick in math problems – they often give you some information but require you to find the missing pieces before you can solve it. So, to fully answer this question, we would need the coordinates of points JJJ and KKK.

Directed Line Segments: The Key to Understanding Order

The question specifically mentions a directed line segment. This term is crucial because it tells us the order in which we're considering the points. A directed line segment from JJJ to KKK means we start at point JJJ and move towards point KKK. This direction matters when we're calculating the ratio and applying the section formula.
